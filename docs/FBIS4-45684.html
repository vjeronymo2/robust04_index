BFN [Text] Budapest, 24 May (MTI) -- A sophisticated environment-protection programme for the Danube region has been initiated recently by the Hungarian Ministry of Environment and Regional Policy. Hungary also claims that an international project should be approved in order to provide for all drainage areas of the river. Hungary is the only European country threatened by critial conditions in water supply around the year 2000. The Food and Agriculture Organization of the United Nations (FAO) registers Hungary among the first 30 (mainly desert) states whose drinking water supply is not secured for the future. Ninety-five percent of the surface waterways of the country originate abroad, which causes purity and quantity problems. In 1992 Hungary initiated an environment-protection programme for the Danube Basin. In order to solve present problems, which foreshadow further ones in the future, water management should be regulated under an agreement, with strong commitment by all concerned to carry through their respective obligations, Hungarian authorities say. Hungarian specialists say that although the country's water reserves are expected to decline in the following years, they will probably still meet all demand after the turn of the century. Waiting to see the issue of surface waterways settled, Hungary is striving to preserve its underground water reserves. There is no agreement with Ukraine, Romania, or Slovakia that would oblige these countries to inform Hungary on water pollution occurring in their own territories. Slovakia, for example, takes out such a large amount of water from the river Sajo each summer that the Sajo enters Hungary no longer as a river but rather as a small stream. Besides, Slovakia operates two barrage irrigation systems on the river Ipoly. Another major problem is the decline in water reserves near the Gabcikovo barrage system. Water filtered through gravel beds is excellent for consumption. Wells operating on this principle supply the population of the Danube Bend and Budapest through a pipe water system and might serve as the drinking water source of around 4 million people in the future. (The population of Hungary is 10.3 million.) Nitrate pollutants have to be filtered in these wells too, although 15 years ago this was not necessary. (Most of the pollution is due to the lack of sewage networks in nearby settlements.) The river Tisza in the eastern part of Hungary, which springs from Ukraine and has been known for its clean water over the past 25 years, has recently become polluted at places. The river Koros, originating in Romania, has also declined in quality. "Karst" and artesian waters are the real preserves of Hungary. According to recent figures, their quantity exceeds the consumption of the public, industry, and agriculture. Though mining has caused long-lasting and difficult-to-remedy changes, experts expect earlier, better conditions to return within a decade's time. The Danube-Tisza region in eastern Hungary has been seriously endangered by the lack of irrigation water. In the past 15-20 years, successive droughts have reduced the ground water surface. Traditional plants being unable to absorb water from 3-6 metres down, new species had to be introduced. However, declines in the region's agricultural production have been considerable. Ninety-eight per cent of Hungary's population drinks piped water. Per capita water consumption has fallen to 150 litres per day from 250 litres in earlier years. Experts say that drinking water should be separated from water used for other purposes (washing, watering, etc.) with an extra pipe system added, as it has been already in some western European countries.
