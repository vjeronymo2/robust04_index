BFN [Text] Beijing, May 20 KYODO -- North Korea on Friday [20 May] rejected demands by the International Atomic Energy Agency (IAEA) that it halt removal of spent fuel rods without proper inspections at an experimental nuclear reactor 90 kilometers north of Pyongyang. North Korean Ambassador to China Chu Chang-chun told reporters that a resolution of the issue would be conditioned on resumption of bilateral talks with the United States, suspended after two rounds last July. "When the third round of talks between the DPRK (North Korea) and the U.S. are held and a package solution is realized, then the agency (IAEA) can take the measurements at any time," he said. Earlier in the day, the IAEA confirmed that despite its warnings, North Korea had started removing spent fuel rods from its 5-megawatt experimental reactor without IAEA inspectors present. The U.N. nuclear watchdog agency said it has sent a letter to North Korea, warning that further removal of the rods without proper inspections would be a violation of the Nuclear Nonproliferation Treaty (NPT) and safeguard agreements concluded between North Korea and the IAEA. The IAEA demanded the operations be suspended until arrangements can be made to ensure the rods are properly marked and separated for sampling. The three IAEA inspectors now at the Yongbyon nuclear complex since Wednesday have not been allowed to take samples or make tests on the spent rods. The IAEA has insisted that the rods must be sorted and examined to see whether plutonium has been diverted for a nuclear weapons program. Without the samples, the IAEA has said it will not be able to determine whether North Korea has complied with a nuclear safeguards agreement. The Vienna-based agency said it is preparing to dispatch another delegation of experts to North Korea to discuss safeguards measures. U.S. officials have said that spent fuel currently being withdrawn from the reactor could provide enough plutonium to build four or five atomic bombs. Refusal to comply with the IAEA's demands would also raise the possibility the watchdog agency will refer the problem to the U.N. Security Council, which could impose economic sanctions. On Thursday, U.S. President Bill Clinton reacted cautiously to reports that Pyongyang has begun removing spent fuel rods without the presence of international inspectors. "I want to know what the facts are, and when I do, then I will make a more definite statement," he told reporters. Clinton called it "an error" for North Korea to thwart IAEA inspections after it had agreed to comply with them. A South Korean Foreign Ministry spokesman expressed regret and concern Friday that North Korea has already begun removing the rods in defiance of international demands not to do so. Seoul also urged Pyongyang to immediately resume talks with the IAEA to discuss matters of full compliance with their nuclear safeguards agreement. "South Korea will consult closely with allies about what steps it will take, watching the result of talks between North Korea and the IAEA," the spokesman said. Meanwhile, Pyongyang's official KOREAN CENTRAL NEWS AGENCY (KCNA) quoted North Korean President Kim Il-song as saying in a recent interview, "we feel no need to develop nuclear weapons and even if we were to manufacture one or two of them, they would be useless." North Korea insists its nuclear power program is only for peaceful purposes, but refusal to comply with inspection demands has aroused suspicions that it may be trying to build an atomic bomb. A senior U.S. administration official, speaking on condition of anonymity, told reporters in Washington that the U.S. will drop its "patient diplomacy" if Pyongyang is found to have cheated on nuclear inspections. "We're prepared to consider other options," he said, adding that the U.S. sees an advantage in exhausting diplomatic means to persuade the North Koreans to give up their alleged nuclear development before applying sanctions. The U.S. has indicated it would pursue economic sanctions against Pyongyang through the United Nations or together with Japan and South Korea if the nuclear program is not made more transparent. Pyongyang has said it would consider sanctions an act of war.
