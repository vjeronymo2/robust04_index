Language: Spanish Article Type:BFN [Text] Buenos Aires, 15 Mar (NA) -- Foreign Minister Guido Di Tella today confirmed the dispatch of Argentine troops to the Bosnia-Herzegovina region. He pointed out, however, that it still must be determined if the troops will travel from Buenos Aires or if some of the troops serving in Croatia will be transferred. Therefore, Di Tella confirmed that Argentina will provide military cooperation in the Bosnia-Herzegovina region, accepting a petition made by UN Secretary General Butrus-Butrus Ghali, who also asked for a special $10 million financial contribution for the peace operation there. Before opening an OAS-sponsored seminar on the security issue at the Alvear Hotel in this capital, the foreign minister admitted today: "We are studying if the troops deployed in Croatia will be transferred, new troops will travel, or a mixed contingent, involving these two alternatives, will be deployed." For his part, Defense Minister Oscar Camilion said Argentina "will not be squandering money" by dispatching a 150-man contingent to the Bosnia region. He said: "It is a reasonable contribution to the peace of a country whose fate has caught the world's attention." Thus, Camilion challenged the criticism leveled by some political leaders over Foreign Minister Guido Di Tella's compromise with UN Secretary General Butrus Butrus-Ghali. These political leaders understand that there are other priority issues. In this regard, Camilion admitted that "there are other pressing needs," but championed the governmental decision to dispatch troops to Europe's most conflictive region because "it represents a medium-term interest in the existence of a global situation of good relations." The defense minister summarized: "Argentina will play a small role in this peace process, because there will be thousands of troops there and Argentina will merely contribute 150 soldiers." The Argentine compromise was established on 14 March after a meeting between Argentine President Carlos Menem and the UN secretary general, who also asked for a special financial contribution for the funds allocated to the peacekeeping operations. Argentina has more than 800 troops deployed in Croatia, a region in the former Yugoslavia where there are virtually no more armed clashes. Therefore, Butrus-Ghali sought to guarantee Argentina's presence in Bosnia, where the United Nations plans to deploy between 10,000 and 15,000 Blue Helmets.
