BFN [Report by Wilmer Murillo] [Excerpts] Mexico City -- Mexican President Carlos Salinas de Gortari and Costa Rican President Rafael Angel Calderon yesterday signed a treaty that establishes a free trade zone between the two countries. [passage omitted] The Free Trade Agreement [FTA] covers issues such as access to markets, norms of origin, agriculture, technical hours, phytosanitary issues, protection measures, disloyal actions, government purchases, investments, services, intellectual copyright, and settlement of controversies. The agreement will allow a better flow of trade and investments, and it will entail growth and welfare for the two countries. According to political and business leaders, this is the most advanced agreement signed to date between two Latin American nations; and it will have particularly beneficial effects for Costa Rica, whose trade balance with Mexico has a 90-million colones deficit. Certain Mexican newspapers said the treaty was not timely and prompted by the two countries' political interests. Nevertheless, the deep emotion that prevailed during the ceremony where the document was signed proved the need to reassert historical and cultural ties with Latin America after joining the great northern trade bloc -- NAFTA with Canada and the United States. Samuel Yankelewitz, president of the Chamber of Industries, emphasized the treaty is very technical and includes all the necessary elements for things to work correctly. Although he admitted it was certainly necessary to hasten things because the current administration is about to end, he warned that things were done correctly despite the haste. This resulted in a fair and balanced treaty for the two nations. Doris Osterlof, executive director of the Chamber of Exporters, referred to this issue and said businessmen are ready to face the challenge of competing with the Mexicans. "We have an industry that is ready because we have experimented and competed in the Central American market for 30 years. Now our industry will expand its limits to the Mexican market," she said. According to Costa Rican businessmen's estimates, the country may quintuple its current exports to Mexico in five years -- from 60 million colones to 250 million colones. Carlos Alvarado, president of the Chamber of Commerce, announced that businessmen began their contacts this week to attract foreign investments from Costa Rica to Mexico. The Mexican and Costa Rican stock exchanges signed an agreement after the FTA was signed, opening the market to new financial opportunities; and the International Bank of Costa Rica (BICSA) announced it will open a new support office in Guatemala to study the possibility of going to Mexico. This way the bank can continue to support everything dealing with foreign trade, BICSA Vice President Arturo Cuevillas noted. According to official sources, the FTA signed yesterday will allow investments in Costa Rica to increase by approximately 10 billion colones. The possibility is a result of the attraction Costa Rica will have as a point of access to a market of 90 million people. Mexican businessmen will be able to install their factories here and export to their country. Currently 80 billion colones, approximately 8 percent of the GDP, in Mexican capital has been invested here. Meanwhile, businessmen from other countries who are seeking access to the Mexican market through the tariff advantages that the agreement grants products manufactured in Costa Rica, will be able to do so. Hitachi, National Batteries, Ticatex, Cocoa Products, and Salas Lizano are included in that category. The agreement foresees the immediate elimination of tariffs for 70 percent of the Mexican exports; for 20 percent in five years; and for 10 percent in 10 years. Also, 75 percent of national exports will be cleared immidiately; 15 percent will be cleared in five years; and 10 percent will be cleared in 10 years.
