December 3, 1989, Sunday, Home Edition

MOBILIZING AMERICA FOR THE COMING AGE OF PEACE;

ECONOMY: IF WE ARE TO BREAK OUR MILITARY SPENDING HABIT, WE MUST HELP DEFENSE WORKERS TO MAKE THE TRANSITION.

The times, as Bob Dylan once sang, they are a-changin'. Walls are tumbling down in Germany. Fences are coming down in Czechoslovakia. The people of the Soviet Bloc are demanding democracy. Elections are happening in Namibia, and mass rallies are taking place in South Africa. The leaders of the United States and the Soviet Union are meeting in a new spirit.

What does this new spirit mean for the United States?

First, it means that we can reduce war tensions and bring down the federal budget deficit, much of which was caused by runaway military spending. The $286-billion military budget signed by President Bush last week is a relic from another era. America's leaders must face the reality that we can no longer afford extravagant spending on weapons we don't need for wars that will never be fought against enemies that don't exist.

It also means that we can finally begin to reinvest in America by moving from preparation for war to preparation for peace and justice. We must prepare now for economic conversion, the transformation of military-related industry into productive civilian enterprise. The prophet Isaiah told us to beat our swords into plowshares and our spears into pruning hooks. The weapons of war must be made into instruments of peace.

As the Cold War thaws and the Warsaw Pact rapidly dissolves, as NATO forces in Europe -- including 305,000 U.S. men and women in uniform there -- look increasingly unnecessary and obsolete, it is incumbent on us to plan an orderly transition for our military-based economy. We must convert our moral vison and social priorities.

From 1981 through 1988, as military spending nearly doubled, spending on education, employment training, housing and community development, urban mass transit, public health and community block grants was cut by close to $60 billion.

It is now time to reverse this drain of budget resources from the human economy. Bridges are falling down; teachers need raises; the environment needs attention; masses of homeless wander the streets; victims of AIDS seek treatment and help, and the drug crisis threatens to overrun our communities.

For the $100-billion cost of the Trident II submarine and F-18 jet fighter programs, we could clean up the 10,000 worst toxic waste dumps in America.

For the cost of six months of outlays for nuclear warheads, roughly $4 billion, we could fund twice over the child-care bill recently put on hold by Congress. Such a shift in funds would bring real hope to working mothers and enable many people to get off welfare.

For the cost of the Stealth bomber program, $68 billion, we could fund a national health insurance program. With 40 million Americans lacking basic health coverage, don't we need a national health plan more than a national Stealth plan?

The major obstacle to the civilian conversion plan we need is our habit of reliance on military spending. All told, according to the National Commission for Economic Conversion and Disarmament, more than 20 million Americans are dependent on the military economy for survival.

These Americans should not be threatened by the outbreak of peace, freedom and justice. On the contrary, we must harness the genius of our scientists, the creativity of our engineers, the courage of our soldiers and the service instinct of our armed forces to plan for peaceful reconstruction of our nation.

Rep. Ted Weiss (D-N.Y.) has introduced a bill, the Defense Economic Adjustment Act, that would make the federal government responsible for supporting economic conversion planning in the defense sector and helping those communities "substantially and seriously affected" by defense cuts to make the transition.

The legislation, moreover, would establish "alternative use" committees at every defense facility employing at least 100 people. These committees of management and labor would develop detailed plans for converting the facility to productive civilian use and re-employing the work force within two years of losing a defense contract or military base. Also, the bill would make displaced workers eligible for retraining and displacement benefits for up to two years.

The Weiss bill is moving in precisely the right direction. The transition to a labor-intensive peace economy will not be easy after so many years of relying on capital-intensive military spending. But the needs of our people are great, and weapons production does nothing to address them. We must become as serious about mobilizing the American people for the coming age of peace and justice as we were about mobilizing ourselves for war and violence.
