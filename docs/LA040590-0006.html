April 5, 1990, Thursday, Home Edition

FOOD AND COMMON SENSE: PEOPLE WHO LIVE ALONE SHOULD AVOID CATCH-AS-CATCH-CAN DINING HABIT

People who live alone can be divided, by diet, into two groups. At one extreme are those who plan, shop and prepare meals with the same vigor as if they were cooking for a family of four. At the other are those whose refrigerators may contain nothing more than a jar of mustard, a loaf of weary bread, a stick of margarine and a six-pack of diet soda.

If you -- or someone you care about -- fits into the latter category, maybe it's time to consider a change.

"Dinner for one, please, James . . . Madam will not be dining tonight" go the words to an old song. But most who dine solo don't have a "James" to prepare the meal for them. And although making single servings might sound easier than feeding an entire family, it sometimes turns out to be as hard or even harder.

When eating alone, we frequently don't bother fixing a nutritious, balanced meal. Instead we grab whatever's quick and handy: Leftovers, snacks or over-processed convenience foods. Sometimes the meal consists of only one kind of food rather than an appetizing mix. Fruits and vegetables get forgotten in the rush.

This type of dining can affect not only the composition of the meal but the whole experience as well. Such a repast might be eaten standing over the sink, in front of the videocassette recorder, while reading, or on the run. Food is consumed either rapidly, absent mindedly, or both, rather than in a relaxed manner in which each bite is savored. And thanks to the microwave, you can stick processed food in the oven, wait for the bell, eat it and, presto, you're done.

Nearly everybody eats this way on occasion. But when the catch-as-catch-can style becomes a habit, you may need to work on improving a nutritional profile.

A place to start is the supermarket. People playing shopper's solitaire rightly point out that it's hard to buy products in small enough quantities. Although some single-serving canned goods are available, most frozen foods (aside from complete dinners) come in large sizes. Half-loaves of bread are rare, and many fresh foods come in family-size packs.

Numerous stores now offer serve-yourself salad bars, but some people find them too costly. Rather than buy a head of lettuce only to find half of it rotting in the refrigerator a week later, they may omit salad entirely. Their only choice, they say, is between wasting food or eating the same thing over and over.

We recognize that it's not easy, but don't throw good nutrition to the wind. Eating alone doesn't make you less important. The same principles of prudent eating apply no matter how many or few are sitting at the table. And with a little extra effort, you can create varied, nutritious meals -- even if you're on your own.

Planning is the key. At the supermarket, use a basic checklist. Included should be leafy greens and yellow vegetables, citrus fruits, low-fat dairy products, meats, fish, eggs, poultry, dried beans and peas, as well as whole-grain or enriched breads and cereals and rice and grain products such as pastas. Make selections with these important categories in mind.

Plan to use highly perishable fruits and vegetables earlier in the week. Later in the week, you can depend on frozen vegetables. Buy these in pourable bags, which are easier to re-close than boxes.

Plan ahead in cooking too. Think in broader terms than just meal by meal. If you've bought larger amounts of ingredients (an economical way to shop), you can cook extra servings and store them. Pasta sauces or soups with dried beans can be kept in the freezer in microwave containers for defrosting later on. Along with pasta, salad and a piece of fresh fruit, they make a delicious, nutritious meal. Or cook a sizeable pot of beef stew, which will serve you in good stead for two or three days. Any extra will last a few months in the freezer.

Some people find it most convenient to do this type of larger-scale cooking on the weekend. If you're having guests over for a meal, you can also cook extra servings at that time and store them for later use too. An added advantage to relying on the freezer in this way: If you decide to go out to dinner with friends on the spur of the moment, the food will keep just fine. Or, if you invite a friend home, you'll have a full larder to share.

The main message here is that while spontaneity in many matters adds zest to life, a little foresight in the supermarket and advance work in the kitchen lets you shop more economically and eat more nutritiously. It also gives the solo meal a touch of class.
