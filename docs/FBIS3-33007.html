Language: Dutch Article Type:BFN [Interview with Rik Coolsaet, deputy chief of cabinet to Foreign Minister Claes, by Jorgen Oosterwaal; place and date not given: "The Eye of Moscow"--first two paragaphs are KNACK introduction] [Excerpts] [passage omitted] Rik Coolsaet is deputy chief of cabinet to Foreign Minister Willy Claes, of the Socialist Party [SP]. He has long been the SP's defense expert and was appointed recently lecturer in international relations at Ghent University. [passage omitted] Coolsaet fully understands why the West is being cautious and why NATO, for the time being, is not offering East Europe more than the Partnership for Peace. A surprising view for a man who for more than a decade has been encouraging the Belgian Government to adopt a more active and daring Ostpolitik. [passage omitted] [Oosterwaal] East and West seem ready to tie the bond, but because Yeltsin is holding back, it seems we will first have to cohabit for a while yet? [Coolsaet] Why should NATO be enlarged? Because we like the company of the East European countries? Because we share the same values and they belong with us? Or in order to ensure the stability of Central and East Europe? NATO is not a cultural organization, which means that only the latter reason is valid. U.S. Assistant Secretary of State Strobe Talbott assumes that stability in Europe will de determined by developments in Russia. I believe he is right in his assumption. We must take full account of Moscow. We must know what the Russians are thinking. Why is Russia so against the enlargement of NATO? Because it sees it as a move toward a dangerous isolation. Since 1917 that has been a permanent fear for the Russians. We must not fuel this paranoia. We can draw a parallel with the Ostpolitik of the former German Chancellor Willy Brandt. He could only begin his detente efforts after he had assured the Russians of the status quo in Europe, and thus by acknowledging Russia's place in Europe. Another example is the Cuban crisis. U.S. President John Kennedy was able to resolve it successfully because he did not start bombing immediately but tried instead to understand how Nikita Khruschev saw the conflict. [Oosterwaal] The West hopes to keep Yeltsin in place because it is horrified by his rival, Zhirinovskiy. But the West has little or no influence on Russia's domestic affairs. [Coolsaet] The best guarantee for stability in Europe is a democratic Russia. Whatever it takes, we must not allow an authoritarian, despotic system to develop in the former Soviet Union which would be opposed to the West. But how? By supporting the forces for democracy. I also know that our impact is very limited. But however limited this influence may be, we must at least try. If we believe that Russia is automatically embarking on a new imperialist adventure, then we cannot hesitate. Then we must enlarge the alliance as quickly as possible. But I do not believe that this scenario is unavoidable. Russian society consists of a Slavic, nationalist component and a Western-oriented component. It has been that way since Peter the Great. It is one of the keys to an understanding of Russia. Zhirinovskiy is the crystallization of a frustrated nation which is feeding off anti-Western Slavic sentiments. His success is not surprising. There is great unease in Russia. By immediately allowing NATO to extend right up to the borders of the former Soviet Union we would be lending force to this Slavophile and xenophobic nationalism. As long as the power play between the Slavophiles and those who look to the West has not been decided, we are right not to enlarge NATO. Because by doing so we would be undermining the stability of the continent and also of Central Europe. [Oosterwaal] But in the recent past the West already counted for too long on a single Soviet leader. [Coolsaet] It is not a question of a single leader, Mikhail Gorbachev, Yeltsin, or anyone else. Do not forget that Russia has no democratic tradition. Russia has always known autocratic government. This has usually been accompanied by a radical opposition. This is the second key which we need in order to understand anything about Russia. The opposition between Yeltsin and Zhirinovskiy is a part of this tradition. The only real alternative comes from political parties which are born out of a pluralist democracy.

Economy
Coolsaet believes in the need for stability not just in Central Europe but throughout the continent. This was evident from his lecture at a recent colloquium at Ghent university. "A new world order is growing up gradually, leading to more pronounced economic regionalism around three major industrial poles : NAFTA in America, Tokyo and Peking in Southeast Asia, and Europe. West Europe is not in the best of positions at the moment. It is surrounded by turbulent regions which are running their economies badly. If Europe still wants to count for anything in the 21st century then we cannot remain blind to developments outside the borders of the Union. I am not only thinking of East Europe. In the long term, the stability of Africa is certainly equally important," he stated. The situation is thus very clear to Coolsaet: The military alone provides for no stability. George Kennan, the father of the postwar policy of containment toward the Soviet Union, knew this. Stability has much more to do with economic factors and the functioning of institutions. The present power vacuum in Central Europe is in some ways comparable to the situation after the Second World War. The sense of insecurity was relieved by linking the economies, via the European Coal and Steel Community and later the European Community. This unification brought harmony, peace and prosperity. The European Union could thus serve East Europe far better than NATO.
