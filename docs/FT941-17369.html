940105 FT 05 JAN 94 / Recruiters 'rank honesty highly' Small businesses place a far higher value on honesty, integrity and enthusiasm for the job when recruiting young people and are less worried about work experience or vocational and academic qualifications, a report said yesterday. The study's findings, reported at the British Psychological Society's occupational psychology conference in Birmingham, appeared alongside other research which showed that managers preferred job applicants to admit their mistakes at an interview rather than blame their failings on others. The study carried out on 498 small businesses by Hull University and Newland Park Associates, a firm of occupational psychology consultants, investigated selection methods used when companies were recruiting people under the age of 20. The researchers also found that companies in manufacturing and production placed less emphasis on education and vocational qualifications than those in the financial and business services sectors. Many companies in the study were happy to recruit from initial telephone inquiries or a 'knock on the door' rather than insisting upon written applications. Job selection was based typically on a single unstructured interview. 'The smaller the employer, the more they seem to rely on gut reaction and first impressions,' said Professor David Bartram of Hull University, who headed the study. In a second report, Ms Jo Silvester, a Leeds University psychologist, told the conference that job seekers who admitted their personal failings at interviews were more likely to be successful than those who hid their mistakes behind a veneer of perfection. The research was based on tape-recorded interviews between employers and 45 undergraduates seeking jobs with a City law firm and a national distribution company. Ms Silvester said: 'People who use excuses or pass the buck on to others are actually seen as quite arrogant by interviewers and create a bad impression.'
