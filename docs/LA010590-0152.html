January 5, 1990, Friday, Orange County Edition

FOR A CATCHY NAME, CONSULT THE PUN & BRADSTREET OF CUTE

Let's Pet is an invitation to . . . buy an animal, A Flush Beats A Full House does plumbing and Honest Engines repairs motors.

Punmanship is giving American entrepreneurship a new name, says a University of Illinois author whose new book lists dozens of businesses nationwide that have been bitten by the pun bug.

Dennis Baron, a professor of English and linguistics at the Urbana campus, said Tuesday that what he calls "cutespeak" is a growing trend aimed at capturing customers' attention as well as their pocketbooks.

"People are going to be attracted to a place with an outrageous name," he said in a telephone interview. "One of the ploys of advertising is to have something that sticks in the consumer's mind."

That ploy seems to work for one of Baron's favorite businesses, The Brick Shirt House in Croton-on-Hudson, N.Y.

"Originally it was named because it moved into a red brick building and we sold T-shirts," said owner Henry Haase. "Everybody laughs when they hear it and they say, 'You know what it almost sounds like."'

"It was something we figured people definitely would remember," Haase said.

They did. The business quickly caught on and since opening about eight years ago has expanded and moved to larger quarters. But the store has kept the same logo -- a little outhouse with a T-shirt for a window.

Dyna Sewer, a Chicago plumbing outfit that calls itself "the rooter giant," also says the punny name has helped business.

"You know it does help business because it does attract peoples' attention," said dispatcher Dennis Weisenritter Jr.

The company is an example of punmanship expanding into previously uncharted territory, because cutesy names used to be reserved mostly for restaurants and bars, Baron said.

"You don't think of plumbers with a trendy name," he said, but there's the San Francisco-area plumber A Flush Beats A Full House. There's also a mechanic in the Southwest called Honest Engines and a gynecologist's office in the Chicago area called Ladies First.

These are among the 100-plus businesses listed in a chapter of Baron's new book, "Declining Grammar," which examines various aspects of language. The book was published in Urbana by the National Council of Teachers of English.

Even Baron thinks puns can go too far.

"A good witticism is enjoyable the first time around, and sometimes the second or third time around," he writes. "But frequent repetition sours even the best of jokes, and . . . the staying power of funny names is seriously limited."

Some names can even be embarrassing, Baron says. Take the Chicago pet store called "Let's Pet."

"What's it like to work in a place like that?" Baron asked. "Do you answer the phone and say, 'Hello, Let's Pet?' Or maybe just have the dogs breathing heavily?"

An employee who answered the phone saying, "Let's Pet" and identified himself only as "Bill" said he didn't know how the store got its name, and referred all questions to the owner, who was not in.

Cutespeak can also be too cute.

That was a worry of Belinda Norris and Barbara Bird when they opened their Boston caterer, Currier & Chives.

In creating the business 6 1/2 years ago, "We wanted it to be memorable more than anything else," Norris said. "I think the only objection on Barbara's part was that (the name) might be too cute."

So far, no one's complained.

"It's been an incredible name for us," she said. "People have really responded to our name. The phone calls are usually, 'I love your name.' "
