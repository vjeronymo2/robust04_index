July 9, 1990, Monday, San Diego County Edition

THREE'S COMPANY LO-TECH DANCE SERIES IS OFF TO A FAST-PACED START

Three's Company launched its Lo-Tec Series with three drop-dead dances by an innovative pair of modernists to anchor the program. Add to that, the striking presence of the most arresting performers seen on this summer dance series in recent memory, and you have an opening concert that will be a hard act to follow.

Lar Lubovitch principal Nancy Colahan was the featured attraction, and she stole the show with two stunning solos from modern dance pioneer Jane Dudley's retrospective concert.

Dudley (one-time Martha Graham soloist) is one of the unsung heroes of modern dance. Fortunately, Colahan's beautifully-realized re-creations of "Harmonica Breakdown," the choreographer's brilliant signature piece, and "Cante Flamenco," a fiery, flamenco-inspired work dedicated to the great Spanish dancer La Passionara, gave San Diego dance buffs an opportunity to see some of the groundbreaking work Dudley did back in the 1930s and '40s.

Colahan was electrifying in the highly-theatrical dances, despite the low level of technical support available at the Hillcrest studio. And her splendid costumes (authentically reproduced from Dudley's marvelous originals) enhanced the effect.

The choppy robotics and angular leg lifts of "Harmonica Breakdown" contrasted sharply with the flamboyant port de bras and defiant backbends of Dudley's flamenco-style dance. Seen together, they left little doubt about Dudley's capacity for invention or her keen choreographic eye.

Although Colahan has danced in San Diego before (as a member of the acclaimed Lubovitch troupe), it took this lo-tec appearance to highlight her powerful stage magic. Let's hope Three's Company gets her back for an encore next year.

The Lo-Tec concerts also showcased a contemporary classic, "Three Tangos," choreographed in 1985 by the late Jack Moore. This staging, danced by a trio of Three's Company summer interns, was set by New York-based dancer Lance Westergard.

The inventive triptych of solos was cleverly juxtaposed against a musical pastiche that underscored "Tango's" parody with refreshing wit. Nicely danced by the well-drilled performers, it got the concert off to a running start Saturday night.

Colahan's husband, Lonne Moretton, was on hand for the kick-off concert -- but only as choreographer.

Moretton created "In Flight" especially for the five local dancers. And they executed the ebullient airborne moves with admirable assurance, their student status notwithstanding.

Two of the interns were invited to dance their own designs. Vicki Tolleson (one-time Merce Cunningham dancer) performed "Some Rhythm, No Reason," which more or less lived up to its name. Gail Olsen came up with "Breeze," a vigorous solo that kept her jumping along the lines of a jazzy score.

Long-limbed Tonnie Haig snared the spotlight in Westergard's "Leaves of Hypnos," an evocative dance that took its cues from the piano plucks of a score by David Koblitz, and showed how far she has come in a short while.

The Lo-Tec series will continue to occupy Three's Company's Hillcrest studio at 8:30 Saturday and Sunday nights through mid-September.

If the rest of the series comes close to upholding last weekend's standards, it will provide quite a summer bounty for local aficionados.
