February 9, 1990, Friday, Home Edition

MARKET BEAT / TOM PETRUNO: HIGH-TECH STOCKS REDISCOVERED

Just in time for Valentine's Day, investors' love-hate relationship with high-tech stocks is back in the I-can't-get-enough-of-you mode.

While the broad market was plunging in January, many high-tech stocks were inching higher. And over the past two weeks, some of the tech stocks have turned red hot. Year-to-date, many are up 20% or more. And some smart money is betting we're just at the start of a major surge.

The buyers have focused not so much on the giants of the computer business but on many of the smaller companies -- especially those that make specialized computer equipment, such as workstations, and those that supply key computer parts, such as disk drives. Some of the big gainers include Seagate Technology, Conner Peripherals and Sun Microsystems -- all California-based.

What's happened in recent weeks, analysts say, is the realization by Wall Street that while the computer business is slowing with the economy, it isn't collapsing -- at least not for personal computers and other desktop equipment. Inventories appear to be under control, and many tech firms are far stronger financially than a few years ago.

Last fall, at the first signs of weakness in the business, many investors assumed the worst and tech stocks were murdered. "Particularly in the personal computer business, there was so much negative stuff," says analyst Steven Ossad at Montgomery Securities in San Francisco. "Now, there's an acceptance that while (PC sales) annual growth isn't 25% to 35%, it's still in the 15%-plus category worldwide. And that's still pretty good."

If investors overreacted last year, their nervousness was understandable. Many high-tech stocks rode a roller coaster in the 1980s as boom-bust cycles repeated. Plenty of companies alive at the start of the '80s don't exist now. Value Line Investment Survey notes that, of 24 computer equipment makers it followed at the start of the '80s, 11 no longer are in the business.

Indeed, tech stocks are in some ways Wall Street's favorite horror story. But as in many horror stories, tech stocks left for dead often return as monster performers just as most investors turn their backs on the group.

That's the case once again. The renewed interest in the stocks is partly attributable to a show-and-tell conference on tech stocks that Goldman, Sachs & Co. hosted in New York this week. Analysts say that while little new information came out of the conference, big investors woke up to the reality that tech stocks are depressed, compared to the companies' healthy earnings prospects for 1990.

Seagate, which makes minicomputer and PC hard disk drives -- the devices that enable data to be stored and retrieved by a computer -- is a good example of what analysts consider an undervalued stock. It is expected to earn about $2 a share in the year ending June 30. So at $18.25 on Thursday, the stock is selling for just nine times earnings. In contrast, the average stock, as measured by the Standard & Poor's 500-stock index, sells for about 13 times estimated 1990 earnings.

Analyst John Rossi of Alex. Brown & Sons in San Francisco thinks that shares of Seagate and several other disk drive makers, including Maxtor Corp., still have much further to rise. His optimism stems partly from the consolidation of the industry in the 1980s: As competitors have fallen away -- including the most recent casualty, MiniScribe Corp., last year -- the survivors are in better shape to make good money off the demand expected for more powerful computers in the '90s.

Rossi's main fear is that the Japanese will somehow figure out how to grab control of the hard disk drive market. Still, he notes, despite their efforts to make inroads in the 1980s, Japanese drive makers were able to raise their U.S. market share to just 15% by last year, from 5% in 1980.

Among tech-stock fans, some picks now include:

* Conner Peripherals. Also a disk drive maker, Conner is a major supplier to Compaq Computer and is "talking with a lot of other companies about the right kind of products" for the '90s, Ossad says. He estimates that Conner will earn $1.55 a share this year. At $15.25 on Thursday, that gives the stock a price/earnings ratio (P/E) of just under 10.

* Autodesk. This firm makes software for computer-aided design and drafting. Richard Frucci, manager of the $100-million Putnam Information Sciences Trust mutual fund in Boston, doubled his holdings in Autodesk in January. He says the company has a "unique position selling their type of software for the desktop computer" market, which continues to boom. As earnings have soared since 1985 ($2.70 a share is estimated for 1990), the stock has risen to $44 from $4.

For Technophobes, a Better Way: If the tech stock rally piques your interest, but you're terrified of ending up with a bankrupt, there's a way to lower your risk: Use mutual funds that specialize in tech stocks. There are 24 such funds, according to Lipper Analytical Services.

Over the past 12 months (through last week), the average tech-stock fund gained just 8.4%, compared to 9.6% for general stock funds, Lipper says. The tech group under-performed because investors shunned the stocks for much of last year. But if the group is indeed being rediscovered in 1990, the tech funds' performance should reflect that. And the funds lower your risk of being hurt by any one stock disaster, because they buy a diversified portfolio of issues. That also means you can't make the kind of money you could by getting lucky on a single stock. But in the volatile world of high-tech, one-company bets are strictly for investors with money to burn.

Too Many Bears? Market pros are more bearish than at any time since June, 1982, the latest weekly survey by Investors Intelligence of Larchmont, N.Y., shows. And that has contrarians -- who believe the majority is always wrong -- wondering if stocks are ready to soar anew.

The Investors Intelligence survey of 130 market analysts shows 57.3% now bearish. The last time it was higher was in June, 1982, at 57.6% -- just before the bull market began in August, 1982.

But the survey's overseer, Michael Burke, admits that even if the majority is wrong again, it could be months before the market turns, and it could go lower in the interim. The high reading for bearishness was 60.9% in March, 1982 -- a full five months before the bear market ended.
