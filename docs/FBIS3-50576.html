Language: Spanish Article Type:BFN [Text] Havana, 1 Jan (PL) -- Carlos Rafael Rodriguez, the vice president of the Council of Ministers, revealed today that during President James Carter's administration Cuba and the United States held high-level contacts with a view to initiating negotiations. The official disclosed these events in an interview with the official GRANMA newspaper. He also said that changes in relations between the two countries were unlikely during William Clinton's administration. Rodriguez said that "a possible solution to the U.S.-Cuba crisis" had been interrupted by Carter's decision to accuse Cuba of being responsible for the internal conflict in Zaire, which was triggered by armed refugee groups on the border with Angola. He recounted that he was scheduled meet secretly with then Secretary of State Cyrus Vance at the apartment of Andrew Young, then U.S. representative to the United Nations, on the same day that hostilities broke out in the African nation. Rodriguez said that Vance was meeting with former USSR Foreign Minister Andrey Gromyko when Carter gave the speech in which he made his charges. Thus, it was the Cuban official who informed Vance of the U.S. president's statements when they met. Vance apparently did not know that Carter had decided to make this announcement; embarrassed, he ended the meeting. The Cuban vice president added that it later became known that Cuba had had nothing to do with the Zaire events "but the opportunity to begin negotiations had passed." Rodriguez indicated that the Clinton administration's position is based on the idea that Cuba could collapse in the immediate future. It is also under pressure from the most recalcitrant sectors of the Cuban-American community and its allies. He added that the Clinton administration is not being pressed to make decisions and "that is why it is difficult to expect a change in relations between the United States and Cuba." The vice president emphasized that Washington has become obstinate in its intent to regain its control over the island. He added that the possibility of a new U.S. attack was greater during the Republican governments of Ronald Reagan and George Bush. Our land has not had a moment of respite in the enjoyment of its independence, he asserted.
