BFN [Text] Santo Domingo, 23 Jun (EFE) -- The Dominican Government warned today it will not allow the presence of foreign troops in its territory to reinforce the embargo against the Haitian military. Deputy Foreign Minister Fabio Herrera Cabral told EFE: "In no way will we (the Dominican Government) accept foreign troops in order to reinforce the border." The high-ranking official referred to Argentine President Carlos Menem's possible offer to send Argentine troops to the Dominican border with Haiti to reinforce the blockade against the military coupists. Menem will present this proposal during a meeting to be held tomorrow in Washington with U.S. President Bill Clinton, according to high-ranking diplomatic sources in Buenos Aires quoted by the Buenos Aires daily CLARIN. Fifteen days ago, Argentina became part of the "Friends of Haiti Club," which also includes the United States, France, Canada, and Venezuela. Herrera said the Dominican Foreign Ministry had no knowledge of Menem's proposal and explained that what the Dominican Government agreed to with the United Nations -- and "the United States is aware of this" -- is the presence of "approximately 20 observers" who would work together with Dominican authorities. The deputy minister added that the mission would consist of individuals, including military, of various nations, but he said five or six military personnel "cannot be considered a troop." He added: "We do not accept any foreign troops in Dominican territory to carry out something that corresponds to a bilateral matter. We are not at war with Haiti or anyone" and thus cannot accept the presence of military troops on Dominican territory. He explained that the Dominican Government is complying with the UN provisions and that it will receive the necessary reinforcements, but this will be in advisory and logistical support. After saying the Argentine Embassy "in no way" had made contact with the Foreign Ministry to discuss the Haitian crisis, he said his government had not received the logistical support which the international organizations has promised. "We are absorbing the economic cost of maintaining the Dominican troops on the border with no assistance, up until now, from any international organization," said the deputy minister. Nevertheless, he said despite the heavy cost the embargo represents for the Dominican Government, his country is still guarding the border while the international community's sanctions prevail against the "de facto" Haitian Government.
