December 11, 1990, Tuesday, P.M. Final

PEOPLE'S PHARMACY: ONLY ONE DRUG -- SO FAR -- WILL SPEED YOUR RECOVERY FROM FLU

Question: I've been seeing television ads for new over-the-counter flu remedies. I remember your writing in past years about a drug for the flu, but I thought it was prescription only. Have they changed it to non-prescription this year?

Answer: No. The ads you've seen are for old cold remedies that have been souped up and given a new marketing spin. These products may offer temporary symptom relief but they can't help you get over the flu faster.

Only amantadine can do that, so far, and it is available only by prescription. If you take it within the first two days of coming down with Type A influenza, it can cut your convalescence time.

Q: Are there any medicines to eliminate dark coarse facial hair? I have to shave it off, but it is really embarrassing. Even foundation and powder don't cover it perfectly.

When I asked my family doctor about it, he said that if my periods are regular there's probably nothing medically wrong. He thinks that I should learn to accept my appearance and not be self-conscious. I honestly don't find that advice very helpful and I hope you can tell me where to turn.

A: Check with a dermatologist or endocrinologist. They have more experience in treating hirsutism.

Facial hair growth is usually associated with an excess of male hormones, or androgens. (That's why men grow beards.)

It is usually treated with a medicine that counteracts these hormones. One that has been used is spironolactone, a drug commonly used to treat high blood pressure, potassium depletion, or certain adrenal gland problems. Spironolactone is often effective but it causes tumors in rats, so there are questions about its long-term safety. Side effects include drowsiness, diarrhea, cramping and menstrual irregularities.

Other medications that may help hirsutism also have potentially serious side effects. Antifungal Nizoral (ketoconazole) tablets reduce facial hair growth, but many women experience problems such as menstrual disturbances, scalp hair loss, nausea, headache or liver conditions.

Anti-androgens developed to treat prostate cancer in men are also being tested against hirsutism. They too can produce side effects.

The most promising area of research is on anti-androgens that could be applied directly to the skin, minimizing the risk of adverse reactions throughout the body.
