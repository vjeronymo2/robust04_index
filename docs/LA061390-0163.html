June 13, 1990, Wednesday, P.M. Final

LIZ SMITH: CAN ALL THE NEGATIVE PUBLICITY TRIP UP TRUMP?

"TRUMP IS the victim of his own peculiar naivete about the way life works. Couldn't he see what all of his shameless self-promotion was setting him up for? Schadenfreude -- a German word for glee over the misfortune of others -- is actually as American as casino gambling," writes Jonathan Alter in the current Newsweek.

These thoughts come under Alter's heading "The Fame Game: Why Everyone's Gloating," in the magazine's cover story, "Trump: The Fall."

IT IS in this issue that Donald Trump is quoted, saying: "Negative publicity has no effect in my life."

If this is true, then Donald is in a class by himself. Negative publicity almost always takes its toll, however, in psychic distress if nowhere else. But then, matters tend to settle, so that the public makes final perceptions and summings-up of high profile people that usually are pretty much correct.

The function of the press, with all its hits and errors, is to add to this ability of people in general to draw fairly accurate conclusions. And, as Americans are more prone to be fair than to be unfair, somehow most stories work out on the fair side.

I SUPPOSE the press can't be blamed too much for falling for that elaborate and absurd hoax about Elizabeth Taylor's 23-year-old "new love." But anybody who has ever dealt with Taylor's formidable press rep, Chen Sam, immediately recognized this story as a fake. Never in a million years would Chen (or anyone working for her) dare to talk of Elizabeth's "depression" or of her intimate affairs. They particularly wouldn't make those National Enquirer-type quotes.

In fact, bamboo shoots under the fingernails couldn't make Chen Sam, a woman with a rather blind-sided view of what constitutes her job, even tell you what color dress Elizabeth Taylor is wearing!

KANIN GAININ' on marryin' Marian! Garson Kanin, the famous screenwriter-playwright-director par excellence, was wed for many happy years to the late actress Ruth Gordon.

Since Ruth's death several years ago, Kanin has been keeping company with Broadway's most enduring actress, Marian Seldes. (She has appeared in two of the Great White Way's longest-running hits, "Equus" and "Deathtrap," and is currently in Chester, Conn., with the out-of-town "Annie II.")

Now Kanin and Seldes plan to tie the knot on June 19 in New York City. It couldn't happen to a nicer couple.
