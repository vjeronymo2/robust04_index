BFN [Report by Barry Parker] [Text] Gaza City, Occupied Gaza Strip, 5 May (AFP)--Palestinian officials admitted Thursday [5 May] they were having "difficulties" launching self-rule, but fended off Israelis accusations off being ill-prepared. PLO spokesman Sufyan Abu-Zayda told AFP there were "technical" problems, a day after the signing of an agreement to launch autonomy in the Gaza Strip and West Bank town of Jericho. "We are not a government, we are an organization with our forces spread around the world," he told AFP. "We have to expect that there will be difficulties, serious difficulties because this conflict has been going on for so many years," said PLO official Rashid Abu-Shibak, founder of the armed group the Black Panthers. West Bank military administration spokeswoman Colonel Elise Shazar said the occupiers were "ready to transfer power at any time. "But we are waiting for the Palestinians to knock on the door and present the list of names of the future heads for various sectors," she said. "Since the Palestinians have asked for a delay to get organized, the administration is waiting and continues, while waiting, to be responsible for everything as usual." The military administration would operate until the withdrawal was completed. Prime Minister Yitzhaq Rabin has already turned on the PLO for its apparent lack of readiness in a devastating outburst. "They're not ready to take things over," he said Wednesday after PLO leader Yasir 'Arafat at first refused to initial part of the autonomy deal, causing chaos at the lavish ceremony. 'Arafat finally appended his reservations to the size of the Jericho enclave. "We could finish our withdrawal in 10 days, the problem is that Yasir 'Arafat has asked for four weeks," Rabin revealed. Palestinian negotiators were "more concerned about symbolic issues than purely practical problems". Rabin urged the PLO to focus on the "economic woes" of Gaza. With more than half the working population unemployed and with little prospect of finding a job in the near future, the size of the task cannot be under-estimated. The PLO, whose offices have just one telephone line working in Gaza City, had announced that 1,000 policemen would enter Gaza on Thursday. But the arrival has now been put back to an imprecise "middle of next week". From Cairo, PLO security chief Jibril Rajub told Israel radio: "There are logistical problems in the deployment of the Palestinian police. It will begin in a few days and will no doubt extend over a few weeks." "We have to coordinate our efforts with Israel and a liaison committee will try to harmonize the deployment and the transfer of responsibilities." The liaison committee met on the strip on Thursday with the aim of agreeing a timetable for the security transfer. Israel has set a "representation" at the Erez crossing point into the strip to assist the new authorities and later to act as a contact point, for example if a Palestinian wants hospital treatment in Tel Aviv. PLO spokesman Abu-Zayda tried to explain the slow pace. "We don't want the police to come before everything is ready for their deployment. We don't want to put them in camps like jails," he said. "We need a week to prepare things, to find places for them to live. The agreement gives us three weeks." However, divisions among the Palestinian ranks cannot have helped. No Palestinian figure from the territories attended the Cairo ceremony. Neither West Bank Fatah chief Faysal al-Husayni, nor ex-PLO spokeswoman Hanan 'Ashrawi, nor ex-head of the delegation to bilateral negotiations Haydar 'Abd-al-Shafi. "After a 27-year pregnancy, we have given birth to a premature son, in weak health and who may not survive," said al-Husayni. Such prognostics were not heard publicly on the Gaza Strip among those charged with running autonomy. "We are ready to set up the authority in 24 hours," claimed Abu-Shibak, who returned to Gaza in early April after escaping abroad to avoid arrest in 1990. No one mentioned whether 'Arafat was using time as a weapon to try to gain more concessions from Israel over the size of Jericho and the release of prisoners, hundreds more of whom were expected to be freed Thursday after an initial 250 following the signing. Hasan Abu-Libdah, deputy director of the PLO-appointed Palestinian Economic Council for Development and Construction, described the problems as "Palestinian insufficiency". "We are not ready to receive authority," he said, pointing out that under occupation Palestinians had never had to take decisions. "Environment questions, for example, are totally new to us," said Abu-Libdah who is also in charge of 36 technical committees the Palestinians set up after the Madrid conference launched the peace process in October 1991. "What we need is to Palestinize the programmes in every sphere. It's not a matter of being a week late, it might take three or six months before we are really ready," he said Palestinian sources added: "They are only ready in the field of health. "It's the only field we have experience in and have a full programme ready. "As for the rest, education, tourism or whatever nothing else is completed. "For direct taxation nothing has been done. We need three months' work with the Israelis on tax matters. "Autonomy grants authority to the Palestinians for indirect tax, social welfare, education, health and tourism."
