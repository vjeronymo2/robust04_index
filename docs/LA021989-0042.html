February 19, 1989, Sunday, Home Edition

LEARNING TO COPE WITH TOO MANY CHOICES

There were scary stories in the Sunday paper: "911 System Crashes," "Medicare Costs Rising," "Jerry Brown Returns to California Politics." Scariest of all was "Overchoice . . . Experts . . . Wonder . . . If 57 Varieties . . . Too Much of a Good Thing."

Only in America would such a complaint be printed in a major newspaper. The gist of the polemic was that the marvelous abundance of goods produced by capitalism causes consumer stress and anxiety. The implied conclusion: These dumb bunnies need to be protected for their own good from greedy, manipulative marketers.

Who are the protectors? Why, experts! Experts like the psychologist/attorney who sells relief from such terrible stress. Experts like author Jeremy Rifkin, who now ventures into pop psychology to tell us that people are really emotionally stressed over product choices. What Rifkin is selling is control over people's lives. He wants rules. And he wants to be the rule-maker.

It's the rule-maker's never ending refrain: People are too stupid, apathetic, irresponsible, to be free. They cannot possibly enjoy America's abundance. . . .

It's ironic that as country after country turns away from rules and regulations and toward free-market capitalism, aspiring rule makers work ever harder here at home. The marketplace will cure "overchoice," if it exists, by bankrupting products and companies which do not provide value.

BEN M. ENIS

Professor of Marketing
