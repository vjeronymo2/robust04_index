February 10, 1990, Saturday, Home Edition

SANDINISTAS FREE 1,190 IMPRISONED FOR POLITICS;

NICARAGUA: SOME VIEW THE MOVE TWO WEEKS BEFORE THE PRESIDENTIAL ELECTION AS A PLOY TO WIN VOTES FOR ORTEGA.

Two weeks before national elections, the Sandinista government on Friday freed 1,190 Contras, rebel collaborators and former National Guardsmen in a prison yard ceremony that officials said emptied Nicaraguan jails of all political prisoners.

Among the 39 National Guardsmen released were allegedly some of the most brutal officers from the dictatorship of Anastasio Somoza, who was deposed by a Sandinista-led insurrection in 1979.

Sandinista officials charged, for instance, that when Col. Isaias Cuadra served as director of the Tipitapa Penitentiary under Somoza, he tortured at least one prominent prisoner -- Sandinista Cmdr. Tomas Borge. Since the Sandinistas came to power, Borge has served as interior minister, overseeing the prisons, and Cuadra has spent 10 1/2 years in jail.

"I have suffered a great deal during these years I have spent in prison," Cuadra, 56, said on his release. "I have come to understand human beings. I understand there are passions, there is pain and that anyone who is a Christian has to know how to forgive."

But he denied having tortured anyone, although reports of torture under Somoza were common.

President Daniel Ortega, who also spent time in jail for being a Sandinista guerrilla, had announced the release last month as a gesture to reunite this country, which has been divided first by the Sandinista revolution and then by the U.S.-backed Contra war.

Under the Central American peace accords signed by the region's five presidents, the Sandinistas are required to pardon and release political prisoners. But Ortega is seeking reelection Feb. 25 as the Sandinista National Liberation Front's candidate for peace and reconciliation, and the release is seen by many as a campaign maneuver to win votes.

Cmdr. Franco Montealegre, head of prisons, read the release decree as the convicts stood in single file on the prison yard's concrete basketball courts. Diplomats, members of the media and international observers here to monitor the elections watched the ceremony.

"The U.S. government must respond to this decree by stopping its support for warlike options and by giving determined support to the peace process," the decree stated.

Four former Contra commanders who have come back to Nicaragua to observe the elections also attended the ceremony. When Montealegre greeted them during his introduction, the prisoners broke into applause.

Many of the Contras and guardsmen interviewed after receiving their release papers said they hope to move to the United States, where most of their relatives live.

"There is nothing for me here," said former National Guard Capt. Orlando Hislop Mercado, 45.

Sandinista officials accuse Hislop of having raped and tortured Cmdr. Doris Tijerino during her imprisonment as a guerrilla. Now, Tijerino is head of the Sandinista police.

"I have been accused of everything you can imagine. My release order says 'atrocious assassination.' I heard on the radio the other day that I had raped a commander. I don't even know all they accuse me of, but . . . all the accusations are false," Hislop said.

Another of the released former guardsmen was Maj. Jose Enrique Munguia Berrios. He allegedly led the patrol that killed Sandinista hero Carlos Fonseca in 1976.

"I fought people who were armed just like me. I could have been killed just as easily as them," Munguia said Friday.

The number of political prisoners in Nicaragua has always been widely disputed and remained so Friday. Sandinista officials said that only common criminals and Sandinista soldiers accused of abuses remain in jail now.
