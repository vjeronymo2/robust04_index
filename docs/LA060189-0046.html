June 1, 1989, Thursday, Home Edition

THE BUTCHER: PORK LEG CAN BE TURNED INTO VARIETY OF MEALS

If you like your pork lean and inexpensive, look to the leg. Fresh leg of pork is often one of the best pork buys in any meat case.

Unfortunately, many people -- even those who love good, lean, low-cost pork -- pass up a good buy on a fresh leg because it's too big.

People can't imagine what to do with one, except roast it and eat it for weeks. But there are ways to work with a fresh leg of pork to provide many different and varied meals -- pork steaks, cutlets, roasts and kebabs for the barbecue.

A whole leg of fresh pork will weigh between 16 and 20 pounds and that is, admittedly, a good-size hunk of meat, but you usually can get the shank half of the leg at the same low price when fresh legs of pork are "on special."

Shank Is Easier

The shank half is by far the best half for a novice to work with. It does not contain the hip bone and that makes it much less difficult than the butt half to divide into many meals.

A shank half leg of fresh pork can be turned into a freezer full of great meal-making meat even for the smallest family.

There are four separate muscles that make up a leg of pork (a leg of any meat for that matter): the top, the tip, the eye and the bottom muscles. Taking one apart is not a difficult project and the marvelous meals that may be made from the component parts make the project more than worth the effort.

First, remove the hock, if there is one. Some legs come with the hock already removed. Find the joint with the point of your knife, then cut through it to separate the hock from the rest of the leg. Use the hock to make a good stick-to-the-ribs pork-and-beans casserole.

Next, remove the skin. Most fresh legs of pork are sold with the skin still on.

Use the Skin

There are several things that you can do with the skin; it should not be wasted. For one, cut it into squares and add it to the hock when you cook beans.

For another, tie it around a piece of lean, boneless pork that you roast over the coals and let it baste the meat as it turns crackin' good and crisp. And if you are into making your own sausage, try using fresh pork skin as an ingredient. It gives sausage a nice texture.

Next, separate each of the muscles of the leg by cutting along the natural seams that divide them. There is really no trick to it, the muscles are clearly defined and easy to separate. What you do with the individual muscles once you have them separated is limited by little but your imagination.

The top and tip muscle are the most tender (although none is apt to be tough). Use them for roasting or frying or cut them into thin strips or cubes for stir-fry dishes or thin slices for cutlets. The bottom and eye muscles are perfect for braised dishes.

Here are a few quick-to-fix and delicious dishes perfect for the meat of the leg.

TUSCAN PORK WITH

PEPPERS

1/4 cup olive oil

2 pounds boneless pork leg, cut into 1-inch cubes

4 cloves garlic, minced

1 (29-ounce) can whole tomatoes, undrained

1/2 teaspoon dried basil

1/2 teaspoon dried rosemary, crushed

1 teaspoon dried oregano

1/4 teaspoon dried thyme

Salt, pepper

1/2 cup dry white wine

1 large onion, sliced

1 sweet red pepper, seeded and sliced

1 green pepper, seeded and sliced

Heat 2 tablespoons oil in large heavy skillet or Dutch oven over medium high heat. Add pork and garlic and cook, stirring, to brown pork, about 4 to 5 minutes.

Add tomatoes, basil, rosemary, oregano, thyme, salt and pepper to taste, and wine. Reduce heat, cover tightly and simmer until pork is tender, about 12 to 15 minutes.

Meanwhile, saute onion and peppers in remaining oil in large skillet, about 3 minutes. Add to pork mixture, blend gently and simmer 3 to 5 minutes. Makes 8 servings.

GINGER MELON PORK

1 tablespoon oil

1 pound fresh pork leg, cubed

1/2 onion, thinly sliced

1 clove garlic, minced

1 tablespoon grated ginger root or 1 teaspoon ground ginger

2 tablespoons soy sauce

1/4 cup dry Sherry

2 tablespoon wine vinegar

1 tablespoon cornstarch

3 cups melon cubes (cantaloupe or honeydew)

1/2 cup diced pickled watermelon rind

Heat oil in large skillet over medium-high heat. Brown pork cubes, stirring until lightly browned, about 4 to 5 minutes.

Stir in onion, garlic and ginger and cook, stirring, 2 to 3 minutes. Mix together soy sauce, Sherry, vinegar and cornstarch. Add to skillet. Cook, stirring, until sauce thickens. Stir in melon and watermelon rind. Heat through. Makes 4 servings.

PLUM-DELICIOUS PORK

CUTLETS

1 tablespoon oil

1 pound boneless pork legs cutlets, 1/2-inch thick

1 (8-ounce) jar plum jelly or preserves

1/2 onion, chopped

1/4 cup wine vinegar

2 teaspoons soy sauce

1 teaspoon ground ginger

Heat oil in large skillet over medium-high heat. Brown cutlets on both sides.

Add jelly, onion, vinegar, soy sauce and ginger. Cover and simmer 8 to 10 minutes. Makes 4 servings.

CURRIED PEACHTREE

PORK

1 tablespoon oil

1 pound boneless pork leg cutlets, 1/4-inch thick

1/2 onion, coarsely chopped

1/2 teaspoon ground ginger

2 teaspoons curry powder

1 cup chicken broth

4 teaspoons cornstarch

1/4 teaspoon seasoned salt

1/2 cup coarsely chopped fresh or canned peaches

2 tablespoons peach jam

Heat oil in large skillet over medium heat. Add cutlets and onion to skillet and saute pork quickly, about 2 minutes per side, until golden brown.

Combine ginger, curry powder, broth, cornstarch, salt, peaches and jam in medium bowl. Pour mixture over pork cutlets. Cook, stirring gently over low heat, until sauce thickens, about 8 to 10 minutes. Makes 4 servings.
