Language: Russian Article Type:BFN [Interview with Ivan Rybkin, chairman of the Russian Federation State Duma, by Valentina Nikiforova, under the general heading "Spring. Another Thaw in Russia?"; place and date not given: "Ivan Rybkin, Chairman of the Russian Federation State Duma: On Amnesty Without Hysteria"] [Text] Hardly had the cell doors closed behind those accused of the events of September-October last year than the State Duma came under fire: "They have released the criminals!" "They have intervened in the president's prerogative!" Prosecutor A. Kazannik, admittedly after a slight delay, called the State Duma decree on the amnesty "the most shameful page in the history of Russian parliamentarism." What actually happened? Your PRAVDA correspondent put that and other questions to Ivan Petrovich Rybkin, chairman of the State Duma. [Rybkin] One has to view the amnesty calmly and without hysteria. The Duma adopted two decrees on the amnesty in connection with the adoption of the Constitution on the president's initiative. The first decree allows a little over 20,000 people to be released from their places of confinement and around 200,000 people on probation. The second decree -- on the economic and political amnesty -- will set 20 times fewer people free. The point of the amnesty is that all those who have erred are being given a chance to enter life at a quite difficult and critical time. The aim is to consolidate society so as to pull the country out of chaos by joint efforts. [Nikiforova] It is a humane act. After all, the idea of the amnesty came from the president himself and was supported by virtually all factions in the Duma. [Rybkin] That clearly does not suit those who look forward to clashes between the branches of power, between the State Duma and the Federation Council, and between the president and the Federal Assembly. But I think that we -- the deputies and the executive -- are intelligent enough not to organize Russian-style gladiatorial contests for everyone else's entertainment. My advice is not to wait with malicious relish until the pendulum swings the other way but to remember that we are fellow countrymen. [Nikiforova] Nevertheless, they are accusing the Duma of haste. [Rybkin] I do not believe that. We did a lot of work in committees. As for the procedure for the application of the decree, we discussed it with the Interior Ministry. The general prosecutor asked for clarification -- and members of the committees on legality and security clarified every point. They worked on Friday and Saturday. [Nikiforova] But on Saturday people were already coming out of jail, in other words they were released immediately. That clearly was alarming. [Rybkin] But that is just the beginning. No amnesty is an instantaneous act. There are different situations, they have to be examined. And an adequate time limit is set for this -- six months. [Nikiforova] How do you assess the general prosecutor's conduct? [Rybkin] It is vexing that A. Kazannik has behaved so inconsistently. He knew that the decree on the amnesty had been adopted as part of a package in which the main document is the "Memorandum on Accord," signed on 22 February by all the factions, even Russia's Choice (although unfortunately the next morning, 23 February, they changed their stance). Now when a mountain of work has been dumped on the General Prosecutor's Office -- work that is necessary for society (it must be cleansed and stabilized) -- the general prosecutor is resigning. The new man will need time to settle in. [Nikiforova] Ivan Petrovich, are those who have been amnestied completely exonerated? [Rybkin] That is a difficult question. In adopting the decree the deputies created an area of accord. In consenting to this release, those under investigation wrote a statement to that effect. For them that is a serious warning. It is no coincidence that the Criminal Code includes amendments increasing punishments which coincide with those contained in the articles of the decree on the amnesty.
