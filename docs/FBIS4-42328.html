BFN [Julio Riquelme report: "Gonzalez Avoids Linking His Future to 12 June Outcome"] [Text] Madrid -- Prime Minister Felipe Gonzalez yesterday opened the election campaign for 12 June with an appearance on the Antena 3 television channel. On several occasions, Gonzalez was at pains to dissociate his political future and that of his government from the results of the dual European and Andalusian elections, acknowledging only that "if there is a catastrophe," there is a possibility of reconsidering the current situation. In the opinion of Gonzalez, each election's results should be confined to its own sphere, even though some political forces -- which he did not name -- wish to turn 12 June into a primary election. Continuing this line of argument, Gonzalez denied that bad results could cause his resignation. Previously, when asked about his state of mind with respect to the successive corruption cases, the prime minister asserted that "I have been in power for many years, and I know that my responsibility is to carry through our blueprint for the country, tackling both the corruption cases and the economic crisis." Nevertheless, Gonzalez stated that "in the political sphere, my most difficult moment was the NATO referendum, and in the personal sphere, it has been these days with the corruption scandals." "I have no limits," he asserted, "in combating corruption, except those characteristic of a rule-of-law state, which it is very difficult to blackmail," referring to Luis Roldan.

Trust Betrayed
After asserting, referring to the Roldan and Rubio cases, that "institutions stand above individuals," praising the work performed by both the Civil Guard and the Bank of Spain, Gonzalez reiterated that personal trust had been betrayed because of a lack of democratic controls, entailing political responsibilities which have been discharged -- referring to the resignations of Asuncion, Corcuera, and Solchaga. "It has been difficult for me to accept the betrayal, and I have had a very bad time," he asserted. "We resign," he said, in a clear reference to the People's Party: "Others do not resign." Gonzalez said that it is very difficult to foresee the emergence of new scandals, although he pointed out that as prime minister "I have approved over 1,000 appointments of senior officials, and only two or three have disappointed me." The prime minister did not rule out the possibility that somebody is directing "a trickle of news" connected with corruption, stating that "some people do not even like the democratic system." He showed himself opposed to the proposal made yesterday by Democratic Union of Catalonia leader Duran Lleida, for making a "fresh start" in the matter of party funding, since he deems it necessary for all citizens to know the origin of the parties' funds, and to create legal arrangements to ensure their transparency, involving an austerity which he deems necessary, plus increased powers for the National Audit Office.
Disenchantment
The prime minister said he realizes that both the economic crisis and corruption, as well as the "lengthy period" of Socialist government, have caused disenchantment among many citizens, and so "it will be necessary to appeal to people's consciences to vote 12 June." Felipe Gonzalez asserted that over the past few days, "rather than a political crisis, there has been political confusion, uncertainty, and anxiety. But it is very strange that this is being said by the People's Party, who are the only people to deny the country's recovery." Gonzalez said stability is needed for this recovery, and so in a further reference to the People's Party he described it as "crass" for them to say "the opposite of what even bodies and institutions very distant politically from the Spanish Socialist Workers Party are stating." In the purely economic sphere, the prime minister asserted that the worst of the crisis is now over; "we will lose no jobs in 1994, and 1995 will be a good year." He used this argument to launch another attack on the People's Party for refusing to acknowledge the good figures and for increasing confusion by even asserting "that there will be a general election this year, when not even 12 months have passed since the last general election." "We will win well in Andalusia," Gonzalez asserted, "because the Andalusians have no inclination to vote for the right." He denied the existence of a Rural Employment Plan captive vote, acknowledging that "patronage would be another form of corruption." In a final personal reference, Gonzalez asserted that "for a long time several European leaders have been urging me to leave the Spanish Government and become the European Commission president. Now they are no longer urging me," he concluded, because "I have decided to remain in charge of my responsibilities as prime minister."
