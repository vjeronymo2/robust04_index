941203 FT 03 DEC 94 / Weekend Money: Finance Week Business Chancellor Kenneth Clarke promised sharp cuts in future government spending and borrowing in a workmanlike Budget designed to put the long-term jobless back to work and help small businesses. The government had its paper majority in the House of Commons wiped out as the price of crushing a Tory rebellion against higher British contributions to the European Union. Sir Bryan Carsberg, Britain's senior competition regulator, is to step down as director-general of fair trading next May, three years into his five-year term. Norway rejected membership of the European Union, for the second time. It last rejected membership in 1972. Skilled workers are less confident about the economy and job security than any other social group, according to the latest FT/MFS Mood of the Nation Index. The overall index has recovered from a two-month decline triggered by the rise in interest rates. The government imposed the toughest financial settlement on local authorities since the 1970s, with a cash cut in the funding they will receive from central government. Council tax is projected to rise at double the rate of inflation. Durlacher & Co, the stockbroking house, has been fined Pounds 25,000 by the Securities and Futures Authority, for breaches of regulations. Fresh evidence of the sluggishness of the mortgage market came as it emerged that new home loans by banks fell more than 10 per cent in October, compared with September. Customs and Excise announced it is to shed more than 4,000 jobs over the next five years. Barclays Bank Pension Fund, one of the UK's largest, is faced with a special assessment by the Inland Revenue. The move will be a test case on whether pension scheme profits earned by underwriting new share deals ought to be taxed. Corporate investment in Lloyd's of London is to increase by more than Pounds 300m next year following a last-minute announcement of the launch of a new company, backed by two US institutional funds, which plans to invest Pounds 88m. Complaints about term assurance and permanent health insurance are likely to be transferred to the ombudsman scheme linked to the Personal Investment Authority (PIA), the watchdog to protect the private investor, even though these policies are outside its scope. Markets The London stock market was virtually unaltered throughout the chancellor's speech, and closed higher on the day. However, renewed fears over interest rates led to heavy selling on Thursday. Sterling traded steadily on the foreign exchanges after the Budget lived up to market expectations. It lost some ground on Friday against a strong US dollar. Foreign ownership of shares in British companies rose significantly last year, for the fourth successive year, official figures showed. Silver plunged below the psychologically-important Dollars 5 a troy ounce level on Thursday, giving a salutary warning about what can happen when investment funds, having driven metal prices up with a buying spree, decide to quit the market. South Korean financial institutions cashed in as overseas investors responded to the raising of foreign shareholding ceilings by buying on Thursday their highest one-day total of shares in the country's leading blue-chip companies. China banned its pension schemes from investing in the country's volatile stock markets, insisting that funds go into approved government securities. Companies British Petroleum is to promote a new chairman and chief executive from within its ranks in the first big reshuffle since the boardroom coup of 1992. Chairman, Lord Ashburton, will be succeeded by David Simon, the current chief executive. He in turn will be succeeded by John Browne, head of the company's exploration arm. Williams Holdings signalled a slowdown in its hectic acquisition campaign as it announced plans to purchase the fire protection arm of BBA Group, the automotive components company, for Pounds 80m, including assumed debt. Browning-Ferris won its Pounds 391m takeover bid for Attwoods with believed acceptances of nearly 60 per cent at the end of yesterday. The Royal Bank of Scotland more than doubled pre-tax profits to Pounds 532m in the year to September 30. The 75 per cent salary increase awarded to Mr Cedric Brown, the chief executive of British Gas, still leaves him underpaid compared with many executives holding similar jobs in British industry, according to Mr Richard Giordano, the company's chairman. T&N, formerly one of the UK's largest asbestos suppliers, announced a surprise Pounds 100m provision against future asbestos-related claims, just three months after a US court ruling that indicated a declining trend in claims. T&N's share price fell sharply on the news. 3i, the recently floated venture capital group concentrating on investment in small and medium-sized companies, reported a 6.7 per cent increase in net asset value per share to 334p in the six months to September 30. Chemical Banking, the fourth-biggest US banking group, announced a range of cost-cutting and other measures, including 3,700 job losses, in an attempt to lift its profitability closer to the levels of more successful rivals. Hanson saw an accelerating improvement in operating profits in the year to September 30. BTR, the industrial conglomerate whose shares have fallen by more than a quarter in the past three months, issued a trading statement which failed to restore City confidence. An increase in beer sales volumes in spite of a fall in the overall market helped Marston, Thompson & Evershed, the Burton-based regional brewer, to lift interim profits by 6.5 per cent. Best Travel, the UK's seventh-largest tour operator, went into liquidation. It was the travel industry's biggest collapse since 1991. Hasbro bought Waddington's games division for Pounds 50m in cash, giving the biggest US toy and games group a monopoly on Monopoly, the world's best-selling board game. A downbeat trading statement sent shares in Argyll, the grocery retailer, down 17p to 255p on Wednesday - the biggest fall of the day among FT-SE 100 stocks - in spite of a 4 per cent increase in interim pre-tax profits. The Advertising Standards Authority is to censure Procter & Gamble over advertising claims in its 'soap wars' with Unilever, according to a leaked draft of an ASA adjudication. Bayer, the German chemicals multinational, is to re-focus its pharmaceuticals research away from crowded market sectors and step up spending on the hunt for treatments for allergies, rheumatic ailments and Alzheimer's disease. A row erupted between Eurotunnel and Klesch & Company, the London-based bank debt trader, over a report issued by Klesch called 'Eurotunnel Interim Results - Disaster Ahead'.
