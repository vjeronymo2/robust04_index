January 4, 1989, Wednesday, Home Edition

AUTO INSURANCE CONTROVERSY

Although the automobile insurance companies profess to be seeking to hold down costs, my recent experience indicates the contrary. The approach utilized by my insurance company is structured to unnecessarily encourage higher repair costs and thus "justify" higher insurance premiums and correspondingly greater profits. The process needs to be changed if insurance costs are to be cut.

The higher than required expenditures result from the way the repair work is authorized. After a recent accident, two bids of $1,100 and $1,000 were obtained for repairing the damage to my vehicle. The company's adjuster authorized $1,495. The company's instructions called for the insured (myself) to take the adjuster's authorization to the repair shop, a mistake. The repair shop then magically found additional work that had to be done which escalated the cost up to the amount authorized, $1,494 in my case. Inasmuch as I knew my insurance rates were going to go up because of the accident and not because of an expenditure related to the cost of repairing my car, and the company reimburses 100% of costs over the deductible, there was no motivation to keep costs in line. The result is that those insured by the company are going to be asked to indirectly pay for nearly $500 in unnecessary repair costs in the form of higher premiums.

Had my insurance company structured its rates and reimbursement schedule to provide for the insured to pay a percentage of the costs in addition to the deductible, as is typically the case for most medical insurance, all parties would have been better motivated to keep costs in line. The insurance companies need to realize that unless they encourage cost savings, the pressure to dictate an arbitrary premium reduction will continue unabated.

MICHAEL N. ERNSTOFF

Los Angeles
