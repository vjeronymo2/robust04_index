December 18, 1989, Monday, Home Edition

DR. JOYCE BROTHERS: HE'S DANGEROUS AS ANGRY DRIVER

DEAR DR. BROTHERS: Whenever my husband gets really angry, he invariably jumps into his car and drives off. He's had several serious accidents and I believe this is no coincidence. Why does he do this and how can I help him?-C.W.

DEAR C.W.: Cars represent power to many people, especially young males. This is one of the many reasons that automobile insurance is frequently more expensive for young male drivers. This is why motors are revved and why tires squeal as young drivers try to go around corners at excessive speed. Now, it's possible that you're saying that your husband isn't young. Some men don't mature as quickly as others and continue to use cars in the same way they did as adolescents.

Anger results from a feeling of powerlessness. When a person leaps in his car in a flash of anger, he's much more likely to have an accident and to injure himself or someone else. When people take their anger out on their car, when they get behind the wheel when they're in a rage, the likelihood of serious accidents soars. Studies reveal that when people are angry, their reflexes aren't as good and they're definitely more accident-prone.

Your husband needs to learn how to talk about his feelings and to find constructive ways to solve his problems. He would profit from assertiveness training. He needs to learn that it's all right to be angry, but it is also possible to talk rationally about feelings without resorting to aggression or violence.

DEAR DR. BROTHERS: As long as I can remember I've had a terrible fear of flying. It was so bad I even contemplated going to a hypnotist to overcome it. What really concerned me was that I was planning a honeymoon trip to Europe. My new husband convinced me that everything would be fine. I didn't have time to get therapy for my phobia, but much to my amazement, when I got on the plane I had no fear and have had none since. What happened? -- R.D.

DEAR R.D.: I'm not sure, but my guess is that your new husband gave you a sense of trust you didn't have before. The fear of flying is frequently related to a fear of losing control. Unconsciously, you feel if you're not in the driver's seat -- at the controls -- something dreadful will happen.

On your honeymoon, you may have discovered that the world didn't come to an end if you lost control. In a satisfying sexual relationship, both partners must have enough trust to be able to surrender themselves and to lose control or to be swept up in the moment of passion.

It may sound absurd, but this surrender may have laid the groundwork for you to enjoy your flight.
