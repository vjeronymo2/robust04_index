March 5, 1989, Sunday, Home Edition

INCLUDING FCA'S KNAPP IN S&L STORY WRONGLY CREATED IMPRESSION OF GUILT BY ASSOCIATION

On Feb. 20, The Times carried an article on five former savings and loan executives, including our client, Charles W. Knapp, who resigned in August, 1984, as chairman and chief executive of Financial Corp. of America ("Behind the Fallen Thrifts"). It was extremely unfair to include Knapp in a story with persons who have been convicted of crimes or who are targets of criminal prosecution; that improperly created the impression with your readers of guilt by association.

Although your article on Knapp refers to "an internal FCA study done in 1986," it fails to state that FCA has refused to disclose the evidence supporting any of the conclusions in its study. More than 4 1/2 years have elapsed since Knapp's resignation and no criminal or administrative charges of wrongdoing have been brought, nor does he have any knowledge of any pending investigation.

In October, 1986, Knapp filed a lawsuit against FCA in Los Angeles federal court in which he alleged that FCA made false and defamatory statements about him and improperly attributed to prior officers the responsibility for the failures of his successors. In addition, Knapp's lawsuit alleges that the matters for which he was blamed were disclosed in advance to and approved by FCA's auditors, attorneys, the Securities and Exchange Commission and the Federal Home Loan Bank Board and that the financial reporting of these matters was approved by FCA's accountants, audit committee and entire board of directors, which included respected members of the business, legal and academic communities.

After Knapp resigned, he was named a defendant in several lawsuits. In the only case in which there was a final ruling on the merits, summary judgment was granted in favor of Knapp because there was no evidence "that Knapp was personally involved in any of the alleged misconduct, conspired to commit any of the alleged misconduct, or that he knew of it, authorized it, or ratified it."

If you intended to report fairly, you would not have included Knapp in an article with convicted or about-to-be-indicted persons and you would have reported the facts set forth in this letter rather than quoting uncorroborated and confusing innuendo from anonymous sources.

ARTHUR N. GREENBERG

Los Angeles

The writer is a senior partner in the law firm of Greenberg, Glusker, Fields, Claman & Machtinger.
