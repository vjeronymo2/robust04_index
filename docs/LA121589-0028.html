December 15, 1989, Friday, Home Edition

IN JERUSALEM, THE LIGHT OF PEACE FLICKERS;

MIDDLE EAST: THE HOLY CITY IS RELATIVELY TRANQUIL, BUT THE INTIFADA DEFIES SETTLEMENT. AND THE BUSH ADMINISTRATION HAS BEEN MORE HINDRANCE THAN HELP.

Whom the gods would destroy they first tempt to resolve the Arab-Israeli conflict, Irving Kristol once observed.

George Bush and James Baker should be extremely wary of the political quicksand into which they are attempting to lead Israelis, Egyptians and still-unidentified Palestinians. The probability is that Bush and Baker will fail in their attempt to promote an Israeli-Palestinian dialogue desired by neither Israelis nor Palestinians.

The Administration has already strained Israeli trust and American good will. The White House's recent very public delay in granting a presidential appointment to Israeli Prime Minister Yitzhak Shamir was an effort to pressure and embarrass Israel. The refusal of the United States to provide Israel clear and complete information about Egypt's responses to Baker's five points creates more mistrust. The extraordinary request by the United States that leaders of the Israeli opposition Labor Party be included in Israel's team preparing for talks in Washington was widely regarded as insulting.

Why have Bush and Baker acted this way? Are they trying to demonstrate to a disbelieving Arab world that the United States is neutral between Israelis and the Palestinians? Do they want to intensify disagreement within Shamir's government in the hope that it will be replaced by a more tractable Labor government? Were they unable to get from Egypt a response to Baker's five points that would be minimally acceptable to the government of Israel? Is it possible that the Bush/Baker team, which is correctly sensitive to Mikhail Gorbachev's pride and Deng Xiao-ping's pride, does not understand that Israelis also are proud?

Always poor, prospects for settlement have become even bleaker in the last year, complicated by the Palestine Liberation Organization's declaration of an independent state, by the withdrawal of King Hussein from a role in the West Bank, by the rise of Muslim fundamentalism and by the extension of Syria's power.

The PLO declaration was intended to make an independent state the only alternative to Israeli occupation of the West Bank and Gaza. King Hussein's "divorce" from the West Bank underscored this narrowed choice by removing "the Jordanian option" for a Palestinian solution. But "the Jordanian option" was precisely the "solution" preferred by Israeli as well as West Bank moderates. Its removal weakened the case of Israelis ready to abandon "Judea and Samaria" to the right kind of neighboring power.

The effort to present Yasser Arafat as the "moderate" solution was never very persuasive, either in Israel or the United States. The case for dealing with him declines further as his own power wanes. The spread of Muslim fundamentalism on the West Bank and in Gaza and the growing power of Arafat's principal rival, Syria's Hafez Assad, mean that Arafat is not in a position to guarantee peace -- even if he were inclined to do so. Events in Lebanon and Jordan have shown that in this area, tendencies to factionalism, extremism and violence are more powerful than Arafat or the Arab League.

In Israel they're describing 1989 as the year the intifada turned on itself. The big development was inter-Arab violence, with the number of Palestinian "collaborators" hacked to death by other Palestinians variously estimated from 118 to 160. Obviously these "collaborators" also testify to the PLO's problem in commanding the loyalty of all West Bankers.

But there is an oasis of relative tranquility where Arabs and Israelis meet in peace if not in harmony. That is Jerusalem.

Life in Jerusalem is nearly normal. The intifada still calls the Arab merchants of East Jerusalem out on strike each day. The merchants respond. They close their shops at noon and do not reopen until the next morning. But the disruption of life is minimal. Street vendors have multiplied. Local inhabitants do their shopping in the morning. Schools are open. Public meetings are held. The streets are safer than those of most American cities. There were only 11 homicides in the first 11 months of this year, about half of them related to politics.

The peace process that pacified Jerusalem is Mayor Teddy Kollek's endless effort and boundless commitment to provide basic municipal services and fairness to all of Jerusalem's inhabitants.

Even Kollek is concerned today about the future of this city he so passionately loves. Jerusalem is not mentioned in Baker's five points, he notes, but Arafat has said again and again that East Jerusalem must be the capital of a Palestinian state.

It cannot be, Kollek vows. He is ready to give Arab citizens of Jerusalem their own schools, public services of all kinds and their own vote in municipal affairs. He respects their right to remain Jordanian citizens. He promises to accord Arabs all the rights a democracy guarantees its citizens.

But even Teddy Kollek has his own non-negotiable demand. "We shan't give up any part of Jerusalem, not under any circumstances."

Most Americans are not neutral between the non-negotiable demands of Teddy Kollek and Yasser Arafat: The one is a paragon of democracy; the other is a man who opposes elections and proclaims himself "the sole representative of the Palestinian people."

Maybe before Bush and Baker take on the Arab-Israeli conflict, they should practice on something easier, like settling the problem of Cyprus.
