930811 FT 11 AUG 93 / People: Engineering a future at Simon It's goodbye for a week to my spouse, For the pleasures of yachting at Cowes. I'll be hosting all day At our corporate chalet And each night I'm obliged to carouse. Two years after penning this winning entry for a competition in The Independent, the author is moving to a company where severe belt-tightening rather than carousing will be the order of the day. Maurice Dixson, who in addition to being a keen limerick writer, has had a distinguished career with British Aerospace (owners of the above-mentioned corporate chalet) and GEC, is moving to Simon Engineering as its new chief executive, in succession to Brian Kemp. Michael Davies, installed as chairman only last month, has lost no time in recruiting a new executive boss who has, as Davies puts it, 'the opportunity to restore the company's fortunes in a way that Brian perhaps doesn't' In May the company warned of a loss for the first half of this year which would then put it in breach of one of its banking covenants. In addition, some institutional shareholders are understood to have been unhappy that Kemp, 49, who was awarded a seven per cent pay increase last year despite a 71 per cent fall in profits. Others blamed him for the unsuccessful diversification into environmental businesses. 'Simon is a very famous name; one of the attractions of the job is to restore it to where it ought to be, in the forefront of British engineering' says Dixson, adding that he will be going in with his 'eyes open'. Dixson, 51, who in the mid 1960s did an Oxford doctoral thesis in the politics and economics of defence procurement, has been on the board of GEC for the past three and a half years, as group managing director in charge of the electronic metrology and components groups. Prior to that he had been at BAe, latterly as managing director of the commercial aircraft side and before that as chief executive of Royal Ordnance. His name, together with that of Dudley Eustace, now finance director of Philips, was at one time in the ring as a possible successor to former chairman Sir Raymond Lygo. He says there will be 'a fairly short-term' handover period with Kemp, followed by 'a longer period of consultation when he will be at the end of a telephone.'
