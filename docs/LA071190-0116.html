July 11, 1990, Wednesday, Home Edition

LEARNING HOW TO SERVE IBSEN'S 'WILD DUCK';

SEMINAR: A RAP SESSION ON THE COMIC TRAGEDY INCLUDES A RECAP ON HOW THE PLAY OFFENDED 1880 AUDIENCES.

As Norwegian playwright Henrik Ibsen was finishing the first draft of "The Wild Duck," he wrote a friend that it "doesn't touch on political or social problems, or indeed any matters of public import. It takes place entirely within the confines of family life. I dare say it will arouse some discussion; but it can't offend anyone."

At a pre-preview seminar at the Los Angeles Theatre Center, titled "Lies of the Mind: Truth and Illusion in 'The Wild Duck,' " it did cause a good bit of discussion, including recaps of how it offended the audiences of the 1880s. LATC's production of the comic tragedy opens Thursday.

On hand for the Saturday discussion were "The Wild Duck's" director, Stein Winge, and this version's translators, Gerry Bamman and Irene B. Berman, along with City University of New York Theatre Program chairman Marvin A. Carlson and moderator Richard Toscan, dean of the School of Theatre at USC.

The big question was how to get Ibsen right. This was the play that signaled Ibsen's abandonment of social criticism, and he was saying new things in a new way.

Bamman, also an actor and director, first noticed a problem with Ibsen translations when asked about 10 years ago to find a good translation of "A Doll's House." There wasn't one. Since establishing a working relationship with the Norwegian-born Berman, they have translated "A Doll House" (the correct title), "Hedda Gabler" and "Peer Gynt," among other plays.

Bamman, commenting that any translation "has a limited life" and should be redone about every 20 years, was adamant about keeping the work colloquial. He pointed to a phrase directly translated from "Doll House" as "fresh as a fish." This would mean nothing to an English-speaking audience, he said. "Healthy as a horse" was the solution.

Quoting the translator's problem, "you can't be beautiful and faithful at the same time," Bamman said that he believes you can -- "if you keep the style in mind and retain your humility."

"You should not hear that it is a translation," Berman added, also elaborating on the problems involved with the Norwegian language's vocabulary, which is one-third that of English, and the difficulties with what she calls "filler words," such as yes, well, etc. When Ibsen repeats a word for effect or meaning, adjustments have to be made.

Director Winge, who was appointed in January as artistic director of the National Theatre of Norway, said that he is really just "getting into" Ibsen. This is the first time he has directed "The Wild Duck," and he has been working not only with the Bamman-Berman translation, but also with the original Norwegian text in his hand. Asked by an audience member how he can work with both texts, he replied: "I've been able to communicate with myself the whole time."

Winge believes "The Wild Duck" is Ibsen's most cruel play, meaning his most deeply cutting, because of the subject matter of a family that lives surrounded by their own lies and illusions, finally resulting in the tragic suicide of the family's daughter, Hedvig. It's a subject Ibsen bequeathed to future playwrights, particularly Eugene O'Neill in "The Iceman Cometh."

Relling, a character in the play, says, "Rob the average man of his basic lie and you rob him of his happiness as well." It is restating this truth in the life of Ibsen's characters that creates the hurdles that translators and directors have to jump without stumbling.

"It's a difficult and personal play," Winge said. "Ibsen couldn't write about how he felt in letters, so he put it all in 'The Wild Duck.' "

Carlson added that "it took people a long time to digest 'The Wild Duck,' " and the Ibsen plays that followed. He had broken with a long dramatic tradition, and "Wild Duck" seemed a parody of an Ibsen play.

"We've got to stop lying to each other," Carlson said the characters in "Wild Duck" are saying, and it's hard to allow the "wonderful richness and ambiguity of the characters to open up into the richness and ambiguity of the play itself."

It's that richness and ambiguity that make the translators' job so difficult, the director's job so hazardous, the scholar's so endless. Hunting for the right connotation, the right nuance that will help to explain why Ibsen's characters hide behind their mendacity and illusions, is as intriguing as trying to discover why they, like the wild duck when wounded, dive for the bottom to escape predators.
