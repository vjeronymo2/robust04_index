February 7, 1989, Tuesday, Home Edition

HEALTH;

BOOK REVIEW;

BENEFITS OF SCIENTIFIC RESEARCH: THE AWARENESS AND THE WARINESS

Genethics: The Clash Between the New Genetics and Human Values by David Suzuki and Peter Knudtson (Harvard University Press: $25; 384 pages)

The theme is an old one. Science for good and science for evil. It recurs in literature, in thought and in reality. Unfortunately, there is no solution, only a warning: Be aware and beware.

Until this century, science for evil, while recognized, seemed an aberration. Since World War II, we have known that it is more than that. The destructive power of knowledge is at least as great as its positive applications. The two are inseparably linked.

Microbiology is the latest discipline to discover that science is a two-edged sword. The explosion of knowledge in the last several decades into the chemical basis of life has opened vast possibilities for use and for abuse. What to do about it is the subject of "Genethics: The Clash Between the New Genetics and Human Values" by David Suzuki and Peter Knudtson, a book that offers more questions than answers.

Biotechnology has been a controversial subject since the early 1970s, when the techniques of genetic engineering were first developed. Mindful of the dilemma that the atomic scientists of World War II had created, biologists feared unleashing a new uncontrollable weapon on the world.

Specifically, they were concerned that a genetically engineered organism could escape from a laboratory and wreak havoc, and, for the first time in the history of science, they voluntarily adopted a moratorium on research until the potential for such a catastrophe could be assessed.

Having assured themselves -- and many of the rest of us -- that the possibility was remote, they resumed and expanded their work. Now the question is a different and much more fundamental one: Regardless of the potential benefits, is it wise to tinker with the essence of life?

"As we embark on this new era of applied molecular genetics, we are in some ways incredibly shortsighted," Suzuki and Knudtson warn. "We are so intent on rushing to exploit our newly acquired insights that we often do not have the faintest idea of the long-term consequences of our technologies."

Genetic research makes possible the creation of new forms of life. The authors concede the potential benefits of manufacturing new medicines and repairing genetic defects. But they fear that "repairing genetic defects" sounds a lot like "improving the race," a slogan that has already been used and could still be used for great harm.

And, in an echo of the earlier concern about the escape of pathological new organisms, the authors fear that some unknown, unforeseen consequence is still lurking in genetic engineering.

"When science creates new technological tools, they often yield instant social and financial rewards," they write. "But these successes can blind us to the fact that science seldom has much to say about the possible future effects of these applications on our societies and ecosystems. We need look no farther than the long-term effects of the automobile, nuclear fission, oral contraceptives, agricultural pesticides or television to appreciate the myopia of science. The effects of each of these applications of science extend far beyond the immediate uses for which they were intended."

Answers in Morality

Go slow, the authors urge. We don't know what we're doing. The answers to these questions lie in morality, not in science, in wisdom, not in knowledge. They call for new ethical principles, which, the authors say, should be drawn from as many and wide-ranging sources as possible.

Who can quarrel with such a noble sentiment? On the other hand, is it moral to refuse to cure a genetic defect today because of the fear that the knowledge will be misused tomorrow?

No one has yet figured out a solution to this problem, and no one is likely to. In some sense, it is the problem of modern society. We want -- we need -- the benefits of science. We cannot feed ourselves without every technological trick at hand. There are too many people on the planet.

By definition, no one knows what the unforeseen problems of knowledge will be in the long run. But we cannot give up the short-term benefits. We cannot let go of the tiger's tail.

Suzuki, a scientist, and Knudtson, a science writer, are right to point out the hazards and to urge and reurge that we treat knowledge of the chemistry of life with awe. But can we really turn our backs on this technology? Should we?

Should the printing press have been banned at the outset because one day Hitler would publish "Mein Kampf"?
