BFN [Unattributed article: "Netherlands Has Units Ready for UN Duties"] [Text] New York, 22 June -- The Netherlands has offered units from all sections of its Armed Forces to the United Nations. These units could carry out duties anywhere in the world with a one to four weeks' notice. Defense Minister Ter Beek announced this yesterday at the UN headquarters after long talks with Secretary General Butrus Butrus-Ghali. A total of 3,500 military are involved in these forces, which include two frigates, two minesweepers and a support ship, a battalion of marines, and a Navy PC3 Orion reconnaissance aircraft. The Land Forces involved include an infantry battalion with armored vehicles, a command unit, and an engineering company for mine clearance and road repair. The Air Force would be able to supply a squadron of 18 F-16 fighter aircraft and two F-27 transport planes. Fifty military policemen could be deployed for police tasks. Ter Beek said the units would fit in a UN organizational system enabling the secretary general to know at a glance what troops were available in the world, how long it would take for them to arrive, their specialities, and equipment. The Netherlands is the 18th country to follow Butrus-Ghali's call for a pool of military personnel for peacekeeping duties. Ter Beek explained that the units could only be deployed if the Netherlands' Cabinet gave its approval to the UN mission and after the Netherlands' Parliament had been consulted. In addition to these units on standby for UN tasks, the Netherlands would also supply other troops and material for implementing UN peacekeeping duties at any time. During his talks with Butrus-Ghali, Ter Beek repeated that the Netherlands did not want to send any troops to Rwanda. Ter Beek said that Butrus-Ghali showed understanding for the Netherlands' position that Netherlands soldiers looked too much like the Belgians, the former colonial power, and therefore would run extra risks. But the Netherlands would supply a transport plane to fly freight and military personnel for air traffic control into Rwanda's neighboring countries. Butrus-Ghali praised the Netherlands' military efforts in Bosnia and Croatia. He was happy with the cabinet's intention to relieve the troops in Bosnia on 1 July with the 12th Battalion of the Airborne Brigade. "A purple coalition will not change this," Ter Beek said. During the talks the secretary general declared that he was having great difficulty in carrying out UN peacekeeping duties. Sometimes there are not enough soldiers, often they have inadequate equipment, and if they are offered equipment from another member state then they first have to be trained in its use. This way precious months can be lost and the United Nations is unable to implement the peacekeeping decisions made by the Security Council. He said he was very happy with the Netherlands' initiative to make a number of units permanently available. "If that could be done on a wider scale, Butrus-Ghali could decide quicker which soldiers were to be sent out, because finally he knows what he has in store," Ter Beek added.
