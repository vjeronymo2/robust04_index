Following the 1929 stock market crash and amid public furor concerning financial intermediaries that had engaged in flagrant manipulation in the securities markets, Congress enacted the Exchange Act to put an end to the practices that it found had contributed to the economic problems facing the Nation. 3 In drafting the legislation, Congress determined that the available common law remedies for fraud were inadequate to combat manipulation in the securities markets. 4 Because Congress recognized that market manipulation can assume many forms, it did not define the term in the Exchange Act or elsewhere. Statutory Provisions This section describes the principal antimanipulation provisions of the Exchange Act, sections 9(a), 10, and 15(c), 8 and the implementation of the concepts underlying those provisions in the regulation of securities offerings. It has long been recognized that securities offerings, which can be affected dramatically by short-term movements in security prices, are susceptible to manipulation. 9 8 15 U.S.C. 78i(a), 78j, and 78 o (c), respectively. 9 See Comment, Market Manipulation and the Securities Exchange Act, 46 Yale L.J. 624, 626 (1937).
