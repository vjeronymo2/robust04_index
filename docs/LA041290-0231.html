April 12, 1990, Thursday, P.M. Final

LIZ SMITH: 'STEEL MAGNOLIAS' TAKE ROOT FOR TELEVISION PILOT

"BLOOM WHERE you're planted!" This is the motto of actress Elaine Stritch, who is in Los Angeles getting ready to make the two-hour pilot for the TV version of "Steel Magnolias."

"I have decided to enjoy myself no matter what I am doing or where I am acting," says Elaine, who is most enthusiastic about this project. As you know, the movie version of the off-Broadway play won fans worldwide and earned much acclaim for playwright Robert Harling, director Herb Ross and producer Ray Stark. Now Stark and Columbia are gearing up to do the TV version for CBS. The cast goes to New Orleans next week to work on location and pick up their Southern accents.

Stritch is cast in what is now referred to as "the Shirley Mac-Laine role" of Ouiser. Polly Bergen is portraying the mayor's widow, Clairee, or "the Olympia Dukakis role." Sally Kirkland will essay the beauty shop owner, Truvy, "the Dolly Parton role." Sheila McCarthy is cast as the "born-again" beauty operator, Annelle, "the Daryl Hannah role." "Laverne and Shirley's" Cindy Williams is to portray the mother, M'Lynn, "the Sally Field role." The pilot will move right on from after the death of the beloved young Shelby, who was played on screen by Julia Roberts.

MONEY TALKS! At 20th Century Fox, they are said to be waxing nostalgic -- and pessimistic -- over reports that the new Bruce Willis movie "Die Hard 2" is $20 million over budget, bringing the current total to $60 million -- and it's not finished yet. It reminds some people of the good old, bad old days of "Cleopatra," which cost $40 million of those famous 1962 dollars and nearly destroyed the studio.

AT PARAMOUNT, they are nervously observing the soaring costs of the Tom Cruise movie "Days of Thunder." This racing car epic has also reached the $60-million mark. Its freewheeling producers, Don Simpson and Jerry Bruckheimer, have a certain total autonomy that makes the bookkeepers nervous.

The facts are that since Cruise lost the Oscar he was expected to win for "Born on the 4th of July," and since the Oliver Stone movie wasn't quite the usual Cruise smasheroo, "Days of Thunder" rolling on and on does make some people edgy.

SIDNEY ZION, one of New York's last authentic "characters," has turned in a new surprising piece of work to publisher Donald I. Fine, and we'll see his novel "Markers!" at the end of May. The fiction is being touted as this year's "Bonfire of the Vanities" and "Presumed Innocent" -- a tall order indeed.

Zion was a criminal lawyer and federal prosecutor before he went into the newspaper business, and I'd hazard a guess that he knows as much about the real gritty world as did Roy Cohn, whose semi-autobiography Zion helped write a couple of years ago.

"Markers!" has a list of tributes from the likes of Frank Sinatra, Erica Jong, Nick Pileggi, Victor Navasky and Jeffrey Hart. Publishers Weekly gave it a rave and Penthouse excerpts two chapters in its May issue.

THE REWORKED "Annie 2," set to begin rehearsals at the Norma Terris Theater in Chester, Conn., on April 23, will NOT have Dorothy Loudon playing the evil Miss Hannigan. After "Annie 2" closed down in Washington some months ago, Loudon says she was never "invited" to go to Chester.

It looks as if Helen Gallagher, a Broadway belter and favorite from the "Pal Joey" and "Hazel Flagg" days of big musicals, will play the part of the mean orphanage overseer.
