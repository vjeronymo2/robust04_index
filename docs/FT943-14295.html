940715 FT 15 JUL 94 / 'Tri-service' plan triumphs over tough vested interests: Operational command unified The plan announced yesterday represents a hard-won victory by defence ministry officials, over some stubborn vested interests, for the commonsense idea of joint or 'tri-service' facilities. The ministry has had to contend with the staunchly independent traditions of the three services, as well as some more practical objections. Under the new principle - referred by the ugly new word 'jointery' - there is to be a tri-service hospital and a tri-service staff college. Joint arrangements will be promoted in areas such as flying instruction, music lessons and veterinary care. Operational commands are by definition 'tri-service' as most wars are fought on land, sea and air. A permanent tri-service operational headquarters will be set up at Northwood, north-west of London. Ministry officials believe a single establishment to oversee operations in conflict zones will be better than what one called 'the current ad hoc approach of setting up such a headquarters only in response to a developing crisis'. Senior officers are known to have doubts about concentrating operational command in one, potentially vulnerable spot, however. Plans to bring the services together in a 20,000-strong Rapid Deployment Force, including men from the Parachute Regiment, Royal Marine commandos and RAF aircraft, are expected to receive a warmer welcome. The ministry has decided to make the naval hospital at Haslar, Hampshire a joint institution and to close the hospitals at Aldershot, Hampshire, and Wroughton, Wiltshire. All secondary medical care will be concentrated in a tri-service agency. A joint staff college is to be established by 1997, where officers will be sent on 10-month courses in tactical and operational doctrine.
