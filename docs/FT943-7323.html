940824 FT 24 AUG 94 / Bottomley in row on private healthcare Mrs Virginia Bottomley, the health secretary, yesterday forecast an increase in private care in the National Health Service, but played down the prospect of greater independence for trusts. Her remarks prompted an angry response from Labour, which said that the government was accelerating its drive to privatise the NHS. Mrs Bottomley's comments followed a suggestion by Mr Peter Griffiths, the former deputy chief executive of the NHS, that health trusts should be given greater independence. Mr Griffiths, who is now director of the King's Fund College, an independent medical training organisation, told BBC Radio that trusts could become 'non-profit-making organisations with the right kind of social ethos'. Mrs Bottomley said greater independence for trusts was 'certainly not on my agenda'. Trusts should be 'accountable to the secretary of state, and to local health authorities'. She said there was no reason why devolution of control to local level should ultimately lead to independence for health trusts. 'I think the model works extremely well. It is showing results. We don't foresee wholesale upheaval having once established the trusts across the country,' she said. However, Mrs Bottomley said the private or voluntary sector could sometimes provide a better deal for patients than the state sector. 'If the private sector can give a better deal for patients, then I welcome it,' she said. 'Where the private or public sector mean we can go further faster, provide an additional service, that is something we should certainly embrace.' Mr David Blunkett, Labour's health spokesman, said that Mrs Bottomley's comments confirmed that the government was trying to fragment the health service. He told her in a letter: 'The NHS is being sold off in parts, with the most lucrative segments on sale to the highest bidder.'
