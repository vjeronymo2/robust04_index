January 4, 1990, Thursday, Home Edition

DR. JOYCE BROTHERS: GROUP DOESN'T WELCOME HIM

DEAR DR. BROTHERS: I was sexually molested by my stepfather for three years starting when I was 9 years old. I blocked this all out of my memory until recently, when a shocking incident I won't describe made me recall it. Recently, I've joined a support group -- women who have experienced the same trauma as I. My husband, whom I love dearly, would like to accompany me to these meetings and I don't know how to explain that he isn't wanted. It's not that we're hostile, but it would be wrong. -- K.E.

DEAR K.E.: As I'm sure you know, there are lasting effects of incest and those who survive and are able to live relatively normal lives, as you have, will always carry with them a certain distrust and a sense of extreme cautiousness about those of the opposite sex. It would be insensitive to the women in your support group for you to bring your husband. Even though you love and trust him, obviously they don't.

It takes a long time for most incest victims to be able to discuss what happened to them and they certainly wouldn't be able to share their tragic past with strangers. Most women who have been sexually abused feel more comfortable with their own sex. Incidentally, this is also true of men who have been sexually abused in childhood.

I'm sure if you explain this to your husband he'll understand. Let him know how much you need this support group and how valuable it is to you and all the women who participate.

DEAR DR. BROTHERS: A new kid in our neighborhood found some pornographic magazines in his father's room and, unfortunately, showed them to my 15-year-old son. I'd certainly have preferred that he not be exposed to this kind of trash, but now, as his mother, I don't know how to erase the memories he must be carrying around in his mind. What he saw was not just "girlie stuff" but hard-core sadistic pornography. What can I do? -- W.P.

DEAR W.P.: Youngsters are much more influenced by what they see in their homes day after day than they are by any single exposure to pornography. If they've been raised in a climate where men and women treat each other kindly and respectfully, the sadistic behavior typified in the photos may shock them, but they won't be tempted to imitate it. They'll be much more likely to be offended than to be titillated.

Ideally, this would be a time when your son's father would have a talk with him. However, if his father isn't around, and there's no surrogate father you trust, then it might be wise for you to point out that physical power and violence are not an appropriate part of sexuality. Any sexual act that demeans or humiliates one of the sexual partners is wrong and is actually destructive to everyone.

You might also pick up the phone and have a talk with this boy's parents to let them know that you don't want your son to be exposed to this garbage.
