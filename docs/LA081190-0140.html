August 11, 1990, Saturday, Orange County Edition

LAGUNA MUSEUM HONORS EARLIER L.A. MODERNISTS;

EXHIBIT: 'TURNING THE TIDE: EARLY LOS ANGELES MODERNISTS 1920-1956' DISCREDITS THE NOTION THAT MODERN ART DIDN'T BEGIN IN CALIFORNIA UNTIL THE '50S AND '60S.

On Oct. 27, 1941, Life magazine did a story on the new wave of art combining "freedom and tradition" that was coming out of California. This art was created in a land less cramped for space than the East or Midwest, the article burbled, the "land of experiment."

But, as Susan Ehrlich pointed out in her lecture at the Laguna Art Museum on Thursday, the article never mentioned any of the modernist artists who really embodied the spirit of the new in Southern California.

Ehrlich, 47, is co-curator with Barry M. Heisler of "Turning the Tide: Early Los Angeles Modernists 1920-1956," an exhibit (at the Laguna museum through Sept. 16) that aims to correct the prevailing notion that no truly innovative work was done in the Southland before the crush of well-known artists of the late '50s and '60s.

Moving with brisk good humor through a discussion of works by several of the artists in the show, Ehrlich, a professor of art history at USC, made a point of discussing interwoven influences on the artists' work.

These diverse influences included the still-pristine expanse of coastal landscape, friendships (with other artists as well as collectors and members of the film community, a few of whom dabbled in art dealing), outside employment (particularly on the team that made the Disney movie "Fantasia"), political pressures from the far right, personal interests in Eastern philosophy and the quirks of a larger community that remained suspicious of serious culture.

One member of the close-knit arts community of the '30s was Peter Krasnow, who drove to Glendale from New York City with his wife, Rose, in 1922. A gregarious man, he befriended the photographer Edward Weston, modernist architects Rudolph Schindler and Richard Neutra, Expressionist artists' representative Galka Scheyer and numerous painters, film directors and others.

In Ehrlich's view, the strict horizontals and verticals in Krasnow's hot-colored abstract paintings can be compared to the framing elements of houses by his architect friends.

Krasnow showed at the Stendhal Art Gallery on Wilshire Boulevard, which also handled work by such European artists as Klee and Kandinsky. But during the Great Depression, buyers were scarce, and in any case the knowledgeable art community was very small. So owner Erle Stendhal opened a candy store above the gallery where he sold chocolates made by his own plant in boxes decorated with reproductions of the art in his gallery.

Ehrlich said she came across a photograph of Stendhal's goodies and noticed a familiar-looking gold foil-wrapped chocolate object in the background. On closer observation it proved to be an edible copy of Krasnow's 1927 bronze sculpture, "Atlantis."
