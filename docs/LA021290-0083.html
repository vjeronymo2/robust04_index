February 12, 1990, Monday, Home Edition

DEALS: MARKET WATCH;

LORENZO'S LINE JUST WON'T FLY IN FINANCIAL MARKETS

The empire of Frank Lorenzo, the chairman of Texas Air Corp., is in serious, serious trouble. And it couldn't happen to a nicer guy.

Lorenzo's problems don't involve airline unions or the managers of conventional airlines, who loathe and fear Lorenzo and for years have wished that he would shrivel up and blow away. The problems don't even directly involve the humongous 1989 loss of $886 million that Texas Air, which owns Eastern Air Lines and Continental Airlines, reported last week.

Part of Lorenzo's problem is the evaporation of the financial markets' enormous enthusiasm for airline assets. The enthusiasm -- which peaked in late summer, with Texas Air stock in the $20 range -- burst like a bubble in October when the $300-a-share United Airlines buyout cratered. Suddenly, everyone remembered that airlines are cyclical businesses.

But most of Lorenzo's problem is self-made. Lorenzo, whose debt-laden Continental Airlines couldn't make money on its operations last year, a boom time in the airline business, has had his credibility shattered by recent developments at Eastern.

Until recently, the creditors of Eastern, which has been operating under Chapter 11 of the U.S. Bankruptcy Code since March 9, 1989, loved Lorenzo and believed his every word. Especially the magic words: 'I'll pay 100% of your claim." Lorenzo had paid everyone 100% after he put Continental into bankruptcy in the early 1980s, so he could do it again.

But the affair between Lorenzo and Eastern's creditors came to a shocking end -- shocking for the creditors, that is -- in late January, when Lorenzo unveiled Eastern's plan to emerge from Chapter 11. Oops! Even though some of Eastern's creditors were to get 100% of face value, lots of people were being offered securities and cash ranging from maybe 45 cents of face value (for unsecured creditors) to less than 5 cents (for some preferred stockholders).

The jilted creditors feel betrayed, to put it mildly. As William Congreve would have said if he wrote about bankruptcy rather than more earthy passions, "Hell hath no fury like a creditor scorned."

To sweeten his plan, Lorenzo offered the unsecured creditors 40% of the stock of the new, post-bankruptcy Eastern. Given that Lorenzo, renowned for tough bargaining, is making an initial offer of 40%, it seems reasonable to assume that when and if the New Eastern emerges from Chapter 11, Lorenzo's Texas Air will own much less than the 60% that it proposes to keep. It may end up with very little of Eastern -- or none at all.

Then there's another problem, involving Eastern's dealings with other Texas Air companies. Up to now, no one in the bankruptcy proceedings except Eastern's labor unions seemed to care very much about these deals in which Eastern always seemed to get the short end. And the unions, as the Miami Review showed in two excellent articles in December, haven't been a factor in the bankruptcy proceedings.

Under the new circumstances, Eastern's creditors now care about these deals. A lot. A report by a court-appointed examiner is due one of these days, and it should make interesting reading. Lorenzo, who always defended the deals, told airline securities analysts in a Jan. 30 meeting that he has made a settlement offer for those transactions. How much, he didn't say.

But isn't Lorenzo well rid of Eastern, which wasn't worth anything anyway? No, because Texas Air can't just walk away from Eastern. It has guaranteed payment of interest and principal on $240 million of Eastern preferred stock that it gave to Eastern's stockholders in 1986 when it bought the company. The dividends run about $6.7 million every three months, and Texas Air has made the last three quarterly payments.

And then there's the real doozy: Eastern's unfunded pension liability, an obligation for which not only Eastern but also Texas Air and all its companies are "jointly and severally liable." That's lawyer talk for "on the hook." Last month, Lorenzo talked about a $450-million pension shortfall at Eastern. The U.S. Pension Benefit Guaranty Corp., which crunches the numbers using different assumptions, puts the shortfall at $1.1 billion and has gotten involved in the bankruptcy proceedings to protect its interests.

How deep is the pension hole, and how much will Texas Air have to put in toward filling it? Who knows? But it's obviously a lot.

Given the confusing way in which Lorenzo's empire reports financial results -- for example, the reported cash positions of Texas Air, Eastern and Continental as of Sept. 30 seemed to add up to $100 million more than the cash actually on hand -- it's hard to tell the true circumstances of Continental or Texas Air. But they can't be very good.

The bond market is treating Texas Air as if it's going to default on its debts. The last time I looked, the Texas Air bonds maturing this October were yielding about 25% a year. Bonds due in 1992 had annual yields in the 35% to 40% range. If the bond market's pricing is even close to right, Texas Air stock, recently at about $6, is overvalued.

Lorenzo has escaped disaster before. But this time, with the financial markets against him, fuel prices rising and his credibility in tatters, he may be on his final flight.

When Lorenzo took over Eastern in 1986, he was hailed as a genius, and Texas Air stock rose to the $50 range. What an irony it will be if Eastern turns out to be what sinks him.
