October 8, 1989, Sunday, Home Edition

UNFAIR TO COMMUNISTS

Jules Feiffer's depiction of the aging communist of the '30s (Sept. 24) couldn't be more unfair. Most people who joined the Communist Party ended their membership when they were able to divine the villainy of Stalin. Some stayed with the resolve to improve their party. I am aware of none who have concealed from their consciences the many ugly realities of the Soviet Union.

To those types of Depression-era communists, the right-headedness of Gorbachev is certainly no embarrassment. Given their disillusionment, their dedication to progress and the persecution they have suffered in this country, it should be of surprise to no one that they may look upon his actions with a certain amount of suspicion.

Then again, Jules Feiffer is a rather admirable fellow himself, not to mention creative and intelligent. It's entirely possible that the grumpy old man in his cartoon is not the run-of-the-mine American Red at all, but rather the breed apart: that sanctimonious coward who will call himself by whatever name is fashionable at the time.

RONALD WEBSTER

Long Beach
