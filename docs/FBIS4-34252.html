BFN [Text] The International Seabed Authority is now located in Jamaica. The Authority is expected to hold its first meeting in Kingston on the 16th of November. This follows an agreement reached by the developed nations of the world on June 3 to ratify the United Nations convention on the Law of the Sea. This disclosure was made by Minister of Foreign Affairs and Trade Dr. Paul Robertson during his contribution to the 1994-95 sectoral debate. Philip Hamilton has more: [Begin recording] [Hamilton] The breakthrough follows years of intense debate between the United Nations and several industrialzed countries where technology [words indistinct]. The United States had long opposed the section of the convention dealing with the mining of seabed resources, an issue which saw them refusing to ratify the convention for well over a decade. Minister Robertson told parliament that Jamaica had played a key role in the agreement, which will be presented for adoption next month at the special UN General Assembly. [Robertson] The main, outstanding issue involved the role and activities of the multilaterally owned enterprise in the mining of the seabed as against private entities. That was the sticking point why a number of the industrialized countries did not sign. [Hamilton] But there has been much controversy over the site for the Seabed Authority's headquarters. Although Kingston was chosen as the headquarters interim site several years ago, Montego Bay has been lobbying hard for the Authority's permanent home. Minister Robertson says it will be some time before the permanent headquarters is constructed in Jamaica. He says the site will be determined by the United Nations based on several criteria, among them infrastructure as well as security.
