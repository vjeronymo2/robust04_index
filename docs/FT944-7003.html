941126 FT 26 NOV 94 / Weekend Money: Finance Week BUSINESS The bitter confrontation between John Major and the Tory party's Euro-sceptics escalated as leaders of the anti-Brussels rebellion threatened to turn it into a direct challenge to the prime minister's leadership. The Tories were embarrassed by a leaked campaign strategy document which suggested backbench 'yobbos' should, metaphorically, beat up Labour leader Tony Blair. It also claimed that excessive executive pay packages caused 'real offence'. The document came to light on the day it was revealed that Cedric Brown, chairman of British Gas, was awarded a Pounds 205,000 pay rise. Major later condemned 'unjustifiable' salary increases as Brown said he was considering whether to forego part of his increase. Shareholders may be given powers to curb excessive pay increases for top company executives, chancellor Kenneth Clarke disclosed in the Commons. George Soros, the international financier, is pulling out of UK commercial property only 18 months after announcing plans to make a 'long-term' investment of up to Pounds 250m. Underwriting for the biggest bank loan of the year, the Dollars 5bn loan for the Swedish government, should be complete by the middle of this week. Sweden is the biggest borrower on the world's bond markets but has turned to loans as their price has fallen. The Data Protection Registrar has called for talks with British Telecommunications over a breach of security that has resulted in the leak of some of the UK's most sensitive security telephone numbers. The two main opposition parties unveiled alternative budgets focusing on tax reform, job creation and investment in industry and education. At least 51 per cent of Railtrack, owner of Britain's railway lines, signalling equipment and stations, will be sold in the life of the present parliament, the government said. The Treasury has abandoned its review of whether companies' dividend payments are too high and there will be no measures in the Budget next week aimed at controlling dividends. Allied Dunbar, the life insurer owned by BAT Industries, the tobacco and financial services group, is to replace its range of pension policies with a single adaptable plan intended to give customers greater flexibility. The Securities and Investments Board, the chief City watchdog, has agreed to become closely involved in monitoring complaints against Knight Williams, one of the UK's largest independent financial advisers. A 1933 penny, one of only a few struck in that year, was sold at Spink's coin auction in London to a Hertfordshire dealer for a record Pounds 25,300. MARKETS US share prices plummeted amid fears that US interest rates would have to rise further to head off inflationary pressures. On Tuesday, the Dow Jones Industrial Average closed down 91 at 3,677.99, its lowest level in almost five months and its largest one-day fall since February 4. Share prices fell worldwide as the after-shock of Tuesday's drop in the Dow rippled through international stock markets. The FT-SE 100 fell 51 points on Wednesday, its fifth largest fall this year. In Tokyo, the Nikkei 225 average fell 262 points on Thursday and closed lower than on any day since January 25. London Stock Exchange rules which allow the prices of large blocks of shares to be kept secret for up to five days are anti-competitive, the Office of Fair Trading said. Analysts expect a volatile period for Norwegian share prices after Monday's referendum on membership of the European Union, irrespective of the outcome of the vote. European potato prices have doubled in recent months as production has fallen to its lowest level for five years, according to a survey by the UK's Potato Marketing Board. COMPANIES Johnson Matthey, the precious metals technology group, reported a record first-half performance and said there was every chance that this would continue in the second half. Its merger talks with Cookson, the industrial materials concern, collapsed on Tuesday, after a failure to reach agreement on terms. British Assets Trust announced a structural reorganisation because it is not producing enough income to pay promised dividends. The reorganisation is likely to affect some shareholders' dividends for several years. An increase in selling space helped Storehouse, owner of the BHS and Mothercare retail chains, to lift interim sales by 7 per cent and operating profits from retailing by a third. Welsh Water stunned the market with a reversal into loss and hefty exceptional charges for a non-regulated business (Acer). North West Water has struck a deal with Bechtel of the US to pursue international water and sewage contracts. Sharelink Investment Services, the Birmingham-based share dealing company, continues to lose money on its trading. For the half-year to September 30, it announced a pre-tax loss of Pounds 468,000, compared with profits of Pounds 3.68m. The share price dipped on Wednesday. Halifax Building Society, the UK's largest with assets of more than Pounds 60bn, and Leeds Permanent, the fifth-largest society, are planning to merge and float the new company on the stock market. SmithKline Beecham, the UK drugs company, agreed to sell its animal health business to US rival Pfizer and called a halt to the Dollars 7.7bn of corporate transactions it began last May. TeleWest made a storming stock market debut on Tuesday, closing ahead of the flotation price. Shares in the regional electricity companies fell sharply after the Labour party's shadow chancellor, Gordon Brown, repeated plans for a tax on windfall gains arising from the sale of the National Grid. Leading pharmaceuticals groups Glaxo and Wellcome performed well on the London stock market during the week following encouraging reports from a conference on Aids. RJB Mining, the coal company chosen by the government as preferred bidder for British Coal's English regions, is seeking to shave at least Pounds 35m off its Pounds 914m bid after finding coal stocks are below expectations. Vodafone, the UK market leader in cellular phones, returned better-than- expected interim profits despite the impact of fraud and international losses. A strong performance from US cereal sweeteners and starches helped Tate & Lyle overcome weaker US sugar markets and modest growth in Europe to report a 23 per cent rise in pre-tax profits. The National Grid, the England and Wales power transmission company, is to increase its interim dividend to the regional electricity companies which own it by 17 per cent, to Pounds 54m. Ciba, the Swiss drug company, made the biggest corporate acquisition in biotechnology for almost five years with a deal which lifts its stake in the California-based Chiron to 49.9 per cent. Yorkshire Water announced plans to cut another 600 jobs. The latest cuts are in addition to the 400 redundancies announced four months ago, before the July price review.
