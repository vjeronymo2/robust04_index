March 22, 1990, Thursday, Home Edition

FOOD AND COMMON SENSE: SOLUTIONS TO INTESTINAL PROBLEMS

The advertising is delicately phrased in terms like "intestinal irregularity." But a rose by any other name is still a rose, and constipation, however discreetly described, is constipation. In this country, easing the problem is a booming business. Over-the-counter remedies sell like hot cakes, even though some carry risk of side effects.

Just as mouthwash, soap and deodorant ads can cause hitherto unconcerned consumers to worry if their breaths or bodies don't smell fresh enough, manufacturers of products to step up bowel activity may encourage us to question whether we are as "regular" as we should be. If not, they hope we'll turn to laxatives or stool softeners for relief.

But is the concept of "normal" bowel activity a valid one? And when difficulties do occur, are drugs the solution? In both cases, the answer is not always yes.

Now, a recent large-scale study under the leadership of Dr. James P. Everhart of the National Institutes of Health, has gathered new and useful information about our bowel habits. Two nationwide surveys, encompassing more than 10,000 people, were conducted 10 years apart in order to learn about bowel function and how patterns changed over time.

At the first interview, subjects were asked three questions:

-- Do you have trouble with your bowels that makes you constipated or gives you diarrhea?

-- How often do you usually have a bowel movement?

-- During the past six months, have you used any medicine, drug or pills for bowel trouble?

At the second interview, the first two questions were repeated and two more asked:

-- How often do you use a laxative or stool softener?

-- Which of the following best describes your usual bowel movement: Normal, loose or watery, or hard and very firm?

Findings revealed that women were more likely than men to report constipation and infrequent bowel movements -- defined as three or fewer per week. Older people who reported constipation were more prone to use stool softeners or laxatives than were younger subjects who said they were constipated. Indeed, the proportion who said at both interviews that they used bowel medication increased from about 28% among the youngest group to nearly 58% in the oldest.

But -- perhaps because they did use these products -- older individuals also were less likely than younger ones to have infrequent bowel movements. Researchers noted that whites were more likely to have daily bowel movements than were blacks (73% versus 64%). The reason for this finding remains unexplained.

Impaired bowel function was associated with several factors, among them lower educational level, depression and decreased physical activity. No link to fiber consumption was identified, except at the extreme low end of intake, and then only among men. Bowel functioning was unrelated to relative fatness, coffee and tea consumption, smoking habits and, among women, to the number of children they had or use of estrogens.

Statistics are interesting, but how do they apply to you? The first -- and perhaps most important -- point is that "normal" does not necessarily mean daily bowel movements. According to the study, many of us do not have daily bowel movements. Yet a much smaller number reported constipation at both interviews. Thus, normal bowel function spans a broad range, anywhere from two or three movements a day to as infrequently as once in three days. Constipation, therefore, must be understood in terms of what is normal for you.

As for the failure to find an association between fiber and bowel function, that may well be explained by the generally low-fiber intake of the study population. It does not mean that fiber isn't helpful in promoting normal bowel functioning. Rather, ample evidence suggests that generous amounts of fiber -- in the form of fresh and lightly cooked fruits and vegetables, whole grains and dried legumes -- contribute to maintaining a healthy bowel. So too does drinking large amounts of fluid, as much as six to eight tall glasses a day.

Practically speaking, one of the most crucial revelations of this study was the relationship between physical activity and bowel function. Sluggishness, headache, a bloated, gassy feeling and general abdominal discomfort, as well as hard feces that are difficult to pass, are symptoms that may not be life-threatening but can cause a general malaise.

The link between impaired bowel function and low levels of physical activity (which, in other studies, has been strongly associated with depression) is just one more reason to exercise. In other words, if your bowel is sluggish, maybe you need to exercise.

We suggest that people who worry about constipation conduct their own study. Ask yourself three questions:

-- What bowel-activity pattern is normal for me?

-- Am I eating fiber-rich foods and drinking plenty of liquids?

-- What is my exercise level?

By thinking in these terms, you may be able to work out a solution that lets you skip a trip to the drugstore.

Putting Potatoes in Cold Storage

Question: My daughter saw me taking potatoes out of the refrigerator and informed me that it was not the correct way to store them. She couldn't tell me why I was wrong, just that I was. I've always kept them there to avoid sprouting. Can you comment?

Answer: At refrigerator temperatures, the starch in potatoes gradually converts to sugar. When cooked, potatoes with a higher sugar content tend to darken.

Control of the conversion of starch to sugar is important commercially, where the optimal ratio of starch to sugar depends on how the potato is to be used. For example, if it's going into a prepared food such as canned hash or beef stew, the high temperatures used in processing could cause changes in flavor and color.

On the other hand, if some browning is desirable, as in frozen French fries or hash browns, then there must be enough sugar to allow the outsides to brown without extensive heat. Similarly, the light brown color of potato chips is dependent on adequate amounts of sugar. If sugar content of the potatoes rises too high, though, the chips can burn or become too dark. In order to achieve the correct sugar concentration, potatoes are sometimes stored under controlled conditions for as long as three weeks.

But let's get back to your kitchen. You can minimize risk of sprouting by storing potatoes in a cool, moist (but not humid) environment and by buying only as many as you can use within a reasonable period of time.

Sprouting does not render potatoes unsafe. Just break or peel off the sprouts before cooking.

Q: Our daughter recently visited a nutritionist who claimed that just by looking in her eyes he could tell she was allergic to all dairy products and wheat. He said if she didn't cut out these foods, then eventually she would get colitis. He prescribed a B vitamin complex, an herb combination and a calcium supplement. He charged only $5 for the exam, which made me wonder if he was on the up-and-up or if he just gives these exams so he can charge a fortune for the vitamins.

A: Your suspicions probably are right on target. From what you describe, he is basing his prognosis for your daughter on some variation of pseudoscientific practice sometimes called "irilogy." It's true that checking the eye, including the iris, can be an important part of health examinations that go well beyond the eye itself. However, irilogy carries the use of the eye for diagnosis to unreasonable limits.

You mentioned that the supplements were expensive. That's not the only issue. Safety is another. Some B vitamins can be toxic if taken in large doses for a period of time. Similarly, the safety of herbal preparations depends on what they contain and in what amounts. Most herb teas, for example, have not been tested for safety.

But perhaps most important is that this man has placed your daughter on a restricted diet for no scientifically acceptable reason. If your daughter does have symptoms that suggest allergies, she may want to discuss them with her physician. He or she may, in turn, refer her to an allergy specialist, who is trained to make an accurate diagnosis and, if he finds an allergy, suggest a diet plan.

Q: Is buckwheat the same as bulgur wheat?

A: No. Bulgur is a lightly processed form of whole wheat produced by parboiling, drying and then breaking the whole-wheat kernels. It may be soaked, seasoned and eaten without further cooking, or it can be cooked with broth as a pilaf. It can also be cooked with water and served as breakfast cereal. Many people like to add yogurt, dried fruits or nuts to it. In a Middle Eastern version of meat loaf called kibbe, bulgur is mixed with ground lamb.

Buckwheat, on the other hand, is not a true grain cereal. As with bulgur, the whole seed -- called buckwheat groats -- can be cooked and eaten by itself. But it is often combined with noodles. In this country, most of the buckwheat we consume is processed into flour and used in pancake mix.

When cooked, both buckwheat groats and bulgur are calorically similar, providing about 200 calories per cup.
