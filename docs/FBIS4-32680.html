BFN [Article by Vjekoslav Radovic] [Text] New York, June 25 (TANJUG) -- Ousted from Bosnia by the ruling of the 1878 Berlin Congress after centuries of occupation, Turkey is now being sent back by the U.N. secretary-general. The former occupier is returning 116 years later under the guise of a "peacekeeper." Apparently aware of the paradox, Secretary-General Butrus Butrus-Ghali has long opposed the idea of including Turkey in the U.N. Protection Force (UNPROFOR) in the breakaway Yugoslav republic of Bosnia-Herzegovina, seeing this as being sharply at odds with the proclaimed principle that neighbouring countries and former occupiers could not serve as peacekeepers in the Balkan crisis. Eventually, because of a poor response from the other nations to an appeal to donate troops to UNPROFOR, the principle has gone the way of many another and was trampled underfoot by Butrus-Ghali's "yes" to the Turks. However, Turkey's historical occupation of Bosnia and return, this time under the wing of the United Nations, is not the only paradox which has made the peacekeeping force a mockery. Turkey has a notoriously smudged record in international relations, which the West is inclined to turn a blind eye to because of the country's strategic importance to NATO. One has only to remember Turkey's presence in Cyprus, where its forces have held one-half of the island under occupation for twenty years. One might think that Turkey has spent the past twenty years under sanctions for the occupation. Not so. Turks remain in Cyprus, the United Nations wastes billions of dollars on maintaining a peacekeeping force there, but there is no mention of sanctions from any quarter. Sanctions seem to be reserved for the likes of Serbs, Haitians, Iraqis and, possibly, North Koreans. Nobody is even trying to answer the question whether a country can be an occupier of a neighbour and still serve as a peacekeeper in another country, which it held under subjugation for centuries, at that. U.N. press spokesmen have recently been stunned by the provocative question whether Serb peacekeeping troops might be sent to Cyprus. The question seemed absurd and provoked gales of mirth from the assembled reporters. Nobody laughed about the deployment of Turks in Bosnia- Herzegovina. And indeed, this is no laughing matter. The Western media raised hue and cry in February when 300 Russian troops were to be deployed in Sarajevo. However, the world is taking in its stride the deployment of 1,500 Turks, in addition to the Malaysians, Jordanians and Egyptians already in Bosnia and the announced arrival of Bangladeshis and Pakistanis. The hypocrisy of the international community knows no bounds. Another important and unanswered question emerges, viz. can a country enforce law and order in another state, while being unable to keep its own house in order. Turkey is notorious for its violation of human rights, and ranks fifth in the world for the number of journalists in prison. The world has forgotten the first big genocide of the 20th century, which was perpetrated by Turks killing more than a million Armenians. Similarly, the Western media today have deliberately forgotten the genocide perpetrated by the Nazis against the Serbs in World War Two. This, then, is the picture of the state which is returning to Bosnia-Herzegovina as a "peacekeeper." All this should be reason enough for the powers that be on the east river to take a closer look at the moral criteria to be applied to would-be peacekeepers. There is no sign, however, that anybody is likely to give the matter a thought. According to the latest information, the 1,500 Turkish troops and experts will be deployed along the lines of separation between Moslems and Croats in the areas of Zenica and Kakanj in central Bosnia. But, can one really believe that this will be so, after so many principles have been trampled on. According to a statement submitted by the Turkish mission to the United Nations, the Turkish troops are bringing to Bosnia 393 vehicles, including 70 trucks and 33 armoured cars. The statement was withdrawn before it could be seen by most of the reporters, leaving behind a massive speculation about 200 vehicles which are mentioned en masse, but not separately described. It is a generally recognised fact that Turkey has been smuggling arms to the Moslems in Bosnia. So now it would appear that it is helping their brothers in Allah with one hand, while tipping its blue helmet with the other. When to this is added the ease with which international institutions and the Western media are ignoring the latest Moslem offensive in Bosnia, it is not clear whether the world wants peace or war in Bosnia. Or is it.
