May 27, 1990, Sunday, Home Edition

COLUMN LEFT;

THE MAJORITY RULES, BUT NOT FOR LONG;

CALIFORNIA'S VOTING POPULATION IS 80% WHITE; THE URBAN SCHOOLS' IS 20%. A 'NEW POLITICS' IS DAWNING.

There will be an election next week in a state called California, a pleasant and orderly nation of white people living well in Mediterranean climates along the Pacific Ocean between Oregon and Mexico. The big contest is between Atty. Gen. John Van de Kamp and former San Francisco Mayor Dianne Feinstein for the Democratic nomination for governor.

The winner will face Republican Sen. Pete Wilson in November for the chance to govern a place called California, an unruly and rather wary conglomeration of people of many colors, many incomes, many cultures.

There are two Californias sharing the same space these days, in different decades, different time zones. There is a 20-year gap, at least, between California on election days and California on any other day of the year. Next Tuesday, more than 80% of the voters will be white. Next Wednesday, almost half of California will be black and many shades of brown and tan.

The current coexistence of old and new Californias is one of the most extraordinary episodes of change in American history -- and most American history is about change. The voting population of California in the 1980s was, and will be on June 5, between 80% and 85% white -- and 7% black, 7% Latino and 2% or 3% Asian. But the overall population is now estimated at only 58% white -- or Anglo, as most say here. "Euro-American" is used by some scholars in anticipation of the day, not far away, when Anglos are again a minority here, as they were until the 19th Century.

The overall population, as opposed to the voting citizenry, includes both children and non-citizens, including immigrants, some legal, some not. But if you used demographic statistics including only actual U.S. citizens above the voting age of 18, the white total would still reach only a little more than 60%.

"There are new and potentially dangerous pressures in the demographic gaps now," said Mervyn Field, director of the California Poll, which produced some of the figures used here. "The non-voters are becoming more and more different from voters in color, class and life-style."

The time and place to see that is any Sunday at the intersection of Alvarado and 7th streets near downtown Los Angeles. If you are white and middle-aged, as I am, you can sit in Langer's delicatessen with a collection of urban types as American as bagels and lox -- a cross-section of the people who vote in Los Angeles and most other American cities, Jewish and Christian, most white but a couple of blacks.

Step outside and you magically transcend great distance and time. You are in Guatemala City. The streets are crowded with families, all Latino, speaking Spanish in the accents of Guatemala, Mexico, El Salvador, Nicaragua and Honduras. Men wait in long lines to buy ices or to make telephone calls. Children chase each other through a maze of food stalls along the edge of Westlake Park. There is nothing visible to indicate that this is the United States until three Los Angeles Fire Department trucks come screaming down Alvarado as if they were spaceships from another planet. How many people on the streets are legally in this country? Maybe half, but the ones who have American citizenship don't often vote -- and neither do the Asians, mostly legal, in other parts of the state.

If Langer's customers reflect today's voting, Alvarado, Koreatown and Asian neighborhoods up and down the coast reflect tomorrow's California. More than 80% of the children in the lower grades of Los Angeles schools are "people of color." Only 33% of the students are white at Berkeley, the best of the fine schools that make up the University of California system. Ten years ago, the proportion of whites at Berkeley was 58%, but their numbers have eroded with affirmative action and the scholastic achievements of Asians and Asian-Americans.

The schools, from kindergarten to college, are where the races and cultures of modern California come together -- and divide. White resentment over admission to the state university is balanced by minority-group resentment about the flight of white students from public schools, usually followed by the reluctance of voters and politicians to pay or levy taxes to maintain the kind of high-quality California schools represented by the Norman Rockwell past.

So next Tuesday, white voters will choose Van de Kamp or Feinstein to oppose Wilson. But the victory photos will look like old tintypes the moment they are developed. This is already the politics of the past in the California.
