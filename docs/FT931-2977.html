930317 FT 17 MAR 93 / Group debates life after Taurus MORE THAN 60 represent-atives of custody and registration companies met yesterday at the offices of Royal Bank of Scotland in the City in an attempt to agree a joint approach on a successor to the Taurus settlement system. Some large institutional investors were also present, and the meeting marked the start of concerted lobbying on the issue. Custodians and registrars, who provide securities services to large investors and listed companies, potentially have the most to lose from the stock-market settlement system chosen in place of Taurus. The representatives at the meeting agreed to submit a joint proposal to the Bank of England task force on settlement, under associate director Mr Pen Kent, by the end of this week. This will recommend that the Bank pushes ahead with developing an automated settlement system for large institutional investors. Also, it will recommend setting up a clearing house to take over the running of settlement from the Stock Exchange, said Mr Pearson. A further recommendation will be for a quick move to rolling settlement, under which all share bargains are settled after a set number of days. Mr Terry Pearson, head of global custody at Royal Bank of Scotland, said yesterday's meeting had shown a positive reaction to the Bank of England's push to find a quick replacement for Taurus. He said the initiative did not represent an attempt by particular vested interests in the City to protect their position. Registrars derailed the original Taurus proposal in the late 1980s by opposing the setting up of a central database to hold all records of shareholdings. It was also considered technically difficult and expensive to build such a large database, although computing costs have fallen since then. Yesterday Mr Pearson said the idea of a central share depository and registration service remained technically difficult. 'That was kicked into touch before,' he said. 'It was an impossible task.'
