Language: English Article Type:BFN [Text] Bucharest ROMPRES, 9/3/1994 -- The Bucharest newspapers of 9 March comment on Romanian President Ion Iliescu's tour of countries in Europe and Asia. "The first stage of the new presidential tour" daily LIBERTATEA writes, "was Vilnius, Lithuania's capital, where, considering that Romania and Lithuania have had similar political evolution and principles, Presidents Ion Iliescu and Algirdas Brazauskas signed the first Treaty of Friendship and Collaboration between the two countries." The government daily VOCEA ROMANIEI emphasizes in a commentary entitled "The First Steps Toward Rewarding Cooperation" the Lithuanian head of state's assessment made during a dinner according to which "this visit has given new impetus to the Lithuanian-Romanian relations, materialized among other things in bilateral agreements, a political agreement included." Also, the Lithuanian head of state expressed the confidence that "it is about the first steps on the path of a cooperation rewarding for either party." In turn, President Iliescu noted that "the visit is a welcome opportunity to single out the traditions of the relations between our countries and peoples, the similarity of certain moments and stages in their remote history." "What seems noteworthy to me," writes the CRONICA ROMANA special envoy, "is the moment at which Romania is signing the treaty with Lithuania, a country which has not regretted for a moment its disjoining from the CIS. The contacts between Romanian and Lithuanian businessmen proved promising for either party. A fine winter tale has been written in Vilnius, of two countries that are being united faster and more thoroughly than we could suspect, by their poverty and problems. And they, the Lithuanians, are close enough for the relations between the two countries to follow the growth confirmed through the treaty signed yesterday". The DIMINEATA envoy in Vilnius opines in his dispatch that "the presence of Romania's president in the Lithuanian capital and especially the political Treaty of Friendship and Collaboration between the two states have meanings that cannot be missed by analysts. Above all, both states were among the first (Romania was the very first) to sign the Partnership for Peace with the NATO and that was not prompted by the wish to remake the 'sanitary belt' through which the west seemed to isolate the Soviet empire in the inter-bellum period, but in order to build a system of relations meant to secure their quiet which they need so much in order to build a market economy and a state of law, in the lastingness of which they are equally interested".
