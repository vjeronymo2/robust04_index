BFN [Text] Bucharest, ROMPRES, 13/6/1994 -- The transition process has inevitably diminished Romania's capacity to ensure its own security. Romania does not have, as at now, allies and firm security guarantees. The main danger lies in having its own domestic weaknesses exploited. There is a direct relationship between the reform and the national security. These are some of the main ideas Ioan Mircea Pascu, secretary of state in the Romanian Ministry of National Defense, elaborated on in the comprehensive interview for the CURIERUL NATIONAL of 13 June. Speaking of the integrated concept of Romania's national security, a recently issued document, Pascu says the study ends with the basic conclusion that for now and for a while to come, "Romania cannot rely on allies and firm security guarantees." Besides, the transition from one type of society to another has diminished its capacity to ensure security by its own means, "particularly as we are located in an area characterized by a high degree of conflict potential." In Mr. Pascu's opinion, the most important sign of Romania's insecurity since December 1989 is the very fact that threats are no longer mostly external, and that "the main danger is posed by the possibility of having its domestic weaknesses exploited." And among the main internal risk factors he listed the economic situation, the social costs required by the changes brought about by the transition period, a diminished social cohesion etc. To face the domestic risk factors this country needs to ensure the continuity of the reform process: "the success of the reform, however cannot be obtained in the absence of security, and to have security, we need to take the reform to the end successfully." The defense official also mentioned some of the external risk factors for Romania's security, such as the appearance and worsening of certain conflicts, the existence of zones with a high potential of political and military instability close to Romania. As directly concerning Romania's security, Secretary of State Mircea Pascu names international developments like the recent stability plan adopted in Paris, the agreement for the reduction of the conventional armaments and troops in Europe, the Partnership for Peace plan, Romania's geopolitical position, the relations with Russia. Concluding, the dignitary predicted about the future of Romania's security in the following terms: "If we ignore the unpredictable, which, unfortunately, will continue to be there both in the near and medium future, I think we can be optimistic about Romania's long- term chances of integration in the European and Euro-Atlantic security systems."
