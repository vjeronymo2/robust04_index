Language: English Article Type:BFN [Text] Tokyo, March 3 KYODO -- Pollees who consider Japan-U.S. relations to be good plunged to a record low of 20 percent, down from 37 percent two months earlier, an ASAHI SHIMBUN poll released Thursday showed. Those who do not consider the bilateral relations to be good increased to 64 percent, from 42 percent in December last year, the ASAHI said. In the survey, 16 percent, down from the previous 26 percent, said the bilateral relations will head in a good direction, while 23 percent predicted relations would head in a bad direction. As for Prime Minister Morihiro Hosokawa's refusal to accept the U.S. demand for numerical targets to measure the degree of openness of the Japanese market, voiced during a summit meeting with U.S. President Bill Clinton last month, 20 percent said the action was only natural, 57 percent said it was unavoidable, and 12 percent said it was not good. The ASAHI conducted the survey on February 27 and 28, polling 3,000 people aged 20 and older, of whom 2,318 people responded.
