May 16, 1990, Wednesday, Home Edition

'DIE HARD 2': BITING THE BULLET OVER BUDGET;

MOVIES: THE DELAYED OPENING OF BRUCE WILLIS' ACTION-THRILLER ADDS FUEL TO TALK OF A $20-MILLION COST OVERRUN.

Twentieth Century Fox appears to have further underscored Hollywood talk that "Die Hard 2" may be as much as $20 million over its $40 million production budget. Fox officials vehemently deny huge overruns, but this week they delayed release of the Bruce Willis thriller from June to July.

On Tuesday, Fox said the producers of "Die Hard 2" would deliver the final version of the film June 17, making the advertised opening of June 22 nearly impossible to meet. As a result, the movie will have a July 2 premiere in Los Angeles, followed by a national release on the July 4th weekend.

Saying "We're saving the best for last" and "We're waiting for a whole weekend of Friday nights," Fox officials attempted to put the best face on the delay. They have pointed out that June is already loaded with a potential blockbuster movie a week.

For weeks, the sequel to the 1988 box-office hit, set in a snowed-in, terrorist-overrun Dulles International Airport outside of Washington, has been the subject of speculation about runaway production costs. The original "Die Hard," set in a Los Angeles high rise, cost $28 million to make and turned into an $80 million hit.

But at a lavish 35th birthday bowling party for Willis, rumors of a studio-ordered audit to see how producer Joel Silver handled production funds and other stories have clouded the prospects for this summer's sequel.

The huge cost overrun stories are all bunk, according to Fox production president Roger Birnbaum. He declined to say how much "Die Hard 2" might have gone over its reported $40 million budget, but he dismissed reports that the cost of the film may have soared past the $60 million mark.

"The only thing I will tell you is that (the movie) went five days over budget," Birnbaum told The Times. "That was due to unfortunate circumstances. Quite honestly, I don't know where all these rumors emanate from."

Birnbaum pointed to similar over-budget rumors surrounding Carolco's "Total Recall" and two Paramount films, "Days of Thunder" and "Godfather III," adding, "I wouldn't be surprised if rival companies put these stories out to take the heat off themselves."

The "unfortunate circumstances" that put "Die Hard 2" over budget was the weather, not Willis' birthday party or Silver's alleged profligacy, according to a Fox spokeswoman. "Our problems were either finding actual snow or a temperature in which we could make snow," she said. "We needed a blanket as far as the eye could see for the exterior of the airport."

Pre-production, special effects and initial sound-stage filming began last December in Los Angeles, but then the Larry & Charles Gordon/Joel Silver production hit the road. First stop in January was normally snowed-in Stapleton International Airport in Denver, where Deputy Director of Aviation Richard Boulware said the cast and crew spent an estimated $50,000 a day for three and a half weeks, just on per-diem expenses such as hotels, car rentals and food.

"We loved having them here," said Boulware.

But the snow melted early and the "Die Hard 2" odyssey moved farther north to Moses Lake, Wash. By March, the snow melted again and the crew packed up and headed to Alpena, Mich. Spring caught up with them there as well and they had to go even farther north to Kincheloe Air Force Base near Kinross, Mich., in April.

Willis's salary is a factor in the higher cost of producing "Die Hard 2," but not a significant one. He is earning $7.5 million for reprising his role as a police officer who single-handedly devastates terrorists -- $2.5 million more than he got the original.

"Die Hard" also transformed Willis from a TV-comedy star into a full-fledged action-thriller movie star, rivaling Sylvester Stallone and Arnold Schwarzenegger at the box office. The original shooting budget climbed proportionately, from $28 million for "Die Hard" to a reported $40 million for "Die Hard 2."

When he turned 35 on March 19, Willis was feted with a Los Angeles bash that had a bowling alley theme. In homage to the actor's blue collar New Jersey persona, "Bruno," bowling shirts for each guest were flown to New York, personalized with their names at a company that specializes in embroidering bowling shirts, and messengered back to each guest who had to wear them to the door as their invitation, according to sources.

Eddie Murphy, who came to the party in street clothes, supposedly had one of his gofers carry his shirt to the door on a hanger in order to get inside.

But the cost of the party did not come out of "Die Hard 2" production funds, according to the Fox spokeswoman.

And Silver, who has a longstanding reputation as a free-spending producer, did not permanently alienate Fox executives by going over budget, according to Birnbaum. He has just completed two other movies for Fox -- "The Adventures of Ford Fairlane" and "Predator II" -- and is under contract to produce two more.

"Let me clarify all of this very, very simply: at the premiere of 'Die Hard 2,' you will see Joel Silver sitting next to me," said Birnbaum. "We audit every single movie that we make during and in post-production. That's what we're doing with 'Die Hard 2,' just like every other movie. There's no special movie audit going on."

Fox's current strategy calls for a "Die Hard 2"opening on 2,000 screens. Several exhibitors are expected to use more expensive, but higher quality 70 millimeter prints to maximize the impact of the opening.

But postponing a promised blockbuster can have disastrous results, as the studio learned last summer when it repeatedly put off release of "The Abyss" until August, after the momentum from such early summer entries as Warner's "Batman" and Disney's "Honey, I Shrunk the Kids" had already eaten up the lion's share of America's summer movie dollars.
