February 18, 1990, Sunday, Home Edition

WALL STREET / JOHN CRUDELE: DREXEL WAS NO SURPRISE BUT MARKET'S CALM WAS

What was the biggest surprise last week? To many on Wall Street, it wasn't that Drexel Burnham Lambert was forced into bankruptcy. That was expected.

More surprising was that the financial markets reacted as calmly as they did as Drexel was going out of business.

"I'm amazed at how the Drexel event was absorbed without a ripple," says Donald Straszheim, chief economist for Merrill Lynch Capital Markets Group. "It does seem that somebody in Washington has done a good bid of homework since 1987 to handle special events."

The fact that the stock and bond markets barely shrugged this past Monday, Tuesday and Wednesday as Drexel was self-destructing brought out the conspiracy theories. These are the same theories that surfaced Oct. 16, the first trading day after the Oct. 13 mini-crash. And the theories are similar to those expressed by many after the crash of October, 1987, when people speculated that somehow the government intervened to stabilize stock prices.

The government has always denied that it would interfere in the stock market, and nothing can prove that to the contrary. But that didn't stop some market pros last week from suspecting that the subtle hand of Washington was again at work.

In Chicago, for instance, the stock index futures markets were buzzing with rumors that Washington was putting pressure on big trading houses to give the market a lift.

"I heard those rumors," says one futures trader. "I don't think the government is pressuring people to buy. Maybe there is a certain amount of jawboning and encouragement not to force the market down."

There could, of course, be another explanation for the nonchalance. Drexel has been in trouble for a long time, and the markets may have already made price adjustments in case of its demise.

"The (bankruptcy) filing was largely anticipated by the market," says James Rosenfeld, finance professor at Emory University. "Everybody was aware of the financial woes." In fact, Rosenfeld feels that the markets may have skipped right over the bad news and started looking at the bright side.

The Federal Reserve may have to reduce interest rates to keep other Wall Street firms solvent, says Rosenfeld.

But keep the Drexel situation in perspective.

Interest rates are all that the financial markets really care about. If the Drexel bankruptcy had affected borrowing costs, then it would have been of major importance to the stock market.

In the weeks ahead -- when Drexel is long forgotten -- Wall Street will still be concerned about rising foreign interest rates and this nation's sluggish economy.

And unless there is a sudden improvement in rates there or the economy here, the stock market will still be on shaky ground.

Where are interest rates going? John Paulus, Morgan Stanley & Co.'s chief economist, said last week that he thinks they'll climb.

Coniston Bid for UAL May Depend on Arbs

Are Coniston Partners' chances of beating UAL Corp.'s board in a proxy battle slipping away?

Coniston and UAL would agree that the number of shares of the airline company in the hands of arbitragers, or speculators, has fallen dramatically in the past few months. Arbs, of course, are the natural allies of Coniston in any proxy fight.

Coniston and UAL try to keep track of the holdings of arbs through informal surveys. Last week, UAL's proxy solicitation firm, Kissel-Blake Inc., quietly called arbs and, I'm told, estimated that these speculators hold about 6 million of UAL's 22 million outstanding shares. At the peak, they were believed to have owned 9.5 million to 10 million. But the sharp plunge in UAL's stock in recent months has caused many to liquidate.

Coniston Partners, which has been threatening a proxy fight since last year, owns 2.6 million UAL shares. That means that if Coniston decides to go forward with a proxy contest -- or its close cousin, a consent solicitation -- it would have to win the vote of approximately 2.5 million shares to achieve a majority.

While it is still very likely that Coniston will win, any further reduction in the number of shares held by arbs won't make the effort any easier. It could be especially difficult to oust the board if Coniston can't come up with an alternative that UAL shareholders find more attractive than whatever the company's board ends up proposing.

Coniston was to have begun a consent solicitation in January, but it backed off from that threat when it looked as though UAL was making progress in its efforts for a buyout led by management and the airline's unions.

It is now February, and UAL is still trying to structure some sort of buyout or restructuring. A couple of times each week, Coniston, the unions representing UAL employees and the company's management get together at the New York offices of First Boston Corp., the investment banking firm, to discuss the situation. While these meeting are supposed to be secret, Wall Street keeps very close tabs of when they occur. The only thing that Wall Street doesn't know is the amount of progress being made behind closed doors.

Coniston is said to be happy with the progress of the talks. That's why there hasn't been much gossip lately about a proxy battle. But if Coniston should become dissatisfied at some point, it is going to wish it had more help from arbs.

Long-Distance Carriers Not Much Different

You've seen the commercials: A guy on a pay phone tries to call Phoenix and accidentally gets Fiji. He becomes so frustrated that he vows to go back to the good service of AT&T.

MCI Communications makes similar claims for the quality of its long-distance telephone service. So does US Sprint.

Which service is really the best? Smith Barney, Harris Upham recently surveyed managers of corporate telephone systems and found that there really isn't much difference in quality.

Smith Barney says it sent out 110 questionnaires in its second annual survey. Of the 52 people who returned them, 48 said they managed annual budgets of $1 million or more.

The managers were asked to rate the long-distance carriers on a scale of 1 to 5 (with 5 being best) in network quality, service and sales. In quality, AT&T got a 4.2 rating, Sprint 3.9. and MCI 3.5. AT&T also led in service with a 3.6 rating, followed by Sprint and MCI tied at 3.3. In sales, Sprint led with 3.4, AT&T was second at 3.2 and MCI third with 3.1.

Overall, AT&T's rating was 4.2, Sprint's 3.9 and MCI's 3.5.

Analyst Charles Schelke, who conducted the poll, concluded that the long-distance phone companies were "generally comparable." He told clients in a report called TeleTrends that "while all carriers now appear to be providing satisfactory service, no one is consistently providing exceptional service."

Incidentally, AT&T did moderately better in this survey than in a similar poll Smith Barney conducted a year earlier. MCI did a tiny bit better, and Sprint's rate was unchanged.

Beware Greenspan's Soothing Forecasts

Federal Reserve Chairman Alan Greenspan pulled off a magic trick a few weeks ago when he calmed a jittery stock market and pacified Congress by saying the chances of an economic recession have been reduced. But just how good a forecaster has Greenspan been?

Take a look at his predictions in 1981, the last time that the nation's economy slipped into a recession, and you'll see that sometimes his crystal ball gets a little foggy.

In April, 1981, Greenspan -- then a private economist but also an unofficial adviser to President Reagan -- predicted that the U.S.' gross national product would grow 0.7% in the third quarter. But by August, Greenspan had dramatically changed his opinion -- predicting, instead, that GNP would decline 1.1%.

The change came after a government report in July said business conditions were weakening. According to the Commerce Department, the GNP actually grew by 1.8% in third quarter 1981.

Greenspan further predicted that April that GNP would grow 3.6% in the fourth quarter. But by August he was predicting a decline of 0.2%. The economy actually declined 5.5% that quarter.

Also in that month, Greenspan predicted that the U.S. economy would grow by 3.8% in the first quarter of 1982. He changed that estimate in August to an increase of just 0.8%. GNP actually declined 5.9%.

Two successive quarters of contraction -- as happened in the fourth quarter of 1981 and the first quarter of 1982 -- are popularly defined as a recession. Greenspan had correctly predicted an economic downturn (albeit a slight one), but it did take two tries and a little hint from the government before he got it right.

Greenspan's prediction of a rebound in the first quarter of 1982, of course, was way off target.

His record got a little better in the second quarter of 1982. Greenspan's revised estimate of a 3.5% gain in the second quarter of 1982 wasn't too far off the actual 1.2% growth experienced in that period. But Greenspan went back to his losing ways with his long-range prediction for the third quarter.

Greenspan's revised forecast in August, 1981, was for 4.3% growth in the third quarter of 1982. The economy actually declined 3.2% that quarter.

In short, the closest Greenspan had come to spotting a recession was his prediction of a decline in GNP in the third and fourth quarters of 1981. But he was forecasting a short, painless downturn. The recession actually lasted, according to the Commerce Department, from July, 1981, to November, 1982. And it was anything but painless.

Greenspan, one hopes, has a better feel for the economy now that he has access to all those government statistics. But the financial markets should also remember that a Fed chairman can't always be as candid as he'd like to be -- especially if he is trying to calm a jittery nation.
