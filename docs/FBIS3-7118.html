Language: English Article Type:BFN [Text] Tokyo, March 24 KYODO -- South Korean President Kim Yong-sam began a three-day state visit to Japan on Thursday [24 March] receiving red-carpet treatment and cheering students at a welcome ceremony. Kim and the South Korean first lady, who wore a pastel-yellow chima chogori traditional Korean dress, shook hands and exchanged courtesies with the Emperor Akihito and Empress Michiko in the hall of the Akasaka Palace. Students waiting in front of the Akasaka Palace waved small South Korean and Japanese flags when Kim and his wife Son Myong-sun emerged together with the imperial couple. Kim, who was given a full-fledged military welcome attended by most cabinet members and heads of both houses of the Diet, looked tense during the ceremony despite an occasional smile. After being saluted by honor guards and listening to the South Korean and Japanese anthems, Kim and his wife were greeted by Crown Prince Naruhito, Crown Princess Masako, Princess Nori and Prime Minister Morihiro Hosokawa. Following Kim's pacing off the honor guards under a partly cloudy sky and a short encounter with attending politicians, the presidential couple left for an audience with the emperor and empress at the Imperial Palace. Ways to avert an open confrontation over North Korea's alleged nuclear program are the main focus of Kim's talks in Japan and with Chinese leaders later. He told Foreign Minister Tsutomu Hata, who welcomed the president at Tokyo's Haneda Airport, that "Russia's cooperation is necessary" to solve the dispute stemming from North Korea's refusal of full inspections of its nuclear facilities. In response Hata, who returned from talks in Russia on Monday, told Kim that Moscow seems to be dissatisfied with Pyongyang's reactions, Japanese officials said. North Korea says its nuclear program is strictly nonmilitary. On Wednesday, Kim put South Korea's 650,000-strong army under special alert after North Korea warned that Washington's decision to dispatch six Patriot antimissile batteries to the south will push the Korean peninsula "to the brink of war." Foreign Minister Han Sung-chu, originally scheduled to accompany Kim, withdrew from the traveling party on short notice Wednesday probably due to the increasingly tense situation on the Korean peninsula. Kim's Japan visit will be directly followed by a five-day state visit to China, starting Saturday afternoon. In a departure ceremony at Seoul airport Thursday, Kim said he will try to obtain support from Japan and China to resolve the nuclear issue and bring North Korea around to reform and openness. "I will explain to the two leaders of Japan and China that preserving reconciliation and peace on the Korean peninsula is important to ensuring stability and prosperity in the Asia-Pacific region as well as in Northeast Asia," Kim said. He said North Korea had created confrontation on the Korean peninsula by failing to allow unhindered inspections of its nuclear facilities and make progress in inter-Korean dialogue. "While keeping the channel of dialogue open, South Korea, with the firm security commitment of the United States, is also fully determined to fight against any possible provocation by North Korea," he said.
