920203 FT 03 FEB 92 / Observer: Time to reflect Great minds think alike. Or so it would seem from the Times Literary Supplement's observations on Melvyn Bragg's novel A Time to Dance about an affair between a bank manager in his mid-50s and an 18-year-old secretary, whose TV adaptation has variously titillated or appalled Britain's chattering classes over the past three weeks. In the novel, the TLS points out, Bragg's hero rhapsodises about his young lover's name, Bernadette, as follows: The three syllables, softly clacking my tongue against my palate. Bern - how urgent, how hot] A - the pause, a sigh the fulcrum of anticipation; dette - the stab, the claim. What a coincidence, then, that 30-odd years in advance of Bragg's 1990 novel Vladimir Nabokov's middle-aged character Humbert Humbert should have rhapsodised thus about the name of his 12-year-old step-daughter lover, Lolita: Lo-lee-ta: the tip of the tongue taking a trip of three steps down the palate to tap, at three, on the teeth. Lo. Lee. Ta.
