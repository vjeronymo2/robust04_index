Language: English Article Type:BFN [By Clarisse Lucas] [Text] Cairo, March 27 (AFP) -- The PLO and Israel are optimistic that a Hebron security accord will be signed here on Tuesday [29 March] to clear the way for the resumption of autonomy talks after a one-month break. A senior Palestinian official said Sunday the PLO was putting to one side its demand for the evacuation of the 400 Jewish settlers who live among 120,000 Palestinians in the massacre town of Hebron. "The departure of the Hebron settlers is a pressing demand so as to prevent further violence, but it is not a precondition," Muhammad Subayh, secretary general of the Palestine National Council or parliament-in-exile, told AFP. Although Israel's Prime Minister Yitzhaq Rabin has ruled out their withdrawal, an Israeli source said the settlers living in five areas of Hebron could be regrouped. The PLO and Israel agreed over the weekend that the only serious issue left was the number of Palestinian policemen and Norwegian observers to be deployed in the occupied West Bank town of Hebron. Israel accepted the deployment of lightly armed police and foreign observers in talks with the Palestinians last week in Tunis and Cairo. The accord on Hebron could be signed when the two sides reconvene on Tuesday morning in Cairo if the final details are ironed out in time, the PLO's chief negotiator Nabil Sha'th said Saturday. "As soon as this accord is signed, the negotiations on launching autonomy in Gaza and Jericho will resume immediately," after a one-month suspension by the PLO because of the February 25 massacre of 30 Palestinian worshippers by a settler, he said. The Palestinian police will be posted at fixed points around Hebron to guard schools, hospitals and the university, said Sha'th. Joint Israeli-Palestinian units would patrol the town. He implicitly acknowledged that the Israeli army would have the last word on security, saying "the situation in Hebron will be different from Gaza and Jericho," where autonomy is to be launched. As for the autonomy negotiations, a final accord on the launch will be signed by April 13, the original date on which the Israeli army was to have pulled out of the Gaza Strip and West Bank town of Jericho, said Sha'th. Israeli Prime Minister Yitzhaq Rabin said earlier that he hoped the autonomy talks would resume on Tuesday along with a last session on the security arrangements for Hebron. Rabin said an accord was in sight on security for Hebron Palestinians, as demanded by the PLO as a condition for resuming the autonomy negotiations. "I believe we have advanced in the right direction," he said. Israeli Foreign Minister Shim'on Peres said implementation of the autonomy accord could start "in one or two months." A deal on the launch of autonomy was to have come into effect on December 13, under the September 13 Declaration of Principles signed in Washington, but it has been held up mainly by Israeli security concerns. Israeli state radio said Saturday that Palestinian police would be deployed in nine day's time in Hebron, Jericho and Gaza. Subayh raised an additional Palestinian demand on Sunday -- for police also to be sent to the rest of the West Bank. "The settlers are everywhere and risks of a massacre exist everywhere they live," he said. "The question of the security of the Palestinians does not only present itself in Hebron," said Subayh. But Rabin has said the Hebron arrangements were "exceptional," and would not be extended to other West Bank towns such as Nablus or Ramallah. UN Security Council Resolution 904, adopted on March 18, condemned the Hebron massacre and called for protection for Palestinians in the occupied territories. After the massacre, the PLO demanded an armed international protection force, the disarming of Jewish settlers and dismantling of certain flashpoint settlements, including those in Hebron.
