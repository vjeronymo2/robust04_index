BFN [Studio interview with UN Secretary General Butrus Butrus-Ghali and with Foreign Minister Andrey Kozyrev by "Itogi" program announcer Yevgeniy Kiselev; in English with superimposed Russian translation -- recorded] [Text] [Kiselev] It is a well known fact that in its aspiration to play a key role in resolving the Georgian-Abkhaz conflict, Russia has long been exerting diplomatic efforts with the aim of gaining the status of peacekeeping forces for the Russian Federation troops in that region. Issues relating to the peacekeeping operations, in which, on the one hand, Russia and, on the other, the United Nations are involved, were the main subject of the negotiations in Moscow between Foreign Minister Andrey Kozyrev and UN Secretary General Butrus-Ghali. Our interview with them focused on this very topic. Mr. secretary general, as everyone knows, the UN peacekeeping forces must not include servicemen from countries which are in one way or another involved in the conflict or whose territories neighbor on the conflict. Russia would today wish, on the one hand, to widen the UN participation in peacekeeping operations in the post-Soviet space, but on the other hand, it does not want to refuse to get involved itself in resolving conflicts near its borders, nor, indeed, can it refuse to do so. A very contradictory situation is thus created. Where do you see the way out of this, if there is one? [Butrus-Ghali] Of course there is a way out. Take former Yugoslavia, for instance. The United Nations, the European Community and, finally, NATO are all taking part in the efforts to establish peace in this region. I see no obstacles to Russian forces taking part in peacekeeping operations on the territory of the former USSR under the Russian Federation flag. But at the same time, independent peacekeeping operations would be taking place there in close cooperation with Russia, involving UN observers, for example. This sort of approach has not been adopted previously. Previously, for the United Nations, it was an indispensable condition that the entire command of any peacekeeping operation remain in UN hands. However, now we do not have enough forces for everything. Only recently, the United Nations never had more than two or three peacekeeping operations on the go at the same time. Today 17 such operations are being condutcted around the globe at the same time. The annual budget of the UN peacekeeping forces was at one time $500-600 million, whereas now it is up to $3.6 billion per year. In this situation, we cannot do without additional assistance from the UN member countries, and also from various regional organizations. [Kiselev] Nevertheless, if I understood you correctly, you rule out the possibility of Russian Federation troops operating under the UN flag in the capacity of peacekeeping forces in the Transcaucasus or in Tajikistan, for example. [Butrus-Ghali] Yes, it is impossible because the United Nations Organization can carry out a peacekeeping operation only from the very start, the same way a surgeon will not agree to operate on a patient starting mid-way. But if you have already started an independent peace-making operation, the United Nations Organization can give you assistance and work side by side with you. There is another option which is to include Russian troops in a broader international peacekeeping contingent, under UN command, though. [Kiselev] What countries can send their servicemen to participate in such a contingent, for instance, in Georgia, Abkhazia, or Tajikistan? Is it at all feasible to form such a contingent? [Butrus-Ghali] It is feasible if a relevant agreement is attained and if the UN Security Council is convinced that this is necessary, because all this has to be paid for but the Security Council is not always willing to do this. Many of its members are disappointed by the fact that not all recent UN peacekeeping operations have been successful, such as Somalia and former Yugoslavia. So I repeat, Security Council members have yet to be convinced, talks with them are needed. At the same time, there are conditions stipulating an international nature of the peacekeeping contingent, that is, Russian servicemen will account for no more than 20-30 percent of its total number while the remaining 70 percent will be made up of servicemen from five or six other countries. Only in this case will the United Nations defray the cost of the operation and, again, on the condition of retaining the political leadership of the operation. [Kiselev] So you discussed all the above with our Foreign Minister Kozyrev? [Butrus-Ghali] Yes, we did. [Kiselev] And what was the result: sharp disagreements or mutual understanding? [Butrus-Ghali] No, on the contrary, I hope that an accord will be attained within the next few days, and on its basis we will later discuss at the Security Council the issue of dispatching UN peacekeeping forces or UN observers to the areas of conflict in the former USSR. [Kiselev] But you must agree that UN observers and peacekeeping forces are not the same. [Butrus-Ghali] You are right. However, if we do not get the Security Council's consent to dispatching peacekeeping forces we will still be able to obtain a mandate to dispatch observers. [Interview cuts to Foreign Minister Kozyrev without introduction or question from Kiselev] [Kozyrev] Yes indeed, the role of observers is slightly different. They are not troops. Therefore, they are taking part. But this is useful. We would indeed like our contingent to be granted the status of a UN contingent. Incidentally, this option is being discussed. We are proposing this option for Karabakh, and also for Abkhazia. But the problem once again is this: if our contingent officially becomes a UN contingent, ie if our servicemen literally don blue helmets, then the United Nations has to pay for this in full. But unfortunately this is what they do not want to do. Moreover, the United Nations has to share with us political responsibility for these efforts. Here again, as I have already mentioned, there is a budget deficit, and there is also what I would call a deficit of political courage and will. But the secretary general cannot solve this question on his own. This question has to be solved by the UN Security Council. Hence this question is not in the hands of the secretary general, but in the hands of, above all, permanent members and also other member states of the Security Council. Another aspect that we often forget is that the United Nations operates on precedents. If the United Nations directly agrees to involvement by a neighboring state in some conflicts directly on its behalf -- I mean on behalf of the United Nations -- one can imagine that in other conflicts, in other regions, some other states would demand a similar mandate from the United Nations. And here certain difficulties may arise. I will not give any examples, but one can easily imagine this. Moreover, it may well be that we ourselves will be very much concerned if a particular neighboring state starts conducting operations in its neighboring state under the UN flag, as it were. [Butrus-Ghali] Security Council members' hesitations are not just about you -- though I know that people in Russia do think that the United Nations does not want to embark on peacekeeping operations particularly in your case, on the territory of the former [Soviet] Union. But this is not so. For example, we would undoubtedly like to send 600 blue helmets to Mozambique. We have been discussing it for six months, but not one of UN member states shows any willingness to designate its servicemen to be part of this contingent. So I have to go to various capitals as a petitioner: please do send you soldiers to Mozambique. But after all, UN members have to bear responsibility to each other. Suppose tomorrow the United Nations asks Russia to send 2,000 Russian servicemen to Angola. Public opinion in your country will certainly start buzzing: why should we send 2,000 of our soldiers to Angola? Why Angola? Where is this Angola anyway? Why should my son risk his life in Angola? Preparing public opinion is a huge problem. Say, if you want Argentina or Chile --two weeks ago there was negotiation about the possibility of those two countries allocating some of their servicemen to be sent to Tajikistan in the future. So you have to be prepared, in turn, to send your servicemen, say, to Angola, however unpopular this measure might be. One of my tasks is precisely this. I am telling Russia: I know that you have many internal problems at home, but don't forget about the United Nations.
