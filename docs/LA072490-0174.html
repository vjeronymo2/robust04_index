July 24, 1990, Tuesday, P.M. Final

LIZ SMITH: NORA DUNN TAKES A STAND BUT LOSES 'SNL' JOB

FOR FIVE seasons on "Saturday Night Live," comic actress Nora Dunn was best known for her characterizations on that long-running NBC institution: vapid talk show hostess Pat Stevens . . . one half of the singing Sweeney Sisters. But then came Andrew Dice Clay, chosen to host the May 12 edition of "SNL."

Dunn publicly repudiated Clay and absented herself from the show, as did the scheduled musical performer Sinead O'Connor. The firestorm of publicity brought both praise for her stand and skepticism of her motives. And many felt that Dunn had, no doubt inadvertently, helped Clay's career. Now Nora Dunn is out of "Saturday Night Live," her contract not renewed. Producers insist this is not some sort of punishment for bucking the male-dominated environment of "SNL." They say it's just show biz, you know?

Nora Dunn sat for an interview with Mother Jones magazine, conducted by Davis Beers, shortly before NBC announced her services would no longer be required. In the September/October issue, she speaks out on the Andrew Dice Clay debacle and its aftermath.

"I was ostracized by most of the people I work with, including most of the cast," she said, "and I was never given any reason for that other than what I read in the paper." Dunn said, months later, very few cast members or co-workers have spoken to her.

Her stance on Clay is unwavering: "He has the right to say whatever he wants; you then have the responsibility to stand up against it. You can't disconnect yourself from reality -- the kind of rapes that are happening, people beating up gay men. This kind of stuff Clay does fuels that kind of thought. It's too easy to slip into that fascist way of thinking."

And yet for all this, Dunn has not joined the National Organization for Women in its condemnation of Esquire's June issue, to which she contributed a four-page photo parody: "I think when you start condemning things you need to focus on what's important. I would not compare Esquire with Andrew Dice Clay. . . . I don't take it seriously. It's not about rape."
