BFN [Editorial: "Practical Success"] [Text] It is impractical to look for a spectacular outcome to the Rao-Clinton talks; this was not expected from Prime Minister Narasimha Rao's U.S. visit. There were already indications that no solution would be found to the differences between India and America, and it was not expected that India's standing would suddenly rise in the eyes of U.S. statesmen and businessmen. The only hope was that the summit meeting between India and America would produce a better understanding, which had been weak for a number of years. With this personal communication between the heads of government of the two countries, more businesslike ties might develop between India and America. Relations have previously been bogged down in a quagmire of bureaucratic language and prejudice. From this point of view, Narasimha Rao's visit can be termed a success. In his joint news conference, the prime minister stated that the talks were "constructive, useful, and forthright." The forthrightness is reflected in the fact that both leaders agreed there are differences that still persist. India reiterated its stance regarding the NPT [Nuclear Nonproliferation Treaty], human rights, and Kashmir, but America remained steadfast in its reasoning. The talks were useful in the sense that India could properly gauge U.S. priorities for the first time. For his part, the prime minister tried to emphasize not only that India is the largest democratic country with a potential to emerge as the greatest market in the world, but its humanitarian and pacifist concerns are no less than those of America. The constructive nature of the talks will only become known in the future. The prime minister proposed that for the sake of a nuclear-free world there should be agreement on "no first strike" with nuclear weapons. He also agreed that a program of joint exercises between Indian and U.S. forces should be undertaken to safeguard South Asian and Pacific security. If these things are considered constructive, that is one matter; the true usefulness of the prime minister's visit was the possibility of heavy U.S. investment in India. How far Narasimha Rao succeeded in this regard will become known only after concrete results are achieved. The prime minister's visit proved useful in one sense. Even though President Clinton declared India the biggest successful democracy and praised its contributions, this feeling was neither reflected in the U.S. media nor in the behavior of U.S. senators. The U.S. media ignored the prime minister's visit from the beginning, and when they did notice, they treated it as an insignificant event. U.S. statesmen were so indifferent toward the prime minister's address to a joint session of Congress that only three dozen members from both houses came to listen. Newspapers ignored the event completely. Most of the questions asked in the joint news conference were addressed to President Clinton, and many had no bearing on Indo-U.S. relations. This indifference came despite the fact that this was the first state visit by an Indian prime minister in many years; Rajiv Gandhi was the last prime minister to visit the United States. This attitude has nothing to do with the prime minister's success or failure, but the inherent fact that India is not a U.S. priority cannot be ignored. It is therefore easy to understand that in the present atmosphere we will not be able to carve the proper niche for ourselves in Western politics -- which move on the basis of power and prosperity -- by extolling goodwill and our cultural superiority. India is not considered great on the basis of population, natural resources, or democratic tradition. It will have to transfer these attributes into solid results. It is futile to repeat Jawaharlal's prophecy that the next century would be a U.S. century; we will have to find a strategy to make the next century belong to India.
