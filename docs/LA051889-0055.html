May 18, 1989, Thursday, Home Edition

MOVIE REVIEW;

'WOMAN DEMON HUMAN': CHINESE ARTISTIC VISION

Writer-director Huang Shuqin's "Woman Demon Human" (opening Friday at the Little Tokyo Cinema 2) begins as many films about actresses do: with the heroine sitting at her dressing table looking back on her life as she makes up. Although classic in form, this exquisite film by one of China's few women film makers has so many distinctive details and is so clearly the work of a liberated, reflective sensibility that it offers an altogether fresh experience.

Huang's lovely heroine Qiu Yun (Xu Shouli) is only 30, but her life has already been filled with accomplishment and heartbreak. The time is the present, as we begin to flash back to Qiu Yun's childhood, but the settings are so primitive as to seem in the distant past. For Qiu Yun is the child of traveling opera performers who tour rural China in oxcarts and perform in the most makeshift circumstances.

Her happy childhood is shattered when her mother runs off with another man. From then on Qiu Yin will deal with every one of life's challenges and disappointments by throwing herself into her career -- often quite literally, for stardom in the Chinese opera requires the skills of both the gymnast and the martial artist. Early on she has determined that she will break precedent and demand to play the more physically demanding male roles.

The part that brings her fame is that of the ferocious-looking ghost of ancient Chinese folklore, Zhong Kui, who zealously tries to marry off his beloved sister. Throughout the film, a dreamlike performance of the one-act opera, "Zhong Kui Marries Off His Sister," is intercut with Qiu Yun's memories as she gradually accepts that this stellar demon (played by opera actress Pei Yanling) has in effect married her to the stage.

In "Woman Demon Human" (Times-rated Mature for complex themes) the most elaborate and glittery of costumes contrast with both the natural beauty of the countryside and the simplicity of the opera company's attire and life style offstage. What could have been a soapy old-fashioned "woman's picture" has become a reverie on art as a way to salvation.

The second feature is another fine Chinese film, "The Old Well." On May 26: "Desperation" and "Hibiscus Town," two more Chinese films. KEVIN THOMAS
