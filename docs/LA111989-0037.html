November 19, 1989, Sunday, Home Edition

FOR ALL OF THOSE WHO FOUGHT, A TIME TO GIVE OUR THANKS;

COLD WAR: WE HAVE BEEN PORTRAYED AS INEPT DIPLOMATS, ROGUES, IMPERIALISTS, BUT U.S. STEADFASTNESS BROUGHT PEACE.

For more than 40 years the United States has waged the Cold War, despite ceaseless deprecation from many who benefited from American sacrifices without ever pausing to say thanks.

Sophisticated Europeans such as Gen. Charles de Gaulle made us out to be reckless one minute and faithless the next. Through the decades we have been portrayed as inept diplomats, trigger-happy rogues, imperialists and, in the 1980s, cowboys -- Hollywood cowboys, not even real cowboys! Now Chancellor Helmut Kohl of West Germany has expressed his gratitude, a wondrously appropriate sentiment. We who may now enjoy the benefits of peace should be grateful to many veterans of the Cold War, many of whom were Europeans and even Soviets.

There are the writers who braved abuse and poverty by exposing the brutal nature of communism. When Malcolm Muggeridge returned to Britain from Russia in the 1930s to refute such celebrated defenders of Stalin as the playwright George Bernard Shaw, Muggeridge lost his job, leaving his wife and four children on hard times.

George Orwell was not accorded a much friendlier reception for blowing the whistle on the communists in the Spanish Civil War and for satirizing communist totalitarianism. Let us also express thanks to Arthur Koestler, whose "Darkness at Noon" exposed Stalin's show trials, and to Whittaker Chambers, who in "Witness" exposed communism's versatile evil and who died in despair for the West.

And let us give thanks for the Cold Warriors themselves, beginning with Winston Churchill, who had the grit to notify the world in his March 5, 1946, speech in Fulton, Mo., of the Iron Curtain drawn across Europe and of the dangers ahead.

Let us be grateful for America's greatest secretary of state, Dean Acheson, whose diplomatic career was crucial to the establishment of the Truman Doctrine, Point Four, the Marshall Plan, NATO, the end of the Berlin air lift and the pursuit of the Korean War. Let us be grateful for Presidents Harry Truman and Dwight D. Eisenhower, who kept the United States on an internationalist course. The academic treatise of George Kennan gave the United States a scheme for pursuing the East-West conflict, namely: "containment." And a remarkable collection of American diplomats implemented the policy.

Through the years every President, from John F. Kennedy to Richard Nixon, paid an onerous price for waging the Cold War. In the 1980s Ronald Reagan faced the renewed challenge of Leonid Brezhnev and his successors and demonstrated that an arms race is a race the United States could and would win. If there was one politician who brought the Cold War to an end, it was Reagan. And if there was one writer to rouse the West to a final awareness of the horrors behind the Iron Curtain, it was Alexander Solzhenitsyn, whose pitiless "One Day in the Life of Ivan Denisovich" began the tolling of the bell on Marxism.

But of course no one person can be cited for bringing to an end so epic a conflict. Millions deserve gratitude. For instance: the voters who supported the aforementioned leaders and paid the bills; the intelligence agents and military personnel; the dissidents behind the Iron Curtain countries, many still incarcerated; and even the Communist leaders who now seem to realize that their opposites in the West only want peace and prosperity for all.
