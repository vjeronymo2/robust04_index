930205 FT 05 FEB 93 / Parliament and Politics: Faster shrinking of state controls promised AN acceleration of the shift in power from the state to the individual was last night predicted by Mr John Patten, education secretary, in a lecture designed to pull together the threads of Conservative policy towards the public services. Mr Patten said that the government's approach to health, education, social provision and housing would by the end of the century have redefined the relationship between citizens and government. Individuals would be entrusted with 'as much power as they wish to take. That diffusion of authority would leave Britain with more 'relaxed' government, lighter in touch and playing the role of a 'creative agent' rather than a remote bureaucracy. Mr Patten suggested that the reorganisation of health and education to devolve power to individual hospitals, GPs and schools could be accompanied by a fundamental reshaping of state help for the elderly and the infirm. Central management of funds could give way to the creation of local 'care associations' which would eventually allocate the resources provided by Whitehall. Arguing that individual choice remained central to Conservative philosophy during the 1990s, he said that the government's housing policies would further buttress the shift from government to the citizen. Home-ownership would be within the reach of 75 per cent of the population by the year 2000 while tenants in social housing would have more more control over their accommodation. Echoing some of the themes set out by Mr John Major in his speech to the Carlton Club this week, Mr Patten suggested that the impact of the reforms would be far more radical than generally appreciated. He told the Conservative Political Centre: 'We have set out a range of changes that, by the start of the 21st century, will have effected an enormous shift of power and responsibility from government.'
