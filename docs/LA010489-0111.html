January 4, 1989, Wednesday, Orange County Edition

BROKERAGE BUYS CONTROL OF IRVINE'S CYCLE INDUSTRIES;

MERRILL LYNCH TURNS BULLISH ON SURFWEAR

Merrill Lynch is hitting the surf.

The big brokerage house with the button-down image has become majority owner of an Irvine company that owns Off Shore Sportswear, a surfwear manufacturer, and holds licenses to produce Hobie T-shirts and Catchit T-shirts.

Merrill Lynch Pierce Fenner & Smith bought the interest in Cycle Industries -- and its surfwear labels -- several weeks ago from Steve MacBaisey, Cycle's founder. MacBaisey, Cycle employees and Ex-Sto Capital, an investment banking house, also own interests in Cycle, MacBaisey said Tuesday.

MacBaisey on Tuesday confirmed reports of the sale, which was completed in November, but declined to reveal financial details. Merrill Lynch officials could not be reached for comment.

MacBaisey will continue to run the company as chairman, chief executive and president under a 4-year management contract.

Merrill Lynch's entree into surfwear marks the second major shake-up recently in the volatile industry, which has hit rough waters in the last 2 years.

Catchit -- a Tustin-based manufacturer of surf trunks, board shorts and a women's line called Haute Rush -- was sold Dec. 22 for an undisclosed sum by Pacific Trail of Seattle to its founder and president, Ian Forman, and two other investors. The company has been in a slump for more than a year.

Catchit owns the rights to its T-shirt line and now will collect royalties from its sub-licensees, including Merrill Lynch.

The changes are seen as more evidence of a financial wipeout within the $1-billion-a-year surfwear business.

"There's been an oversaturation of the beach look in the marketplace, and it's a cyclical industry," said Glenn Mounger, president of the Mounger Corp. in Seattle, parent company of Pacific Trail. "When everybody jumps on a product, the uniqueness isn't there. The consumer got tired of it."

Forman, the Catchit founder, acknowledged that the firm's sales were "flat" last year and that the company lost money.

Burbank-based Hobie Apparel -- which licenses its entire line, including T-shirts -- has retail sales of about $120 million and is doing well -- particularly in such chains as Mervyn's and J.C. Penney, industry observers said.

Both Off Shore and Catchit have suffered among surfwear retailers in the last 2 years, according to some industry insiders.

"Off Shore and Catchit mean nothing -- they've been gone for years," said Paul Heussenstamm, owner of Newport Surf & Sport in Newport Beach, a leading retailer and industry trend-setter. "Two years ago, I used to buy a ton from Catchit. But their line got real bad -- bad design, bad quality, poor taste."

To revive the lines, Merrill Lynch and MacBaisey will have to increase sales of the labels to such major retail outlets as Walmart, Sears and J.C. Penney, Heussenstamm said.

While he declined to discuss strategy, MacBaisey predicted that Cycle Industries' sales will climb 25% in 1989.

Cycle was founded as a silk-screening company by MacBaisey about 10 years ago. Today, about 65% of its business is silk-screening of clothing made by others, MacBaisey said, while the remaining 35% is manufacturing, including the Off Shore line of surfwear.

Industry sources estimated Cycle's retail revenue at $30 million to $40 million annually.

According to MacBaisey, Cycle is "probably the most profitable garment manufacturer in California."
