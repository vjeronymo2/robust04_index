BFN ["Pool" item; by Vjekoslav Radovic] [Text] New York, May 3 (TANJUG) -- It appears that the U.N. Protection Force in the former Yugoslavia (UNPROFOR) is allowed only to open fire, as it did recently at Kalesi and Gorazde, when 12 members of the Army of the Bosnian Serb Republic (VRS) were killed, according to news reports, but is not allowed to see what it sees and to hear what it hears, let alone to say publicly anything about it. The above results from U.N. Secretary-General Butrus Butrus-Ghali's reply to a letter received from United States U.N. Ambassador Madeleine Albright, in which Butrus-Ghali was sharply censured for the statements made by his generals and civilian representatives in Bosnia-Herzegovina, in which the truth was told about the conflict in that former Yugoslav republic and the U.S. foreign policy was criticized. Albright a week ago vehemently criticized Butrus-Ghali's special envoy for the former Yugoslavia Yasushi Akashi for refusing to order new air strikes against Serbs at Gorazde, a town in eastern Bosnia-Herzegovina. There followed statements of UNPROFOR commander for Bosnia-Herzegovina, General Michael Rose and other unidentified U.N. officials who insisted that the reports from Gorazde were false, that the figures about the casualties and destruction were overinflated, and that the U.S. had nearly drawn the United Nations into a larger-scale war on the basis of incorrect assumptions. In the latest letter to the U.N. secretary-general, Albright said she was writing to protest in the strongest possible terms the comments of U.N. officials who keep the secretary-general directly informed through Akashi. Butrus-Ghali replied the same day to the closely-spaced two-page letter, saying he shared the U.S. ambassador's deep concern over the fact that the criticism of the U.S. policy in the former Yugoslavia by ranking UNPROFOR officials had been quoted in news reports. The U.N. secretary-general said he had sent a letter to Akashi informing him that he considered such comments totally unacceptable and requesting a thorough investigation. Butrus-Ghali said he had asked of Akashi to take all steps for such things not to happen again. Without speculating about whether "the gentleman had backed down," as some claim, or just the opposite was true, the message is clear: UNPROFOR must keep silent and dance to the U.S. tune. Butrus-Ghali's spokesman Joe Sills has told the press that both Akashi and General Rose continue to have the secretary-general's confidence. However, it is obvious that they do not have Washington's confidence and that whether they will keep their current posts will most likely depend on the degree of their obedience. Sills practically quoted a part of Albright's letter when he said that the secretary-general felt it was not all right for ranking U.N. officials or peace operation personnel to criticise the policy of Western U.N. member-countries. Sills said U.N. officials in the field should use other means than the press to get the information to their superior on the east river. It is well known, however, that Butrus-Ghali has several times "misplaced" key reports about the guilt of the Muslim side in Bosnia-Herzegovina, and that he has rejected to publish the report of a commission which has investigated crimes committed by U.N. troops, primarily U.S., in Somalia. Under the constant threat from Washington that it will cut off funds, Butrus-Ghali has decided to close the eyes and shut the ears of the UNPROFOR personnel, and has thus reduced the United Nations to a blind follower of U.S. policy. The U.S. has put a "dollar muzzle" on UNPROFOR and released the safety on their rifles. Butrus-Ghali's acquiescence with such a state leads the United Nations to an abyss in which the League of Nations had found itself prior to World War Two.
