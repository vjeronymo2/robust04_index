November 16, 1989, Thursday, Home Edition

DR. JOYCE BROTHERS: IT'S ALL RIGHT TO BE LEFT-HANDED

DEAR DR. BROTHERS: Even though our son has always been encouraged and has never (as far as we know) been made to feel strange or inferior because he's left-handed, he still makes comments about it and wishes he were right-handed. Since he's 19 and an excellent student who seems to be adjusted in other ways, we're wondering if he'll ever accept this condition. -- A.Y.

DEAR A.Y.: There's no doubt about it that, in a world designed primarily for right-handed people, lefties can often feel they're getting a raw deal or that they've been treated unfairly. However, most adjust very well and consider it a minor irritation.

You might remind your son that many people who have made lasting contributions in many different fields have been lefties. Michelangelo, Babe Ruth, Thomas Edison, Picasso, Leonardo da Vinci, Paul Klee, Cole Porter, Charlie Chaplin, Julius Caesar, Alexander the Great, even Paul McCartney, Sandy Koufax and Jimi Hendrix were all left-handed.

It's important for all children to be taught that it's all right to be a little different. In some way, each of us is unique and this is one of the many things that helps to make the world a better and more interesting place in which to live.

DEAR DR. BROTHERS: My son is 5 years old and my husband feels there's no reason to tell him about the possibility of having a baby sister or brother. Since our son will be at my mother's for the last few months of my pregnancy, my husband thinks our little boy would have a better time on his holiday if he didn't know. Is there anything wrong with letting the new baby be a surprise? -- H.S.

DEAR H.S.: Yes, there's really a lot wrong with this idea. First of all, children have a great need for predictability in their lives. Youngsters are amazingly resilient if they know what to expect and if they're properly prepared well in advance. Your son has been the center of attention for some time and he's now going to have to learn to share the spotlight. This will present much less of a problem if you let him know that he'll soon be a big brother and encourage him to be an important part of the planning for this new addition.

Talk with your son about what he was like as an infant, for this will help him to empathize and identify. Once the baby has arrived, make sure you talk with your son not only about his feelings but also about how he thinks the baby feels. Let your son take part in preparing the baby's room and planning in the care and feeding.

I think it would be a good idea if your son could return a short time before the birth of this baby. The more he's included, the less likelihood for sibling rivalry later.
