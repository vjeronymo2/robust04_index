940517 FT 17 MAY 94 / Company rescue plans under fire Banks have criticised government proposals to help companies in financial difficulties to survive, arguing that they will discourage banks from lending and could be 'a recipe for abuse' by company directors. The British Bankers' Association made its attack in its response to a Department of Trade and Industry consultative paper on company voluntary arrangements and administration orders published yesterday. The banks say the proposals would weaken their right to preferential treatment over other creditors in the case of insolvency, and limit their freedom to appoint receivers to troubled companies. The response describes the suggestion that a debtor should be able to obtain a 28-day moratorium binding all creditors, without having to obtain bank agreement, as 'a recipe for abuse in the hands of unscrupulous or desperate businessmen'. Mr Brian Pitman, chief executive of Lloyds Bank and the association's vice-chairman, said: 'If you have got to wait 28 days to enforce debts, there may not be much left when you arrive.'
