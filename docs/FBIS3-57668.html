Language: Georgian Article Type:BFN [Text] UN Secretary General Butrus Butrus-Ghali advised the Security Council [UNSC] to prolong the mandate of the UN mission of observers in Georgia until 15 March 1994. It was mentioned in the speech by the UN secretary general on the situation in Abkhazia, which was circulated here in the United Nations as a document of the Security Council. It is mentioned in the speech by Butrus-Ghali that the Security Council on 4 November 1993 adopted Resolution No. 881 and approved maintaining a presence of a small UN mission of observers in Georgia until 31 January 1994 for establishing links with both of the conflicting sides as well as Russian military contingents. The Security Council decreed not to prolong the mandate of the mission beyond 31 January 1994 unless the secretary general reported to the council of considerable progress in carrying out the measures aimed at achieving a solid peace. The Geneva memorandum of understanding signed on 1 December 1993 points to a hopeful progress in establishing a solid peace in the region, said Butrus-Ghali. It was exactly in this connection that on 22 January 1994 the Security Council adopted Resolution No. 892, said Butrus-Ghali, which gives a sanction to the United Nations to gradually deploy about 50 additional military observers -- besides the five who are already there [in Georgia]. Butrus-Ghali said: In Paris, the Georgian head of state Eduard Shevardnadze and I thoroughly discussed the situation in Abkhazia. He asked me earnestly to give recommendations to the Security Council for a speedy deployment of the UN military observers in Abkhazia. Considering the results of my talks with Shevardnadze, I ordered my colleagues and the chief military observer of the UN observers' mission in Georgia to study the expediency of a further growth -- over 55 observers -- of the number of UN observers in Georgia.
