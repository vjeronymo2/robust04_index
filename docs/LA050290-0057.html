May 2, 1990, Wednesday, Home Edition

DARMAN URGES OPTIMISM ON U.S. FUTURE

In one of his rare public speeches, Richard G. Darman, director of the White House Office of Management and Budget, Tuesday attacked the "America-is-in-decline" mood that he said he worries has captured the popular imagination in recent years.

"As history seems to be moving America's way, America is less than fully conscious of its own continuing primacy," Darman said in a speech delivered at Harvard University's Kennedy School of Government. "U.S. opinion leaders seem less than fully confident of America's vital destiny, its unique capacity to move the world toward new frontiers in the 21st Century."

The speech marks an ironic turnabout for Darman, who himself attracted widespread attention in the past for his attacks on the self-indulgence of American society in the 1980s and implied criticism of certain policies of the Ronald Reagan Administration that he helped to carry out. A text of the speech was made available by Darman's office here.

This time, while repeating some of his familiar themes, Darman's emphasis was much more upbeat, encouraging Americans to be more optimistic about their future by overcoming what he called the nation's "fear of flying."

Characteristically indulging his own taste for mixing metaphors from pop culture with historical imagery, Darman challenged those he claimed are threatening to undermine the American romantic spirit, with its love of freedom, distrust of authority and pioneering taste for heroics.

To Darman, one enemy of the American romantic spirit is the "alluring romantic image" of the "simple, bucolic, blue-green planet, untainted by competitive industrial advance, unmarked by 'artificial' boundaries of competing nation-states." The image, he argued, holds a strong appeal to many environmental activists.

Darman has emerged as one of the Bush Administration's strongest internal skeptics of environmental activism and a harsh critic of Environmental Protection Agency head William K. Reilly. While saying that "in the main, environmentalism is benign and well-intended," and half-jokingly acknowledging that "my wife and children are environmentalists. The President is an environmentalist," Darman also argued that it is "a green mask under which different faces of politico-economic ideology can hide."

He argued that some environmentalists see the movement as a way to promote an anti-growth global management regime that is at odds with the free market, progress-oriented approach so popular in the United States.

"Americans did not fight and win the wars of the 20th Century to make the world safe for green vegetables," he said.

In a relatively brief discussion of the challenges facing him directly as President Bush's budget director, Darman set out his goals for budget negotiations with congressional leaders -- talks widely expected to begin soon.

He ticked off several goals, including a reform of the budget process, a slowdown in the growth of benefit programs like Medicare, Social Security and farm subsidies, an effort to cap the government's hidden liabilities and reform of federal accounting controls.

Darman also repeated recent statements that the Bush Administration might go along with certain increases in revenues, but stated more clearly that he means by that a cut in the capital gains tax and higher user fees rather than any rise in income taxes.
