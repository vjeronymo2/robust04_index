March 29, 1990, Thursday, Home Edition

NORTHROP

Les Daly, a spokesman for the Northrop Corp., responded (letter, March 15) to an article by Richard Reeves (Op-Ed Page, March 2) about the recent conviction of Northrop for fraud on two of our nation's critical weapons systems: the nuclear air launched cruise missile, and the U.S. Navy Harrier jet. Daly's letter contains several misstatements of fact and seeks to perpetuate Northrop's attempt to minimize its guilty conduct.

The letter nowhere mentions the two most significant facts of Northrop's conviction. First, this was the first time that a defense contractor has been convicted for failing to conduct critical tests, and covering up that failure, on a nuclear weapons system. Second, the $17-million criminal fine paid by Northrop is by far the largest ever imposed on a defense contractor and reflects the seriousness of Northrop's criminal conduct.

Daly's statement that "three or four" people failed to perform tests overlooks the fact that it was Northrop which was responsible for the tests, failed to do the tests, and then covered up these failures. Moreover, the Northrop employees involved in the cheating included the general manager of a Northrop factory (who was convicted of eight felony counts of fraud and false statements) and the chief engineer of the same factory (who was convicted of three similar felony counts). Finally, there were at least five test technicians and supervisors who falsified test results at the direction of the general manager and chief engineer.

Daly is also incorrect when he implies that there was a full disclosure by Northrop of the cheating on the missile and jet programs. To the contrary, Northrop sought from the beginning to suggest that cheating had occurred on only about two dozen components for the cruise missile. Northrop also sought to minimize its guilt as to the false testing on the jet. However, Northrop was indicted in 1989 by a federal grand jury for cheating, and covering up, on stabilization systems which went into more than 600 cruise missiles and more than 100 jets. Ultimately, Northrop was convicted of cheating on testing of components for more than 110 missiles and jets.

We must also take exception to Northrop's claim that it "accepted responsibility for the unauthorized and unacceptable acts of three or four of its employees even though they had acted on their own." In fact, there was no evidence introduced in any court hearing that the convicted employees were acting on their own. Instead, the general manager and chief engineer of Northrop's factory acted to benefit and did benefit Northrop Corp. The value of Northrop's contracts on the missile and jet programs was in the millions of dollars.

It seems to us that the public interest would be better served by Northrop and other convicted defense contractors if they would step up to their responsibilities when convicted of serious criminal conduct instead of attempting to minimize their culpability by a distortion of the facts.

WILLIAM F. FAHEY

Assistant United States Attorney

Chief, Public Corruption and

Government Fraud Section

JULIE FOX BLACKSHAW

Assistant United States Attorney

Los Angeles
