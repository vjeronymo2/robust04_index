Language: Japanese Article Type:BFN ["Core" column by editorial staff member Tadahiko Nasa and political department reporter Uichiro Oshima: "`Butrus-Ghali's Diplomacy Toward Japan' Has Changed"; first paragraph is newspaper introduction] [Text] After finishing a five-day visit to Japan, UN Secretary General Butrus-Ghali headed toward Korea on 22 December. It was his second visit to Japan following his visit last February. Compared to his first visit, which gave an impression that "the UN secretary general is a hard-liner," his remarks during his recent visit on such issues as Japan's Self-Defense Forces' [SDF] participation in UN peacekeeping operations (PKO) were extremely moderate. In his talks with Japanese leaders, he expressed his strong hope that in the near future Japan will play a nonmilitary role as a permanent member of the UN Security Council [UNSC]. The following is a report on the purpose of the visit by UN Secretary General Butrus-Ghali, whose position on Japan has softened.

Stand of "Paying Attention to Japan"
Although it is unusual for the UN secretary general to visit Japan twice in one year, there was a reason. In Egypt, UN Secretary General Butrus-Ghali's homeland, there is a prominent poet named "Hafez Ibrahim" [as published], who wrote a poem called "The Mikado" [Emperor] which praises Japan's victory in its war with Russia as a model for the Third World. This poem is very famous in Egypt and is even in a textbook. UN Secretary General Butrus-Ghali, who is 71 years old now, said he also learned from this poem in his younger days and then deepened his understanding of Japan. The fact that his itinerary for both the first and second visits specially included a visit to the Togo Shrine in Harajuku, Tokyo, reflects his interest in Japan. In a 22 December news conference at the National Press Club, the secretary general said "Japan's winning a permanent seat on the UNSC will lead to strengthening the United Nations," showing his stand of attaching importance to Japan. It can be said that underlying such remarks is his understanding of Japan, which he fostered in his younger days. Meanwhile, however, we felt that the secretary general, who is regarded as "an experienced politician" (from a Foreign Ministry source), made such remarks in consideration of the Hosokawa government, which is regarded as being "fragile." In preparation for his visit to Japan, the secretary general reportedly gathered detailed information from Yoshio Hatano, the ambassador accredited to the United Nations, on the ruling coalition's internal situation, Prime Minister Hosokawa's political position, and other problems facing Japan. In separate talks with Prime Minister Hosokawa, Foreign Minister Hata, and leaders of the ruling and opposition parties, UN Secretary General Butrus-Ghali stressed that "there seems to be a misunderstanding in Japan that the dispatch of military members to the UN PKO is the precondition for Japan's winning a UNSC permanent membership." He also said: "Of UN activities, the PKO accounts for about 30 percent and the remaining 70 percent is for environmental protection, economic development, and the other activities." In this way, in an attempt to help promote the people's interest in gaining a UNSC permanent membership, he emphasized Japan's greater contributions to nonmilitary affairs.
"Bitter Experience" in February
Behind the UN secretary general's strong desire for a Japanese visit in December is his bitter experience during his last visit in February. Before he visited in February, he had said in an interview in New York that "Japan should take part in the UN peace enforcement troops, even by revising its Constitution." This remark shocked Japanese Government officials including (then) Prime Minister Kiichi Miyazawa. Shocked at his remark, Yohei Kono, the then chief cabinet secretary (currently the president of the Liberal Democratic Party], in his meeting with UN Secretary General Butrus-Ghali, affirmed that, "Japan has no intention of becoming a UNSC permanent member." (Then) Prime Minister Miyazawa also showed cautious views on Butrus-Ghali's remarks on the plea of "Japan's Constitution" and "the people's feelings."
Issues Facing the United Nations
There are other reasons for UN Secretary General Butrus-Ghali's taking a favorable position on Japan. One is that he, in his two-year service so far, has come to realize how Japan holds an important position in the United Nations. Although Japan's current assessed contribution to the United Nations is 12.45 percent, it seems it may be raised to about 13 percent starting in 1995. In addition, Japan, in line with Prime Minister Hosokawa's attendance at the September UN General Assembly session, generously contributed approximately $100 million to PKO activities. For the United Nations, which is facing financial difficulties, Japan is an important sponsor. Therefore, it is natural for the secretary general to consider it expedient to win Japan over to his side. Another reason is that because of a PKO success in Cambodia, the secretary general himself has started considering that a traditional PKO method is more realistic than a forcible method under the "peace enforcement troops," which he proposed in July 1992. Under the traditional PKO, there would be room for Japan to make human resources contributions. As far as Japan's PKO is concerned, what is drawing UN attention is how Japan will deal with the issue concerning former Yugoslavia. The Japanese Government has announced its plan to send fact-finding missions there early next January. After the completion of the fact-finding survey, attention will then be focused on whether Japan will make personnel contributions in addition to its financial cooperation. On the issue of personnel contributions, Mr. Yasushi Akashi, chief of the UN peacekeeping forces [PKF] in former Yugoslavia, has recently expressed the hope that Japan will take part in rear-echelon support troops. Although the connection between his remarks and Secretary General Butrus-Ghali's position is not clear, the government is negative about sending SDF troops to former Yugoslavia at present. The Defense Agency [DA] was nervous about UN Secretary General Butrus-Ghali's Japanese visit because it feared that "Butrus-Ghali might ask the government to send the SDF to former Yugoslavia." Underlying the DA's anxiety is the fact that just before Butrus-Ghali's visit, Yasushi Akashi, who is to head the UN PKF in the former Yugoslavia, indicated his view that it is possible for Japan to join the PKF under the current PKO cooperation law if Japan's activity is limited to rear-echelon support. In response, DA Director General Kazuo Aichi met with Prime Minister Hosokawa and indicated the DA's cautious view on Akashi's proposal; and senior DA officials, including Shigeru Hatayama, DA administrative vice minister, also indicated a cautious attitude in an effort to check General Secretary Butrus-Ghali's move. A voice within the DA has been expressed that what made Butrus-Ghali refrain from referring to this issue is "the DA's defensive move" (according to a senior DA official), thus reflecting DA officials' relief. The reason the DA is cautious about sending the SDF to former Yugoslavia is that its dispatch would be criticized for running counter to the PKO cooperation law that stipulates that "a cease-fire agreement should be reached between conflicting parties" before the dispatch of Japanese troops. Since a cease-fire accord has often been scrapped in former Yugoslavia, the DA points out that the case in former Yugoslavia is different from that in Cambodia, wherein the DA says "the cease-fire accord was kept although there were sporadic conflicts." In this way, the DA stresses that "it is not a matter of a type of PKO activities there but of the PKO law." With regard to Secretary General Butrus-Ghali's remarks, there is a voice within the DA that "Butrus-Ghali now fully understands Japan's difficult position on the PKO." Meanwhile, among senior officials, another view has also been expressed: "Secretary General Butrus-Ghali must have given priority to Japan's contribution in such areas as financial affairs other than PKO activities."
