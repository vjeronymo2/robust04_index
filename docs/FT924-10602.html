921030 FT 30 OCT 92 / Staff revert to trusted radio and telephones LONDON Ambulance Service staff have gone back to using paper, telephones and radios to co-ordinate ambulances after Monday's failure of the new computer system. Mr John Wilby, the service's chief executive, resigned on Wednesday after ambulance staff claimed that up to 20 people died unnecessarily on Monday because of delays caused by the computer system. Officers of the National Union of Public Employees, the main union representing ambulance service staff, say management had been warned repeatedly that the system was faulty, but employees' objections were overridden. Mr Paul Webster, a Nupe branch secretary who works at the ambulance service headquarters, said: 'We had been telling them for eight or nine months. We had said all along that it didn't work.' He blames the escalation of the troubles on a combination of management's refusal to listen, inadequate training, and political pressure. The computer system is still operating this week, but once the computer has allocated a job to an ambulance crew, details are being printed out so that control room staff can contact ambulance stations and crew directly by telephone or radio. 'We are using the computer and the paper back-up system so that nothing can be missed. Calls are not getting lost any more,' said Mr Webster. Nupe is still not happy about using the 'part and part' system, which has been in use since March. Mr Chris Humphreys, Nupe regional officer for London, said: 'It is by no means a secure system. There is still a lot of risk.' Mr David Adamthwaite, an ambulance crew member and Nupe senior staff co-ordinator, says the new system is 'fundamentally flawed'. 'Whatever system you apply, people have to have confidence in it. We want to go back to the simple system, telephone and radio. Anybody can use it. Crews have confidence in it.'
