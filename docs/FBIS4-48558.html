BFN [Text] Blantyre May 21 Sapa -- Fifty-one-year-old newly-elected Malawian President Bakili Muluzi was given a standing ovation on Saturday when he announced a general amnesty for all political prisoners, and commuted to life imprisonment the death sentences of ordinary prisoners. Mr Muluzi made the announcement in his first speech to the nation shortly after his presidential inauguration. The inauguration followed the country's first multiparty election, which stripped one of Africa's longest serving autocrats, Dr Kamuzu Banda, of power. A crowd of 60,000 supporters packed the Kamuzu stadium to hear Mr Muluzi and to witness the birth of the new Malawi. They chanted: "Scenes have changed in Malawi." The amnesty and life imprisonment sentences will take immediate effect. Along with these measures President Muluzi also ordered immediate closure of the country's three notorious political detention camps. Amid wild applause from the crowd he said these camps were closely associated with the "shameful" violation of human rights under the rule of Dr Banda's Malawi Congress Party. The president also invited all international human rights organisations to Malawi, saying: "They are free to visit this country and inspect our prisons as they wish." Mr Muluzi said the 300 million kwacha State House built by Dr Banda in Lilongwe should be turned into a parliamentary chamber. He said Malawi could not afford the luxury of having state residences in each of the country's 24 districts. A committee would be set up to work towards disposing of all the unwanted state residences. Mr Muluzi said the day was a special and memorable occasion which would be cherished forever as the moment that ushered real change into Malawi. "We have earned our freedom from the autocratic ruler who oppressed us and pounced on our rights ruthlessly." He pledged to uphold the principle of genuine democracy and to work for the betterment of all the people irrespective of their tribal, political, or ethnic differences. It is expected Mr Muluzi, whose party won 84 of the 177 parliamentary seats, will now need to form a coalition government with the Alliance for Democracy, headed by Mr Chakufwa Chihana, 55. He has already chosen former civil servant Justin Malewezi, who was booted out as secretary to the president and cabinet during Dr Banda's regime, as vice-president. The choice apparently dashed the expectations of many Malawians, who hoped this position would go to Mr Chihana in a bid to bring about national unity. Results of the general election have clearly shown that Malawi is a divided nation, desperately in need of someone who can unite it.
