BFN [Editorial Report] The following is a compilation of reports on economic activities in Chile monitored through 2 June. A public sector and private business commission will visit the PRC between 25 and 31 May and then go to Hong Kong to promote commercial relations. The negotiations will be conducted by Chilean exporters. The delegation will be headed by Economy Minister Alvaro Garcia and by Exporters Association President Ronald Bown. Chilean exports to PRC last year totalled $183.5 million, while imports totaled $212.5 million. (Santiago LA TERCERA DE LA HORA in Spanish 21 May 94 p 5) The Chilean Exporters Association reported that 122,063,885 boxes of fruits were exported during the 1993-1994 season, up by 3.5 percent compared with the previous season. The United States is the leading importer with $49.8 million worth of fruit, while Europe imported $46.8 million worth. (Santiago EL MERCURIO in Spanish 21 May 94 pp 1, 23) The Chilean and Venezuelan trade balance has been unfavorable for Chile since the economic complementation accord was enforced in April 1993. The Central Bank of Chile reported that exports to that country dropped by 6.2 percent between July and December 1993, and by 5.8 percent during the first quarter of this year, when sales totalled $14.5 million. The trade balance registered a $68-million deficit in 1993 following the purchase of oil and heating oil. (Madrid EFE in Spanish 1458 GMT 25 May 94) The Central Bank of Chile on 22 May reported that Chilean foreign investments rose $188.8 million in the first four months of the year. The biggest investments were made in Panama--$215.5 million; Argentina--$61 million; and Peru--$33.7 million. The cumulated foreign investment was $1,233 billion from 1975 to April 1994 (Santiago EL MERCURIO in Spanish 23 May 94 pp B1-B2) Preliminary figures released by the Central Bank of Chile show the GDP increased 3.4 percent during the first quarter of the year. Fishing registered the highest increase with 21.3 percent; transport and communications, 8.1 percent; industry, 5.5 percent; electricity, gas, and water, 5 percent; financial services, 4.3 percent; and personal services, including education and public and private health, 3.4 percent. Mining activities dropped by 0.7 percent and forestry activities dropped by 0.5 percent. (Santiago EL MERCURIO in Spanish 24 May 94 pp A1-A12)
