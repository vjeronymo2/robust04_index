May 5, 1990, Saturday, Orange County Edition

REMOVING LENNON PHOTO FROM EXHIBIT A HUGE MISTAKE

Like a mythological Hydra -- cut off one head and two more grow -- censorship is increasing apace. The most recent local example (at least in appearance but contradicted by a museum spokeswoman) is the removal, and subsequent return, of a photo depicting a naked, fetal-curled John Lennon lying next to a clothed Yoko Ono, which had been selected for an art exhibit at the Muckenthaler Cultural Center in Fullerton. ("Nude Photo of Lennon Removed in Fullerton," April 25.)

While I am indifferent toward the photo itself, and whether the cultural center displays it or not, I am concerned about the museum trustees' usurped decision of "perceived community concerns," which led to the photo's removal.

I am also disturbed about the local arts community's silence, except for a few perfunctory squeaks.

Where were the public comments from Orange County's artists, performers and writers? The dearth of reaction from professionals, arts organizations and museums only serves to reinforce the "little ado in Orange County" attitude that accompanied a recent Times article about the lack of local response to the controversy surrounding artistic content and funding for the National Endowment for the Arts.

In contrast to arts organizations across the United States that are picking up the gauntlet against the encroachment of subtle restrictions and outright censorship, members of Orange County's arts community were quoted as being "too busy," among other things, to be actively involved in the issue.

Can we afford the portent of such complacency?

No. Waiting until we aren't too busy (which never happens), or until we have a reason to act (i.e., when we are finally directly affected by censorship) is dangerous and shortsighted.

Dangerous because our apathy allows others to make choices for us. Shortsighted because through our inaction we have permitted the self-appointed monitors of morality and taste to lay a foundation for future restraints in other areas of our lives.

Tacit approval clings to silence that is maintained in the face of decisions made by "guardians" of what we will see, hear or read.

The point has been made more than once in recent months that as the countries of Eastern Europe and Latin America approach greater freedom of movement and free expression, we in the United States are, paradoxically, tightening (through evermore refined means) the restrictions on our own rights.

The incidents at the Muckenthaler Cultural Center reflect more than just the removal of a photograph in a moment of poor judgment. It mirrors growing forces that are determined to curtail personal choice, and not just in the arts.

We should all be more aware and more engaged in issues affecting our right to exercise freedom of choice. If not us, who? If not now, when?

JANET E. GRAEBNER, Santa Ana
