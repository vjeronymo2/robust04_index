930505 FT 05 MAY 93 / Parliament and Politics: County council election issues THE council tax is widely regarded as the 'dog which did not bark' in this year's county council elections - but all parties agree that it would have bulked much larger if it had not followed the poll tax, or community charge, John Authers writes. Conservatives have tried to make the tax an election issue, and claim a degree of success. A spokesman said: 'We would argue quite strongly that the council tax and the level of bills people have to pay is going to be absolutely vital in the election.' They argue that councils spend an average of Pounds 1,500 per household, making a county council vote 'the biggest expenditure decision many people are going to make'. The party has also tried to demonstrate that Labour councils charge a higher level of tax. However, the Tories still admit that it has not been the central issue on the doorsteps, taking second place variously to education, community care, and local issues. They are also deeply relieved that the tax is not as controversial as its predecessor. Labour believes the tax has not been important in most areas, but it has had to work hard to rebut Conservative claims. Its campaigners have relied on a survey by the Local Government Chronicle magazine, which showed that average council tax payments in Labour councils were Pounds 14 lower than in Conservative councils. A Labour party spokesman said: 'The point we are making is that we are actually a bit cheaper than the Conservatives, but we're certainly no more expensive.'
