Language: Hungarian Article Type:BFN [Excerpts] Foreign Minister Geza Jeszenszky has said in Washington that although Hungarian foreign policy is basically directed toward the West, ties with Russia also have to be consolidated. Following his talks with U.S. Defense Secretary William Perry, Geza Jeszenszky said that the United States is beginning to understand Hungary's special position in connection with Vojvodina and that the Serbs are wrong to think that the Hungarians treat them as an enemy. Further details about Geza Jesznszky's visit to Wasington are given by Miklos Martin Kovacs: [Kovacs] The United States sees clearly that the Hungarian Government's intention is also to settle the Balkan crisis as soon as possible and in the fairest way. [passage omitted] Also in Washington, in the White House's Roosevelt Hall, President Clinton met members of organizations representing the Visegrad countries in America -- Czechs, Poles, Hungarians, and Slovaks. Laszlo Papp, one of the five Hungarian participants in the meeting representing the local branch of the World Federation of Hungarians, told Hungarian Radio that President Clinton made it clear that his government continues to rely on their opinion, since it pays special attention to Eastern-Central Europe and the U.S. Government is clear about the interests of the region. Bill Clinton added that the two main American concerns when evaluating the various governments are the progress of privatization and the enforcement of democracy.
