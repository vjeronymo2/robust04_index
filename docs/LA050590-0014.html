May 5, 1990, Saturday, Home Edition

CONRAD'S JERUSALEM CARTOON

Conrad either has not bothered to read your paper's news reports or has chosen to create his own facts upon which to base his false and misleading cartoon. As reported in these pages, approximately 150 Jewish settlers moved into a building in Jerusalem's Jewish quarter.

Contrary to Conrad's depiction, the building was not the site of the Last Supper, nor is it a holy site at all. No places holy of any faith were violated in any way by the act of the settlers.

When Jordan seized the Old City of Jerusalem during the 1948 Arab-Israeli war, all Jewish inhabitants were expelled and denied access to the Western Wall and Jewish holy places; edifices and cemeteries were desecrated.

Israel reunited Jerusalem after the 1967 war and the Israeli government passed the Protection of Holy Places law guaranteeing the preservation of all holy sites in the city in accordance with Israeli law which protects the freedom of religion and worship for people of all faiths.

The settlers broke no law in their actions; any illegality relates to the issue of tenancy and subtenancy. Israel's Supreme Court has upheld the eviction of the settlers and ordered that they must leave the site.

While the settlers and the Israeli Housing Ministry were rightfully condemned for their insensitivity, this was not and should not be wrongfully portrayed as an Israeli attempt to conquer the holy sites of any other faith. Conrad's placement of Prime Minister Shamir and an Israeli flag on the table of Da Vinci's Last Supper is inaccurate and is not based on the facts as they were reported by The Times.

TZIVIA SCHWARTZ

Associate Western States Counsel

Anti-Defamation League

Los Angeles
