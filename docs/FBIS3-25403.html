Language: English Article Type:BFN [Text] Durban Feb 17 SAPA -- Zulu King Goodwill Zwelithini would not be prevented from promulgating a constitution for his kingdom, despite it being an unprecedented act, a kwaZulu government spokesman said on Thursday [17 February]. "It is unfortunate that we have been so subdued by apartheid that we think we can do nothing that has not been done before. This is slave mentality," said a statement from kwaZulu Deputy Minister of Works Velaphi Ndolvu. "That no other king in the world has promulgated the constitution of his kingdom, if this is true, will not prevent the king of the Zulus from promulgating the constitution," he said. The monarch said on Monday he was preparing to promulgate a constitution for a sovereign kwaZulu and Natal kingdom, but declined to elaborate at a later media briefing. Mr Ndlovu's remarks were in response to various African National Congress statements on the king's demands. On allegations the king was an IFP [Inkatha Freedom Party] mouthpiece, Mr Ndlovu said these were was based on the fact that kwaZulu Chief Minister and IFP President Mangosuthu Buthelezi regularly accompanied the monarch at public functions. "The people of South Africa must accept once and for all that Dr M B Buthelezi is the traditional prime minister to the king and when he appears with the king he does so as both prime minister and chief minister and not as president of the IFP." Mr Ndlovu further rejected charges that the king had intervened in South Africa's constitutional impasse on behalf of the Freedom Alliance. He said King Zwelithini had made a demand for the return of his land and the recognition of the "kwaZulu kingdom" as a sovereign state as far back as in July last year, at his imbizo (traditional gathering) at the FNB Stadium outside Johannesburg. The king's demand for the "reinstatement of the kwaZulu kingdom emanated form the king himself and not from the Freedom Alliance". To allege the king was being used by the IFP was an insult, said Mr Ndlovu. "It is the IFP which has supported his majesty and not the other way around." The king was genuinely concerned about all Zulus, no matter what their political affiliation, and had never expressed himself in favour of the IFP with regard to violence, Mr Ndlovu added.
