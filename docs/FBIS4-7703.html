BFN [Text] Belgrade, April 4 (TANJUG) -- Croatia will sell out cheaply at least 20,000 apartments, summer homes and other facilities worth over three billion U.S. dollars, whose owners are nationals of the Federal Republic of Yugoslavia, the president of the Association for the Equitable Treatment and Protection of the Property of Yugoslav Citizens, Mirko Lalovic, said on Monday. That such a sale will take place is also evident from the fact that the Croatian Fund for Privatization announced in March the placing on sale of 18 holiday complexes, which are the property of firms from Serbia and Montenegro (federal units of the Federal Republic of Yugoslavia), the president of the Belgrade Association told TANJUG. Croatia has usurped the facilities, following its forcible secession from the former Yugoslav Federation in 1991 under the pretext that they were public property and has entered them into the land register as state property. In order to usurp the private property of FRY nationals, Croatia introduced an exorbitant tax on the private property of non-Croatian nationals, 15 German marks per square meter annually. The same tax for Croatian nationals is 3.75 marks, proving that it was a well thought out plan to coerce property owners from the Federal Republic of Yugoslavia to give away or sell them to Croatians at bargain price. Since payment operations between Croatia and the Federal Repubic of Yugoslavia is non-existant, citizens from Serbia and Montenegro, Republics making up the Yugoslav Federation, are not able to pay their taxes. The Croatian legislator has thus provided, when indedebtedness exceeds a third of the value of the taxed property, for selling it. The beneficiary will be the state. This will happen soon due to the accumulated interest (two percent daily), so that summer homes, houses, apartments and other facilities, property of FRY nationals have already been put on sale, Lalovic said. Such an usurpation of assets contributes to the creation of an ethnically clean Croatia, because Serbs and Montenegrans will lose their properties in that republic, thus denying them the right to return once relations are normalized, he added. Stressing that the Croation attitude towards real estate owners from Serbia and Montenegro is unprecedented in international law, Lalovic added that they acquired the property at the time when Croatia was part of the former Yugoslavia, which means under the same conditions as Croats have so that they should be treated equally, regardless of the fact that in the meantime they have become foreigners. The usurpation of property in Croatia has been recordered by over 16,000 Yugoslav nationals. It is assessed however that 25 to 30 percent of owners have not done so, expecting that the situation will sort itself out, so that it is certain that the total value of properties exceeds three billion dollars, Lalovic said in conclusion.
