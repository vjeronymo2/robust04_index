940314 FT 14 MAR 94 / Opinion divided on Tube Charter Ms Jane Eyes, a senior account executive at PR firm Burson-Marsteller, is occasionally delayed on her journey to work on the London Underground, Motoko Rich writes. But she has never applied for the refund on the ticket price to which she is entitled under the London Underground Customer Charter. 'I was not aware I could,' she said. Mr Martin Bradshaw, a freelance journalist who uses the Circle or Central Line every day, applied for a refund in February and has still not received a voucher. 'As with a lot of things, it's all upfront PR with very little behind it,' he said. Yet the Underground is regarded by the Cabinet Office Citizen's Charter Unit as having one of the best charters - one that fulfils a large proportion of the original promises. It is one of the few agencies that provides a clear compensation policy, and growing numbers of consumers are taking advantage of it. Travellers delayed by more than 15 minutes can claim a refund. Managers' pay is linked to area performance targets. In the first five months of the charter, 12,500 claimed refunds. In 1993, 60,000 claimed and in the first two months of 1994, 19,000 applied. The 15-minute rule also represents the toughening of standards that Mr Major promised. The service's first charter limited compensation to 20-minute delays. And there is an independent auditor and adjudicator for the service -the London Regional Passengers Committee. LU said it had exceeded its 1992-93 targets on almost all standards. Now that she has heard about the LU charter, Ms Eyes believes it could be a good idea. 'But at the end of the day what you want is to be able to get from A to B and not be stuck with signal failures or smoke in the tunnel.'
