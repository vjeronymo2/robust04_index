Language: Spanish Article Type:BFN [Report by Joe Alberto Briceno] [Text] Actions by the large American countries, headed by the United States, were stepped up on 10 January to expedite the official announcement of Colombian President Cesar Gaviria's candidacy to the post of OAS secretary general, diplomatic sources have reported. Those sources have even said that in Washington the United States, Canada, Mexico, Argentina, and Brazil--the main promoters of Gaviria's candidacy--are the ones lobbying the most with other diplomatic representations to obtain the votes the Colombian president needs. The sources have underscored that the United States does not want Costa Rican Foreign Minister Bernd Niehaus to head the OAS, because, according to them, he does not meet the necessary conditions. The North Americans want a statesman to head the organization, because they expect this will be the means to get their inter-American policy implemented with more multinational criteria, just as has been cited by Bill Clinton's administration. According to news agencies, the State Department has already obtained the support of Ecuador, Venezuela, Peru, and Bolivia for Gaviria's candidacy. Chilean Foreign Minister Enrique Silva Cimma has said his government views this candidacy with much interest, although President-elect Eduardo Frei will have to make the decision. Costa Rican President Rafael Angel Calderon has said there will be "a hard confrontation with Colombia" if Gaviria formalizes his candidacy. He also reiterated that efforts will continue to unite all the small countries on the continent and elect the Central American and Caribbean representative. Luis Guardia, the Costa Rican ambassador at the OAS, has told LA REPUBLICA that only Peru had advanced its backing to the Colombian in Washington and that this was not surprising, "because Costa Rica was the first country to protest after President Alberto Fujimori broke the constitutional order." The Peruvian president has said his country supported strongly the Colombian nominee, and he said Gaviria is a statesman who also has a good integrationist spirit. Guardia has explained that the 13 Caribbean countries have confirmed their support for Niehaus' candidacy and will maintain it if Central America "does not separate." In talks with U.S. officials, Guardia has requested "they not take sides yet" and that, as has been their tradition, they not announce their choice until the end. Likewise, he has stressed the importance of respecting the management rotation principle, which prevails in Washington-based institutions, such as in the Inter-American Development Bank (IDB) and the Pan-American Health Organization. Until now the Costa Rican minister has maintained the support of the 13 Caribbean and six isthmian states, as well as the Dominican Republic, Chile, Ecuador, and Argentina. The latter two seem to have modified their positions, however, according to reports coming from Santa Fe de Bogota. Niehaus has said much speculation has been reported to stop the regional option, but he said: "We have a comfortable majority, and I am sure President Gaviria will think twice before making his candidacy official." He has charged that certain big countries consider they are "first category" countries and that small countries are "second category" ones and thus unable to manage inter-American forums. Niehaus believes this attitude violates international law. He has added that the 28 March election will reflect if regulations will be respected or slighted.
