Language: Spanish Article Type:BFN [Text] Colonia, 17 Jan (EFE) -- A series of differences were revealed on 17 January in the first results of a meeting the presidents of Argentina, Brazil, Paraguay, Bolivia, and Uruguay held to give political support to the Parana and Paraguay Rivers "waterway." Carlos Menem, Juan Carlos Wasmosy, Itamar Franco, Luis Alberto Lacalle -- the presidents of Argentina, Paraguay, Brazil, and Uruguay -- are in Colonia, 180 km from Montevideo, attending the fifth Common Market of the South [Mercosur] summit, whose agenda centers around resolution of discrepancies for implementation of a common market. Bolivia, which is not a Mercosur member, is being represented by its president, Gonzalo Sanchez de Lozada, who is attending as an observer. This country has been considered part of the River Plate Basin in the "waterway" project, which will give Bolivia its longed- for access to the sea. Uruguayan Foreign Minister Sergio Abreu told journalists that "the waterway will be included as a topic in the final declaration" of the fifth summit, which will end this evening, but did not report on the project's difficulties. The common channels that Argentina and Uruguay -- the two River Plate coastal nations -- have under the terms of the River Plate Treaty have been used so far to reach the sea from the Paraguay and Parana Rivers. But an Argentine initiative to build a private channel within its territorial waters through a public works concession to a private party, a project that will be financed through tolls, has created displeasure among the system's partners. Uruguay has conveyed to Argentina its concern over the Mitre channel, which is the name of the Menem government's privatization project. This channel will join the Argentine port of Rosario on the Parana River with the Atlantic Ocean. This will eliminate the Uruguayan port of Nueva Palmira as the river's final port, where Paraguay and Bolivia have been allowed to operate duty-free warehouses. Uruguay also is concerned that the deepening of the new channel in Argentine territory will displace the channel the two countries share in Martin Garcia. River navigation experts also have voiced their concern about the Mitre channel, which could force Uruguayan ships to pay tolls to the enterprises awarded the construction of Argentine public works, even for navigation to Uruguayan ports on the Uruguay River.
