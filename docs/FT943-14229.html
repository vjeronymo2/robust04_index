940715 FT 15 JUL 94 / Hole left in local pockets If there was one thing that Mr James Millar liked emphasising to visitors to Wm Low's Dundee offices it was the contribution the headquarters made to the local economy, writes James Buxton. 'We obtain almost all our professional services locally,' the chairman would say. 'Even the taxi driver taking you to the station is benefiting. None of that would happen if we were part of a group based in London.' So it is poignant that the loss of Wm Low's independence will involve the closure of its headquarters and probable redundancy for many of its 300 staff. The takeover will mean the disappearance of a historic name from the Scottish business scene. It also means a reduction in the modest number of Scottish quoted companies. Wm Low was founded in 1867 by two Dundee families. By 1914 the chain had grown to 80 stores. Mr Millar, a terrier of a man, became managing director in 1981, upgrading the supermarkets and expanding into northern England. Mr Campbell Christie, general secretary of the Scottish Trade Union Congress, said yesterday he was disappointed to see 'another Scottish company bite the dust'. But it was better for Wm Low to be taken over than to see it closing stores. Dundee can absorb the loss of Wm Low's head office, despite unemployment of 11 per cent. But it badly needs some good news after last year's rancourous industrial dispute and closure at the Timex electronics plant. As for the customers, one said: 'The staff at Willie Low's are the nicest and most polite of the supermarkets I go to.' Would she keep shopping there when it became Tesco? 'Yes I would.'
