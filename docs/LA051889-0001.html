RECIPES WITH A HISTORY OF PLEASING;

'CENTENNIAL COOKBOOK' SERVES UP OLD-TIME DISHES

Traditional American food is experiencing a renaissance, which makes the Orange County Pioneer Council's "Centennial Cookbook, 1889-1989" a work of special interest.

Recipes that might have been disregarded when nouvelle, "trendy" food dominated the scene receive the attention they deserve in its pages. They are old-time dishes from area pioneers, compiled along with segments of oral history by the Orange County Pioneer Council. The book was printed at the Friis-Pioneer Press in Santa Ana and celebrates the county's centennial year.

Scattered throughout are recipes with titles that indicate strong family ties, among them mom's applesauce cake, my Grandmother Steffen's persimmon pudding, Grandmother Yost's sour cream-raisin pie, Grandma Ross' Lemon Cream Pie, mama's Boston brown bread and Great-Grandfather Lunt's spoon bread.

Oldies but Goodies

A chile sauce originated with a woman born in Santa Ana in 1893. A bride who arrived in California in 1885 handed down a recipe for rock cookies, and a family of El Toro pioneers passed a recipe for pickled green beans from one generation to the next. A recipe for strawberry mousse was found in "Aunt Lois' old recipe book," and a golden loaf cake appeared in a cookbook dated Santa Ana, 1885.

The contributor's name and the pioneer family represented accompany each recipe, along with remembrances of times almost forgotten.

"There was no refrigeration in those days. Only ice boxes and no ice man. People would go to town to get ice," reads an entry attributed to Irvin Clarke Chapman.

Insight into the way people once ate is provided by such statements as these:

"Mrs. Dowling had a garden and the family had lots of fruits and vegetables in their diet. Breakfast, however, was always steak, gravy and bread or waffles or pancakes." -- Marquita Salveson Dowling.

Living on Vegetables

"We used to practically live on vegetables and then have meat maybe two or three times a week. I remember mama would send us to the store for ground steak for dinner. She would give us a quarter to buy it." -- Jo Sawdey Taylor.

"The only two kinds of ready-made cereal were shredded wheat and corn flakes. All those other things that fill the shelves down there in the store nowadays were never thought of, which was a darn good thing. Mostly, we cooked cereals. Mom would make corn meal the night before and let it solidify. The next morning she would slice it and fry it for breakfast." -- Claire Sharpless Marks.

Other passages recount the roles played by family members in Orange County history. Photographs of early homes were also gathered for the book.

The recipes remain in their original state, which means such essentials as pan sizes and baking times and temperatures are sometimes omitted. But most of the recipes are quite workable. And the four chosen to accompany this story not only worked but disappeared rapidly when they were set out for tasting.

Unusual Procedure

Grandma Ross' Lemon Cream Pie, provided by Elsa Wilson Edwards, includes an unusual procedure for making a meringue that, according to the recipe, "will not weep."

Esther Ridgway Cramer of La Habra contributed an old family recipe for holiday cookies, Leutwiler Leckerlis. According to Cramer, Jacob Leutwiler was an early Swiss settler who came to La Habra before 1900. Cramer's mother, Ida, moved to La Habra in 1912 with the John Leutwiler family. "John helped found the first bank in La Habra and became La Habra's first photographer," Cramer said.

The Leutwiler Leckerlis are rock hard when first baked and require a month of storage to mellow. The softening procedure can be hastened by adding an apple wedge to the airtight storage container.

Mother's Date and Nut Cookies were contributed by Mr. and Mrs. Leonard Brewster Parker of San Diego. The cookies are firm when first baked but then soften and become chewy, revealing the rich taste of the dates.

Cookhouse Recipe

Crazy Cake no doubt got its name because it includes neither eggs nor butter nor milk, yet is moist and full of good chocolate flavor. According to Charlotte Moulton Mathis of Hollister, the recipe "came from a cook in the old Moulton Ranch cookhouse." Mathis' father, Lewis Fenno Moulton, purchased Rancho Niguel, an old Spanish land grant that came to be known as the Moulton Ranch, in the 1890s.

The centennial cookbook can be ordered by mail. The price is $12.50, which includes $2.50 to cover postage and handling. Make checks out to the Orange County Pioneer Council and send them to OCPC, c/o Shirley Parker, 400 Vista Parada, Newport Beach, Calif. 92660.

Here are the pioneer recipes, slightly edited to amplify the instructions.

GRANDMA ROSS' LEMON CREAM PIE

1 cup sugar

5 tablespoons cornstarch

1/4 teaspoon salt

2 cups milk

3 egg yolks

3 tablespoons butter

1 teaspoon grated lemon zest

1/3 cup lemon juice

1 (9-inch) baked pie shell

Meringue

Mix sugar, cornstarch, salt and milk in saucepan. Stir until smooth. Cook and stir over low heat until thickened. Remove from heat and pour small amount into lightly beaten egg yolks. Add egg yolks to pan, return to heat and cook and stir 5 minutes. Add butter, lemon zest and juice. Pour hot filling into cooled pie shell. Top with Meringue, spreading to edge of pie shell. Bake at 400 degrees about 10 minutes. Makes 1 (9-inch) pie.

Meringue

1 tablespoon cornstarch

2 tablespoons cold water

1/2 cup boiling water

3 egg whites

1/8 teaspoon cream of tartar

6 tablespoons sugar

Blend cornstarch with cold water in small saucepan, then add boiling water. Cook and stir until transparent. Cool mixture. Beat eggs whites with cream of tartar until foamy. Add sugar gradually and beat until shiny. Drizzle cornstarch mixture over egg whites, beating constantly until blended.

LEUTWILER LECKERLIS

3/4 cup honey

1/3 cup butter

1 cup sugar

1/2 cup orange marmalade

Grated zest of 1 orange

5 cups flour

2 teaspoons baking powder

1 teaspoon nutmeg

1/2 teaspoon cinnamon

1/2 teaspoon cloves

1/2 teaspoon salt

1 cup chopped slivered blanched almonds

1 cup powdered sugar

2 tablespoons milk

In 2-quart saucepan, melt honey and butter together over low heat, stirring constantly. Remove from heat. Stir in sugar, marmalade and orange zest. Sift flour, baking powder, nutmeg, cinnamon, cloves and salt together and gradually add to warm mixture. Add almonds. (Dough will be very stiff and must be worked by hand.)

Roll dough 1/4 inch thick on floured surface. Cut into 2-inch squares. Place on wax paper-lined baking sheets and bake at 325 degrees 15 to 20 minutes. Blend powdered sugar and milk. Remove cookies from pan. While still warm, brush with powdered sugar mixture. Cool and store in airtight container.

Bars are very hard and inedible until mellow. To speed mellowing, add cut apple to container. Makes about 5 1/2 dozen.

MOTHER'S DATE AND NUT COOKIES

1 cup butter

2 cups brown sugar, packed

2 eggs, beaten

3 1/2 cups flour

1 teaspoon soda

3/4 teaspoon salt

Filling

Cream butter and sugar. Add eggs. Sift together flour, soda and salt and add slowly to creamed mixture. Divide dough in half. Roll out each half on floured surface and spread with Filling. Roll up dough, wrap and chill several hours. Cut into 1/4-inch-thick slices. Place on greased baking sheets and bake at 350 degrees about 15 minutes, or until lightly browned. Makes about 5 dozen.

Filling

1 pound dates, seeded and chopped

1/2 cup chopped nuts

1/2 cup sugar

1/2 cup water

Combine dates, nuts, sugar and water in saucepan. Cover and simmer about 10 minutes. Stir to form smooth paste.

CRAZY CAKE

1 1/2 cups sifted cake flour

1 cup sugar

3 tablespoons unsweetened cocoa

1 teaspoon soda

1/2 teaspoon salt

6 tablespoons oil

1 tablespoon vinegar

1 teaspoon vanilla

1 cup cold water

Sift together cake flour, sugar, cocoa, soda and salt. Add oil, vinegar and vanilla. Pour water over all. Mix well. Pour into ungreased 8-inch cake pan. Bake at 350 degrees 25 to 30 minutes or until tester comes out clean. Makes one 8-inch cake.
