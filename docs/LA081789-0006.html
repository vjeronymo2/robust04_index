August 17, 1989, Thursday, Home Edition

GIVE INSURANCE CONSUMERS AN ADVOCATE

Insurance Commissioner Roxani Gillespie has finally begun hearings to determine whether individual insurers should be required to roll back their rates as directed by Proposition 103. These hearings will also determine the size of the refund checks that consumers will receive from insurers for excess premiums charged during the past year.

In evaluating insurers' petitions for exemptions from the rollbacks, Gillespie will hear abundant assertions from legions of lawyers and actuaries working for the insurance companies. But she will hear little from insurance experts for consumers. That's because consumers lack their own team of actuaries and lawyers to scrutinize the thousand-plus exemption petitions. Instead, consumers will have little recourse but to cross their fingers in the largely vain hope that Gillespie, a former insurance-company executive, will act in good faith to implement the Proposition 103 rollbacks.

It doesn't have to be this way. The authors of Proposition 103 knew full well that consumers would have to be empowered to safeguard the initiative from subversion by the industry and its official allies. So when they drafted the measure, they included a provision to establish an independent consumer insurance organization staffed by insurance experts to intervene on behalf of consumers in regulatory proceedings before the commissioner. This is exactly what consumers need.

Unfortunately, the California Supreme Court ruled that such a provision technically violated a state institutional prohibition against naming a private corporation in a ballot initiative. But the court did made it clear that the Legislature was free to pass a law creating the rate-payer organization.

Shortly thereafter, Senate President David A. Roberti (D-Los Angeles) introduced such legislation. Taken verbatim from Proposition 103, the Roberti bill has been approved by the Senate but faces an uncertain fate in the Assembly Finance and Insurance Committee, where a vote has been scheduled for Tuesday.

While the measure has thus far received scant attention from the media, it is the most innovative consumer protection legislation given serious consideration by the Legislature in years. If enacted, California policyholders will be the first consumers in the country to have an institutional, yet independent, role in their state's insurance rate-review process.

Here is how it would work: Whenever an insurance company mails out its bills, it would be required to enclose a notice inviting policyholders to join an organization that would fight for their rights and interests before the Insurance Department, the courts and the Legislature. After voluntarily paying a modest membership fee, member policyholders would elect a board that would then hire a staff of professional insurance experts. Because these experts would be accountable to consumers only, there would be no danger they would be co-opted by special interests (as frequently happens with government consumer protection agencies). In addition, the Consumer Insurance Organization would involve no expenditure of taxpayer money, nor any out-of-pocket expense for insurance companies.

While those legislators who front for the insurance industry argue that consumers are already well enough represented by existing consumer groups, they know full well that on the regulatory battlefield these groups are badly outnumbered by an infinitely larger and wealthier insurance industry. The fact is that the half-dozen or so consumer groups in California that are active on insurance issues simply do not have the staff or technical capability to challenge more than a few of the thousands of exemption petitions and rate filings that the commissioner will rule on over the next few months.

After being trounced by consumers at the polls last fall, insurers must find it very comforting indeed to know that when it comes to implementing the rollbacks and rate regulation ordered by Proposition 103, consumers have their hands tied behind their backs. For the industry, the next few months of hearings before the Insurance Department represent an opportunity to seize the power to determine prices that it lost to the voters last November.

For consumers, the inevitable domination of the upcoming hearings by insurance-industry lawyers represents a serious threat to their victory last fall. Like other consumer protection measures, Proposition 103 is an instrument rather than a result. Unless it is applied aggressively and by government officials who are serving their rightful masters, it will not produce the intended result. And the only way for consumers to make sure that officials such as Gillespie act in the public interest is to be there, watching over her shoulder every step of the way.

After being written off by insurance consumers as cowards, the Legislature has unexpectedly been given an opportunity by the state Supreme Court to join with consumers in standing up to the industry and ensuring that Proposition 103 works for consumers. The Legislature must seize this opportunity by passing the Roberti bill to establish the Consumer Insurance Organization.
