August 10, 1990, Friday, San Diego County Edition

SAN DIEGO SPOTLIGHT: STAGE / NANCY CHURNIN: GASLAMP ANNOUNCES PLANS TO RESUME ITS 1990 SEASON

The Gaslamp Quarter Theatre Company is about$31,000 away from resuming -- and concluding -- its 1990 season in November at the Hahn Cosmopolitan Theatre, with the long-planned San Diego premiere of Terrence McNally's off-Broadway hit, "Frankie and Johnny in the Clair de Lune."

That was the figure given this week by the Gaslamp's managing director, Kit Goldman, and its board of trustees president, George Saadeh.

Even earlier, in mid-October, the company intends to resume the 1990 season at its second, smaller space, the Elizabeth North Theatre, as a co-producer of the Ensemble Arts Theatre production of "Dusk to Dawn at the Sunset" by Terry Dodd.

The Gaslamp, which is in its 10th season, suspended operations in May when a long-accumulated financial deficit reached a critical state. During the intervening months, the organization has rented out both its venues, providing revenues that, along with fund raising, have replenished the theater company's coffers sufficiently to schedule the reopenings, Saadeh said.

The Gaslamp has also been helped by a reduced rental fee negotiated with the limited partners that own the Hahn. Until this summer, the Gaslamp paid a back-breaking $10,146 a month for the 250-seat facility; it now pays half that.

It is unclear, however, whether resuming the season at the smaller venue will influence the outcome of a dispute with former patron Elizabeth North, who pledged $75,000 in exchange for the Gaslamp naming the theater after her. When operations were suspended last spring, North withheld the final $35,000 installment of her pledge, saying she would not donate the money until the theater resumed operations.

The theater is also studying a restructuring plan written by Harris Goldman (no relation to Kit), a consultant who was the former executive producer at Starlight Musical Theatre. He would not comment on the plan except to say that he is "very optimistic in terms of the Gaslamp's future."

"We have most of the money in hand," Saadeh said, referring to what is needed for the two upcoming productions. "But we like to do everything very cautiously now. The bottom line is that the theater has to run like a business. It may not come back like a boomerang. It is going to come out slowly but wisely."

The lion's share of the money needed for the 1991 season -- a figure no one is willing to name -- still needs to be raised, but Kit Goldman and Saadeh hope the fund raising will be easier when the theater is producing.

"We're in a chicken-and-egg situation," Goldman said. "Two of the things major funders want to see is that we can do quality work and sell tickets."

Consequently, investors are likely to be looking closely at the Gaslamp's choices. The theater has acquired a reputation for bland, monochromatic period pieces during the past few years. Although its plays invariably have been skillfully done, they failed to bring in ample audiences, dropping to about 50% at the Hahn last season.

While Goldman said she is not sure whether she will be changing her title, she is, however, changing her job. The board of trustees is interviewing managing director candidates to handle the business matters that used to be Goldman's responsibility. And Goldman, who this spring fired her original artistic director and co-founder, Will Simpson, will continue to make artistic choices.

Both Saadeh and Goldman said the Ensemble Arts Theatre production of "Dusk to Dawn at the Sunset" is the sort of production they hope to be known for in the future.

"It's a slice of life from the Southwest, exploring the relationships between families," Saadeh said. "It fits in well with what we want to offer our subscribers." Ensemble Arts Theatre is now touring "Dusk to Dawn at the Sunset" at the Edinburgh Fringe Festival in Scotland.

Goldman said that, more than a change in content, the significance of "Dusk to Dawn at the Sunset" may be that it is a new show and a cooperative venture with another company. She predicted more co-productions of new works at the Elizabeth North and less at the Hahn. And, with the possible exception of "Tales From Tinseltown," a new musical under consideration for the Hahn in 1991, most of the work there will already have a track record even if it is new to San Diego.

Along with "Tales From Tinseltown," proposed to the Gaslamp by New York producers Celso Gonzalez-Falla and his wife, Sondra Gilman, other shows under consideration are "Oil City Symphony," canceled last season, and "The Debutante," a black Pygmalion story set in turn-of-the-century Philadelphia and commissioned by Goldman for her own independent production company.

Meanwhile, the fund raising and rentals continue. The Gaslamp's next celebrity staged reading stars Harold Gould at the Hahn Cosmopolitan on Sept. 24. Upcoming rentals at the Hahn include the new musical "I Wanna-Be," Aug. 17-Sept. 9, and the Oct. 11-Nov. 4 run of "Family Secrets," a one-woman show starring Sheri Glaser and directed by Robyn Samuels. At the Elizabeth North, rentals include the San Diego Actors Theatre, which will produce "Eleeomosynary" beginning tonight, and "Party Favors," a new play by local playwright Mark Melden, opening in September.
