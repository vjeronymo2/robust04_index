931130 FT 30 NOV 93 / The FT Review of Business Books (30): Don't mickle with a muckle THE OXFORD DICTIONARY FOR THE BUSINESS WORLD Oxford University Press Pounds 15.95 996 pages It is possible that a Sassenach closing a deal on the Isle of Skye or some similar place might need to know the meaning of the Scots word 'mickle'. It's easy to confuse with muckle; both mean a large amount. But by the time a business person had consulted this dictionary for the meaning of 'Mickey Finn' (a drugged drink), would it not be too late? From Dr Johnson onwards, lexicographers have rejoiced in idiosyncracy when selecting words for inclusion in their works and so it is here. 'Yuppie' (young, ambitious and so on) is just about acceptable, but 'yum-yum' (expressing pleasure from eating or the prospect of eating') seems to have escaped from a dictionary of nursery expressions. To be fair, however,in just under a thousand pages it describes most important business terms and even manages quite a few basic computing expressions, although remarkably neither facilities management nor outsourcing are included. And the businessperson looking for enlightenment on data communications and networking will have only intermittent success. Local and wide area networks are included, but these terms are comparatively well understood. Where, however, is the explanation of 'hub' or 'router'? Such a definition could prove invaluable to, say, a marketmaker faced with a bland 'Sorry guv, the hub's gone down' when inquiring about the loss of stock prices from the trading screen. There are tips for making a business speech ('the speaker should appear enthusiastic without being irrational or emotional') and constructing a business plan ('if an outline projection of cash flow for 10 years can be provided, the picture becomes increasingly credible'). Uruguay is described and the Uruguay Round defined. Is 'thatcher - n. (Old English)' a joke? Thatcherism does not feature. Alas, some of the country definitions are already dated. Under 'United Kingdom': 'the economy is based on manufacturing industry.'
