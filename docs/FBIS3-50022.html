Language: English Article Type:BFN [Text] Bucharest, ROMPRES, 17/1/1994 -- "It was an attempt against the constitutional democracy in Romania," said Romanian Constitutional Court Judge Antonie Iorgovan, cited by the daily ADEVARUL, referring to the news broadcasted by the National Television on Saturday, January 14, informing that the Hungarian Democratic Union of Romania [UDMR], the ethnic party of the Hungarian minority, had met with mayors of several localities to discuss the issue of "self-governing." Judge Antonie Iorgovan, who is also professor of constitutional law of the Bucharest University, reminds that Romania's constitution does not provide such a concept because of the simple reason that there is only one government in a unitary state, politically responsible in front of the single parliament as the representative body of the Romanian nation." Antonie Iorgovan reminded also that the "self-governing" wording was not stipulated by any unitary state's constitution in Western Europe as they consecrated a regional system of local administration and not a departmental system." Antonie Iorgovan draws attention also to the fact that even though only a departmental system of administrative-territorial organization in counties exists in Romania and not one recognizing regions, lands, provinces or communities, "there are political forces who employ such wording." Judge Iorgovan cites the Article 31, Paragraph 7, of the constitution as saying: "Any defamation of the country and the nation, instigation to a war of aggression, to national, racial, class or religious hatred, any incitement to discrimination, territorial separatism, public violence as well as an obscene conduct contrary to morality are prohibited by law." The public opinion in Romania has repeatedly showed in the recent years, its concern with regard to the statements made by several UDMR leaders questioning directly or in a dissimulated way the issue of Transylvania, ancient Romanian territory, temporarily under the rule of the Austro-Hungarian Habsburg empire, till 1918. According to the January 1992 census, the population of the counties of the historic province of Transylvania is made up by Romanians with 72.3 percent.
