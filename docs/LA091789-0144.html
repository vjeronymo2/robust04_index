September 17, 1989, Sunday, Home Edition

RICHARD EDER: RESISTING THE PULL OF TRADITION;

JASMINE BY BHARATI MUKHERJEE (GROVE: $17.95; 190 PP.)

We receive periodic messages -- daily, it sometimes seems -- to the effect that the line between our First World and the Third World grows less and less distinct.

The news from the urban slums and ghettos, the fragility of the suburbs, the swamping of public systems of education and health, the widening gap between rich and poor, corruption or laxity in the IRS, the savings and loan industry and the housing agencies, the drug and AIDS epidemics -- all these suggest that the control of life that we always thought of as marking a developed society is slipping away.

The messages come in; we hear them but don't really absorb them. Each new one -- the video of a hanged Marine colonel, for example -- is a new shock. "Yes, there is a world out there," we think, and the thought dissipates.

Bharati Mukherjee's novel, "Jasmine," is an artful and arresting distillation of these messages. A Hindu woman flees her family's poverty, and the Sikh terrorism that bloodies her village and claims her husband's life, and comes to the United States.

After a time in New York -- only a foreign eye could fix the world of the Upper West Side with such hilarious and revealing estrangement -- she moves to a small town in Iowa. In corn and hog country -- now prey to farm foreclosures and despair -- she marks with unsparing brilliance the symptoms of a new Third World.

"Iowa's dull and flat," a New York friend had protested when he heard she would move there. "So is Punjab," she replied. It could be the epigraph for this unsettling and finely written book. Jyoti -- moving westward, she becomes successively Jasmine and Jane -- is an immigrant of a different kind. Traditionally, America thinks of its immigrants coming here in order to be able to say: We are you. Jasmine's message is: You are us.

Mukherjee interweaves Jasmine's past and present, juxtaposing her childhood and growing-up in the Indian countryside with her time in New York and her present life in Iowa. The juxtaposition, which can be breathtaking, serves to fortify her comparisons. Her childhood took place amid the collapse of a millennial way of life. In Iowa, her symbol for America, a briefer but even more powerful way of life is ending.

Jasmine is a rebel; she will not get caught. She flees India and, at the end, she will flee Iowa. The book begins when, at age 7, she refuses to accept the prophesy of a holy man that she will become a widow and an exile. He swats her and she falls down, gouging a hole in her forehead. The scar, she insists, is her third eye, denoting wisdom.

She is precocious, beautiful and arrogant. She feels the pull of tradition, both in India and in Iowa; but she will not let it pull her down. She can look both ways -- her argument to a New York physicist for the submersion of the individual will in the mind of God is comedy that singes -- but ultimately she moves one way: On.

Her wavering, though, gives richness to the book. She defies her traditionalist father, who wants her to marry a farmer, and insists on studying. When she meets an independent-minded young man, Prakash, she marries him in totally untraditional fashion. In their brief married life, though, he must constantly challenge her to go beyond the customary role of the Indian wife.

After youth comes violence. Prakash is killed by a Sikh bomb on the eve of emigrating to the United States. Jasmine goes instead, entering Florida as an illegal. The ship captain who smuggles her in rapes her in a motel; she stabs him to death. The incident is horrifying but she tells it with fatalistic levelness. All experience is experience.

In New York, she gets a job as an au pair to a trendy academic couple. Taylor and Wylie treat her, to her astonishment, as a member of the family. Since their daughter, Duff, is enrolled in a progressive kindergarten, her duties are light. She finds herself earning $40 an hour as a language tutor to students in the Asian studies program at Columbia.

It is an idyll but, as she puts it: "In America nothing lasts." Wylie falls in love with Stuart, a Columbia colleague of Taylor's, while out Christmas-caroling on the upper West Side. "Taylor's such a sweetheart," she tells Jasmine, "and there's Duff and Stuart's three kids, but this is my chance at real happiness. What can I do? I've got to go for it, right?"

Jasmine might have stayed -- she and Taylor have fallen in love -- but one day she sees the Sikh, who killed her husband, selling ice cream in the park. It is a premonitory hint of Third World infiltration. She flies to Iowa; in America's heartland, she will surely be safe from history's turbulence.

But Iowa is not what it was. Desperate farmers attack bankers or commit suicide. Bud, a local banker, has come back from a trip to the Far East with a radically enlarged outlook. He is smitten with Jasmine, and she moves in with him after he leaves his wife. Not only that; they adopt Du, a Vietnamese refugee.

Jasmine tries to become "Jane," but it's no use. A farmer on whom Bud is foreclosing shoots him in the back. He is confined to a wheelchair; Jasmine must not only nurse him but offer him sexual relief in a highly complex fashion. "I will do it," she says stoically.

"Care-giver" is indeed part of her divided nature, but only part. When Taylor comes to get her -- it seems a hastily contrived ending -- she goes.

If Jasmine as a small and high-strung Goddess of Flux is comical in New York, she is devastating in Iowa. Her level voice delicately but relentlessly brings out the contradictions of a world trying in vain to resist or ignore the passing of its self-confidence.

Bud is the perfect symbol. He is all-American yet he has left his all-American wife to take an Indian mistress and adopt a Vietnamese son. He believes passionately in farming but forecloses on the farmers.

In Mukherjee's tale -- glib and a trifle haughty, sometimes, but always absorbing -- he is America itself; believing sincerely in its own good life and good intentions, yet crippled to paralysis by the retribution that life takes on any empire of faith that fails to move on with it.
