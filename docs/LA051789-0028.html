LITTLE HOOVER COMMISSION EXAMINES DFG'S EFFECTIVENESS

California's Little Hoover Commission, which monitors the efficiency of the state's governmental agencies, is examining the performance of the Department of Fish and Game.

The investigation, which was requested by a Northern California Assemblyman Stan Statham (R-Oak Run), started Monday at a hearing in Los Angeles.

DFG Director Pete Bontadelli, recovering from a back injury, was unable to attend, and his assistant, Paul Jensen, represented the department. Harold Cribbs, executive secretary of the DFG Commission, represented his group, which is appointed by the governor to set DFG policy.

Hoover Commission Chairman Nathan Shapell said he wants DFG Commission President Bob Bryant of Yuba City to testify at the next hearing June 27 in Sacramento.

"We will send him a very nice letter," Shapell said. "We do not like to issue subpoenas."

During Monday's session, the Hoover panel was primarily interested in how the DFG deals with commercial developments that it perceives to affect fish and wildlife. The first witness was housing developer Devere Anderson, who complained that "in recent years . . . there's been a dramatic change in policies and procedures of Fish and Game, and it seems as though there's been a new-found power that they're exerting to accomplish their goals and objectives. And I think that's what is causing some problems."

But the probing quickly turned to how the Fish and Game Commission, which sets DFG policy, correlates with the department, which implements policy.

Vice Chairman Haig Mardikian said he found it strange that Bontadelli's job is to follow the lead of a commission that has no power to fire him. The DFG director is appointed by the governor, as are the commissioners.

The Hoover members were suspicious that the commission, rather than determining policy, merely reacted to department recommendations. Mary Ann Chalker challenged Cribbs' testimony that the commission, with seven full-time employees, is able to monitor the 1,568 DFG employees around the state.

Asked about the commission's budget, Cribbs speculated that it was about $200,000 a year. Jeannine English, executive director of the Hoover panel, told Cribbs that it was $429,000, according to her information. Cribbs didn't dispute the figure.

Albert Gersten told Cribbs: "You say that on a day-to-day basis you keep track of all correspondence and what's going on in the state, yet you don't know your own budget."

The June 27 hearing will primarily focus on sportsmen's issues. The Hoover panel will then issue its report and recommendations to Gov. George Deukmejian and the state legislature.

Frank LoPreste couldn't find any yellowfin tuna to speak of at Clipperton Island off Costa Rica, and he met with only marginal success at the Revillagigedo Islands off mainland Mexico.

But two Orange County couples, fishing just off the coast of Mexico's Zihuatanejo, had little trouble locating the big yellowfins so popular among long-range fishermen.

"We caught six over a hundred pounds," said Linda Knowles of Costa Mesa, adding that 26 fish were caught throughout last week.

The biggest was a 187-pound yellowfin taken last Saturday after just an hour's boat ride. The smallest fish were in the 40-pound class.

LoPreste, meanwhile, on a 23-day trip aboard his Royal Polaris, found conditions at Clipperton to be "tough, tough, tough, with few fish and several sharks," according to a report he made to a fishing information telephone line.

Conditions weren't much better at the Revillagigedos, where his passengers will wind up what so far has been a less-than-memorable trip.

The State Fish and Game Commission recently adopted the 1989 hunting regulations and license forms should be available by June 1.

New hunts include:

-- The Camp Roberts antlerless deer hunt in Monterey County (Zone S-5, Aug. 28-Sept. 4).

-- Camp Pendleton either-sex deer hunt in San Diego County (S-10, Aug. 5-Sept. 24).

-- Santa Catalina Island either-sex deer hunt (S-29, Oct. 14-Jan. 31).

Changes in deer hunting regulations are few and indicated in the 1989 California Hunting Regulations, also due by June.

Citing concerns about the condition of pronghorn antelope herds, the number of tags has been cut from 645 to 355 and doe permits from 100 to 50.

Tule elk hunts, canceled by court order last year, will offer 40 permits for Bishop, 40 for Lone Pine and 15 for Cache Creek.
