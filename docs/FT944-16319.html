941014 FT 14 OCT 94 / Currency collapse fuels fear of higher inflation in Ukraine Ukraine's volatile currency, the karbovanets, has fallen sharply in recent days, losing 6 per cent of its value against dollar yesterday and 12 per cent since Monday, raising fears of higher inflation this winter. The karbovanets, also called the coupon, sold at 100,000 to the dollar on the open market yesterday, a 50 per cent fall since September 12. The currency's latest fall follows Ukraine's decision last Friday to abolish the government's artificial exchange rate and reopen free currency auctions. The move was the first step in a comprehensive economic reform programme announced by President Leonid Kuchma earlier this week, but, thanks to inflationary credits inherited from the previous administration, the initial impact of reform has been further destabilisation. Unlike the panic caused by the Russian rouble's sharp fluctuations, Ukrainians this week remained nonplussed about the latest devaluations. Public confidence in the karbovanets has never recovered from last year's hyper-inflation - when the annual inflation rate exceeded 10,000 per cent - and, although many purchases are made in karbovantsi, few Ukrainians hold their savings in the national currency. This autumn's devaluation stems largely from a massive credit release to agriculture this summer. The credits, authorised by parliament, derailed the Ukrainian central bank's tight monetary policy, which brought the inflation rate down to single digits during the first half of the year. As a result, commercial banks are releasing around Kbv5,000bn (Dollars 53.2m) onto the currency exchange market each week, according Mr Mykola Melnychuk, a Ukrainian national bank official. Economists expect inflation, up from 2.9 per cent for the month of August to 7.3 per cent for last month, to rise to 60 per cent for this month. The rise in inflation does not necessarily threaten the reform effort because the International Monetary Fund, set to approve a Dollars 360m loan by October 31, anticipated the karbovanets' decline and a resulting price hike. However, the Russian rouble's dramatic turns this week do pose a threat to Ukraine and other former Soviet neighbours. Ukraine and Belarus, among others, would be hit hard if Russian exporters compensated for the rouble's fall by raising prices. Many former Soviet republics depend heavily on imports from Russia. Mr Stanislav Bukovinsky, Ukraine's deputy finance minister, told Interfax news agency yesterday that Russian price increases for oil, coal and nuclear fuel might, like last year, push the country into hyperinflation. Half the country's imports are for Russian energy supplies, although gas - the largest purchase - is fixed at a low dollar rate. Ironically, before the collapse of the rouble zone in 1992 Moscow's reformers warned that other former Soviet republics where exporting inflation to Russia and thereby jeopardising Russian economic reform. Mr Bukovinsky said the rouble's impact on Ukraine simply underscores the need for quick economic restructuring to lessen dependence on Russian energy supplies.
