March 29, 1990, Thursday, P.M. Final

LOCKHEED CHAIRMAN VOWS CONCESSIONS;

TAKEOVER BID: PRESSURE FROM BIG SHAREHOLDERS PROMPTS OFFER IN MOVE TO BLOCK ATTACK ON BOARD.

Lockheed Corp. Chairman Daniel Tellep, bowing to pressure from his big shareholders, promised today at the aerospace company's annual meeting to make major concessions to them in the future.

The concessions came at the behest of big pension funds and other institutional shareholders, many of whom defected to the side of Harold Simmons, the Texas investor who bought 19% of Lockheed and wants to oust the company's current board and replace them with his nominees.

Results of voting at the meeting, taking place in a vast hangar, won't be known for at least a week.

Tellep said that he met with 70 institutions during the proxy contest over the last two weeks and that he considered this his "baptism by fire and a highly valuable experience."

"We believe in shareholder democracy," he said, offering the following concessions to prove it:

-- If there is significant support, as expected, for a resolution that voting by shareholders be confidential, Lockheed will develop such a procedure to be used in the future.

-- A provision to opt out of the strict anti-takeover provisions of law in Delaware, where Lockheed is incorporated. Tellep said even if that provision does not pass but there is a "significant" minority vote for it, he will recommend that the Lockheed board support an amendment at the next meeting to have Lockheed opt out of those provisions.

-- A promise that after the meeting he will ask for a list of shareholder representatives to Lockheed's 15-member board and will select up to three directors from that list. He earlier offered to select only one.

-- An offer to meet regularly with representatives of Simmons' NL Industries Inc. to discuss the future of Lockheed. "We hope the foregoing will provide recognition of our commitment to shareholder democracy," he said.

Also at the meeting, the president of NL Industries, J. Landis Martin, said again he believes that Simmons could better focus Lockheed on its core businesses -- aeronautics, space and missiles.

"We believe Lockheed must return to its roots," Martin said.

Simmons says Lockheed, with a recent history of sagging earnings and underestimating costs of developing new programs, must insist on better profits at its non-core businesses or sell them off.

The company portrays him as a corporate raider intent on personal profit, with no knowledge of how to run Lockheed, the nation's sixth-largest defense contractor with 1989 sales of $9.9 billion.

Simmons, who has lost $100 million on his $530-million investment in Lockheed, insists that he can better focus the company on profiting from its core businesses and pull up the share price for all investors.
