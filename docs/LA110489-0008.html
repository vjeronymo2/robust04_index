November 4, 1989, Saturday, Home Edition

WORLD WAR II MINORITY STUDENTS

I am writing to applaud the effort to obtain justice on the part of Hayao Shishino and the other Japanese Americans from Los Angeles High School who were denied diplomas in 1942 (Part A, Oct. 25). It is shocking that they were prevented from graduating then, but no less sickening was the way that Japanese Americans in general were deprived of their rights.

As a fellow alumnus (Los Angeles High, winter, 1943), I can recall the sorrow that many of us felt, some time after Pearl Harbor, when classmates and their families were being sent off to a camp for no other reason than that of ethnic prejudice, not felt, for example, toward Americans of German or Italian ancestry.

Those of us who were Jews (and we did know what was happening in Germany), were perhaps particularly sensitive to their plight, but felt helpless to do anything. I remember with great sadness the weeping of embarrassment of many of us.

Several years ago, when I was invited to lecture in Japan, I was pleased to mention some of my classmates by name (Yuki Mishima, Kimiko Naruse, Ruby Okubo, Louise Yamazaki), in the land of their ancestry, and to ask their forgiveness for what my/our country had done to them.

It is a source of satisfaction and healing for me to know that our government now recognizes our error and has agreed to rectify it. It is equally rewarding to see Shishino and the others standing up for their rights with both dignity and forbearance.

J. MARVIN SPIEGELMAN

Studio City
