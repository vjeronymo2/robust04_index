941205 FT 05 DEC 94 / Arts: Today's Television There was a golden age of childhood in the late Victorian and Edwardian periods, to judge from the great children's books of the time: E. Nesbit, Kenneth Grahame, JM Barrie, the greatest of them marked by an underlying sadness - perhaps at innocence lost or consciousness of how different the adult world be from the youthful ideal. At least it made a change from the days when children were considered little adults. The history of how we look at childhood, how sacrosanct, fleeting and vulnerable adults may have considered it, is the subject of The End of Childhood (11.15pm BBC2). The heavy-breathing Sarah Dunant, looking more than ever like the travesti caricature of a Late Show presenter, touches on such fascinating questions as whether present-day kids are actually young any more, what the nature of innocence is, or whether it ever existed. I suspect we have turned full circle and kids are little adults again; but this time on equal terms with the big ones.
