Language: Greek Article Type:BFN [Excerpts] Greek Prime Minister Andreas Papandreou and British Foreign Secretary Douglas Hurd concluded their meeting just a short time ago. The 30-minute meeting reportedly examined the latest developments in the Bosnia-Herzegovina crisis and on the issue of Skopje [The Former Yugoslav Republic of Macedonia--FYROM]. There were no comments following the meeting. This morning, the British foreign secretary had successive meetings with New Democracy Chairman Miltiadhis Evert, National Defense Minister Yerasimos Arsenis, and Foreign Minister Karolos Papoulias. The talks centered on Skopje, the situation in Bosnia-Herzegovina, and the Cyprus issue. During his meeting with British Foreign Secretary Hurd, the chairman of New Democracy outlined the Greek positions on Skopje and his proposal for peace in the Balkans. Evert also stressed the need to accelerate the dialogue on the Cyprus issue so that there will be a specific timetable for the solution of this national issue. During his meeting with the British foreign secretary, National Defense Minister Arsenis outlined the Greek positions regarding the meaning of the unified defense space [between Greece and Cyprus] as a factor in peace and security in the Mediterranean. Their meeting also examined the situation in the Balkans and Greek-Turkish relations. During their two-hour meeting this morning, Foreign Minister Papoulias and British Foreign Secretary Hurd discussed Skopje, Cyprus, and Greece's relations with the other Balkan countries. [passage omitted] Government spokesman Evangelos Venizelos, who was asked today to comment on a statement by British Foreign Secretary Hurd according to which the measures against Skopje are illegal, reiterated that the measures adopted by the Greek Government have a political character. He added that the measures have a political aim of security and stability in the area. The spokesman said that yesterday, the Greek Government finally submitted to the responsible European Union committee the legal arguments on the issue, which, he said, are of secondary importance to the Greek Government.
