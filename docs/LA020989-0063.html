February 9, 1989, Thursday, Home Edition

FOOD AND COMMON SENSE: THE BUILDING BLOCKS OF NUTRITION, THE EIGHT ESSENTIAL AMINO ACIDS

The caricature of early man is of a brawny fellow with a huge club, dressed in animal skins and gnawing on a bone. While it's true that our ancient ancestors did engage in hunting, we know they were not the carnivores portrayed in cartoons.

Roots, berries, leaves, nuts, eggs and fruits were staples of their diet. And such game as they did catch was far leaner than the farm-grown meats we eat today.

The 20th-Century stereotype of cave men perpetuates the myth that meat is power. Eating large quantities is linked in many people's minds to surging growth, bulging muscles and athletic prowess.

To be sure, meat is rich in protein and is a vital nutrient. Carbohydrates, fats and proteins can all provide us with energy, but only protein has the capacity to build, repair and maintain the body's tissues, including those in the kidneys, liver and skin. Proteins are components of enzymes and of certain hormones, and are needed to make blood cells and infection-fighting antibodies, as well as for growing hair and nails. Proteins are part of the carpentry that helps keep the body erect; it is the grout that holds the cells together.

Like other constituents of the body, proteins eventually get used up and must be replaced. The body constructs its own proteins from those in the foods we eat. Every animal, vegetable or microbe has special types of proteins, so there are countless kinds, designed to meet the needs of particular organisms. These millions of proteins are all formed from only 20 different building blocks -- amino acids -- put together in different ways, just as words are made from the alphabet or structures from a Lego set.

Our body's task is to take the proteins of other organisms and transform them into types we can use to repair and maintain our cells. By breaking down the proteins in foods into their component amino acids, and then reassembling them, our bodies can manufacture some of the amino acids and from them some of the proteins. These are called "nonessential" because we do not have to get them ready-made in foods.

But there are eight amino acids we cannot make from raw materials and that must be included in our diets. These "essential amino acids" have to be present in the foods we eat or we will be placed in nutritional jeopardy.

Here's where meat comes in. Animal foods most closely resemble the proteins in our bodies. They contain all the amino acids in the right proportions, which makes them "of highest biologic value." A smaller, overall amount of protein of high biological value will give us the same amount of amino acids (and thus overall usable protein) as a large amount of a "poor" protein, like those in plant foods.

Does that mean we should don cave-man clothes and go out hunting for meat? Absolutely not. While meat certainly provides generous amounts of amino acids, we don't need to depend exclusively on high-quality proteins (some of which are laden with fat and/or cholesterol) to meet our amino-acid needs. Habitually, we eat a variety of foods containing proteins of differing qualities. The daily Recommended Dietary Allowance is 56 grams (or two ounces) of pure protein for a man and 46 grams (1.6 ounces) for a non-pregnant, non-nursing woman.

These quotas are not that hard to fill. One cup of milk, for instance, has nine grams of protein. An ounce of ground beef has seven, an egg has six, and one tablespoon of peanut butter, four. A woman, then, can get nearly 40% of her protein requirement from two cups of milk. Add an egg and three ounces of meat, fish or poultry, and she'll have all the protein she needs for the day.

Many other foods are also good protein sources. A half-cup of oatmeal has three grams, as does one slice of whole-wheat bread. A cup of spinach has five grams, a stalk of broccoli six. One medium baked potato has three grams -- and the quality is impressive too.

A very small amount of protein of high quality that contributes the missing essential amino acids can be mixed in to upgrade the quality of "poor" proteins. For instance, cereals are generally low in the amino acid lysine, whereas milk contains ample amounts. When we pour milk on breakfast cereal, we create a complete protein.

A meal that supplements rice with beans or combines the two with small portions of fish, poultry or other meat is an excellent model of this mutual supplementation of protein.

Simply by virtue of the variety of foods consumed, the typical American diet provides all the protein -- and thus the essential amino acids -- we require, and more. So if you eat a varied diet, your protein profile is one thing you need not worry about.

Don't Depend on Herb Teas as Substitutes for Medical Care

Question: I have a friend who insists that in the interests of my health I should switch from regular tea and coffee to herb teas. Is this really necessary?

Answer: From our perspective, a more sensible course might be to use all three in moderation. While herb teas may convey a sense of the "natural" (though they are no more so than coffee or tea), the fact is that herbs contain a variety of compounds that are extracted when the herbs are steeped in hot water. Some of these compounds are potentially harmful.

Drinking herb teas to avoid the known effects of caffeine may expose individuals to chemicals about which we know far less. Moreover, herb teas have not been subjected to animal studies that simulate the effects of drinking tea.

And despite claims of benefit in the popular press, it is never a good idea to depend on herb teas as substitutes for medical care.

Q: For the most part we eat plain fruit for dessert, but every few weeks I bake a fruit tart as a special treat. I would prefer to make the crust out of oil instead of solid shortening, but so far have been unable to find a recipe. Since I don't have time to experiment on my own, can you help me out?

A: We can. For a simple crust, mix 1 1/3 cups flour, 1/2 teaspoon salt, 1/3 cup oil and three tablespoons cold milk. Then mix and roll.

In a study done several years ago, trained panelists found that vegetable oils produced better flavor and more flakiness than crust made with solid shortening. No color differences among the crusts were detected, and the only negative observation was that crusts made with solid shortening shrunk less than the ones made with vegetable oils.

Q: I have been put on diuretic medication and advised to eat lots of fruits and vegetables to get extra potassium. Can you provide me with specific information about how to get good sources into my diet?

A: A large glass of orange juice and a banana sliced over breakfast cereal are two good ways to build up your potassium intake. But there are many more. Among the best vegetable sources are winter squash, dandelion and mustard greens, and cooked kidney beans. Baked potatoes provide generous amounts, and so do sweet potatoes, broccoli, Brussels sprouts, cabbage, cauliflower, corn, beets, summer squash and spinach. In addition to oranges and bananas, fruits that contain the greatest amounts include cantaloupe, watermelon and grapefruit.

And remember that other fruits and vegetables may not contribute as much, but they do add to the total. So the advice to eat plenty of fruits and vegetables of all types really is the key to ensuring that you get your needed potassium, along with goodly amounts of other vitamins and minerals.
