VIEWPOINTS;

IS CONSUMERISM A REBEL CAUSE?;

PUBLIC OPINION WATCHERS SEE A MOVEMENT BUT AREN'T SURE IT CAN BE CALLED A REVOLT

On several fronts, consumerism appears to be on the rise. Consumers have protested everything from the handling of the Exxon Valdez oil spill to the rise of "reality-based" television to the use of pesticides. Earlier this month, another blow was struck for consumerism when the U.S. Supreme Court upheld most of California's Proposition 103 initiative to tighten regulation of the insurance industry. Does all of this signal the start of a powerful and widespread consumer revolt? What's influencing the changing mood of consumers, and what are its implications? Times researcher Melanie Pickett raised these questions with various people who keep a close eye on public opinion, and excerpts of their comments follow.

Andrew Kohut, president of the Gallup Organization:

"The most important thing to recognize is that a Populist attitude toward American business is a fundamental American value. Americans consistently believe that companies have profits that are way too large, and feel that large companies have too much power. And when they see incidents such as Valdez, or they read about pesticide pollution or what have you, it strikes a chord that resonates with the public. They are situations that tap into people's general distrust of business. Whether consumerism will become once again a very focused, crystallized movement in the way it was in the early '70s, I don't know. I don't have any evidence of that. . . .

"One of the important values that animates public opinion is the suspicion of business. . . . What our studies show is that the feeling is greater among women than it is among men, that women tend to be consumerist to a far greater degree than men, and that older people are much more suspicious of the motives and power of American business than younger people. So if you're going to find a rebirth of consumerism, it is probably not going to be among young people. It's going to be among middle-aged and older people, and it's probably going to be among women."

Paul Ambrosino, a San Francisco political consultant:

"Just watching debate, listening to candidates and looking at referendums, I think it would be an overstatement to suggest that a consumer revolt is out there in the making. You may get some initial reactions, such as an AK-47 ban, that will dominate public policy as a dramatic event takes place as in Stockton. And who knows if you'll see some sort of similar type initiative as a result of the (Exxon Valdez oil spill) or an incident like that.

"I think what's going on in places like California is that some of the traditional (consumer issues) that have been debated for a long, long time without being resolved are going to end up more and more on the ballot. (People are) figuring out that what they can't get in the legislature, they can put before consumers and voters through initiatives. The successful passage of the insurance proposition is likely to whet the appetite of activists to go after other items they have not been able to achieve in the legislative process. I'm not sure I think that's a consumer revolt, I think it's the interests taking the battleground to a different arena.

"What I think you see in the citizens out there is that they are receptive to voting in favor of those types of measures. . . . Until consumers sense they lose by passing one of these, you can always find people interested in giving themselves a break at the expense of a large institution or company."

Craig Gordon, director of Grassroots Research, which tracks new products and trends:

"The consumer is getting more active. I don't know if it's a revolt yet. . . . Manufacturers have become real sensitive to it. Almost every consumer product now has an "800" complaint line. A lot of manufacturers have discovered they'd rather have the person vent the frustration at them instead of going to other avenues, whether it's government or boycotting the product or whatever. There's a lot more choices and a lot less brand loyalty than there was before. . . . I think businesses are becoming more aware of how important it is to get the consumer in the decision-making process."

Dave Stewart, marketing professor at the USC Graduate School of Business:

"If you look at the whole history of the consumer movement in the U.S., it has tended to go in cycles. And I think we've come through a cycle where there has been a de-emphasis on regulation, a de-emphasis on protecting the consumer. Consumers have been more dormant.

"We are probably now moving into another up cycle because of a host of things that have come to the fore: concerns about the oil spill, concerns about general increases in the price of oil, concerns about pesticides, we could go on and on. These things are now beginning to receive more play in the press, they receive a receptive ear. In addition, the Bush Administration seems to be more open to regulation so that the Federal Trade Commission, among others, has shown evidence of beginning to become more active. They've been pushed somewhat as well by more activist groups of state attorneys general. I think it's fair to say we're seeing an upsurge. How far it will go I think remains to be seen.

"What you'll begin to see -- (and) already are beginning to see -- are efforts on the part of industry to tell its story so that the people have more information, more correct information. You'll see companies running more campaigns that document what they're doing to clean up the environment. And there are a number of companies which are well known already for that kind of campaign -- Weyerhaeuser has for years talked about the number of trees it plants as opposed to the number they chop down. You'll hear more of that so that business gets across the idea that there is another side to the story, that they do try to behave responsibly."

Michael Cunningham, associate professor of psychology at the University of Louisville:

"Look at demographics -- that's the first issue. One of the facts of political life is that the baby boom generation that was always somewhat cynical toward institutions is becoming more politically potent. The second aspect is that the education level in America, although incredibly uneven in its distribution, is gradually creeping upward. There are more college-educated people who tend to be skeptical of what is offered to them.

"Those two highly correlated demographic facts mean there are opportunities for people to be engaged in political activities on those things that touch their lives.

"Now, regulation gets to be a very tricky issue. One of the interesting facts is those people who are cynical about business are also cynical about government. One fragment goes for greater regulation and that will be continuing to increase. But another group probably has two types in its numbers -- one are the Reagan 'true believers' who believe in deregulation and don't believe in government intrusion, and a second group that is cynical about government and prefers to handle things on the local level. Some people will say, for example, 'Let's regulate apples. Make sure there's no Alar.' The other group will say, 'Well, let's just buy organic and let's not waste our time trying to move through Congress a series of regulations.'

"A consumer movement is going to, I suspect, be a very fragmented thing. Different types of industries will encounter different types of pressure groups because behind the consumer movement are a series of very different kinds of people with very different kinds of agendas."

Gary Flint, director of Consumer Protection Agency, a nonprofit foundation:

"California has probably the best consumer protection laws in the country. What concerns a lot of consumers is not that we have inadequate laws, it's just that it's not possible to enforce every law in every case.

"In general, if you have more regulation than we have now, it could hurt the consumer. . . . We have to retrain our thought to be more goal oriented than legislation oriented. The government became too big, in many ways, in the '60s, which actually hurt the consumers. It raised taxes and increased the size of government. . . . If you were to ask people across the country what they think about consumerism today as opposed to the way it was several years ago, I think they'd say there is somewhat of a voter revolt afoot."

Ed Zwick, co-producer of the television show "thirtysomething":

"Deregulation in a lot of areas isn't immediately perceived but I think it clearly is now beginning to be, and its implications for consumer issues are pretty obvious. The real interesting question over the next number of years will be whether what now seems to be a grass-roots sense of indignation will translate into any real shift in policy politically. Has consumerism been enough of an issue to sway electoral politics and legislation? Not in recent memory. . . . Will it become more important? I feel certain candidates (are) beginning to position themselves as the beneficiaries of what they perceive to be a movement."
