June 25, 1990, Monday, P.M. Final

LIZ SMITH: CHUTZPAH PAYS OFF: MANDELA'S AUTOBIOGRAPHY

THE PUBLISHING world is agog over the clever and efficient manner in which Little, Brown acted to snag the Nelson Mandela autobiography. Hardly was the South African revolutionary out of prison last February when editor-in-chief Bill Phillips was winging his way to Johannesburg in a real cloak-and-dagger maneuver. People who called Bill's office in Boston were told that even his staff had no idea where he was.

Upon his return to South Africa, Mandela will fall to and really begin work on this book. There is, as yet, no title for this tome, but Little, Brown calls it "The Memoir." They hope to publish in 1992.

BOY GEORGE just headlined a benefit in L.A. for a center that deals with the emotional problems of young drug addicts. Show biz in general seems to have slammed the door and forgotten the British star since his own headlines of arrest and drug addiction. But the fact is that it was Boy George who caused the California benefit to sell out. He also won raves from American critics.

He sang live to prerecorded backing and sounded amazing. His new songs are his best to date. And he ended using the Hare Krishna Temple Choir for a backup. They are saying the young talent is now more spiritually attuned and his next album for Virgin Records will be titled "Jesus Loves You."

ELIZABETH TAYLOR acted against doctor's orders and the wishes of her own four children when she insisted on appearing at the AIDS conference in San Francisco. Do the weekly tabloids, who had the star dead and buried, experience even a twinge of shame when they see this living legend emerge Phoenix-like from her illness and come out of the hospital? Naaaaa!

SUZY HAS written lots about record tycoon David Geffen's buying of the Jack and Ann Warner house and grounds in Beverly Hills. Here are a few additions to that news. Geffen considers his $47-million purchase to have been "a steal." You can subtract big bucks from the purchase price because David is selling the furniture and many of the contents at auction. He could make as much as $9 million on same.

Already Geffen sold to Warner Bros. the collection of Jack Warner's eight movie Oscars and all his leather-bound film scripts. Geffen earned $3 million from this sale alone. So take $12 million from $47 million and the buy is costing the golden boy $35 million.

Geffen will keep the room in the house designed by the famous architect William Kent. This decor is so famous it once appeared on the front page of The New York Times.
