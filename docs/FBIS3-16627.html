Language: English Article Type:BFN [Vassan Georgiyev commentary] [Text] Foreign Minister of Pakistan Asif Ahmad Ali has charged the United States with supplying outdated weapons to the UN peacekeeping force in Somalia. Together with his country's Defense Minister, Asif Ahmad Ali visited Somalia to inspect the Pakistani contingent of the UN force. Now our commentary by Vassan Georgiyev: The United States and the other Western countries want to withdraw their troops from Somalia by the end of this month. The UN force remaining in Somalia will consist of troops of Afro-Asian countries only. The Pakistani contingent will be the largest, more than 5,000 people. As the Pakistani foreign minister says, the United Nations troops remaining in Somalia would be left with outdated weapons, the Cobra helicopters, which have served their time, old models of tanks, and other military equipment. Asif Ahmad Ali also called attention to the shortage of drugs and dressings. The Pakistani foreign minister described as racist such an approach to the United Nations peacekeepers staying in Somalia. He accused the United States of contempt of the lives and health of servicemen from Pakistan and other states of Asia and Africa.The racism charges were immediately rejected by the United States Defense Department and Washington agreed to consider the issue of replacing outdated weapons. At this point one may consider the matter settled. Yet one might consider at the same time whether out-of-date weapons were the only source of the Pakistani foreign minister's irritation. Until recently, as it received large amounts of weapons from the United States, Islamabad could hardly afford to criticize the Pentagon publicly. Yet the United States embargo on arms shipments to Pakistan following its attempts to create a nuclear bomb of its own, has changed Islamabad's policy toward Washington. It should not be ruled out that as he criticizes Washington for leaving outdated military equipment for the Pakistani contingent in Somalia, the foreign minister of Pakistan wants to remind Washington of the problem of his country's army in general; it is no secret that it is still equipped largely with American weapons but these weapons were manufactured in the '70s. Islamabad wants the United States to resume military aid to Pakistan, including the F-16 fighter bombers and other latest weapons. It appears that the foreign minister of Pakistan took advantage of the participation of the country's contingent in the United Nations peacekeeping forces to remind Washington of the needs of the Pakistani Army once again.
