BFN [Report by Francisco Valeriano Giron; from "The Morning Paper" newscast] [Text] After participating in the Fourth Ibero-American Summit of Heads of State and Government, Honduran President Carlos Roberto Reina returned to the country very optimistic. The economic arena was the predominant subject at this meeting of presidents. President Reina said that Central America has much to gain and can even obtain some benefits in keeping with the other economic blocs that have been formed. He was asked about the benefits that Honduras and the Central American region, in particular, can obtain from this meeting and replied: [Begin Reina recording] Central America's treatment in trade treaties vis-a-vis the other economic blocs. For example, in the face of NAFTA, all of Central America is endeavoring to obtain parity. This means that in the Caribbean Basin and in Central America we can achieve integration with the area that encompasses Mexico, the United States, and Canada. That is a very specific point. Another specific point is the capitalization of the Central American Bank of Economic Integration [BCIE] with extraregional partners. This will permit the bank to dedicate a large amount of funds to our country's development, affording the treatment that international law, trade, and integration can provide. It is an asymmetrical treatment. This means that the same conditions are not going to be required of all the partners. For example, for Central American products and Honduran products, particularly, asymmetry would mean that products that are not strictly of Honduran origin can leave the country, because part of the raw materials come from abroad. That asymmetry would help enormously within the old concept of Central American development, which is a form of balanced development. This does not mean that the most developed countries are going to wait for the less developed. No, the development of the less developed countries is expedited in order to balance them all off later in a big push toward development. Those specific points are very important and very encouraging in our country's development. [end recording] President Reina also noted that another important achievement was the fact that the BCIE achieved more capitalization through extraregional partners. Also, he met with Commander Fidel Castro. We asked him what he discussed with Castro. [Begin Reina recording] It was strictly a courtesy call. I was accompanied by four or five persons when receiving him. Our talks were very general. He was very interested in learning about Honduras. He recalls that Jose Marti visited Honduras. He remembers that patriot Gomez was also in Honduras. That offers us the perspective that we are neighbors of Cuba at a time when the Inter-American system should resolve that problem. I had the opportunity to present this on behalf of Central America. For me the case of Cuba, which has been sanctioned by the OAS, should be resolved within the OAS. That is the appropriate forum for resolving this problem. This holds true especially now that a great Colombian, President Cesar Gaviria, is now the secretary general-elect of the OAS. He can again provide a golden era for that continental organization, as was provided by his illustrious predecessor [Former Colombian President] Alberto Lleras Camargo. Let there be no doubt, it was a courtesy visit by a chief of state to another chief of state on the occasion of a summit. Second, the solution of the Cuban problem is through the OAS. Those two points cover your question. [end recording]
