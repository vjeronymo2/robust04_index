December 6, 1989, Wednesday, Home Edition

TREND WATCH

A roundup of business developments spotted by other publications.

Managing Travel: As travel costs continue to escalate, more companies are hiring travel managers, up from less than 10% in 1982 to 30% in 1988. These managers work with travel agencies to guarantee the best bargains in lodging and transportation. The big hotels and major car rental agencies are open to volume discount negotiation, and the airlines, while not admitting it openly, will also "work with" companies for lower fares. Midway Airlines, in a bold departure from implicit policy, announced publicly in July that it will provide, in its words, "long-term packages with discounts based on travel volume." Now that the cards are on the table, and as prices continue to rise, more companies are likely to get into the game by hiring their own travel managers, and then the airlines really will have to deal. (Tampa (Fla.) Tribune)

Circuit City Expands: In the midst of an industrywide slowdown, Circuit City is expanding rapidly in the Philadelphia area. The area's first four stores opened in November, and another four are scheduled to open within a year. The retailer of electronic items hopes eventually to have 15 stores in the Northeast, from Philadelphia to Atlantic City, where company insiders expect Circuit City to gain 15% to 20% of the market. Despite existing chain competition, Circuit City looks to its wide selection, knowledgeable sales force and slick presentation to help establish the chain in the Northeast. (Philadelphia Inquirer)

Encyclopedia on a Cassette: Franklin Computer Corp., the company that gave us the electronic Bible and other hand-held, computerized reference works, has plans for a new video tome. It has acquired the rights to the Concise Columbia Encyclopedia, a single-volume work, which it expects to produce electronically as the first of its kind. Marketing of the new reference work will follow tried-and-true channels -- mail order, specialty department stores and college bookstores. No date has yet been set for its debut. (Newark (N.J.) Star Ledger)

Trend Toward Premium Liquor: Looking for a holiday gift for the person who has everything? Brown-Forman is hoping you'll turn to its bottled libations, particularly its pricier premium liquors such as Gentleman Jack, for the answer. Retailing at about $20 a bottle, the premium whiskey is targeted at 25- to 45-year-olds and those who invest some ego in their liquor purchases. Brown-Forman is counting on status-seeking consumers to pull this beverage out to impress friends with the taste as well as the price. The company hopes that the trend toward premium liquors will continue to pick up steam as exports grow and as aging baby boomers pick up dad's drink of choice, as long as it's a choice drink. (New Orleans Times-Picayune)

The Pizza Push: In their race to catch up with the burger biggies, pizza purveyors are striking out in new directions. A pizza chain in the Southwest, Nunzio's, has developed and built its first Nunzio's Express in Albuquerque, N.M. This drive-up outlet, which takes up about as much space as a Fotomat booth, promises a $1.79 pizza in one minute. Outlets are planned for Denver and Phoenix for next year. At the same time, Pizza Hut has taken the cue from McDonald's and entered the arena -- literally. McNichols Sports Arena in Denver is the first stadium to sell Pizza Hut products, with Detroit and Cleveland next on the list. Although it's not expected to be extremely profitable, the exposure will be worth millions. (Rocky Mountain News, Nation's Restaurant News)

Money for Mementos: Baseball cards are a booming business these days, and the cards you kept in a shoe box under the bed may be worth a lot more than the sentiment attached to them. Collecting the cards is the fastest-growing hobby in the United States, and 30% of card purchases are made by adults, which is one reason prices are skyrocketing. The card for San Francisco Giant Will Clark when he was a rookie, for instance, went for a nickel in 1986 and now sells for $26. Topps, the biggest name in baseball cards and the company that brings you Bazooka bubble gum, will introduce its Topps magazine in January, a quarterly aimed at the 2 million serious collectors in the country. (Chicago Tribune)
