July 16, 1989, Sunday, Home Edition

ALICE KAHN: A BIRTHDAY PARTY TAKES THE CAKE

Like a mythic figure somewhere between Mother Goose and the Good Witch of the North, Dear Abby came for us. "What should I call you?" asked my 10-year-old daughter, Hannah. "Should I call you Abby or Dear?"

"Call me Abby, honey," said Pauline Esther (Popo) Friedman Phillips, a.k.a. Abigail Van Buren. She sat not in a golden coach or a pumpkin shell but in a huge white stretch limo with smoked-glass windows and a bar and a phone and a television set and a VCR.

"This is so cool!" Hannah whispered in my ear as we drove off into the Beverly Hills sunset.

But what do you talk about with Dear Abby? Perhaps I should just turn to her and say what's on my mind:

DEAR ABBY: My mother died before my daughter Hannah was born. She was such an indulgent grandma, always sending my older daughter, Emma, beautiful little dresses and toys, even if she couldn't afford them. I know it sounds irrational, but I keep dreaming she'll come back and do something special for Hannah. Am I nuts?

FAIRY-TALE BELIEVER

Abby had written to me. Not because I wrote to her, but out of the blue -- wonderful letters saying kind things about my column. I called and asked to take her to lunch, but she was busy churning out the six columns a week a working girl must do to pay the limo bill. Now, Hannah and I are her dinner guests. We follow her into an elegant French restaurant. I realize that Emma will kill me for this later.

Rows of young waiters, like a Busby Berkeley chorus line, fall in with, "Good evening, Mrs. Phillips" and "Nice to see you, Abby." We are seated in her private dining room, which looks out over the dimly lit scene. "This way, we can still watch the couples smooching," she says.

I'm reminded that she was no sob sister when she entered the old boys' world of journalism in the '50s. She made it on saucy one-liners, like her response to the reader who wrote, "What's the difference between a wife and a mistress?" "Night and day," Her Dearness responded.

Perhaps others, besides her hairdresser, know the secret of how Abby, who turned 71 on the Fourth of July, still looks like a girl of 50. As she sits between Hannah and me, I note her blue-and-white silk dress and matching coat, her pearl-and-diamond necklace, her purple pumps and purple Chanel bag, and the perfect red nails on her tiny white hands.

Hannah struggles to eat the huge filet mignon, even after she has brushed aside the yucky stuff called foie gras. I am enjoying my duck in raspberry sauce, which at 24 bucks a pop ain't chopped liver. I'm acquainted with Abby's biography because my older daughter had done a report last year on the famous Sioux City twins -- Abby and her sister, Esther Pauline (Eppie) Friedman Lederer, a.k.a. Ann Landers.

I tell Abby that we have a few things in common besides the fact that her dad from Minsk and my dad from Pinsk came to Midwestern America and opened movie theaters. Like her, I didn't start writing a column until my late 30s and, like her, I began with the San Francisco Chronicle.

"When I went to the Chronicle, I didn't even have a Social Security number," Abby says. "I said I'd gladly do it for free, but they insisted I be paid."

I suggest, "They'd take it for free today."

Every question I ask Abby turns out to be a cliche. "What did you think of Nathanael West's 'Miss Lonelyhearts'?"

"Honey, everyone always asks me that."

Hannah asks the questions nobody has ever asked before: "Are those real diamonds? . . . Is that really your chauffeur? . . . Are you friends with any movie stars that you like?"

Singing waiters arrive bearing a special chocolate Charlotte cake with a candle in it for Hannah's "birthday," which was actually three months ago. It is just the kind of dramatic deed my mother would have done.

Sated with cake, Hannah announces: "This is the fanciest restaurant I have ever been to. This is the longest dinner I have ever sat through."

We leave. The coach awaits us. The chauffeur-cum-bodyguard takes us to Rodeo Drive and follows us as we stroll. Cameras roll as celebs and long-legged ladies enter a Leroy Neiman gallery opening. A few doors down, there's another bash for an artist whose oeuvre consists of paintings of Brooke Shields on motorcycles. A man gets out of his Rolls-Royce and says to us, "Three girls out on the town?"

Abby takes Hannah's hand and leads her up Rodeo on a docent's tour through this big-ticket Disneyland. Then she kisses us good night and sends us to bed.

Later, I tell Hannah, "I wish I hadn't had wine. It seemed like such a dream."

"But it was like a dream to me too," she says. "Did you see her rings? We're talking big ones."

Hannah may remember the sparkly things -- the chandelier in the restaurant and Abby's diamond earrings. But when I think back to that night in Beverly Hills, I'll remember Mama.
