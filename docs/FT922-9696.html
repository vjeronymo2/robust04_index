920506 FT 06 MAY 92 / Wary traders see signs of modest recovery THE HESITANT twitchings of life in high streets across the country since the general election have been encouraged by yesterday's cut in interest rates. Mr James May, director-general of the British Retail Consortium, which represents nine tenths of the retail sector, sees modest recovery in most sectors. He says: 'It is hard to say what the figures are with certainty but there does seem to have been a sustained pick-up in demand over the past two to three weeks.' Special factors cloud the picture: last April's increase in VAT to 17.5 per cent from April 1, this year's late Easter, the uncertainty caused by the election, and the recent fine weather. Yet confidence is recovering, although no one expects a rapid switch in mood. Mr Brian Stewart, chief executive of Scottish & Newcastle, the brewing, retail and leisure group, said: 'As yet, we have seen only spasmodic signs of recovery and I do not think anyone should expect a sudden surge in consumer confidence and spending - but this is a positive and timely move.' Mr Terry Maher, chief executive of Pentos, which runs Dillons bookshops and Ryman stationery shops, predicts only an 'anaemic' recovery. 'People's confidence has been shattered and that does not change suddenly or dramatically,' he says. 'In my view there is nothing more important than that interest rates are now brought down as rapidly as possible. There will be no sustained improvement in consumer demand unless and until that happens.' Other voices still urge restraint. Mr May, of the BRC, says: 'Many retailers would like to see a bigger and quicker reductions in interest rates, but the cautious approach is right in the long run. It is vitally important that interest rate cuts are sustainable and sustained.' That largely depends on what happens elsewhere in Europe. As Mr Derek Bowden, media director at the Saatchi & Saatchi advertising agency, says: 'Until the German economy has fully resolved its problems, this does not herald a significant move for the UK economy.'
