BFN [Report by "eli/mes": "Geissler Warns of Growing Nationalism"] [Text] Heiner Geissler, acting chairman of the Christian Democratic Union [CDU] faction, has issued a warning about increasing nationalism and a return to thinking in nationalist terms. In an interview with SUEDDEUTSCHE ZEITUNG, he said that nationalist thinking has moved to the forefront since unification. But, he said, Germany's future lies not in the reestablishment of a national state, but in opening up to other ideas, and in the creation of a federal European structure. "A national state is too little for big things and too big for little things," he said. With his statements, Geissler confirmed similar opinions by CDU politicians recently expressed at the traditional "Roemeberg" talks in Frankfurt. The CDU had reacted with some concern to these opinions because Wolfgang Schaeuble, CDU/Christian-Social Union [CSU] chairman, wrote in his book that the nation's thoughts must be filled with vitality to prevent society from drifting apart. Schaueble's book is to be presented by Chancellor Kohl this Monday [20 Jun]. Geissler said that although he agrees with Schaueble that society is splitting up into groups, "nationalist feelings as a collective ethnic emotion cannot be considered the basis of collective responsibility. One must love one's neighbor, not one's people." "The citizens of East Berlin and Leipzig did not create the revolution in 1989 to reestablish the nationalist state, but to push through democracy, freedom, and justice," Geissler continues. "But, in 1990, the center of gravity changed. Nationalist feelings moved to the forefront." Although equality and justice still form the mainstays of internal unity, "we will never attain them by means of nationalist feelings, but by means of a feeling of responsibility toward one's fellow man," said Geissler. Geissler does not think the CDU is full of nationalist sentiment. "Were it not for Helmut Kohl, the situation would certainly have been more difficult," he says. Geissler does not think it is necessary to seek an identity in Germany. "That is an artificial task. Nationalist feelings are good only if they are premeditated and restrained. The idea of nationalism has been discredited, and rightly so. Nationalism is an ideological plague, just like fundamentalism, fascism, and communism." Geissler calls for "unity, justice, and freedom, instead of Germany above all else. When the flag flutters in the air, one's mind lies in the trumpet that blows the fanfare," he says. "The patrons of the new Germany are not Kaiser Wilhelm or Otto von Bismarck, but Konrad Adenauer, Willy Brandt, Richard von Weizsaecker, and Helmut Kohl."
