920817 FT 17 AUG 92 / Tecs sounded out over possible cuts CHAIRMEN of the 82 Training and Enterprise Councils are being canvassed individually by the government about their spending priorities should there be large-scale cuts in their budgets next year. The Department of Employment is seeking full information on Tecs' response to possible cuts before Mrs Gillian Shephard, the employment secretary, meets Mr Michael Portillo, the chief secretary to the Treasury, at the end of the month to discuss next year's spending. The canvassing of a broad swathe of opinion is indicative of the new relationship that Mrs Shephard has tried to forge with Tecs. She may have no more to offer them in practical terms than Mr Michael Howard, her predecessor, but she has appeared keen to listen to their concerns and, in doing so, appears to have improved the relationship with them. This may be vital in preventing large-scale resignations among members of Tec boards, most of whom are business executives. Many joined Tecs with ambitions to transform the UK's skills base and may be unwilling to remain on boards if training programmes were severely pared. One Tec chief executive said the employment department was indirectly inviting comment on whether directors would leave should there be cuts. Tecs have said that they want more flexibility in how they spend their budgets. Mrs Shephard has told Tecs she would try to get them three-year instead of annual funding, on the same lines as the new hospital trusts. Mr Rodney Skidmore, chief executive of the Central England Tec, said: 'Government has to be very clear about what it wants from Tecs and then provide sufficient resources to provide that. If it makes cuts, government then has to cut its cloth appropriately. We must not expand the volume of training at the expense of quality.'
