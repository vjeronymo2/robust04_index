SUMMARY Pyongyang has used the occasion of rejecting a UN statement on the North Korean nuclear inspection issue to reinforce its earlier hints of a willingness to negotiate with the International Atomic Energy Agency (IAEA) on the issue. However, North Korea also warned that should the United States persists in applying "pressure" on the DPRK, it would resume reprocessing activities. END SUMMARY Pyongyang seems to be underscoring an earlier hint that it might be willing to try to resolve its differences with the IAEA in talks with the IAEA itself. This new hint of openness to DPRK- IAEA talks came in a 4 April DPRK-Foreign Ministry spokesman's statement ostensibly designed to reject a statement by the president of the UN Security Council calling on the DPRK to allow IAEA inspectors to complete their unfinished inspection activities in North Korea (Pyongyang radio, 4 April). While accusing the UN Security Council of "politicizing" the North Korean nuclear issue, the spokesman's statement contended that, whatever "differences of views" that may have arisen in the course of recent IAEA inspections of North Korean nuclear facilities, they should be settled "strictly between" the IAEA and the "inspected country." The 4 April North Korean statement, in line with most earlier North Korean pronouncements on the nuclear issue, also contained a threat that seemed designed to show that Pyongyang will not back down under U.S. pressure. Thus the statement accused the United States and "some Western powers" of using the UN Security Council as a forum to apply pressure on Pyongyang, claiming that the Security Council based its discussions on the "unjust and biased" findings of the IAEA Board of Governors. Once again blaming Washington for "deliberately aggravating" the situation in its quest to "crush" Pyongyang, the statement warned that Washington's "total reliance" on pressure tactics has given Pyongyang "no choice but to conduct" unspecified "peaceful nuclear activities" that it had "unilaterally frozen" in order to promote high-level bilateral talks with Washington. Those nuclear activities presumably include the refueling of the controversial 5-megawatt reactor in Yongbyon, with resultant spent fuel made available for plutonium extraction. The statement, however, failed to call for concrete action on anyone's part.(SEE NOTE) (NOTE: By contrast, the 10 April 1993 Foreign Ministry spokesman's statement that was issued in reply to an 8 April 1993 UN Security Council president's statement on the North Korean nuclear issue called on the Security Council to take "practical measures" to promote direct negotiations between Pyongyang and Washington to resolve the issue.) Implications The absence of a call for specific action in the Foreign Ministry spokesman's statement seemed designed to convey the impression that Pyongyang, having urged Washington to take unspecified steps to restore the "foundation" of bilateral talks, is awaiting Washington's next move.
