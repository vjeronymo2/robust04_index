940531 FT 31 MAY 94 / Begging split blows Labour off-course Labour's European election campaign was blown off-course yesterday by differences among shadow ministers about the party's response to Conservative attacks on begging. Claims by Mr John Major, the prime minister, that begging is unnecessary and offensive have been widely condemned by opposition and Church leaders. Mr Gordon Brown, shadow chancellor, said yesterday that Mr Major had worsened the problem of poverty by withdrawing benefits from those aged 16 and 17 as a minister in 1988. But Mr Brown refused to commit Labour to providing funds to reintroduce teenage benefits. He said that the problem could be solved 'within existing budgets'. Mr Donald Dewar, shadow social security secretary, said teenagers needed more support. 'If more money is required then that will have to be considered,' he said. Ms Joan Lestor, shadow minister for the family, said that there would be less homelessness if 'the benefits to the young, in particular, equalled the benefits in most of Europe'. Conservative ministers were quick to exploit the differences of emphasis between Labour's treasury and social security teams. Mr Michael Portillo, chief secretary to the treasury, wrote to Mr Brown demanding details of Labour's plans for financing a resumption of teenage benefits. Mr John MacGregor, transport secretary, said it was clear that Labour 'would not find more facilities beyond what we have done ourselves'. Labour's disarray will hearten Conservative strategists, who believe that the first week of their campaign was far from the disaster predicted by many commentators. Conservative divisions on Europe have been minimised by Labour's split on begging, and the Liberal Democrats' divisions on the national veto in the council of ministers. Mr Douglas Hurd, foreign secretary, told Conservatives in Aberdeen: 'The campaign has been going well for us.'
