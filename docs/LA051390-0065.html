May 13, 1990, Sunday, Home Edition

ALICE KAHN: HAPPILY, SHE'S DRIVEN TO MEMORIES

I remember what her face looked like in the delivery room mirror. I remember seeing her bouncing up and down in her Jolly Jump-Up. I remember her in that red, checkered party dress taking her first steps.

I wiped her nose. I counted her toes. I changed her nondisposable diapers. And now, I am teaching my baby to drive.

"Where do you go to practice driving?" my friend asked.

"Up to the regional park," I said. "I figure if I'm going to die, I might as well do it in a beautiful place."

Ju-u-u-st kidding, as my daughter Emma, now 15 1/2, would say. Actually, the odd thing is I don't feel frightened with my baby behind the wheel. I feel exhilarated. There are several reasons for this.

One is that driving with my very own Student Driver is no more frightening than driving with myself. Not that I'm a terrible driver. I'm fine, really, once I'm on the road. My main problem has always been those darned parked cars that suddenly materialize while you're backing out of the driveway. You know, those little phantoms that are always ducking out of range of your rear-view mirror, taunting you with, "Hit us if you can."

The second thing I like is that Emma driving represents a certain amount of freedom for me. Not only will I be off chauffeur duty for her, but she'll be so eager to get in the car that she'll end up driving around her younger sister, Hannah. Right? My days Driving Miss Hannah and being forced to listen to Paula Abdul are numbered.

Another reason I like driving with Emma is that it gives us something to do together. It's a chance to heal whatever wounds remain from our boxing-kangaroo days of mother-daughter junior high hassles. And I like to be with her. She's fun. When she's driving, boys pull up to the car and wave.

But most of all, what I love is the look on her face when she's driving. You can see in her eyes when the light changes from red to green, the very gates to the universe open up.

When she was little, we had so many nicknames for her. "Nucky" was the most common, short for Nucknose, short for Nucknosed Nuckersucker. She hated that one. "It's not even a common nickname," she complained once. "You know, like Snugglebunny."

For a while, we called her "Sockeye," short for sockeye salmon. That came on a trip to British Columbia when she was 9 months old. She accidentally gave her dad a black eye by beaning him with her diaper-rash ointment jar after a wonderful salmon cookout.

I won't tell you the names she's called me over the years. A sarcastic "Yes, Your Majesty" was one of the nicest. Then there was the time she threatened to write a tell-all biography of her mother, "Blankety-Blank, Dearest."

But when I see her behind the wheel, I think of another nickname we gave old Sockeye, old Nucky, old Erma La Dooby. She used to have a rattle, one of those Educational Playthings-type things. Maybe your baby has one. It was a flower with a mirror in the middle.

We used to call her: Flower-With-a-Face-on-It. And she really did look like that, wrapped in her blanket, with her peachy skin, her little black baby bangs, her tufts of curls around her ears.

So today, when I see her maintaining an exact 25 on Spruce Street or executing a perfect left onto Cedar, I just kind of look at her and sit back and gasp. She's still my little Flower-With-a-Face-on-It coming into full bloom.
