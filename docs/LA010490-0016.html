January 4, 1990, Thursday, Home Edition

THE MONSTER OF 'ZERO DEFECTS' STILL LIVES;

MILITARY: THE INVASION OF PANAMA REVEALED WEAKNESSES IN OUR FORCES' CAPABILITIES -- AND IN THE PUBLIC'S OVER-HYPED EXPECTATIONS.

The U.S. military invasion of Panama highlighted some fundamentals that will continue to influence the application of American power. First is that Karl von Clausewitz's 160-year-old concept of "friction" -- i.e., nothing will ever proceed exactly as planned -- is still with us. For example, at Ft. Bragg, N.C., the takeoff of elements of the 82nd Airborne Division was delayed by an unexpected ice storm; on the opposite coast, at Ft. Ord, Calif., the takeoff of elements of the 7th Infantry Division was delayed by dense fog. Despite all the advances of modern technology, the caprices of weather still have a major effect on the execution of military operations.

And despite the most detailed and precise planning -- and the planning for Operation Just Cause was thorough indeed -- mistakes still happen, just as they did at the Normandy beachhead 45 years ago. From a military point of view, such glitches are to be expected: The best way to deal with friction, Clausewitz said, is to anticipate and be prepared for it. But how civilians perceive these mistakes may be quite another matter.

The American public has been led into false expectations by the Pentagon brass. Trying to build public support, Defense Department bureaucrats discarded Clausewitz as inimical to the public relations image they wished to create. That was the image of an American military that could conduct flawless operations where everything went exactly as planned and where practically no one got hurt.

Unwittingly, this "zero defects" mentality created a monster, for "surgical precision" became the standard by which the media and the public measured military prowess. The successes of the Libyan raid and the Grenada incursion, for example, were overshadowed by the "frictions" that denied the perfection that Americans had been led to expect. Pentagon powers, realizing they had created a false and misleading picture, have since made a concerted effort to drive a stake through the heart of the "surgical precision" monster. The mythology lives on: Initial media reporting on the Panama invasion once again focused on glitches rather than successes.

This is serious business that goes far beyond public-relations glitz. In the 1990s, when fewer and fewer Americans will have firsthand combat knowledge, the false notion that military operations can be "surgical" could have disastrous consequences. It could tempt us into actions we would soon regret. Americans must be told and understand the bloody and imprecise nature of military force.

Another fundamental highlighted by the Panama invasion is that the United States remains a world island that must project its power beyond its borders in order to influence international events. With geography education in such disrepute that some college students believe the Soviet Union is somewhere in Central America, such elementary facts bear repeating.

The first requirement of American military strategy is to get there from here. In the Panamanian invasion, that reality was somewhat hidden by the fact that airlift alone was able to move most of the combat forces into position. But Panama was a special case. Not only was it within flying distance from continental military bases, there was also a major logistical support foundation already in place.

If, as would be the case in most other projections of American power, that support base had had to be established concurrent with the invasion, our lift capability would have been severely strained. Taking nothing away from the enormous effort put forth by the Military Airlift Command, it still remains a fact that America's strategic lift capability is in sad shape. Sealift in particular is in shambles as America's maritime fleet continues to shrink, and it is sealift that normally moves the majority of oil, ammunition and heavy combat arms and equipment.

The lack of such heavy lift could be deadly. Our success in Panamashould not blind us to the fact that the lack of sufficient airlift and sealift remains our greatest strategic military shortcoming. Correcting that deficiency should be one of the major priorities for the 1990s.
