November 18, 1990, Sunday, Home Edition

BALZER ON WINE: TIME FOR WINE

WINE HAS ALWAYS BEEN a hard sell in the United States because it has not been understood as a natural companion for food -- part of a healthful regimen. The autumn harvest of 1990 arrived under threatening clouds of "sin tax" ballot measures and federal legislation threatening the very existence of grape growing and winemaking in the state. Yet, it is always time for wine.

"We've got to put the glass or bottle of wine on the table, right there with the hamburgers, pizzas and tacos," says Sheldon (Hack) Wilson, owner /vintner of Chimney Rock in the Napa Valley. "What we need," he says, "is a definition that comes as easy as Coke's 'Pause that refreshes,' which tells the story without any insinuating stuffy rigmarole."

There was a challenge. We left it with his suggestion: "It's always time for wine . . . the civilized refreshment."

While I was in the Napa Valley, I also stopped at Monticello Cellars, where the Napa Valley Vintners Assn. was having its annual Crush Celebration. Guest harvesters from all over came to pick grapes and assist in the crush and fermentation (while wearing rubber boots), alongside all the regulars. There were tales to tell and good laughs.

One week later, I flew to Switzerland to observe the harvest in the Canton of Vaud, which fronts Lac Leman of Geneva, home of the vine since Roman times. The harvest was in full swing. As in the Napa Valley, laughter, songs and shouts of merriment were everywhere.

Swiss wine growers promote the ubiquitous golden Chasselas grape as having a very subtle flavor . . . and taking character from the different soils in which it is grown. True. I tasted dozens of wines of Chasselas origins from the separate appellations. None were the same. Some were earth-earthy, some had subtle aromas, and others defied description. But all were delicate, with an appetizing dryness.

In Switzerland, as in other parts of Europe, wine is part of the food regimen and is consumed regularly.

Back in America, with Thanksgiving days away, it's almost redundant to say, "It's time for wine!" But it is time, now, at Thanksgiving and whenever. "It's always time for wine . . . the civilized refreshment." Robert Lawrence Balzer
