March 22, 1990, Thursday, Home Edition

CONSUMER VIEWS: SEVERAL WAYS TO ELIMINATE TERMITES

Question: As a subscriber, an urban entomologist and a technical service and development specialist for Dow Elanco & Co., I would like to provide you with additional information relative to your article of Feb. 7, 1990, "Tallying the Benefits of a Cool, New Method of Termite Extermination" (using liquid nitrogen). As you stated, fumigation has been the primary method of eliminating dry-wood termites from structures. What you failed to tell your concerned readers is that it remains the only proven method of eliminating dry-wood termites from structures.

The Blizzard System requires the extent of the infestation to be determined before treatment. The problem with attempting to control dry-wood termites with local treatments such as Blizzard is that most colonies are very difficult to find. Fiber-optics will not reveal most colonies . . . because dry-wood termites leave the outer layer of wood beams untouched as a protective shield. I am sure that most homeowners are not informed of this drawback.

A bill is being introduced in the coming session of the California Legislature which will require evidence to support the claims of persons proposing the use of non-pesticidal insect-control methods. Without the field testing, which Jim Wells of the California Department of Food and Agriculture said was not conducted, how can one know whether the Blizzard System actually is effective?

The Blizzard System probably will kill dry-wood termites in exposed locations when colonies can be located. In such cases, spot injections of very small quantities of an aerosol insecticide into the termite galleries would also kill the colonies, and at about 25% of the cost of the Blizzard System.

Often, one method of handling complaints (of dissatisfied customers) is fumigation, since fumigation eliminates all the termites in the structure, not just those that can be found by the termite inspector. It is our right to choose, but it is also our right and obligation to be well informed when making our choice. -- BRIAN M. SCHNEIDER

Answer: Since Tallon Termite & Pest Control's use of liquid nitrogen to exterminate termites is relatively new (1985), and is a patented technique to boot, I didn't think for a minute that the previous column describing it would slip by without rebuttal. I am in the middle, just as much as the lowly termite is. We took the points you made to Joe Tallon Sr., the firm's president, because you obviously have broad expertise in the field.

I'm sure you aren't surprised that Tallon found little merit in your arguments. He concedes that the extent of infestation does, indeed, have to be determined before treatment with liquid nitrogen. But he takes exception to the statement that "fiber-optics will not reveal most colonies there (in wall voids or floor beams) because dry-wood termites leave the outer layer of wood beams untouched as a protective shield."

Tallon points out that fiber-optics are used extensively in such diverse professions as the medical field and in the inspection of aircraft engines and are quite successful. He also adds that while termites do "leave the outer layer of wood beams untouched," this misses the point of the fiber-optics inspection -- which is to pick up "the termite pellets that they kick out in order to clean their channels."

And, Tallon continues, while the California Department of Food and Agriculture may not have conducted field tests of the Blizzard System, his company conducted field tests "extensively before we applied for and got EPA (Environmental Protection Agency) approval. In all, we've probably done 2,000 to 3,000 field tests."

And he agrees with you completely that termites in exposed locations can, indeed, be killed with small quantities of aerosol insecticide at about 25% of the Blizzard cost. "Sure," Tallon agrees, "if you can see them you can kill them -- with insecticide, WD 40, aerosol hair spray or with a hammer, for that matter. They aren't the problem."

Neither does Tallon dispute a Wall Street Journal story about a dissatisfied Blizzard System customer.

"In about 15% of the cases it doesn't work, and so we go back and do it conventionally, with tenting. What the fumigation people don't tell you is that they have their misses, too. If the temperature drops or stays cold after tenting they've got a strong chance of not getting a kill, either."
