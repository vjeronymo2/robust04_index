December 2, 1989, Saturday, Home Edition

'DO WE REALLY NEED THE B-2?'

The Times has always been considered a thoughtful news organization fully aware of the influence it wields over a larger readership. But we find your editorial on the B-2 very disappointing.

One concludes that your position was based on an "analysis" by the International Institute for Strategic Studies.

Let me cite a few points to clear up some of the misunderstanding the Strategic Studies report obviously created.

Cost per warhead studies are what you want to make of them. For example, the Harvard analysis is used by The Times to conclude that using a B-2 would cost more than using a Midgetman missile. It would seem a system that can carry a wide variety of weapons, in an equally wide variety of sizes and strengths, and be able to do it time and again across the spectrum of conflict (from conventional to nuclear) is a very "cost-effective" weapon system. The small ICBM does have its utility as a deterrent. But doing a warhead "return on investment analysis" is in this instance, specious.

Let's turn to another issue -- whether or not the "stealthy" quality of the B-2 is of any value. Once again The Times focuses on only part of the picture. Even in the most intense exchange (an unlikely scenario) the number of surface-to-air missile sites -- at last count more than 10,000 -- would be considerbly reduced but not all would be destroyed. The Soviets overbuilt their system for just that reason.

Weapon systems must be developed based upon what can best provide the highest deterrent value this country's technology can produce. At the same time it must give us the most effective war-fighting capability should deterrence fail. The B-2 bomber falls into the "highly qualified" category on both counts.

Together with land- and sea-based missiles, the other two legs of our strategic triad, it will keep the peace and lend stability as we work our way through this critical period in history.

One final point. What has been left out of the editorial is any discussion of the pivotal role the B-2 plays in the START negotiations. The underlying rationale of START seeks to shift a greater portion of the deterrent mission to the more stable bomber element of the triad. The premium on the strategic bombers in the START negotiations makes it imperative we have a bomber capable of penetrating the sophisticated Soviet air defenses into the next century. That system is the B-2.

RONALD O. ALDRIDGE

Lieutenant General, U.S. Air Force

Vice Commander in Chief

Strategic Air Command

Offutt AFB, Neb.
