May 14, 1989, Sunday, Home Edition

BLOODY SUNDAY

Simon Brett, whose hard-drinking actor-sleuth Charles Paris is one of the joys of the mystery form, has begun a new series starring Melita Pargeter, a handsome widow whose late husband, a member of the criminal class, has left her plenty of money and a host of seedy but invaluable friends.

In Mrs, Presumed Dead, Simon Brett (unequaled as an inventor of titles) has Mrs. Pargeter leasing a house in a spiffy suburban tract for the nouveau-mortgaged. The previous owner has left a false forwarding address and Pargeter correctly senses foul deeds. Her researches -- and Brett's dissection of the neighborhood and its upwardly striving residents -- make for very enjoyable reading.

Santa Barbara's Sue Grafton has worked her way through Alibi, Burglar, Corpse, Deadbeat and Evidence and, with 'F' Is for Fugitive, shows no sign of faltering or flagging. Her Kinsey Millhone, one of the principal ladies in the private investigator line, is this time holed up in a California coast town that could be, let's say, Pismo Beach.

A man, then a teen-ager, convicted of strangling his pregnant girlfriend, escaped from prison and now has been recaptured after 17 years of a blameless life under another name. Millhone has to rake through the dead past to see if he was, in fact, guilty as charged.

After all those years there are enough lively suspects to fill a jail and enough evil to make "Peyton Place" seem like a retirement home for the serenely virtuous. Millhone's own life is finally at high risk. She is a wonderful character, tough but not brutish, resourceful and sensitive, a fit knight to walk those mean streets with her male predecessors, and high time.

Robert B. Parker's Spenser books have seemed slighter right along, the paddings of sex and food ever more evident. But his new one, Playmates, while the pausings for play and nourishment are still overabundant, has an unusually interesting and affecting main thrust.

A star college basketballer, who is black, has been shaving points and the scandal could imperil the team's shot at an NCAA title. Parker is here having his say about college athletics as the tail that wags the academic dog. He is also showing his wrath at the corrupting of the athletes themselves. There are corrosive portraits of a faculty choosing to ignore (and conceal) the fact that the star is functionally illiterate.

The gamblers are of course murderously resentful of Spenser's interference, although murder when it happens is almost a side issue. Spenser, as is his admirable custom, is risking his own image in hope of reclaiming a single, sullen life, redirecting an athlete whose physical skills, seemingly a way out of poverty, have only made him a pawn in other people's nasty games. "Playmates" is one of Parker's best in some time.

It's not surprising to learn that Bernard Schopen has completed a critical study of Ross Macdonald's work. Schopen's own first mystery, The Big Silence, is very strongly in the Macdonald tradition.

There is, to begin with, a knowing and affectionate celebration of a particular turf. In Schopen's case it is the Nevada desert (he studied in Reno but now lives in New Hampshire). More important, there are the roots of present murder which go deep into an incredibly entangled past of hidden and concealed identities and undisclosed crimes.

Schopen's hero is a lawyer-investigator named Jack Ross, who has about had it with death and betrayal, and is teetering at the brink of total emotional collapse. But when a young hooker asks him to find her grandfather, missing these 40 years, and Ross' old friend, an Indian, asks him to help her, what can he do? The writing occasionally strains too hard for poetic and rhetorical effects, trying for but missing Macdonald's heightened feeling for but careful control of language. The impersonations, the cross-breedings, the blood and marital ties, are almost too confusing, even compared with the messes Lew Archer ran into. But the action is vigorous and well-described, the Reno environs (even including the Mustang Ranch) excellently evoked, and Schopen and Ross are names to watch for henceforth.

The prolific Lawrence Sanders ("The Anderson Tapes" and "Timothy's Game" most recently) has built Capital Crimes around a fascinating central figure. He is a vodka-swilling, womanizing faith healer who calls himself Brother Kristos and operates out of a decrepit tobacco barn in Virginia, with a pair of sluttish women as his handmaidens.

The fascination is that he is not a fake; the cures appear to work, inexplicably, and word of him gets to the White House, where the President -- Abner Hawkins by name -- has a hemophiliac son, whom Kristos helps.

Kristos quickly becomes a political issue, living in Washington, frequenting the White House, advising on social policy, becoming the inadvertent catalyst in a conspiracy. Sanders, infusing his thriller with an unusual amount of ambiguity, has written a suspenseful story that doubles as an essay on the corruptions of power, including power that may be unearthly.

In the way of most softcover originals, Gerald Laurence's One Bang-Up Job, is a rocketingly fast, sleek-surfaced, larger-than-anything thriller. It confirms that the lessons of eventful pacing that made the pulp magazines as good as they were in their heyday have been noted and retained.

Laurence's caper is a master-planned robbery of everything in San Francisco simultaneously, with a resulting haul of $30 million in cash, which seems modest enough, every till in the city having been raked clear, although naturally mastermind Daniel Langer had expenses.

The author enlivens his fable with alternating first-person narratives, the pursuer reciting one chapter, the pursued the next. The chase leads to the jungles of South America, and Things Happen. Do they ever. In the end, of course, crime does not pay, but getting to this ancient wisdom is all the fun.

While My Pretty One Sleeps is the seventh and latest by Mary Higgins Clark (15 million copies in print, her publisher says). This one begins with the macabre disposal of the body of a murdered gossip columnist who had a flair for exposes.

The rest of the book charts the slow realization by the columnist's friends (few) and acquaintances (many) that she is not simply unaccountably away, but dead. The central worrier is a boutique owner whose father, conveniently, is a former police commissioner. The suspects include a weak and sponging nephew, an ex-husband and his shrewish wife and some garment trade executives about whom she knew too much.

The denouement is exciting if not overly surprising, and the book, for which a high competence is the accurate accolade, is hammock fare, if anyone still has hammocks.
