Language: English Article Type:BFN [Text] Former U.S. President Richard Nixon considers Boris Yeltsin's reaction to his meetings with Russian opposition leaders as an "unfortunate misunderstanding," according to Nixon's advisor, Dmitri Simes, who is accompanying him during his visit to Russia. Simes made this statement at a news conference in Moscow Wednesday [9 March]. "We did not know that permission is needed to hold meetings in a democratic country, taking into account the fact-finding character of the visit," Simes said. As has already been reported, Russian President Boris Yeltsin said this afternoon that neither he, nor his chief of staff, Sergey Filatov, would meet with Nixon. A scheduled meeting between the ex-President and Russian Prime Minister Viktor Chernomyrdin was also not held. "This was even more strange as we have received confirmation for the meeting more than once," Simes was quoted as saying. Simes believes that Yeltsin's reaction was provoked by distorted information he had been given about Nixon's meetings with some prominent figures of the Russian opposition such as the leader of the Communist faction in the State Duma, Gennadiy Zyuganov, and ex-Vice President Aleksandr Rutskoy. Simes said the meeting with Rutskoy could not be planned beforehand as Rutskoy was kept in custody until recently. According to Simes, the goal of the ex-President's visit was to familiarize himself with "political forces which have weight in Russia and report his findings to the Congress, administration and the American public." Simes put a special emphasis on the "private and fact-finding character" of Nixon's visit to Moscow. At the same time, he said that Nixon had a telephone conversation with U.S. President Bill Clinton on March 3. He said that Clinton supported the idea of the visit and hoped that Nixon would make an unprejudiced report on the state of affairs in Russia to his administration. According to Simes, Nixon will have to amend his schedule of meetings in Moscow. "Under the current circumstances," a number of meetings with "many high-ranking members of the Russian leadership" who had said they were ready to meet with the ex-President would be impossible. At the same time, Simes did not rule out that Nixon could meet with the leader of the Liberal Democratic Party of Russia, Vladimir Zhirinovskiy. Simes said many Russian government officials took part in the organization of Nixon's visit. The official invitation for Nixon to visit Russia was signed by Security Council Secretary Oleg Lobov.
