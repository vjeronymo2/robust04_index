BFN [Commentary by Peter Seidlitz: "Triumph of the Realists"] [Text] President Bill Clinton's decision to renew the most-favored-nation [MFN] clause will have a favorable effect on trade and cooperation between the United States and China. This is the gist of a statement published by the Chinese Government in Beijing as a reaction. The business-like statement does not reflect the overt triumph the Chinese Government feels. A few days before the fifth anniversary of the clearing of Tienanmen Square, which claimed at least several hundred if not thousands of lives on 4 June 1989, the United States has now also joined those who follow the popular slogan of "business first." The Europeans assumed such a pragmatic position a long time ago. Li Peng, China's head of government who held his own with his tough course in 1989, forcing reform-minded CPC head Zhao Zhiang into political exile, is expected on official visits in Austria, Germany, and Romania. That is realpolitik. President Clinton decided that trade and human rights would henceforth be separated. This ends the annual tug-of-war about the renewal of tariff privileges. One year ago, the same President still linked the two issues and explicitly made the granting of preferential trade treatment dependent on an improvement of the human rights situation in China and Tibet. Under the pressure of Big Business, the United States has now backed out and admitted its helplessness. After all, if the West is not even able to intervene in the cruel massacres in Africa and the Balkans, exerting pressure on China, which is bursting with strength, is absolutely impossible. The renewal of the tariff privileges means that Chinese consumer goods -- such as shoes, clothes, toys, electronic equipment -- remain cheap in the United States. The United States is China's biggest market. Products worth $33 billion go from China to the United States. Investments and trade are helping China to modernize the country. In the best case, this will in time lead to political transformation. There is no alternative. Nobody can wish for political chaos in the overpopulated country. Reversely, U.S. and multinational concerns, the U.S. service sector enterprises such as banks and bond houses, and even fastfood restaurants such as McDonald's, Kentucky Fried Chicken, Pizza Hut, and Burger King, are now hoping for big business. So far, as with Japan, the United States has had a huge trade balance deficit with China. The United States only sells products worth $8 million to China. According to reports, China is said to order 50 Boeing aircraft in a single mammoth order worth $5 billion in return, so to speak, for granting the tariff privileges. The resumption of military contacts with the objective of armament sales has also been initiated. The U.S. business world is, therefore, satisfied with President Clinton's decision. And Hong Kong rejoices, too. After all, the bulk of exports to China is shipped through this city, which will be a British colony until 1997. Hong Kong would have lost enormous transactions. After this political U-turn, the Americans argue that changes or an easing of the politically tense situation would be achieved through economic relations rather than through political pressure. The slogan is change through business.
