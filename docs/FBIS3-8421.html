Language: English Article Type:BFN ["Pool" item by Stevan Cordas] [Text] Belgrade, March 9 (TANJUG) -- Americans have invested all their political credibility in finding a comprehensive settlement to the conflict in the war-torn former Yugoslav Republic of Bosnia-Herzegovina after their own prescription. Judging by the U.S. President's special envoy Charles Redman's visit to Belgrade on Tuesday, the Americans seem to be very anxious to find the solution, but, at the moment, they fail to come up with a clear conception of how to give a definite shape to the peace package. The reason for the U.S. anxiousness is obviously a result of its wish to neutralize, as efficiently as possible, the Russian initiative in seeking a solution to the Bosnian conflict. Following the talks with Serbian President Slobodan Milosevic, Redman indicated two phases of the peace process. He said that the first phase, which was almost finished, would complete a Moslem-Croat deal on setting up a federation made up of territories controlled by the two peoples and its later entering into a confederation with Croatia. The key to the solution, however, could not be found before reaching the following (second) phase, for which the U.S. needs the participation of the third warring nation, i.e., the Serbs. Redman's visit to Belgrade was a "feel the pulse" operation and an attempt to solve essential matters "in passing." It is hard to tell whether the U.S. official, while conferring with Milosevic and later with Bosnian Serb Republic Vice-President Nikola Koljevic, had found out what he came to Belgrade for. It was made clear to him, however, that the Moslem-Croat agreement is not binding for the Serb side. Redman in Belgrade insisted on "an overall settlement" of the Bosnian crisis, but failed to specify what exactly he meant by that. He was also told that under the circumstances it was very hard for the Bosnian Serbs to take an integral stand. The statements he made in Belgrade differ a great deal from a statement by U.S. Ambassador to Croatia Peter Galbraith. Redman, for instance, categorically argued that the Moslem-Croat agreement was not aimed against the Bosnian Serbs. On the other hand, Galbraith on Tuesday told the press conference in Zagreb the agreement's main aim was to put an end to the senseless conflict between the two nations, victims of the Serb aggression. At a point when Redman tried to persuade the Bosnian Serbs of the necessity of their involvement in the latest peace process, Galbraith has offered them only an alternative -- either to join the Moslem-Croat agreement and the West, or to continue the war. Be that as it may, Charles Redman left unfinished the job he came to Belgrade for, so he is likely to come back for talks shortly. Meanwhile, the Russians don't sit on their hands, either. Bosnian Moslem Prime Minister Haris Silajdzic and Croatian Foreign Minister Mate Granic were in Moscow on Wednesday. Russian Foreign Minister Andrey Kozyrev and U.S. State Secretary Warren Christopher are scheduled to meet next Monday. To all appearances, the situation would somewhat clear up only after all these talks about the situation in Bosnia-Herzegovina.
