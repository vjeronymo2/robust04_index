BFN [Editorial: "Superfluous 301"] [Text] There are many reasons behind the U.S. decision to give India a two-month reprieve from the U.S. "Special" 301 trade sword. One reason, perhaps, is the proposed Washington visit by Prime Minister P.V. Narasimha Rao. The subject of the Washington talks is going to be the NPT [Nuclear Nonproliferation Treaty]; the imposition of Special 301 on the eve of these talks would have been an unnecessary diplomatic irritant. In addition, India and the United States hold divergent views on a number of issues. Questions about political-strategic issues are more serious than trade questions. All of this is going to be discussed by Prime Minister Narasimha Rao and President Clinton. Therefore, it is impossible to understand why the U.S. Commerce Department brandished the Special 301 sword. Agreed, the previous two-month reprieve has just ended. Meanwhile, the official GATT accord has been confirmed, and India has signed it. The GATT preamble answers all the questions the U.S. Congress cited in proposing the Special 301 act. India's acceptance of the GATT's Dunkel draft should satisfy the U.S. Administration, and it should be thinking about ending Special 301. To the contrary, however, the United States has threatened to bring India under the purview of Special 301. It seems that India must face Special 301 again because of China. China is going to become a GATT member; it has already applied. China had made an issue of the delay in approving its application. Until China joins the GATT agreement, the U.S. will continue to threaten it over U.S. patent and copyright issues. China and India are in the same category on the U.S. list. Therefore, India is being warned time and again because of China. Perhaps it is because of China that the U.S. Congress is not thinking about revoking Special 301, despite the GATT signing. China, India, and Argentina are the countries currently being threatened with Special 301. India and Argentina have already signed the GATT agreement. As a result, they must make changes in their patent and copyright laws in accordance with this agreement. GATT has allocated enough time for this. The United States is applying psychological pressure on them to enact new laws quickly by giving two months' warning. If India ignores this, nothing is going to happen. India should not care about Special 301 anymore. After all, why should the United States be accepted as a godfather in place of the new multilateral trade organization? All patent and copyright issues are now the concern of the new trade organization. How can the United States become a godfather in bilateral trade when a multilateral trade system is being launched? Perhaps the U.S. Administration refrained from applying Special 301 because it has a proper understanding of this perspective, and the Indian Government did not waste any time reacting to it.
