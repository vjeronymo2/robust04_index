BFN ["Pool" item] [Text] Belgrade, May 16 (TANJUG) -- Yugoslav President Zoran Lilic said on Monday that the visit of Russian Patriarch Aleksiy II to the people of Serbia and Montenegro was "a great contribution to the efforts being invested for peace and harmony in the lands of former Yugoslavia...which has been Yugoslavia's policy from the start." Lilic was speaking in a meeting with the visiting pontiff of the Russian church, Patriarch Aleksiy II, and his host, Patriarch Pavle of the Serbian Orthodox Church. Patriarch Aleksiy II said this was a difficult time for both Russia and Yugoslavia (Serbia/Montenegro) and said it was his dearest wish -- in his capacity as emissary of the Russian Orthodox Church -- to witness a reconciliation among the nations living in the former Yugoslav lands. He explained that his main task on this visit was to put into practice an initiative, moved jointly with Patriarch Pavle, to effect a meeting with the leaders of the Croatian Roman Catholic community and the Bosnian Islamic community -- Cardinal Franjo Kuharic and Reis ul-Ulema Mustafa Ceric, respectively -- in an effort to show the people that conflicts should be dealt with through talks, not war. President Lilic said that the two churches had every spiritual and moral right to embark on such a mission, and described Effendi Ceric's refusal to take part in such a meeting at this time as proof that the Moslems are still committed to the war option for settling the dispute in Bosnia-Herzegovina. "Experience teaches us that, whatever compromises the Bosnian Serbs might make, the Moslems invariably upset all agreements and stage-manage incidents at crucial times, in which they kill innocent civilians," Lilic said. He added that the Moslems' chief objective was to keep up the campaign of satanising the entire Serbian nation in the global media, to which the Russian patriarch added that not even the Russian media had painted an objective picture of the position of the Serbs. Patriarch Aleksiy II said the Russian church and people believed that sanctions against Yugoslavia were unjust and should be lifted, a position that had been supported by the State Duma (parliament lower house), which had asked that Russia lift the blockade of Yugoslavia. The United Nations imposed draconian sanctions on Yugoslavia in May 1992, because of the country's alleged involvement in the civil war in neighbouring Bosnia-Herzegovina. President Lilic said it was clear to the whole world that the sanctions had missed their target, and that the Bosnian war could be ended only if all Bosnia's warring nations -- Moslems, Serbs and Croats -- were treated equally, i.e. if the Serbs, too, were given the right to self-determination, just as the other Yugoslav nations. Brotherly nations, such as the Montenegrins, Serbs and Russians, must be united on the basic questions of nation and state, said Lilic and added that these nations "should do their all to cherish their tradition, their spiritual orthodox culture, by living in peace and harmony with the other nations and confessions, as is the case in Yugoslavia where the 50 religious communities practice their religious affairs unhindered."
