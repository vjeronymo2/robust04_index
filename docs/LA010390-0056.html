January 3, 1990, Wednesday, Home Edition

FIRM SAYS IT WAS A 'STRAW MAN' IN LINCOLN DEAL;

S&LS: A BUSINESSMAN SAYS HE SIGNED DOCUMENTS FOR A $14-MILLION LAND DEAL MERELY AS A FAVOR FOR A FRIEND.

An Arizona businessman testified in a federal court Tuesday that his small real estate investment company was a "straw man" in a $14-million land transaction involving Lincoln Savings & Loan.

Fernando Acosta, who was president of Westcontinental Mortgage & Investment in Tucson, said his limited partnership was too small to buy the property and that he simply signed documents without reading them to help his friend, Lincoln borrower Ernest C. Garcia.

Acosta's testimony prompted U.S. District Judge Stanley Sporkin to question the safety and soundness of the loans that Westcon received.

Acosta was the third witness to testify for the government in a hearing to test the evidence that federal regulators used to justify the April 14 takeover of Irvine-based Lincoln.

Sporkin is taking testimony on four transactions that regulators claim typify the failure of Lincoln's parent, American Continental Corp. in Phoenix, to operate the S&L safely and soundly and conserve assets. He expects to hear from American Continental executives, including its controversial chairman, Charles H. Keating Jr., during the next three days.

American Continental claims that regulators lacked sufficient evidence to show that Lincoln was operating unsafely and unsoundly and was substantially dissipating assets. American Continental had filed for bankruptcy protection the day before Lincoln was seized.

The collapse of Lincoln could become the nation's single biggest thrift failure, with taxpayers forced to pay more than $2 billion to bail out the S&L.

The government's case before Sporkin was jolted Friday when regulators learned that Garcia, the key witness in the Westcon transaction and another deal, would exercise his Fifth Amendment right against self-incrimination and refuse to testify in the civil proceeding.

But James P. Murphy and Paul E. Gutermann, lawyers for the Office of Thrift Supervision, said they still expect to show that regulators had sufficient information to seize Lincoln. They expect to use Garcia's deposition last March in a Securities and Exchange Commission investigation of American Continental to bolster their case.

That deposition, along with a Westcon letter admitting that the firm held the property as a straw owner, provided regulators with the "smoking gun" they were looking for to seal their rationale for seizing Lincoln, according to the testimony of Kevin T. O'Connell, an executive at OTS, the nation's thrift regulatory agency.

But John C. Luntin, a Phoenix attorney for American Continental, said the documents were not the smoking gun and, in fact, showed that the Westcon deal was not a sham transaction, as O'Connell claimed.

Regulators claim that developer Garcia needed a loan to buy back an 80% interest in his firm that he had previously sold, but Lincoln would supply the money only if he bought a vacant parcel in a master-planned community southwest of Phoenix.

Garcia did not want to buy the property but arranged for Westcon to buy it on March 30, 1987. And he loaned Westcon the $3.5-million down payment from Lincoln funds he received, regulators claim.

Acosta said he agreed to be a "nominee" as an "accommodation" to his friend, who wanted the property off the books of E. C. Garcia & Co. Acosta said no payments were expected because the property was supposed to go back to Garcia within 90 days, but it wasn't until more than a year later that Westcon was able to get Garcia to take it back.

Acosta testified that he never read the documents he signed, never knew who owned the property, never learned what the documents were and never knew that he even had received $3.5 million from Garcia and transferred it at the same time to Lincoln as a down payment. The judge was concerned that Lincoln would make a $10.5-million loan for the balance of the purchase price to Westcon, which couldn't pay it back. "It might be considered to be unsafe or unsound," he said.

Luntin, however, said after the court hearing that Lincoln didn't lend Westcon any money. The S&L received $3.5 million in cash as a down payment and simply took a promissory note back for the remainder.

The down payment, he said, came from Garcia's own pocket. Of the $20.2 million loaned to Garcia to purchase his company's stock, $19.6 million was wired directly to the seller, and the rest went to Lincoln as loan fees.
