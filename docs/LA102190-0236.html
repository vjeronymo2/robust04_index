October 21, 1990, Sunday, Home Edition

YOUR MORTGAGE;

FIGURING OUT MATH OF ACCELERATED PAYMENTS

QUESTION: In a recent column about accelerating principal payments on a mortgage for five years, you made much of the fact that, after that time, the family that had accelerated walked off with far more equity than did the other family that didn't accelerate.

What you didn't mention, though, was that the seller who did not accelerate had, during the five years mentioned, the use of the money, and assuming it was invested where the money earning a rate of interest equal to the interest rate on the mortgage, in this case 10%, he would come out exactly the same.

While his equity in the home would be less, he would have an equal amount of equity somewhere else and, in addition, he would have liquidity, which the first party would not have. You also failed to indicate that while interest payments would be lower, so would the tax deductions.

ANSWER: Ah, yes, here we go again on the old high-leverage/low-leverage merry-go-round.

Should we pay as little in down payment on a house as possible and keep the monthly payments to a minimum, or should we make a large down payment and accelerate principal payments as much as possible to build up a large equity and sharply reduce the interest being paid the lender?

And the argument against acceleration is that the money not used for this acceleration can be invested more profitably in something that more than offsets the savings in interest.

It always makes for a lively debate, and while I have always been pretty noisy in advocating fast acceleration it's also true that I'm not enough of a computer whiz to prove my contention in hard figures.

Quite recently, however, an ally has appeared in the form of Colorado-based management consultant, M. Joseph Lockerd who has a fascinating book in the works entitled: "The Myth of the Mortgage Tax Advantage: Spending a Dollar to Save a Dime."

Lockerd has run the invest-versus-acceleration question off in virtually every conceivable way on his computer. The conclusion: Except in a handful of freakish situations, mortgage-principal acceleration is a far better investment than anything else available to the layman. The intervening lack of liquidity notwithstanding.

It's a study that takes clearly into account both the reduction in mortgage-interest deductibility that one incurs in accelerating a payoff, but also the tax consequences of those investments that are made with the money that would otherwise go into acceleration.

One frequently overlooked point: By accelerating the payoff of a 30-year mortgage down to 10 or 15 years, the homeowner has a mortgage-free 15 or 20 years ahead of him in which to further compound his investment program. To an extent that that his invest-in-something-else counterpart -- still burdened with his monthly mortgage payments -- can't begin to approach.

Time-Share and Taxes: Is Interest Deductible?

Q: Recently, my wife and a friend each purchased a one-half ownership in a time-share in Mexico. The total contract balance is $5,000 and they plan to occupy the unit for 30 days every year. My question: Can the interest on the contract be fully deducted, the same as mortgage interest, or is it treated as ordinary personal interest and subject to being phased out?

A: Depends on a couple of tricky things, the Internal Revenue Service tells me. For one thing, to be deductible in the same way that mortgage interest is, it has to be a "deeded" time-share -- one in which you are actually buying your share of the property and can rent it, sell it or pass it on to your heirs.

If it's a non-deeded time-share, however, all you're buying is a "right to use" the unit for X number of years, after which it reverts to the developer -- and the interest isn't deductible.

The other fly in the ointment is that if you already have one "vacation home" somewhere on which you are deducting the mortgage interest, then that's it -- the time-share won't qualify.

I might add, too, that most if not all Mexican time-shares with which I am familiar are strictly non-deeded. Mexican law prohibits conventional land ownership by foreigners along its coast line -- which is where most time- shares are located.
