October 24, 1989, Tuesday, Home Edition

PENTAGON CONCERNED ABOUT EFFECTS OF THE 'BIG ONE';

DEFENSE: OFFICIALS, WORRIED ABOUT THE CONCENTRATION OF WEAPONS-MAKING PLANTS IN

Even as the Bay Area begins to recover from Tuesday's earthquake, some Pentagon officials are trembling at the prospect of a still-deadlier temblor in the heart of the nation's defense industry.

Damage to most of the area's 900 defense contractors was relatively light, and weapons giants such as Lockheed Corp. and Ford Aerospace reported that employees are back at work and defense programs are back on schedule.

But those responsible for ensuring the health of the nation's weapons-production capability point to the concentration of defense plants in earthquake-prone California and warn that quakes are not only human tragedies but a national security problem too.

"If an 8.3 (magnitude) earthquake strikes California, it will not only be a California but a national disaster," Richard E. Donnelly, assistant secretary of defense for acquisition and logistics, told an industry group studying the problem in 1986. "It is conceivable the President would declare a national emergency and place U.S. forces on alert."

Experts warn that California's more than 5,000 defense contractors -- representing more than 50% of the nation's missile and space business and about 75% of the domestic microchip industry -- must take further precautions to prepare for the "big one" that scientists believe is likely to strike in the next 30 years.

A March, 1989, white paper prepared by the American Defense Preparedness Assn., a Washington-based industry group, concluded that "only 5% of the companies in earthquake-prone California are estimated to be adequately prepared to cope with earthquakes that could potentially wipe out their computer facilities and operational capabilities."

"We industrial-base fanatics are never satisfied," Donnelly said in an interview. "But in the past decade, there's been a substantial improvement. . . . We think the system is working."

Donnelly said the Pentagon, working with contractors through the Business and Industry Council on Earthquake Preparedness, has helped encourage and guide defense firms to take voluntary measures to protect their ability to produce in the wake of an earthquake.

But some, including the members of the ADPA, believe that the Defense Department should move more aggressively to protect the nation's weapon-making capacity, requiring earthquake preparations as a condition for doing business with the Pentagon and looking outside the vulnerable quake zone for alternative manufacturers for certain systems.

Donnelly said such measures go beyond the Pentagon's existing power over contractors. He said the Pentagon's most effective earthquake initiative has been a system of monitoring the locations and activities of defense contractors.

Last Tuesday evening, Donnelly's office used a new database called DINET -- short for defense industrial network -- to identify those companies and defense programs that might be affected by disasters. The database had been used in Pentagon "command post exercises" but was used for the first time in a real disaster when Hurricane Hugo struck the South Carolina coast.

On Tuesday night, DINET gave defense officials the names of 900 Bay Area firms likely to be affected by the quake, as well as the defense programs that might first be delayed if production lines shut down. Donnelly said the Pentagon had not received reports of substantial disruptions in equipment deliveries as a result of the quake.

But in Southern California, where the concentration of defense contractors is greater than in the Bay Area, the delays could be severe -- and costly -- in event of a major temblor, the ADPA group warned.

A 1986 Defense Department survey of 32 major contractors in the Los Angeles quake zone indicated that an earthquake measuring more than 8 on the Richter scale would cause delays in the delivery of equipment ranging from two months to more than two years.

Companies that rely heavily on robotics and automated production have the most urgent need to make earthquake preparations, warned the ADPA white paper, which was prepared under the direction of Northrop Corp. disaster planner Nelson Cyr.

For instance, at FMC Corp.'s San Jose and Santa Clara production plants, where armored troop carriers and fighting vehicles are produced, engineers worked quickly to check and recalibrate automated equipment by Thursday.

But contractors who specialize in delicate, hand-assembled equipment have challenges of their own. At Ford Aerospace's satellite assembly facility in Palo Alto, satellites withstood the quake atop a 40-foot thick block of concrete set on pilings that are sunk deep into the bedrock, according to Ford spokesman Susan Pearce.
