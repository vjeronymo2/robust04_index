May 14, 1989, Sunday, Home Edition

SEARCH FOR A FIRST

While I agree with Boyd that "it's high time someone set the record straight on when the first TV movie was made," his assignation of that honor to NBC's "The Pied Piper of Hamelin" is seriously misplaced.

During the first season of "Playhouse 90" on CBS (1956-57), no fewer than 10 of this renowned live drama show's 39 presentations were feature-length films produced especially for television. (The first was "The Country Husband," based on a John Cheever story, which aired on Nov. 1, 1956.) It seems to me that these qualify as "made-for-TV movies" by any but the most hair-splitting definition.

Even these had at least one antecedent, however: a 72-minute film called "Black Forest," starring Peggy Ann Garner and Akim Tamiroff, made in Germany by an American company, for distribution to independent stations. Variety reviewed this production on Feb. 3, 1954, noting that "the 'expressly-for-TV' angle lends itself strongly to prestige-type ballyhoo on the part of station and sponsor, and its effect on the public is not to be minimized."

HOWARD H. PROUTY

Van Nuys
