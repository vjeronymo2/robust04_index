BFN [By BUSINESS TASS] [Text] Moscow June 9 -- The favorable balance in Russia's foreign trade continued to grow in January-May this year and amounts now to 9.7 billion U.S. dollars, a spokesman for the ministry of foreign trade relations of the Russian Federation told BUSINESS-TASS on Thursday. The total volume of export in five months of 1994 was 16.8 billion U.S. dollars which is 9 percent higher than at the similar period in 1993. Export to industrialized countries also continued to increase and totaled 10.3 billion U.S. dollars, 8.6 percent more as compared with the similar period of the past year. Export to developing countries was 2.2 billion U.S. dollars, to the former Comecon countries -- 2.7 billion U.S. dollars and to other countries -- 1.6 billion U.S. dollars. The trend towards the growth in export of main raw materials remained. For instance, oil export in five months of 1994 rose 10 percent, oil products -- 17 percent and natural gas -- 14 percent. Deliveries of fish, iron ore, ammonia, methanol, fertilizers, timber, cast iron and non-ferrous metals exceeded the last year's level. Aluminium export almost doubled as compared with 1993. Russia's import in January-May of 1994 amounted to 7.1 billion U.S. dollars which is 28 percent less than at the similar period of the last year. Especially sharp fall -- 44 percent was registered in the volume of purchases in developing countries. Import from industrialized countries was 4.7 billion U.S. dollars, 18 percent down as compared with the first five months of 1993. Grain import reduced by 94 percent and only 411,000 tons were imported in five months. Import deliveries of flour and macaroni reduced 75-90 percent. Foreign purchases of vegetable oil, coffee, tea, rubber, sugar and clothes fell 25-50 percent as compared with five months of 1993. At the same time import of butter, dry milk, meat, including poultry somewhat grew. Import of aluminium raw materials, alloy steel, steel pipes, furniture and some other commodities remained above the last year's level.
