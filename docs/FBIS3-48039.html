Language: English Article Type:BFN [Station commentary] [Summary from poor reception] "A new era has been forged in economic cooperation between Malaysia and Indonesia. This follows the highly professional visit of Deputy Prime Minister Datuk Sri Anwar Ibrahim to Jakarta recently. A record 12 memoranda of understanding were signed between both governments and respective private sectors." The high-level business trip was intended to set up a joint venture company called Malindo. The joint venture will help identify small- and medium-scale industries in Indonesia. The venture is meaningful because it will promote closer ties between the private sector in the two countries. "The partnership is all the more timely and vital in view of ASEAN's goal to form a single market early next century. Malaysia's trade and investment linkages will help the two countries to become growth centers within ASEAN." Of course, as in any business venture, problems are bound to crop up. The challenge is to overcome these problems and work for mutual benefit. There will be many rewards, including for the two countries' markets. There are differences in the business relationship between the two countries, but more importantly there are great similarities. It is encouraging to see that a memorandum of understanding was signed to market the Proton in Indonesia. This is the national car's first entry into the protected Indonesian market. Indonesia should become involved in Malaysia's national car project by supplying auto components for the project. An Indonesian group and the manufacturer of the national car are already discussing this possibility. Two-way cooperation has also been extended to the financial sector. "Malaysia has promised technical assistance in establishing Islamic financial and insurance system. There is much Malaysia can contribute in this area, as it has one of the most successful Islamic banking system in the world." "It is also all important in a world that is increasingly becoming more competitive, and where developing countries seem to be always pushed around."
