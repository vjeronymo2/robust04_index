940905 FT 05 SEP 94 / Call to reform market franchises The National Association of British Market Authorities is expected to call for the government to put forward new plans to reform market franchise rights at its annual conference in Llandudno, north Wales, today. The association, a group of 135 local authorities, is proposing a system of local authority registration or licensing of all commercial markets, including car boot sales. It will today debate a resolution recognising the need for market franchise rights to be reviewed and calling for the Department of the Environment to start consultations for new legislation. The group spearheaded the successful campaign to persuade the government to withdraw clauses in its deregulation bill abolishing the existing market franchise rights. Under centuries-old laws these allow many local authorities to control street market trading. The association joined forces earlier this year with eight other organisations, including the Association of British Chambers of Commerce, the Federation of Small Businesses and the National Market Traders' Federation to form an umbrella group, the Campaign for Quality Markets. Campaign members say abolishing the ancient rights would lead to an explosion of car boot sales and unauthorised markets. The government says the rules are anachronistic and are misused by some councils to stifle business initiatives. The association says, however, that the government has failed in its pledge to bring forward proposals to reform market franchise rights, which the association admits contain many anomalies and need updating. It is worried that the government will try to reintroduce clauses abolishing the rights into the deregulation bill when it continues its passage through parliament. Mr Brian Ormshaw, secretary of the association, said it was ready to rally cross-party support for its campaign in the autumn. 'The present law needs to be reviewed but market franchise rights are crucial in protecting the health of town centres and in preventing crime - both objectives which are supposed to be dear to the government's heart.'
