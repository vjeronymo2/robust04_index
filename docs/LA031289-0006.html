March 12, 1989, Sunday, Home Edition

CRITIC'S CORNER

Despite the blockbuster success of the miniseries "Lonesome Dove," the three networks have just finished the lowest-rated February sweeps in their history. All the records the networks are setting in television these days seem to be new lows.

February is one of four months annually when the Nielsen and Arbitron ratings services take particularly intensive surveys, and the results are used in determining advertising rates. The most important sweeps are in February and November.

The combined three-network rating for February was down almost four points from the same month last year. The total average share for the three networks -- the number of TV sets that were tuned to ABC, CBS and NBC -- stood at 69 percent. That means that more TV viewers than ever were watching something else.

Is there a way for the networks to put a rosy filter over these downbeat numbers? "We consider them almost a blessing," says David M. Poltrack, a CBS vice president who is the king of network research. Poltrack says that even though the total network numbers are down, CBS actually improved on its performance over last year. So, whoopee.

"The numbers are very positive from CBS' point of view," Poltrack says. "We're up in every single day part." Day parts are what networks divide the broadcast day into: daytime, prime time, late night, and so on. Though still locked in third place, CBS improved on last year's February sweeps by 7 percent. NBC was up 3 percent.

ABC was down 31 percent, but Poltrack says that figure is misleading because last February, ABC had two weeks of Winter Olympics coverage, making its ratings unusually high.

It gets complicated, doesn't it? And television is a business in which there are an infinite number of ways to lie, or at least fib, with statistics. If your show gets a lousy rating, you can always say it gets great "demographics" -- that it's doing well with women 18-to-25, for instance, or with midgets making more than $40,000 a year.

The networks are pitiful helpless giants watching much of their audience take a hike. But in their defense, there's no way of knowing how much of the latest hike-taking can be traced to last year's writers' strike, which disrupted and delayed the new fall prime-time schedules.

"We are gradually recovering from the strike, and it hurt us worse than we thought it would," says Poltrack. "I personally believe we will never fully recover, that we have permanently lost one full share point as a result."

Because of the strike and the abundance of reruns on the networks, viewers went wandering last fall. Among the places they landed is the fledgling Fox Network, which has fewer stations than any of the big three but which continues to score solid gains on Sunday nights, especially with the corrosive sitcom "Married . . . With Children."

For CBS, meanwhile, the miniseries "Lonesome Dove" was a boon, doing better in the ratings than CBS had promised advertisers, and, more importantly, giving network television a shot in the arm.

"It sort of brought people back to network television and got people talking about it again," Poltrack says. "It really was the first big event since the strike to do that. Normally it happens in the fall when the new shows are introduced, but this year, we lost that."

Poltrack thinks that "Lonesome Dove" lured not only viewers who normally don't watch the networks, but also enticed "the upper socioeconomic audience" that normally "isn't watching television at all." The four-night Western spectacular proved to be "a tremendous psychological rallying point for CBS," Poltrack says.

The network is otherwise experiencing a season of embarrassing clunkers and dismal flops.

Figures from the February sweeps will be analyzed, dissected, stood on their heads and turned inside out. Independent stations, those not affiliated with any network, will claim a great victory because the network share is down again, but the independents as a group were up only two-tenths of a rating point from last February's results.

While the number-crunchers hunch over their computers and the sales departments figure out how to bend and shape them and beat them into meaninglessness (and that may not be too hard to do), the lesson of "Lonesome Dove" would seem to be a simple one:

Put on better programs, and more people will want to watch them.
