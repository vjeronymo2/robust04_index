920713 FT 13 JUL 92 / Chief who steered Cunard to profit dies SIR Basil Smallpeice, one of the UK's best-known industrialists in the 1960s and 1970s, died yesterday after a short illness. He was 85. Sir Basil, who was knighted in 1961, was perhaps best known for transforming the Cunard shipping line from a big loss-maker into profitability during the 1960s. He also played a key role in several other flagship UK companies, including British Overseas Airways Corporation (BOAC) - now British Airways - and the pottery group Royal Doulton, owned by Pearson, publisher of the Financial Times. An accountant, Sir Basil joined Cunard in 1964 after a period as BOAC's managing director. Within two years he became chief executive and started the lengthy process of turning round a passenger-oriented shipping line facing the full consequences of a world turning to air travel. Central to the success of his strategy was a decision to sell off most of Cunard's fleet in order to concentrate on the upper end of the market with just three vessels, including the QE2. By 1969 losses had been turned into a Pounds 3m profit. He retired from industry in 1979, standing down as chairman of Associated Container Transportation (Australia).
