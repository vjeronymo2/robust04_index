Article Type:BFN [Editorial Report] Moscow Russian Television Network in Russian at 1725 GMT on 25 January broadcasts its regular program "Details" presented by Nikolay Svanidze who comments on the Russian Federation defense budget, which is currently in the pipeline. This is followed by an exclusive interview with Russian Deputy Defense Minister Andrey Kokoshin. Kokoshin begins by saying that in its present situation Russia needs a new armaments program but drawing it up is extremely difficult. Asked by the announcer if the Finance Ministry had been putting obstacles in the way of the program, Kokoshin said: "I would not like to criticize this particular ministry because I have a full understanding of their position: They had a clear task of reducing inflation by monetarist methods, and this resulted in chronic underpayments in practically all the main spheres covered by the budget, including the Defense Ministry. We had some disagreements in 1993, sometimes quite bitter, but at the same time, I realize that their life is not easy either. It is another matter that we need to look for new forms of more stable financing." Svanidze then recalls that President Yeltsin had promised to pay state-owned enterprises all the money owed to them by the state by 1 February, and asked Kokoshin if that promise would be fulfilled. Kokoshin says: "I believe that it will be fulfilled, but the question is to what extent. Just now complicated work -- or I might as well call it violent struggle -- is in progress about who should get their money first and how much of it. We order for defense." Asked by the announcer about his personal standpoint when discussing the budget in the government, and if he was capable of reconciling the requirements of the defense industry with the awareness that it is a burden on the national economy, Kokoshin replies: "We have no wish to form our budget through robbing our own country, and I can tell you that in the past too much money was invested in the defense industry, and often those were rather imprudent investments. Now the pendulum has swung in the opposite direction. I think one of my tasks, and those of my colleagues, is to prove that the pendulum should be brought to a more balanced position, and that our Armed Forces should be equipped as they deserve." Finally, Svanidze asks Kokoshin how he thought the current government reshuffle would affect the defense budget. Kokoshin replies: "In my opinion, the approach we had before was really too monetarily oriented, and I have also argued -- both at the open and closed sessions with Boris Fedorov and with other adherents of this approach. I think it insufficient, to put it mildly, and I hope that we will at last begin to stimulate industry.
