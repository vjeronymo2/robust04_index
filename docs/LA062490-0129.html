June 24, 1990, Sunday, Home Edition

HOME ENTERTAINMENT: STEREO SYSTEMS WITH ELBOW ROOM

You've heard of "desktop publishing" -- well, now there's "desktop audio." The move to make modern electronics smaller and smaller has spilled over into the home entertainment arena. Tiny desktop audio components are becoming more and more popular, and are being produced by an ever-increasing variety of manufacturers.

As populations grow and land in urban areas becomes increasingly costly, we are all striving to make more efficient use of space. Whether you live in a studio apartment, a college dormitory, a town house or one of the latest generations of compact homes, you know that every inch of space is dear. "Desktop audio" components are designed and built to take advantage of this need to conserve space.

Some manufacturers refer to the new generation of tiny audio components as "compact" rather than "desktop." The result is the same: several pieces of equipment occupy a very compact area. Desktop audio gear should not be confused with battery-powered portable equipment or "boom boxes." Desktop gear, though very small, is meant to live at home.

While the size of the new audio equipment may have shrunk, the sound produced by it (as well as its price tag) can still be quite large. Here are three systems from different makers. Each is no larger than a foot-high pile of telephone books.

Casio Single Unit

While the Casio MS-202 looks like a stack of four components, it's actually a single unit. It houses (from the top down) a turntable, an FM/AM tuner, an integrated amplifier with three-band graphic equalizer, and a dual-cassette deck. Also included are a wireless remote control and a pair of speakers.

Though small, the system is not without bells and whistles. The full-size turntable is the belt-driven, semi-automatic type, and has a molded plastic dust cover. The tuner can be set to 12 FM and six AM stations. The cassette unit is capable of high-speed dubbing and can play two tapes consecutively. It can record from the turntable, the radio, or tape to tape.

The MS-202 has an external input for attaching a compact disc player, television set, VCR or other audio source, and it has a headphone jack for private listening. The wireless remote controls on/off, volume, mute, and tuner scan. The Casio MS-202 is available now at a suggested retail price of $250.

Compact Magnavox

The model AZ8700GY (where do they come up with these numbers?) from Magnavox is even more compact than the Casio, and while it lacks a turntable, it does feature a compact disc (CD) player.

So, included in this desktop system are a CD player, FM/AM radio, an integrated amplifier with a four-band graphic equalizer, and a dual-cassette deck. A pair of speakers comes as part of the package.

The CD player features repeat and shuffle play modes, as well as 20-track programmability. The FM/AM radio has electronic tuning, 12 station presets, and a built-in, telescoping antenna. The dual cassette deck offers high-speed dubbing and auto-reverse recording and playback. A pair of small, single-driver speakers is part of the system, and a headphone jack is included for private listening.

The Magnavox AZ8700GY, a real mighty-mite of a system, will be available in September with a $320 price tag.

High-End Yamaha

I think the Yamaha YST-C11 is the best sounding of the three, but is three times the price of the others. The YST-C11 consists of a CD player, an FM/AM tuner, a dual-cassette tape recorder/player, an integrated stereo amplifier with four-band graphic equalizer, a wireless remote control and two speakers.

The CD-player offers direct access, random play and full programmability. The FM/AM tuner allows direct entry of station frequencies, as well as 10 presets. The tape deck has auto-reverse playback on both cassettes, as well as Dolby B noise reduction. The speakers are of excellent quality, each with a 4.75-inch mid-range and 1-inch tweeter. The wireless remote contains 34 buttons, enough to control every function of the YST-C11.

The system also includes a built-in timer, so you can be awakened (or lulled to sleep) by your favorite radio station, CD or tape -- or even record a radio program while you're away. The Yamaha YST-C11 desktop audio system sells for $999, and is available now.
