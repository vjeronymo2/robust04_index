Language: Spanish Article Type:BFN [Text] Brasilia, 21 Mar -- Bolivian Foreign Minister Antonio Aranibar said here today that U.S. Vice President Al Gore is interested in knowing the positions of Bolivia, Argentina, and Brazil regarding the continental summit organized by U.S. President Bill Clinton for December in Miami. Bolivia was the first country visited by Gore on his tour to these three countries. Aranibar met with the U.S. vice president during the eight hours he was in Bolivia yesterday before traveling to Buenos Aires, where he finds himself today. Aranibar told EFE that Gore had expressed his wish to receive from the Latin American leaders "topics for the future inter-American dialogue," an initiative that demands appropriate preparation. Gore also discussed drug enforcement with Bolivian President Gonzalo Sanchez de Lozada and other Bolivian personalities, although it was "not the central subject" of conversation, Aranibar said. Following his visit to Bolivia, Gore is currently in Buenos Aires, from where he will travel to Brasilia, the last leg of his Latin American tour. He will stay in Brasilia for six hours. The Bolivian foreign minister arrived today on an official flight from La Paz to attend a meeting of the foreign ministers of the Rio Group. The Rio Group is made up of 13 members: Brazil, Argentina, Bolivia, Peru, Chile, Colombia, Venezuela, Paraguay, Uruguay, Mexico, Ecuador, Guatemala (representing Central America), and Trinidad and Tobago (representing the Caribbean). The 48-hour Rio Group meeting began today at Itamaraty Palace, and is being held precisely to discuss positions for the summit organized by Clinton, and for a preliminary document prepared by experts from several governments and the Economic Commission for Latin America, ECLA, on "poverty, the neglected sectors, and unemployment." Regarding the summit in Miami, Aranibar said that the countries of the continent must prepare "an agenda for the new world" which must include the main subjects that will dominate the inter-American dialogue in the next few years. He also said that certain subjects connected with economic and social development, as well as regional integration -- including the possible membership of other Latin American countries to the North American Free Trade Agreement among the United States, Canada, and Mexico -- must also be included in the agenda. According to Aranibar, the inter-American subjects for the Miami summit must include participative democracy, the struggle against corruption, the stability and solidness of the democratic institutions, and rational development.
