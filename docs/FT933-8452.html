930816 FT 16 AUG 93 / Recovery fails to boost start-ups THE ECONOMIC recovery has not yet stimulated growth in the number of business start-ups, says National Westminster Bank. The NatWest quarterly start-up index, published today, shows that about 90,000 companies started trading in the second quarter of this year. This compares with 95,000 in the first three months and 100,000 in the second quarter of last year. The total of 185,000 for the first six months of this year is unchanged on the figure for the second half of last year. Mrs Jane Bradford, head of NatWest small-business services, said: 'Although there are signs that recovery in the small-business sector is under way, the recession has hit small firms hard and, as a result, they are still very cautious about future prospects.' She forecast that, if small-business confidence continues to grow, about 370,000 companies would start trading this year. There were 400,000 start-ups last year. Sole traders made up 60 per cent of start-ups in this year's second quarter, compared with 50 per cent for the same period in 1987. New businesses took on an average of four full-time staff, compared with five in 1987's second quarter. 'Owners are thinking very carefully about the commitments they are prepared to take on, especially when other people's livelihoods are at risk as well as their own,' Mrs Bradford said. 'On average they say that they spend nearly eight months planning their business venture before they actually start up.'
