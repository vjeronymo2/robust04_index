BFN [Article by Seoul correspondent Toshihiro Ishihara; third in a series entitled "Structure of North Korea's Suspected Development of Nuclear Weapons -- North Korea and the International Community": "Irritated South Korea Concerned by the Possibility of Sanctions Provoking Military Clashes"] [Text] ROK President Kim Yong-sam called a meeting of the National Security Council to discuss North Korea's suspected development of nuclear weapons. During the meeting, the South Korean president stated with a grim expression on his face, "North Korea's nuclear program is targeting us, and we have to keep the DPRK from developing nuclear weapons by all means." This was the first such meeting that the president had called since taking office. Commenting on the North Korean nuclear issue, South Koreans have said: "The DPRK's nuclear program is putting 70 million Koreans both in the North and South at risk. The DPRK's nuclear program is a fragrant violation of the joint declaration on the denuclearization of the Korean peninsula." South Koreans consider the North's nuclear program to be a major obstacle to maintaining peace and stability in Asia. Many South Koreans believe that "if North Korea has nuclear arms, Japan will arm itself with nuclear arms." North Korean President Kim Il-song has repeatedly stated: "We have neither the capability to develop nuclear weapons nor the intention of doing so. We do not need nuclear weapons." The DPRK declared last March that it would leave the nuclear Non- Proliferation Treaty (NPT) after rejecting special inspections of facilities that were suspected of being used to develop nuclear weapons. On 13 June, North Korea announced its withdrawal from the International Atomic Energy Agency (IAEA), further deepening suspicions about its development of nuclear weapons. The ROK has called on North Korea to settle the nuclear issue based on the joint declaration on the denuclearization of the Korean peninsula on which the two Koreas agreed in December 1991. In line with the joint declaration, the two Koreas set up the North-South Joint Committee on Nuclear Management to conduct mutual nuclear inspections in addition to nuclear inspections by the IAEA, but the joint committee has remained stalled since January 1993, when meetings broke off after the two Koreas failed to find areas of agreement to carry out the mutual nuclear inspections. Inter-Korea prime ministerial talks were scheduled to be held in Seoul in December 1992, but North Korea unilaterally called them off. Working-level talks have been held since last October to discuss an exchange of envoys to make arrangements for North-South summit talks. At one point, there existed the mood at a meeting to reach an agreement on an exchange of envoys. At the eighth round of the working-level meetings in March, South Korea asserted that the North Korean nuclear issue should be resolved as a matter of priority. In reacting against the ROK's assertion, the DPRK broke off the meeting after saying, "Seoul will be turned into a sea of fire." Since then, North and South Korea have had no channel for dialogue. The DPRK has made it clear that the United States is its sole counterpart for discussing the nuclear issue, in showing no willingness to hold a dialogue with South Korea. This stand by North Korea has irritated the South. The resumption of the North-South dialogue is a precondition for holding high-level U.S.-DPRK talks. The condition for resuming the North-South dialogue was dropped because the issue of holding the third round of high-level U.S.-DPRK talks encountered rough sailing. Whenever the question has been raised about imposing sanctions on the North since it announced its withdrawal from the NPT, the ROK has persuaded the nations involved to take "mild measures against North Korea." The ROK has been concerned that hard-line measures such as sanctions could incite North Korea to provoke military clashes on the Korean peninsula. Experts on the Korean peninsula point out that debates in the UN Security Council on sanctions against the DPRK mean another "crisis" on the Korean peninsula, following the crisis that arose from an attempt by North Korean commandos to attack Chongwadae (the presidential office) following the end of the 1950-53 Korean war. South Korea has made one concession after another to North Korea in a bid to prevent a recurrence of the past tragedy, but the DPRK nuclear issue has been aggravated. South Korea, a counterpart of North Korea, has been unable to talk directly to North Korea about the nuclear issue. The ROK strongly believes that the only way to have North Korea change its stand is to push for UN sanctions in concert with Japan and the United States while seeking cooperation from Russia and China. On 15 June, former U.S. President Carter entered North Korea through Panmunjom. South Koreans are now watching intently to see what results his trip to North Korea will bring about.
