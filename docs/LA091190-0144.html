September 11, 1990, Tuesday, Orange County Edition

BRIEFCASE: SCIENCE / TECHNOLOGY;

COMPUTER COMPANIES FIGHTING A LEGAL BATTLE OVER MICROPROCESSOR PATENTS

Gilbert Hyatt, the La Palma researcher who shook the computer industry two weeks ago when he announced that he had received a patent for inventing the microprocessor after a 20-year legal fight, isn't the only one seeking royalties on microchip technology.

Stan Victor, spokesman for Texas Instruments Inc., said the company is seeking to enforce nine microprocessor-related patents that cover basic functions such as how a computer's overall system interacts with a microprocessor -- the "brains" of a computer and many other electronic devices.

Unlike Hyatt, TI is seeking to enforce patents covering specific microprocessor functions rather than a patent on the basic device itself.

"We do not think the Hyatt patent will have any effect on our litigation with Zenith Data Systems Corp.," Victor said. TI sued Zenith Data in December for patent infringement.

And on Friday, Tandy Corp. filed suit against TI, alleging that the Dallas-based firm has violated antitrust laws and has charged excessive fees to companies that want to license TI's patented microprocessor technology. Victor said the suit is a "typical counterclaim" without merit.

Victor added, however, that TI claims to hold the basic patent on the invention of the microprocessor through a patent granted to TI engineer Gary W. Boone on Sept. 4, 1973.

He said the company is not yet challenging Hyatt's patent claim in court. But with TI unwilling to accept the ruling by the patent office, the two sides could be headed for a showdown.

Tandy contends TI's patent is invalid because TI's version of the microprocessor was invented after other parties had already invented it.
