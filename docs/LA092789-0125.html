September 27, 1989, Wednesday, Orange County Edition

INTENSIVE CARE;

UNITED WESTERN MEDICAL CENTERS ON MEND AFTER DOSE OF COST-CUTTING

A United Western Medical Centers vice president was driving his BMW company car into the parking lot of the organization's Santa Ana corporate headquarters last December when he heard something that caused him to veer into a concrete bumper.

He had been informed by one of the company's newly appointed administrators, who was sitting next to him, that the fleet of 15 fancy company cars assigned to hospital brass was about to be eliminated.

"He said, 'That's a great idea.' But his actions betrayed his real feelings,' " recalled Charles S. Mack Jr., who had just become the hospital organization's chief operating officer when he advised the executive of the new cost-cutting policy.

Recruited by Directors

Doing away with company cars was symbolic of many more far-ranging changes that Mack and his partner, Douglas L. Drumwright, have initiated at United Western since Dec. 6 when they took over the financially faltering operations of the nonprofit organization.

Mack and Drumwright were recruited by United Western's board of directors to replace Wayne Schroeder, who resigned after serving as president of United Western for 21 years.

United Western directors said in recent interviews that late last year they realized an immediate change in management was necessary to avert financial disaster. Schroeder, they said, was an "optimist" and a "visionary" who had been spending large sums of money on initiating new medical and advertising programs at a time when the organization instead desperately needed to retrench.

Schroeder, who runs a consulting business out of his home in Santa Ana, refuses to directly address criticisms of his performance at United Western. He says only that he is proud of his record. "At the time I left," he contends, "the hospitals had a bright future."

Not so, said company officials. For the fiscal year ended March 31, the hospital lost $30 million to $35 million and was on the brink of bankruptcy, they said.

But the losses have slowed -- the company made a slight profit in July and August -- and company officials say it will be in the black by the end of the year.

The heroes of the turnaround are Mack and Drumwright, United Western's chief executive officer. In just 10 months they have nursed United Western -- which runs a 327-bed hospital in Santa Ana and a 248-bed hospital and 241-bed skilled-nursing facility in Anaheim -- back to financial health.

"Things were pretty dismal when they got there and the turnaround has been nothing short of phenomenal," said United Western director Thomas C. Hill.

And partly on the strength of United Western's recovery, Mack and Drumwright recently got a contract to take over the administration of Care Enterprises Inc., a Tustin nursing home chain, as it struggles to reorganize and emerge from bankruptcy.

Like hospitals nationwide, United Western's have had their profit margins squeezed by the cost-containment requirements of government and private medical insurers. Last year 53% of California hospitals lost money, according to the California Assn. of Hospitals.

At United Western the financial bleeding was a massive hemorrhage. "In December and January bankruptcy was a real option and we told the employees that. We think honesty is the best policy," said Drumwright.

During its last fiscal year, the organization suffered a huge loss. Drumwright said the company had an operating loss of $20 million and another $10 million from writedowns of hospital assets and of a bond refinancing for the Anaheim hospital.

The financial deterioration did not happen overnight. Hill, who is an accountant and member of United Western's finance committee, said the organization would have posted a $4-million loss in fiscal 1988 had it not overestimated the amount of receipts the hospital would receive from Medi-Cal and Medicare. Instead, it posted net income of $600,000.

Until they came to United Western, Drumwright and Mack had no experience in running a hospital. But they had other business experience that they thought would apply. In their resumes, Drumwright said he prepared a turnaround plan for Castle & Cook, the giant Los Angeles-based international food company, and Mack cited his stint as chief financial officer of a health-care company during its transition from losing money to profitability.

Drumwright, 44, and Mack, 38, recall with humor that their first joint business in 1983, a "doc-in-the-box" business in Northern California, was a flop. "It was our first start-up and liquidation in the first year," Mack said.

But through it all, they contend, they have learned a lot about managing troubled companies. A year ago they teamed up and formed Alpha Partners to work with start-up and failing businesses.

New Financial Officer

Drumwright and Mack took the helm of United Western and abruptly changed its course, the company's directors said. They hired a new chief financial officer and restructured the organization's finance department.

Also, they changed management at all three facilities. And throughout the organization they laid off about 300 people -- mostly management, professional and clerical staff -- got agreements to delay payments to vendors, dropped a money-losing Medi-Cal contract and generally quashed the previous management's exuberant spending.

A monument to the spendthrift tendency of the former management, hospital officials say, are the elaborate corporate offices, redecorated at great expense several years ago in a modern gray and mauve motif.

Dr. Joseph Woo, chief of staff at Western Medical Center in Santa Ana, credited Schroeder, whom he described as "a charismatic dynamo," for greatly improving the stature of United Western over the years. He praised Schroeder for helping to establish the Santa Ana hospital's trauma and renal transplant programs. "These are the things that put Western Medical on the map in Orange County," Woo said.

But he said that in the last year Schroeder had launched numerous costly new projects, frequently with little support from the medical staff. These programs, including plans for a 24-hour cancer center and high-tech "gamma knife" radiation therapy to treat brain tumors, were intended to boost United Western's image and future revenues.

"We were just spending too much money too fast," said United Western Chairman Marven Howard.

Bankers 'Horrified'

Drumwright said banks that lend money to United Western were surprised by the size of projected losses and "horrified" when the new management team talked about scrapping some projects that Schroeder had touted. "Wayne was pumping sunshine and his projections to the banks reflected that optimism," Drumwright said.

The past management, Drumwright said, too often had fallen short in execution of its proposals. For instance, he said, United Western had bought and distributed 100 personal computers to staff doctors with the idea of forming a computerized information exchange between the doctors' offices and the hospitals. But the hospital linkup was never developed. The new management team also closed an outpatient diagnostic imaging and surgical center that United Western had opened in Irvine -- which Drumwright said was losing $1 million a year.

And the very first day Drumwright and Mack took over, they said, they eliminated a controversial newspaper advertising campaign costing $50,000 a month that featured testimonials from the hospital's medical staff.

"Employees did not like those ads and were not unhappy to see them gone," said Sandy Crawford, the Santa Ana hospital's director of nursing.

In an attempt to cut costs, the new management team canceled contracts with outside companies for services such as accounting, computers, pharmacy and engineering, and began performing the services in house.

While some new medical programs have been introduced at United Western hospitals in the last 10 months, they are being kept to a minimum, Howard said. Instead of installing expensive technology to attract patients, the new management says it is trying to stress the basics of good service.

'Patient Pavilion' Plan

There are plans, for example, to create a "patient pavilion" on the first floor of the Santa Ana hospital where all outpatient services will be grouped for greater convenience.

Contrary to widespread speculation in the Orange County medical community, the new management and directors of United Western insist that they have no plans to sell any facility or close the Santa Ana hospital's trauma center as part of their cost-cutting drive.

Those who operate United Western admit there still are problems.

The Anaheim hospital first edged into profitability only in August, in response to stringent new cost controls. And Western Medical Center/Bartlett, the skilled nursing facility in Santa Ana, continues to lose money.

Also, United Western officials acknowledged that this year the patient census at the flagship Santa Ana hospital has slipped, a condition they blame partly on the loss of Medi-Cal patients and partly on a countywide decline in hospital business reflecting a trend toward more outpatient care.

So far this fiscal year, Drumwright said, United Western is $500,000 to $1 million in the red.

But there are clear signs of improvement. United Western officials say the Santa Ana hospital has returned to profitability and in July and August -- traditionally slow business months for hospitals -- and United Western as a whole has made a slight profit.

And United Western officials are confident that by staying the current course the company will make a profit of $1 million to $2 million by the end of the current fiscal year.

"We will concentrate more on nuts and bolts," director Hill said. "We are going to mind the store."
