July 20, 1990, Friday, Orange County Edition

IT'S NOT HAPPY TRAILS FOR BIKERS;

ECOLOGY: MOUNTAIN BIKES HAVE BEEN BANNED FROM THE SENSITIVE UPPER NEWPORT BAY PRESERVE UNTIL SPECIFIC TRAILS CAN BE DESIGNATED FOR THEIR USE.

On a recent Saturday, county Park Ranger Nancy Bruland pointed to the dusty bluffs rising above her in the Upper Newport Bay. Along with the established hiking path winding horizontally up the hill, there was a number of deep, bumpy ruts cutting their way down the ridge.

The ruts were largely made by mountain bikers and have been additionally eroded by more bikers, hikers and the natural water runoff in the area. Since Bruland took the job as sole county caretaker of the 115-acre Upper Newport Bay regional park, which lies to the west of Jamboree Road and north of Back Bay Drive, she has seen between five and seven mountain bikers each day on the paths. Some of them, she said, are simply unconcerned about the damage that they can do to park hills and the animal environments.

"Erosion is the biggest concern. When they blaze new trails, they wipe out the vegetation," Bruland explained, adding that since she went to work at the park late last year, several residents have told her how the number of bikers has increased steadily over the last couple of years.

So, until specific trails are designated for mountain bike riders by the county's General Park Development Plan, Bruland is prohibiting bicycles from the park, leaving them the asphalt paths at road level for their racing and riding.

Bruland is not the first to accuse some Orange County cyclists of riding roughshod over the environment. Last fall, complaints from South Laguna residents prompted county officials to abandon plans to run a bike path through part of Aliso Canyon. And in Orange, bike riders were blamed for scaring horses being ridden down a bridle path along the Santa Ana River.

Skip Lauderbaugh, a spokesman for the biking club, SHARE, expressed frustration at the Newport Bay limitations, though he said he sympathizes with Bruland's concern.

"I've lived down the street from the Back Bay for 30 years, and I've been hiking back there since I was 6 years old," Lauderbaugh said. "Instead of restricting access, they need to educate the people as to how sensitive an environment it is."

Lauderbaugh maintains that people have been breaking new trails for as long as he has lived near the park and says that since people come from all over the county to the few parks that are open to mountain bikers, the trails that remain open will have to tolerate a greater number of users.

But he and other members of SHARE agree that there has to be a greater awareness of the environment among mountain bikers. The group has produced an educational pamphlet instructing riders on biking etiquette, on the safe ways to bike and the need to stay on the trail. Their group takes the stance that all trail users in the park need to cooperate to keep the park safe for animals and plants that live there.

Bruland is not just concerned about erosion and crushed vegetation but also

possible damage to the habitats of the burrowing owl and the light-footed clapper rail. The clapper rail, a relatively uncommon bird, makes its nest in the short brush of the west-bluff side of the park. Upper Newport Bay is the home to about 70% of the birds.

"There's a lot of kids on BMX bikes, and they're cutting new trails and causing erosion, and there's no doubt about it," said SHARE member Marcko Popovich, but, he added, conscientious riders do not break new paths, speed or hog the trails. In fact, Lauderbaugh added, "we're more critical of people riding that way than others . . . and 90% of the riders are really safe people."

Lauderbaugh and Bruland agree that there has to be a recognized policy for bikers and other trail users in the Upper Bay, where for years hikers, horseback riders and cyclists have shared the road and paths. They, along with other residents of the area and naturalists, are working to plan a safe course for the park through the county's General Development Plan.

Part of the plan also needs to spell out enforcement of trail restrictions, Bruland added. Despite vigilant patrols, and help from Fish and Game rangers and local residents, some bike riders ignore the signs posted at entrances to the facility. And once they are caught, Bruland doesn't have the authority to issue citations -- though she can have the riders removed from the park by police. In most cases, she said, she tries to explain to the riders the harm that they can do to the park.

"It depends on how you approach them. . . . Sometimes they'll listen and pay attention and sometimes they tell you to, frankly, mind your own business," she said. "I have found that it's a real me-type of generation around here."

Though the blame does not entirely fall on the shoulders of mountain bikers, Bruland feels that until a policy can be worked out that balances the need of the bikers with the protection of the resources, no bikes should be allowed on the dirt paths inside the park. Popovich agrees, though he hopes that with speed limits and additional education for cyclists using the park, sufficient trails can be maintained.

And SHARE also sponsors a program that helps revegetate land that has been eroded by human activities by planting native plants and by shoring up eroding bluffs. Members of the group have spoken with Bruland and are making plans for future projects in the Bay area.

The County Board of Supervisors is tentatively scheduled to approve the temporary ban, along with a number of other interim park provisions, during its Aug. 7 meeting. The General Development Plan for the park is due out within the next two years, county officials said.
