941111 FT 11 NOV 94 / Nutritionists offer a la carte menu: How the recommendations clashed with the industry's taste / Salt consumption 'should be reduced by third' Trying to help the public to choose foods for a healthier diet seemed to the doctors and nutritionists who wrote yesterday's report on prevention of heart disease to be simply a practical extension of years of work. Little new science is revealed in the report by the Committee on Medical Aspects of Food Policy (Coma). The once-a-decade review of cardiovascular disease and ways to prevent it largely reiterated previous findings. The new step, though, is to identify the sort of trade-offs the public could make to achieve widely agreed targets on consumption of fat and other food types. A portion of steamed white fish, for example, would save 33g over a portion of deep-fried fish. But when news of the committee's initiative leaked this summer, large sectors of the food industry mounted a heavy lobbying campaign against it. To many manufacturers it seemed like an attempt to dictate the nation's diet. Worse, it seemed like an attempt to single out products unfairly. Why cut biscuits, for example, when you could achieve the same reduction in fat consumption by eating less cheese? It also seemed to steer consumers away from highly processed foods towards fruit, vegetables and other products closer to the farm gate. United Biscuits, Cadbury Schweppes and Tate & Lyle were but three companies who with their trade body,the Food and Drink Federation, vigorously protested to the government. 'I was completely taken by surprise,' said Professor Michael Marmot, Coma chairman and head of the department of epidemiology and public health at University College London's medical school. 'We agree with previous committees on what constitutes healthy eating,' he added yesterday. 'We are very careful not to say which were 'killer' foods. But we are offering people a choice. If you eat a dozen biscuits a day you have to trade off by reducing something else.' He hopes the Department of Health, through its Nutrition Task Force and other bodies, will disseminate the report's recommendations to the public. Through such education he hopes consumers will modify their eating habits. The department tried to defuse the row yesterday. Dr Kenneth Calman, the government's chief medical officer, said the government's policy remained the food consumption targets identified in 'The Health of the Nation,' the health strategy laid out in a 1992 white paper. Moreover, the food guidelines issued yesterday were only an attempt to express the overall change needed in the nation's diet to meet those targets; and people were free to pick and chose from the list to meet the consumption targets. Food manufacturers seemed broadly to accept the approach except the salt makers. Mr Michael Mackenzie, director-general of the Food and Drink Federation, said that as consumers' awareness and tastes change, companies were likely to adapt their products, Already some 70 per cent of Wrigley's chewing gum and 40 per cent of Coca Cola's drinks are sugar-free. City analysts suggested that given the sort of trade-offs people could make within the committee's guidelines, it would be hard to assess how consumption patterns would change. Tate & Lyle, for example, might lose on sugars but gain on other sweetners while Associated British Foods could lose on cakes but gain on breads. However, change is slower in the UK than in some other markets. For example, Cadbury Schweppes makes a sugar-free, own-label chocolate for J. Sainsbury, the largest UK supermarket chain. But it offered it only because the retailer wanted it. United Biscuits has already responded to US consumers. More than 20 per cent of its output in the US consists of fat-reduced and fat-free biscuits as against 5 per cent in the UK. Nutritionists point out, however, that there is a crucial difference between companies creating new, premium-priced niche products such as reduced-fat biscuits, and reducing fat, sugar and salt in their main products. The latter approach is a more direct attempt to affect what people eat and nutritionists are planning to keep up the pressure on companies to adopt it. Heinz is one of the few companies to make reductions across a range of main products.
