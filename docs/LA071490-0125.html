July 14, 1990, Saturday, Home Edition

SO. BAPTIST UNIT FIRES ANTI-SMUT SALVO IN NEA DISPUTE

A policy committee of the 14.9 million-member Southern Baptist Convention has told members of Congress the denomination will call for abolition of the National Endowment for the Arts unless strict controls are enacted barring federal funds for "obscene, highly offensive, morally repugnant and sacrilegious art."

The Baptist group -- the nation's largest Protestant denomination -- became the first major church body to formally demand strict and religion-oriented controls over the NEA since a controversy broke out last year over endowment funding of controversial artists and artworks.

The new action was taken by the convention's Nashville-based Christian Life Commission, which is empowered to take positions on behalf of the entire denomination, according to commission spokesman Louis Moore. Moore said the commission sent letters to every member of Congress on June 10.

"Clearly, those who are responsible for awarding grants at the NEA have (funded), and will continue to fund, activities which most Americans find highly offensive," said commission executive director Richard D. Land. Land noted that the annual meeting of the Southern Baptist Convention in New Orleans last month had been unable to act on the anti-NEA resolution because the issue came up for action the last day of the meeting, when a quorum was absent.

"We have no doubt that if the convention had been given the opportunity to act upon this resolution, it would have overwhelmingly approved it," Land said.

The commission's letter to Congress complained about photographs by the late Robert Mapplethorpe and Andres Serrano -- the images that set off the controversy last year. It also questioned the propriety of NEA grants to other artists whose work has been the subject of bitter attacks by right-wing religious organizations and conservative politicians.
