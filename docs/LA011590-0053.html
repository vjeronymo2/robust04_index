January 15, 1990, Monday, Orange County Edition

ORANGE COUNTY FOCUS: SAN JUAN CAPISTRANO;

TOP PRIORITY PUT ON KEEPING OPEN SPACE

Open space preservation, a new civic center and financial concerns are the issues facing San Juan Capistrano in 1990, Mayor Gary L. Hausdorfer says.

If city officials have their way this year, a parcel of open farmland in the north end of town will be preserved by voters in a special election scheduled for April.

Hausdorfer targeted a proposed, $21-million general-obligation bond issue to be used toward the purchase of the 120 acres of open space for parkland as the single most important issue for the coming year.

"This is one of the last opportunities we have to preserve the signature of San Juan Capistrano," Hausdorfer said. "There aren't any other areas of open space like it in the city."

To maintain the rural aspects of this 213-year-old community, which became a full-fledged city in 1961, the city decided to sponsor the bond issue as a way of keeping the land out of the hands of private developers.

Some developers have said they would like to convert the acreage, covered with dying orange trees and bisected by two major creeks, into residential and commercial projects. Former farming families who have owned the land for generations are waiting for the election outcome before making a decision on selling, relatives have said.

Other issues to watch in the coming year include a move forward on plans to build a new civic center in the middle of town, one block north of Mission San Juan Capistrano, Hausdorfer said.

He said the staff has outgrown the current facility on Paseo Adelanto and also complained that it is not centrally located. The aluminum building was originally intended as a temporary facility when it opened in the late 1960s, and has been expanded several times since then.

The new city hall would be located next to the library, on a site now occupied by asphalt tennis and basketball courts. The recreation facilities would be relocated, Hausdorfer said.

Financial issues for next year include the council's recent decision to back away from building a joint sewage-treatment plant with the neighboring Capistrano Beach Sanitary District.

Because a joint plant would cost $30 million, city officials have decided to pursue more sewage-treatment capacity at a Dana Point plant that now handles the city's wastes.

"I think the 1990s will see San Juan Capistrano manifest itself as a mature city," Hausdorfer said. "We would like to be a leader in regional issues for South Orange County." WENDY PAULSON
