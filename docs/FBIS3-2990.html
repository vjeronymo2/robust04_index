Language: Chinese Article Type:BFN ["Political Talk" column by Shih Chun-yu (2457 0689 3768): "Common Interest Is the Crux of Sino-U.S. Relations"] [Text]China and United the States Are Still Deadlocked on the Human Rights Issue After winding up his trip to Beijing, U.S. Secretary of State Warren Christopher left Beijing to visit Russia. During his short visit to China, Christopher again applied pressure on the human rights issue, but China remained tough. Will the United States "wield" the "magic wand" of denying China most-favored nation [MFN] status? It seems unlikely, or we can say that both parties will always set store by their common interests and not come to a rupture. Chinese leaders have pointed out on many occasions: Sino-U.S. relations have never been based on common ideology and values, but on common interests. This is a pertinent remark. Since Richard Nixon and Henry Kissinger visited China more than two decades ago, and since the 4 June incident, there have been frequent "cold wars" in Sino-U.S. relations, but never have they fallen out with each other. In the final analysis, this is because the common interests of the two countries, which still servs as a basis, have never changed. Although the two countries have differing views on the human rights issue, China is still ready to discuss it with the United States on the basis of equality in order to increase understanding. It is known to everyone that China gradually is improving human rights, but the United States has no reason whatsoever to pressure China into accepting a U.S.-style concept of human rights.

A Japanese Newspaper Attacks the United States for Applying Pressure on China

ASAHI SHIMBUN carried an editorial on 15 March. From some of its unbiased remarks on this issue, it is not difficult to see where the rights and wrongs lie. The paper says: "Japan is very much concerned about what China will become. China is now Japan's second-largest trading partner, while Japan is China's largest. If China is thrown into chaos, Japan will be tremendously affected. Therefore, Japan does not want to see a deterioration of relations between China and the United States, both of which are nations friendly to Japan." The paper added: "China holds that, as far as developing countries are concerned, 'the rights to survival and development are the basis of human rights.' This argument is not hard to understand. The U.S. practice of forcing China to make concessions on the issues of democratization and human rights is too impatient and is disgusting." Moreover, the paper also has said something to China. It says: "Respect for human rights is a world trend. As the economy develops, it is also natural that people are pressing growing political demands. If China wants to have long-term stability, it needs to attach importance to democratization and human rights." Let us look at the following fact: The Chinese and U.S. foreign ministers reached a five-point agreement in their talks. It includes a memorandum on prison labor products signed between the two parties. If the United States makes a request, it can go to a suspected prison within 60 days to investigate whether prison labor products are still exported. These, of course, are part of the steps China has taken to improve human rights. A Huge Chinese Delegation Will Go to the United States Next Month [subhead] China's democratization must be developed gradually; it cannot be copied from Western practice. This is China's national condition, and it will not waver on this. As the people's living standards improve and as education is popularized on a larger scale, democratization and reform of the political structure naturally will advanced simultaneously. However, in the course of such major reforms as the market economy, there must be an appropriate degree of macroregulation and control in terms of stability, development, and the deepening of reform. Otherwise, if society were to be thrown into chaos, a large country with a population of 1.2 billion would not be able to sustain it. A case in point is the failure of Russia and Eastern Europe in their overdrastic democratization and democratic reform. Based on their common economic interests, it is advantageous to both China and the United States to expand economic cooperation and exchange. China will dispatch a high-ranking 200-man delegation to the United States next month to discuss economic cooperation in 800 industrial and agricultural projects. This is beneficial to the rapidly recovering U.S. economy and to China's construction. Barring ulterior motives, we really cannot see any benefit that can be derived from exerting pressure on China, which leads to losses to both parties.
