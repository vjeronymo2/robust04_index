921110 FT 10 NOV 92 / Cheaper electricity at risk, power chief warns THE CHIEF executive of National Power, the UK's largest electricity generator, warned yesterday that protection of vested interests in the energy industry may jeopardise the prospect of cheaper electricity. Mr John Baker said that 'though it is inevitably going to be a slow process, I have no doubt that electricity prices in the UK will continue to fall in real terms as competition bites and cost savings continue to be passed to the end user.' He added, however, that this scenario assumed no outside intervention to protect vested interests and to reduce the impact of market forces. Mr Baker's warning was made at a Financial Times conference in London on World Electricity, as he prepared to give evidence in the House of Commons today to the select committee inquiring into the future of coal. Mr Baker said the reorganisation of the UK electricity industry had shown there was no 'immutable law which says electricity prices can only go up, not down.' Competition was already providing significant impetus towards lower prices when in Europe as a whole real prices tended to remain stubbornly high. 'One reason is that a number of European countries have preferred a policy of subsidising uneconomic domestic energy sources to buying more competitively prices fuel in open world markets,' he said. Mr James Hann, chairman of Scottish Nuclear, speaking at the same conference, called on the government to take a longer term approach to energy planning and to move more rapidly towards a coherent energy policy for the UK. Mr Hann said that market forces may not be sufficient alone to guarantee security of supply at 'competitive prices and without damaging the environment beyond repair.'
