Language: Spanish Article Type:BFN [Report by William Cespedes from Washington, DC; from the "Radio Reloj Newscast"] [Text] Costa Rican Foreign Minister Bernd Niehaus was hurt after his hopes to become OAS secretary general were dashed. Niehaus said the Inter-American system had committed another injustice against Costa Rica. The pressures exerted against the small countries were the main element that kept Costa Rica's 40-year-old hope of becoming OAS secretary general from coming true. In 1954, former Costa Rican President Otilio Ulate Blanco was also defeated when he ran for the post of OAS secretary general. On this occasion, the excuse was the OAS needed a former president, and the big countries, headed by the United States, nominated Colombian President Cesar Gaviria. Foreign Minister Niehaus said the attitude assumed by some of the small countries in light of those pressures was the main element of the defeat. [Begin Niehaus recording] We did have a big problem with four Caribbean banana-producing countries. This problem has been present for some time, but it was used within the last few days to separate us and break the vote. We have had a very clear position regarding the banana issue. We are working on this, and, as far as I know, an agreement was reached in Brussels. This was done, however, without disregarding our national interests. Our country was interested in promoting my candidacy, but it could not do it at the cost of other interests. On different occasions I told the banana-producing countries our position was one of finding solutions that would not affect the Caribbean or our interests; we talked based on this issue. Surprisingly enough, since 25 March, when the last successful contacts were made, these countries started talking about the Central American position. They started saying not only Costa Rica but other Central American countries were against them. What each country does is its own business. We cannot meddle in what Guatemala, Panama, or Honduras do about this. This began to sound like excuses, but they never told us they were going to break the commitment; this happened only today. Yesterday during the Caribbean Common Market-Central America meeting support for Costa Rica was reiterated, but those four countries said nothing. Those countries are St. Lucia, St. Vincent, Grenada, and Dominica. Those countries remained silent, so I asked them if they authorized me to tell the press everything was all right and that nothing had changed. One of them, St. Lucia, said he had not talked about this. I told him I was not talking about a specific problem, rather, the vote, and he said yes. I then met with you and told you all was fine, but there was an underlying problem. Last night I was told a meeting was going to be held and very faithful and serious countries, countries firm to their commitment, such as Jamaica, Trinidad-Tobago, Barbados, and Bahamas, said they would do all in their power to fix the situation. Shortly before the voting began the St. Lucian ambassador came to see me and said there were four countries that were not happy with Costa Rica's banana policy. I told him I did not know why they were unhappy, because the Costa Rican president had been quite clear about this situation. Well, he said, let's wait and see what happens. At no time did we say--and this is all rumor--Guyana would not support me; quite the contrary, yesterday the foreign minister clearly told me he would support me. Today he told me he was told to vote against. Well. There was one Central American dissident. I do not want to mention the name because I do not wish to harm bilateral relations. [end recording] These were statements by Foreign Minister Bernd Niehaus at the Costa Rican Embassy in Washington after studying the results of the election in which he was defeated by Colombian President Cesar Gaviria. According to versions heard throughout the diplomatic media in Washington, DC, Panama was the Central American country that at the last minute changed its vote and supported Colombia, as did the four Caribbean countries Foreign Minister Niehaus mentioned. This closes another chapter in Costa Rica's hopes to hold the highest office at the OAS. [Panama City ACAN in Spanish at 1719 GMT on 28 March cites San Jose LA NACION as reporting: "Panama broke its promise to Costa Rica and voted for Colombian President Cesar Gaviria for OAS secretary general." The ACAN report adds "Dominica, Ecuador, Grenada, Guyana, St. Lucia, St. Vincent, and the Grenadines changed their vote at the last minute, thus enabling President Gaviria's victory.]"
