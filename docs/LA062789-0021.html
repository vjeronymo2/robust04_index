June 27, 1989, Tuesday, Home Edition

GIAMATTI SHOULD CALL IN THE 'BULLPEN' TO JUDGE ROSE

Pete Rose will probably strike out in his attempt to get the federal courts to prevent baseball's Commissioner A. Bartlett Giamatti from deciding his fate. But "Charlie Hustle" does have a point in arguing that Giamatti may have compromised his objectivity when he wrote a letter to a federal judge in Cincinnati, commenting on Ronald Peters' cooperation with the baseball investigation.

Peters, a convicted drug trafficker, was facing a stiff sentence. Giamatti wrote to the sentencing judge, advising him of Peters' cooperation, calling it "significant and truthful." The obvious purpose of the letter was to reward Peters for his help in the baseball investigation.

In publicly declaring Peters' damning testimony against Rose to be "truthful" -- without having heard from Rose himself -- Giamatti appears to have prejudged the credibility of a major witness. This is simply unfair. Rose has essentially called Peters a liar, and Rose's lawyers have alleged that the "betting slips" Peters has provided to the media -- and presumably to the commissioner -- were altered. Since Peters sought to trade his information for favorable treatment, there is good reason to approach its veracity with several shakers full of salt. It may well be believable -- especially if it is corroborated by objective evidence -- but it certainly cannot be believed simply because Peters says it is true.

If Giamatti is the judge and jury in the Rose case -- as he claims he is -- then he should not be making public (or even private) assessments of the credibility of a crucial witness, before that witness has been subjected to the truth-testing process of confrontation and cross-examination. Would Giamatti really be open to the possibility of now disbelieving Peters? If so, would he have to write a correcting letter to the judge?

Giamatti is not bound, of course, by the provisions of the Bill of Rights, or even by the common-law system of determining truth. Baseball is a law unto itself. It has a unique status as our national pastime, a status recognized by its exemption from antitrust laws that are applicable to other big-time sports. But the public has the right to expect that major league baseball will be fair.

There is nothing inherently wrong with the commissioner of baseball expressing his opinion on the value of a witness' cooperation. Nor is there anything wrong with him writing to a sentencing judge on behalf of a convicted defendant awaiting sentence, even if that letter was part of a deal for that defendant's cooperation with his investigation. Prosecutors do that all the time.

And that is the crux of the issue. Giamatti seems to be acting more like a prosecutor -- a careful and fair one, to be sure -- than like a judge. The investigation appears similar to grand jury inquiries being conducted by an earnest prosecutor determined to learn the truth.

But under the American system of justice, a grand jury only indicts; it does not convict. An independent judiciary, not involved in the investigation, passes the ultimate judgment of guilt or innocence only after hearing both sides. This "adversary system of justice" is widely regarded as the best protection against bias, both conscious and unconscious.

Whether Giamatti eventually wins or loses the federal court suit filed against him by Rose -- and I predict he will win -- he should take Rose's charges of prejudgment seriously. In a high-profile case like this one, it is important that justice not only be done, but also appear to be done. Of course, Giamatti must believe that he can still be fair. And he may even be right. But Pete Rose fans will never be satisfied if the commissioner relies on Peters' testimony after having publicly prejudged his credibility.

Giamatti should proceed with his investigation. If, after hearing Rose's side of the story, he concludes that Rose is guilty of betting, then he should issue his report as an "indictment." That indictment should then be presented to a completely neutral panel of distinguished arbitrators who had nothing to do with the investigation. That panel, which could include such luminaries as retired Supreme Court Justices Arthur A. Goldberg Jr. and Lewis Powell and others of similar unimpeachable integrity, would make the ultimate decision. They would hear both sides of the case, make credibility determinations about the witnesses and render their decision of guilt or innocence.

Under that approach, everything Giamatti has done up to now would be perfectly acceptable. He would be a strong advocate for the integrity of baseball. But in America, it is regarded as unfair to be both investigator and judge. If and when Bart Giamatti releases his indictment of Pete Rose, he should be taken out for a relief pitcher.
