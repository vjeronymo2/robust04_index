920221 FT 21 FEB 92 / Scottish businesses deliver double blow to Tories THE Conservative party's hopes of securing the backing of Scottish businesses for the preservation of the constitutional status quo in Scotland suffered a double blow yesterday. The Confederation of British Industry in Scotland said it now accepted that constitutional change was inevitable and that 'the present mechanisms of government could be improved'. But it said the alternatives on offer from the opposition would be bad for the Scottish economy and challenged all parties to offer better proposals. Earlier, a cross-party group of businessmen heading medium-sized companies launched an organisation named Business Says Yes - to constitutional change. They said many business people were 'eagerly awaiting the establishment of Scotland's own government' and believed it would be good for business. Leading Scottish financial institutions on Wednesday expressed strong misgivings about the possible effects on their business of a devolved Scottish parliament of the kind proposed by Labour and the Liberal Democrats. According to a trade association survey most believed it would damage the financial sector. The acknowledgment by the CBI in Scotland that the constitutional status quo was no longer tenable came only 10 months after a survey showed that three-quarters of its members were opposed to any form of Scottish assembly, with more than half favouring no change. Mr Alasdair MacCallum, chairman of the CBI in Scotland, said that its main objection was to the proposal by Labour that a devolved Scottish parliament could impose higher income tax in Scot-land than in the rest of the UK. But independence, proposed by the Scottish National party, was the most dangerous option of all, he said. In Edinburgh, Mr Tom McGregor, chairman of Business Says Yes, said a Scottish parliament would be responsive to the needs of Scottish business. It would end the over-taxation of Scottish business caused by the fact that business rates in Scotland are 40 per cent higher than in England. It believes the business community would be represented in a Scottish parliament in a way that it is not through the Conservative party, which it sees as unpopular and out of touch in Scotland. In the Commons yesterday Mr John Major, the prime minister, accused the Scottish National party of 'profoundly misleading' the Scottish people in its campaign to end the 1707 union, He insisted: 'The union between England and Scotland is greater than the sum of its parts and always has been.'
