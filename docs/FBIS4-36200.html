BFN [By Suman Chattopadhya] [Text] The Rao-Clinton joint news conference just concluded. When all of us were coming down the stairs of the White House after the conference, a young journalist from Gujarat, who was a member of the prime minister's entourage, loudly told a senior journalist from THE WASHINGTON POST, "Had this been our country then at least for the sake of courtesy, we would have asked your president some questions, but you have insulted our prime minister by not asking him any questions." The American journalist was not at all offended by this remark. He smiled and softly told the youth to come with his prime minister when he visits the White House after five years. He then added that at that time, you will not find us as indifferent. This spontaneous anger of the unexperienced Indian journalist was 100 percent genuine. It is really difficult for professional Indian journalists to tolerate the disregard shown toward their prime minister by American journalists. This proves that American journalists do not give any importance to the country where about one sixth of the total world population lives. Rajiv Gandhi was able to create a lot of enthusiasm in the U.S. media during his first visit to the United States in 1985, but Narasimha Rao failed to generate even a fraction of that enthusiasm. The major U.S. newspapers gave wide coverage to the press conference which was addressed by Rajiv Gandhi at the National Press Club in Washington. This time, the newspapers thought it a waste to spare space to cover Rao. During Rajiv Gandhi's visit, THE NEW YORK TIMES criticized the Reagan administration for trying to impress Gandhi by showing robots to him. The newspaper said "India must be reckoned with in deeper terms than by displaying robots or space capsules to as sophisticated a man as Rajiv Gandhi." This time, due to the request made by the Indian Embassy, only THE WASHINGTON POST agreed to write a short report on Rao's visit. It wrote: "A small bald figure in a Congress Party grey suit, a man with a shy manner and a low voice, a man who is a total stranger to the (?sound bite) [as published], the Indian prime minister is not easily recognizable as a politician." It is unfortunate for the senior, learned, and experienced prime minister of India that popular television networks like NBC or ABC did not feel it necessary to give even one minute's coverage to him. Did Rao really deserve the neglect which was shown him by the U.S. media? Rajiv Gandhi was able to attract American journalists due to his amiable disposition, westernized outlook, and alluring personality. Rao had something valuable in his pocket which the Americans should have been attracted to, that is, a market economy. The media did not give much importance to Narasimha Rao this time as they are not very aware of the developments which are taking place in the Indian subcontinent. Despite this, there is no denying the fact that India will soon become very important to the U.S. media just as China has become. The Clinton administration was reluctant to give most favored nation treatment to Beijing as its human rights record was not satisfactory. This enraged the U.S. media. The U.S. media is of the opinion that nothing should be done which would hamper bilateral trade ties with China. Americans continuously shed tears for the cause of democracy, but why is the U.S. media so keen to keep the communist dictators of Beijing happy. This is so because their only objective is trade. If Beijing does not get most favored nation status due to the rashness of Clinton, then U.S. industrialists will be affected and the interests of the country will be hampered. Their main objective is to protect the economic interests of the United States. It should be achieved at any cost even if it is necessary to renounce the country's policies. It has been observed that Beijing did not make any special efforts to secure most favored nation status. The industrial corporations and the U.S. media are working together to protect the interests of the Chinese leaders, Deng Xiaoping and Li Peng. India will also be able to acquire this importance after the United States becomes involved in its market. The first and foremost requirement is to attract U.S. capital. During his visit to the United States, to some extent Narasimha Rao has been able to attract U.S. businessmen to India. This is the most significant aspect of his visit to that country. These people were able to realize the amount of profit they can make by investing in India. It is true they have been impressed by the reform measures initiated by Rao and Manmohan. U.S. businessmen have started realizing that this country of hermits and snake charmers have a middle class population of about 200 million people, who have great purchasing power. There was no one in the United States to speak in favor of India in the past. At present, a lobby of industrialists has been formed in favor of India. Some key persons of giant U.S. corporations have also joined this group. A new horizon of prospects has been created in front of their eyes -- the largest free market of the 21st century. Their eagerness towards this country is new and it must be admitted that India will gain from it. The first benefit is economic. This is because India is desperately in need of foreign capital, particularly in the sphere of infrastructure. The second gain is political. Due to growing U.S. interests in India, other sensitive political issues like Kashmir and the nonproliferation issue will gradually lose significance. President Clinton and his colleagues did not make any attempt to embarrass Rao on these issues during his recent visit. The main reason behind this is pressure from industrialists. If the United States remains passive on these issues, then Islamabad will have to suffer. In fact, it is the intention of India to blunt the sharp mouth of Islamabad. To sum up, it may be said that in this post Cold War era, it is essential to improve ties with the United States. It is necessary to play the economic card well to establish this relationship. Narasimha Rao's visit has created prospects for the establishment of friendly relations between the two countries. This relationship will be further strengthened if the pledges given by Rao and Manmohan regarding economic reforms are speedily implemented. Once capital is invested in this country, then it is bound to influence the White House and the U.S. media. After this, if the Indian prime minister goes to address a joint news conference with the U.S. president, then all cameras will be focussed on the Indian prime minister. This will fulfill the predictions made by the American journalist to the Gujarati youth.
