Language: English Article Type:BFN [Report by Evgenia Drumeva] [Excerpts] Sofia, January 7 (BTA) -- Today's press allegations that President Zhelyu Zhelev intended to assume direct command of the Bulgarian Army provoked a chain of reactions. Speaking to a National Radio reporter, President Zhelev strongly denied the allegations and described them as "insinuations serving dubious political interests." Zhan Videnov, leader of the Bulgarian Socialist Party (BSP), did not deny he had prior information of the scandal which broke out in today's press and told a radio interviewer the issue had been addressed indirectly at his meeting with Filip Dimitrov, leader of the Union of the Democratic Forces (SDS), two days ago. In a radio statement Edvin Sugarev, MP of the SDS and leader of one of the SDS-affiliated parties, said the president should be asked to tender his resignation. A front-page story in TRUD claims that President Zhelev intended to issue a decree by which he would assume direct command of the Bulgarian Army. The daily runs in full a November 1993 letter from President Zhelev to the defense minister and the chief of General Staff, according to which the advisers at the president's military office would get ranks from second-in-command of the armed services up to deputy chiefs of the General Staff. The daily quotes anonymous Constitutional Law experts as describing the act as "a flagrant violation of the Constitution." The Socialists' daily DUMA, says that Army General Lyuben Petrov, chief of the General Staff, declined to comment on the president's letter yesterday. 24 CHASA quotes an anonymous MP as saying that Prime Minister Lyuben Berov learned about the letter later (after it had been sent) but did not step in to defuse the scandal and his relations with the president had been tense ever since. The MP told 24 CHASA that advisers to the president had been building structures parallel to key units of the cabinet, which was one of the major reasons for the meeting between the SDS and BSP leaders. We had fears over suggestions to give the president greater prerogatives than the ones outlined in the Constitution, Zhan Videnov said in a radio interview. According to him, these ideas originated with "intellectual, journalistic and social circles close to the office of the president" rather than with the office itself. "I must say we were informed in advance of the press scandal over the prerogatives of the president's military office which flared up today. We see it as an alarming symptom and though indirectly, this subject was part of our discussion," Zhan Videnov said about his meeting with SDS leader Filip Dimitrov. In a statement quoted on National Radio Dr. Zhelev commented, among other things, on the meeting between the SDS and BSP leaders, describing it as "a result of the grave crisis in both political forces, which arouses fear of the democratic institutions." The president disapproved the idea of a parliament-appointed caretaker government, as well as of the idea that parliament should work in the run-up to the elections. Edvin Sugarev described today's press allegations as "a huge scandal... for which President Zhelev's resignation should be demanded." The president's spokesman Valentin Stoyanov made an official statement on the press allegations late this afternoon. [passage omitted]. The statement was broadcast on national radio and television. In its main evening newscast television said three Socialist MP's have made an official request today for copies of the president's letter to the Defense Ministry and of the decree. [passage omitted]
