BFN [Weekly news conference by President Fidel V. Ramos with domestic and foreign correspondents at Malacanang Palace in Manila -- live; passages in brackets in Tagalog] [Excerpts] [passage omitted] [Ramos] I also have a statement in regard to the Korean peninsula nuclear issue. You will recall, ladies and gentlemen, that I made a statement on this just four days ago. But of course there are now new developments. This is in regard to the still unconfirmed reports -- these are still unconfirmed -- that Pyongyang intends to withdraw from the International Atomic Energy Agency [IAEA]. And if that step has not yet been officially taken by North Korea, we urge North Korea to remain in the IAEA so that an early and peaceful resolution through patient negotiation and creative diplomacy can be found to resolve this issue. Our concern here stems from the possibility of nuclear proliferation, because such proliferation is a destabilizing factor for global and regional security, and in turn will derail and disturb and possibly [word indistinct] normal economic growth and sustained development in our part of the world, if not in the entire globe itself. And as a developing country, certainly, we want to move our economic, social, and political development forward without disturbances of this nature. I also have a statement in regard to the reported testing by China four days ago, or 10 June, where a nuclear test was reportedly undertaken at its testing site at Lop Nur. And as you all know, as a signatory of the United Nations treaty on nonproliferation of nuclear weapons, the Philippines is strongly against the proliferation of nuclear weapons, and this includes observance of a testing moratorium. So that in the end we hope that all of these tests will stop through a -- or be made to cease -- through a comprehensive test-ban treaty. [passage omitted] [Correspondent Manny Mogato] With Pyongyang ominously sounding the war drums and with Seoul (?gearing) to dance to the same music, in case of actual conflict between the two Koreas, the United States -- being committed to the defense of South Korea -- will certainly be drawn into the war. Mr. President, will the Philippines be also drawn into the same conflict by virtue of our mutual defense treaty with the United States? [Ramos] No, that's very, very farfetched. You will recall that the Philippines joined in the so-called police action on the Korean peninsula -- which was occasioned by the invasion by North Korea of South Korea across the 38th parallel -- because of a UN Security Council resolution, not a defense treaty with any country. But before we could send our troops there, a law had to be passed, and this was Republic Act 573, the so-called Peptok Law, passed by Congress in September 1950. So, it's not automatic that the Philippines rushes into the fray. After all, we are not the policeman of the world. But we have expressed our concern. In my statement four days ago I cited five major reasons: -- Because of our nonnuclear provisions in the Constitution, in the basic law; -- Our membership in the IAEA board of governors, that is, Ambassador Rey Arcilla, who is based in Austria; -- Our proximity to the Korean peninsula. We are close enough, we are much closer than the United States; -- The some 13 -- to maybe more -- thousand Filipinos in South Korea; and; -- I mentioned it a while ago, the possible crippling and paralysis of economic activity in our part of the world. In which, again, [developing countries will become such a pity. The developed countries might benefit because of the war.] What about us? Those are my concerns. So, we have called for patient negotiation, consultation, and creative diplomacy so that perhaps we can find the acceptable solutions before sanctions are applied. [Mogato] Follow-up, Mr. President. As member of the ASEAN, what do you think will be the role of the ASEAN in diffusing the threats of war in the Korean peninsula? [Ramos] Well, certainly the ASEAN-member countries can take positions as we have done on a national basis. We are of course always in consultation within ASEAN with our sister countries. But as far as I know, ASEAN as a bloc has not yet come up with a position. Although in the recent senior officers' meeting of the ASEAN Regional Forum which deals with security and defense matters, there was already at that level -- this is a lower level -- expressed the concerns of the ASEAN countries about the proliferation of nuclear weapons. Of course, I think you are aware of the initiatives of Russia to convoke an international conference that will involve now the principal negotiators with the two Koreas. I hope that something will come out of this, because this is really in the realm of creative diplomacy and patient negotiation. [passage omitted]
