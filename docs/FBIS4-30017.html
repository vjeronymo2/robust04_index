BFN [Text] A stronger yen and a probable rise in US interest rates could solve some of Thailand's economic problems, says Bank of Thailand spokesman Phisit Li-atham. The weakness of the U.S. dollar, stemming from a lack of coordinated efforts among central banks and heavy selling by foreign-exchange traders anticipating intervention, is creating pressure for the U.S. Federal Reserve to increase interest rates. "The economy needs cooling," said Mr Phisit. He said inflationary pressure in Thailand is manageable. The Bank of Thailand is expected to meet next month, however, to re-evaluate the situation based on higher crude oil prices and stronger than expected export performance. Several Thai bankers estimate the Thai inflation rate to be close to 5 percent, a percentage point higher than the central bank's target of 4 percent. Mr Phisit said a Bank of Thailand study found that interest costs amount to a small portion of Thai corporations' total business costs, and the effect of higher interest rates, approximately a quarter-points above the current rate, may not be severe. A rate hike by the U.S. Federal Reserve would affect the Thai financial system and naturally push rates here higher, said Mr Phisit. "The increases are in the range expected by Thai businesses." Higher inflation bought on by higher oil prices would be partially offset by higher foreign currency earnings, gained from exports that have outperformed the central bank's target, he said. The baht has also strengthened against the dollar, standing yesterday at 25.05, the strongest in 18 months. Oil prices are quoted in dollars and Thailand tends to source lower-cost crude oil. Local refining capacity increases have also lessened the impact, said Mr Phisit. On the trade picture, Mr Phisit said in the long run, a strong yen could force Thai importers to turn to other countries, while exports to Japan could benefit. Since Thailand is currently a net importer from Japan, however, increased import costs could add to the inflationary pressure in the medium term. The net impact is the question, said Mr Phisit. "Consumption of imports could decrease and locally manufactured products increase," he said. Thais' propensity to consume imported goods from Japan, has shown little sign of change in the past, however. A recent Bangkok Bank economic forecasting model predicted that since the U.S. economy is primarily driving the world's economic growth, a U.S. slowdown could translate to a drop in Thailand's GNP growth, below the central bank's estimate of 8-8.2 percent. Japan's economy is showing signs of strength, however, and could supplement the U.S. as a market for Thai goods. Japan's economy, surprising most economist with a 3.9 percent annualized growth rate in the first quarter of 1994, saw its growth prospects increase even in a strong yen environment. And with an economy that depends on exports to the U.S., the Japanese central bank had been supporting the dollar in an effort to reduce the pressure on Washington to increase interest rates. Meanwhile, Thai Farmers Bank's executive vice-president Sombun Chinsawanon said the volatility in the international foreign-exchange market, where the yen is appreciating against the dollar and the Deutsche mark, is unlikely to make a strong impact on the Thai economy as a whole. He said the sector that will be affected directly is external trade, particularly exports to the U.S. where exporters have to hedge themselves against foreign-exchange risks, making export prices higher. But Thailand will benefit from exporting to Europe and Japan if they quote their prices in marks and yen respectively. Overall, the positive impact from exporting to Europe and Japan will outweigh the negative impact on exports to the US. "Overall, Thailand will not be losing from the situation," he said. The yen yesterday rose as high as 99 to the dollar. During the past two weeks, the baht has appreciated 12 satang against the dollar from 25.16 to 25.04. Mr Sombun said the sudden weakening of the U.S. dollar stemmed from the recent series of rate increases by the Fed.
