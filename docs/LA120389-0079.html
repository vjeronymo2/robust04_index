December 3, 1989, Sunday, Home Edition

CAPITOL CONSEQUENCES CLOSING IN ON PAT NOLAN;

LAWMAKERS: ASSEMBLY REPUBLICAN BOMB-THROWER FEELS THE STING AS POLITICS TAKES ITS TOLL AND INVESTIGATIONS GRIND ON.

Pat Nolan came to Sacramento exactly 11 years ago as a 28-year-old political bomb-thrower, one of more than a dozen new Republican legislators who called themselves "Proposition 13 babies" and vowed to wage ideological war.

Within weeks, Nolan and the other newcomers had helped engineer a coup against the moderate Republican leadership. And five years later, Nolan led another coup, installing himself as the Republican leader in the Assembly and promising all that he would deliver a GOP majority within a few years.

Despite his right-wing ideological credentials, the Nolan regime resembled that of his counterpart, Democratic Speaker Willie Brown, with whom he reached a semi-secret pact of mutual accommodation.

Brown, the master of internal Capitol politics, understood Nolan's ambition and played on it. He gave Nolan some powers over committee appointments and other matters to prop up his position as Republican leader. In return, Nolan agreed to avoid frontal attacks on Brown's speaker ship.

Nolan, whose ambitions included statewide office, used his Brown-supplied authority to install his sycophants in plum legislative positions and punish rivals.

And he dabbled in matters outside the Capitol, such as trying to handpick Republican legislative candidates and shove them down the throats of local party activists. The Republicans picked up three seats in the 1986 elections, which had Nolan crowing, despite the fact that several of the wins were by GOP candidates who had defeated his anointees in the primary.

The dumb luck that overcame Nolan's tactical errors in the 1986 elections deserted him within months.

A band of five Democratic dissidents came to Nolan with a scheme to unseat Brown by combining their votes with the Republicans. But, honoring his understanding with Brown, Nolan refused to play ball with the "Gang of Five."

A state investigation of Republicans' use of a phony letter carrying President Reagan's signature in the 1986 campaigns finally resulted in an indictment of Assemblyman John Lewis, a top Nolan political lieutenant, for forgery. There were strong indications in grand jury testimony that Nolan and other Republican strategists sanctioned use of the phony Reagan letters, but Lewis alone was indicted.

Nolan, feeling renewed heat from critics within his own caucus, finally agreed to cooperate with the Gang of Five, but it was too late. Brown staved off the challenge and recouped at the polls in 1988, cutting the Republican margin once again and forcing Nolan to give up his leadership position.

In August, 1988, FBI agents raided the Capitol offices of several legislators, including Nolan, to close a multi-year undercover investigation of influence-peddling in the Capitol.

The Capitol sting operation, which involved FBI agents posing as businessmen seeking special legislative favors, has been a yearlong sensation.

One state senator, Joseph Montoya, was indicted along with an aide on a variety of federal corruption charges, and the U.S. attorney's office continued to painstakingly build cases against other targets.

Even as that investigation continued, another political scandal was unfolding in Washington and Sacramento, regarding the collapse of Lincoln Savings & Loan. And once again, Nolan found himself embroiled because he had carried the legislation that loosened regulatory oversight of Lincoln and other state-chartered S&Ls. Nolan had been among the many recipients of campaign checks from the head of Lincoln, Charles H. Keating Jr.

Last week, a woman who had become one of Nolan's top political aides, Karin Watson, pleaded guilty to federal charges of extorting campaign checks and promises of speaking fees from the undercover FBI agents during the Capitol sting operation. And she was promised lenient sentencing in return for cooperating with investigators as they seek charges against legislators.

Nolan and Assemblyman Frank Hill, another of his one-time lieutenants, are the apparent targets. Both Nolan and Hill received campaign checks from the dummy corporation set up by the FBI.

Nolan is learning that real politics are a bit more complicated than the student variety he practiced so earnestly at USC 20 years ago. And a bit more hazardous.
