January 14, 1989, Saturday, Orange County Edition

JOSEPH N. BELL: IN WINTER, THE BEACH BELONGS TO US AGAIN

This is a sparkling, crackling clear day between the rains. I top the rise on Marguerite Street and the Pacific is suddenly spread out before me -- the closest I'll ever come in this life to infinity.

This is a Thursday, 2 hours past noon, midwinter. A few months from now, parking will be hard to find on the street above the beach in Corona del Mar, there will be a profusion of out-of-state license plates, and the sand will be crawling with people in sunglasses dragging deck chairs and small children. But today, the beach belongs to the natives. It's ours in the winter -- at least those of us who can look up from work long enough to discover we can have this magnificence almost all to ourselves.

On the grassy rim above the beach, the regulars have set up shop. An elderly couple in His and Her lawn chairs, reading books and communicating through their silence. Joggers, breathing hard, who have stopped to rest and immerse themselves in the view. Other portable chairs are scattered along the grass, mostly inhabited by older people reading but with folks just sitting and looking.

There is much to see. The winter rains have momentarily washed away the smog with which we shroud ourselves, and it's startling to realize what it could be like if we stopped fouling our own atmosphere. Santa Catalina Island in its full length stands out in sharp bas-relief, breathtaking in its size and proximity, like a lost continent that appears once or twice a year to entice us. From above, the beach looks almost entirely deserted, a vast expanse of sand broken only by multiple refuse containers, fire pits, and two small boys kicking a soccer ball while their mother idles along behind.

I drive down to the parking lot -- no fee on winter weekdays -- and find the look from above is deceptive. The beach just looks deserted; actually, there is much life here. It just has to be seen up close. As I park, several hundred nervous gulls on the sand in front of me scramble, like a crazed air squadron on instant alert. They settle down again 100 yards away, awaiting the next sign of danger. A pickup truck pulls up beside me in the almost deserted parking lot, and two men in work clothes get out. They have a bag from a fast-food restaurant, and they head across the sand, leaving a large, shaggy dog in the cab watching them forlornly.

I walk down to the seawall that parallels the boat channel to the open sea. The sun is deceptive; it proffers warmth but the wind takes it away, gusting and swirling with a real cutting edge. The water in the channel is turbulent, slapping angrily at the seawall. For the first time in my memory, there are no boats in the channel.

But people walk here, some of them bracing against the wind, some oblivious to it. A white-haired man in a Windbreaker emerges from the rocks with his small grandson who struggles along behind; they are in earnest and animated conversation. Two sets of lovers come into view, one young and in blue jeans, the other middle-aged and in street clothes. The older couple sits on a ledge behind the seawall, talking quietly, ignoring the wind. The younger couple climbs out on the jetty, hand-in-hand. A young blond woman is standing atop a rock overlooking the beach, holding a small dog in one hand and trying unsuccessfully to hold her skirt in the howling wind with the other.

Three people, unconnected, walk alone near the shoreline: a young man, a middle-aged woman and an older woman. All are walking slowly, hands deep in their pockets, mostly looking down at the sand. Two young women in office attire take off their shoes and step gingerly into the sand, deep in conversation. The water, breaking over the beach, is subdued, grayish blue, looking a little angry and surly in the bright sun.

I study Catalina and remember that, as a boy in the Midwest, I was told that if I dug a hole deep enough I would emerge in China -- a project I tried unsuccessfully a number of times. I wonder if Orange County kids are told that if they could swim far enough they would reach China. If so, on this day, they could easily believe that Catalina is China -- or maybe kids don't fantasize such things any more.

As I get back to my car, a van pulls up and three Asian men -- dressed against the elements -- get out. They talk excitedly as they assemble a complex maze of fishing gear, then set out across the sand, meeting the workers returning from their sack lunch.

I wish I had brought a book and a lawn chair, but instead I must get to work. But I'll be back. Not in the summer when a few square inches of sand are hard to come by, but in the winter when we have this glorious beach to ourselves and can watch it change character with the weather and time of day.
