August 4, 1990, Saturday, Orange County Edition

CONTROLLING INSECTS NEEDN'T AMOUNT TO CHEMICAL WARFARE

Each year about 20,000 people in this country are rushed to hospitals for pesticide poisoning.

According to Better Homes and Gardens magazine, pesticides have been linked to cancer, birth defects, respiratory ailments, neurological problems, liver and kidney damage and reproductive disorders. These chemicals bulldoze their way up the food chain, killing wildlife and polluting lakes, rivers and drinking water.

People share the planet with insects, many of which are harmful. But keeping these critters at bay need not require chemical warfare. Here is a safer, less toxic approach to home pest control.

The safest way to control bugs is preventive maintenance. The key is to think like a bug.

Walk around the house and look for places where ants, roaches or rodents can enter. Caulk window and door trim. Keep composting piles, trash cans, firewood and other potential food and nesting sites away from the home. Keep debris out of gutters. Trim branches or bushes insects can use as a bridge to the house. Eliminate standing water under, inside or near the house.

Maintaining a healthy, vigorous lawn and garden will also discourage pests. Plants suffering from stress attract bugs. Use pest-resistant plants that are well suited for the climate. A mixture of two or more grass types is often heartier than a single mono-culture. Avoid thatch buildup, and use slow-release organic fertilizers that don't shock the lawn. Regularly check and maintain the lawn's pH.

When some intervention is required, take the least toxic route. Organic, as opposed to commercial, pesticides are generally safer to use and less harmful to the environment. Although some natural pest controls like nicotine sulfate can be as toxic as chemical pesticides, most organic pesticides break down quickly in sunlight, or within 24 hours.

Organic controls use biological, botanical and mechanical methods.
