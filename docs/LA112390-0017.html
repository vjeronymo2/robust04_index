November 23, 1990, Friday, Home Edition

FOOD AND COMMON SENSE: THOSE DARN EXPERTS

"Oat bran, coffee, margarine -- every time I turn around, they're changing their minds. First they tell us one thing, then they tell us something else. I wish they'd make up their minds."

Who this they is isn't always clear. But what consumers lament are discrepancies in nutritional advice that make it hard to tell, at any given time, if you're doing the right thing and eating the right foods.

Some point a finger directly at "you nutritionists." The source of this frustration, which we hear all too often, is not professional nutritionists, who usually provide a consistent dietary message. It more likely stems from the difficulty of interpreting the latest results in light of years of previous research. Putting the two together is a tall order for scientists, let alone the general public.

Public interest in the links between diet and health has never been greater. Responding to -- and fueling -- this interest, the media provide a steady stream of "latest findings," which often make front-page news. The bold headlines look definitive, but conclusions often are drawn from single studies -- some excellent, some quite good, others seriously flawed. Sometimes two or more reports will follow in rapid succession, providing conflicting results.

Take coffee and cardiovascular disease. As a frustrated friend said: "If you don't like the advice, wait a minute." On the surface, answering the question of whether coffee raises the risk of cardiovascular disease sounds straightforward. Yet it has been addressed in at least two-dozen studies dating back more than 25 years, with inconsistent results.

The latest of these, incidentally, found that neither coffee nor caffeine increases risk of coronary heart disease or stroke. It's probably reasonable to assume, given the weight of the evidence, that drinking moderate amounts of coffee as prepared in this country won't boost risk.

Why is it so difficult to reach firm conclusions about diet and disease links? Sometimes, sample sizes are too small. In other cases, studies don't go on long enough to demonstrate whether the measures on trial are truly effective.

Another classic problem -- a phenomenon researchers refer to as "confounding" -- is failure to weigh all factors that might be associated with the disease in question. For example, since people who smoke are more likely to drink as well, exploring a link between alcohol and a particular disease is meaningless unless smoking is taken into account.

Failure to do so could lead to claiming links between alcohol and disease when, in fact, the more powerful link is with cigarettes. While that may seem obvious, it's possible that other, more subtle, "confounders" are not known at the time a study is conducted.

The real pitfall is the natural tendency to over-respond to news of the latest study with respect to a single factor in our diet. Thus, the recent study claiming oat bran had "no effect" on serum cholesterol led consumers who had jumped on the bran wagon and upped consumption of bran products to throw up their hands in frustration.

The fact of the matter is, the bulk of the evidence indicates that oat bran can help in lowering serum cholesterol, but fat in the diet, especially saturated fat and cholesterol, should be reduced at the same time.

A similar crisis in confidence in the nutrition message occurred several weeks ago following a report that so-called "trans"-fatty acids found in margarine may affect serum-cholesterol levels, raising levels of the "bad" type and lowering levels of the "good." While trans-fatty acids occur in foods naturally, they are also formed during the hydrogenation of margarine.

Again, early reports of that study failed to emphasize several important points: The authors themselves noted that their results were preliminary and don't provide a basis for changing current dietary recommendations. For one thing, earlier studies had produced conflicting results. Also, the level of trans-fatty acids consumed by study subjects was four times that normally available in the U.S. diet. And finally, only 10% to 30% of the fatty acids in margarine are the problematical trans-fatty type. (By contrast, about 60% of the fat in butter is the highly saturated kind that drives up serum cholesterol.)

However, because of the press hoopla, the net effect of this highly interesting research was to leave many people anxiously wondering whether switching from butter to margarine in the interest of controlling cholesterol had, in fact, been the right thing to do.

Of course, people should take an interest in the progress of medical research that strives to refine dietary guidelines and reduces risk of chronic disease. But history shows that the findings of the latest diet studies reported on the evening news shouldn't necessarily cause you to shift your dietary habits overnight.

Altering your morning breakfast on the basis of one study rarely makes sense or stands the test of time.

Question: I'm trying to control my fat intake, and I know that chicken skin contains a lot of fat. Is it necessary to remove the skin before baking, or can I cook the chicken with the skin on, as long as I don't eat it?

Answer: Available data indicate that fat doesn't migrate into the meat during cooking. An average half breast of broiler chicken with the skin contains more than 7 grams (about a teaspoon and a half) of fat and 195 calories. The same amount of chicken without the skin, regardless of whether you've cooked it with or without the skin, contains about 140 calories and just 3 grams of fat. And leaving the skin on during cooking produces a juicier dish.

Q: Why are emulsifiers put into processed foods? A: Like an actor who slips easily into different characters, emulsifiers perform a host of functions in numerous foods from bread to sausages. By altering the surface properties of ingredients, they allow them to combine more easily. For example, in making mayonnaise, egg yolk acts as an emulsifier.

Emulsifiers are widely used to help mix oil and water. In bakery products, they improve volume, moisture retention, uniformity and fineness of grain. They also retard staling and help condition dough. They improve the texture of pasta and inhibit clumping, of particular use when pasta is to be used in canned and frozen foods.
