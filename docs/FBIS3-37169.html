Language: Russian Article Type:BFN [Report by Sergey Leskov: "Krikalev's Debut on `Discovery' Was Successful, But the Satellite's Debut was Not"] [Text] The landing of the "Discovery" space shuttle at the Canaveral space center at 2050 on 11 February marks the end of the first ever flight when U.S. astronauts and a Russian cosmonaut have been members of the same crew. Unfortunately, the attempts to place the "Wake Shield" satellite in orbit ended in failure. Sergey Krikalev was going to work with the satellite, which had been carried in the cargo bay. The satellite was to have moved to a distance of 75 km from the space shuttle. At that distance, with no microgravitation at all and no effect from the station's exhaust gases, there would have been an experiment to grow extremely pure semiconductor films for new-generation computers. But the guidance system of the manipulator, which would have been used to move the satellite, turned out to be faulty. This failure is very regrettable, since this faulty manipulator will be used to assemble the components of the "Alfa" international station when it is created. After many attempts the astronauts did manage to obtain seven laminated electronic components of reasonable quality in the satellite factory, but they were not as fine as had been expected. According to the U.S. specialists, Sergey Krikalev performed very well, despite the relative failure of the main experiment. Any aberrations in the operation of the vehicle were instantly eliminated by our engineer. In a year's time a second Russian cosmonaut -- Colonel Vladimir Titov -- will fly in the shuttle. During this flight the shuttle will approach our "Mir" station for the first time and Titov will perform a spacewalk. A docking between "Mir" and the shuttle is planned for spring 1995. Incidentally, this week U.S. astronauts Norman Thagard, 51, and Bonnie Dunbar, 45 (wife of astronaut Ronald Sega, who was on the "Discovery" flight), started training sessions at the Cosmonaut Training Center near Moscow. The long flight by a U.S. astronaut on the Russian "Mir" station is to begin on 1 March 1995.
