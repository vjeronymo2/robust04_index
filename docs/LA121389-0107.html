December 13, 1989, Wednesday, Home Edition

JAMES FLANIGAN: INVESTMENT ISN'T A GOOD REASON TO BUY ART

What does it tell you that Old Master paintings have gone up in value twice as fast as common stocks in 1989? Or that last year, too, Old Masters -- pre-1800 paintings by prominent European artists -- had a greater rise than stocks, according to Salomon Bros., the investment firm that keeps tabs on paintings and sculpture as well as stocks and bonds.

One thing it tells you is that there are a lot of rich individuals in the world, more in the last decade than ever before. But whether art is a good investment is another matter, despite the fabulous prices being paid for Picassos and Van Goghs.

First the money. The 1980s has seen an upsurge in individual wealth, as stock prices have more than trebled since 1982 in the United States and risen more than that in Japan, where a long boom in real estate has also made land and property owners extremely rich.

And lately some of that money has been showing up in art sales. "The market for works priced between $1 million and $20 million is especially strong," says Bonnie Barrett Stretch, editor of the ArtNewsletter, who calls that the "middle market."

The higher-priced market has been simply spectacular. At the end of November, for example, the owner of a Tokyo auto-racing park paid $48.9 million for a Picasso painting -- bidding through a closed-circuit television link between Paris and Tokyo.

"It's a global business," says Salomon Bros. art analyst Harvey Katz, who estimates that Sotheby's and Christie's, the chief auction houses, move about $4.5 billion in art objects each year. And that amounts to less than half the total market, because most buying and selling is done privately through dealers.

It's an exclusive market. Citibank has set up an art advisory service to help its private banking clients -- net worth $1 million and up -- with valuations of art works and advice about collections.

Yet in some ways it's a mass market. Sotheby's boasts that while it auctions 300 art works each year at prices over $1 million apiece, it moves 230,000 works at prices under $10,000 each.

So should you try for a killing in art -- inspired perhaps by the example of Wendell Cherry, the president of Humana Inc., who bought a painting titled "Yo! Picasso!" for $5.6 million in 1981 and sold it this year for $47.8 million?

No, say seasoned pros in the worlds of art and finance. "With notable exceptions for particular objects and timing, art has not been a good investment," says Henry King, who heads Citibank's art advisory service. Paintings pay no dividends and cost money to hold -- for insurance or storage. And transaction costs are stiff: Both buyers and sellers pay 10% commissions at art auctions; dealer markups can be much more in private sales.

And the market is treacherous for the unwary. Forbes magazine recently quoted an art expert ridiculing tennis star John McEnroe for paying $350,000 for a second-rate example of Renoir's work. (Yes, there are second-rate Renoirs).

The market can even be treacherous for the wisest collectors. A Rembrandt owned by Norton Simon is now under a cloud because a Dutch government examination of the painter's works has cast doubt on its authenticity.

Finally, art values often lag the returns of other investments, and sometimes don't even keep up with inflation. Bruno Frey, professor of economics at the University of Zurich, wrote in Art & Antiques magazine recently about the British Railways Pension Fund, which began investing a portion of its assets in art in 1974, built up a collection and began selling the art works in 1987. The fund made good gains: Its art works appreciated 3.5 times. But if the fund had invested in common stocks, it would have fared better, wrote Frey -- more than four-fold appreciation in the same period.

Even the "Yo! Picasso!" that brought Wendell Cherry such a profit in the 1980s, brought a net loss to its owner in the 1970s.

So why do rich folks collect? Many reasons. Art adds class, for one thing. "It's a way of making new money into old money," observes one expert.

But what begins as a quest for status sometimes develops into true intellectual interest. The best collectors specialize in a specific style of art, often becoming scholars. That is how valuable collections are built. In one sense the spirit that drives collectors, and swells the art market today, is the same one that is bringing record attendance to U.S. museums -- a seeking for knowledge so strong and widespread that it amounts to a trend in this country. In fact, predicts futurologist John Naisbitt in his forthcoming book "Megatrends 2000," museum attendance in the 1990s will surpass that of ballparks in America.

So what does all this tell you about investing in art? Buy what you enjoy; if you want to support a local artist by purchasing his or her work, fine. But keep in mind that the work's real value may be intangible.
