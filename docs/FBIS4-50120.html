BFN [Editorial: "MFN Trading Status and Sino-U.S. Ties"] [Text] Reports say that U.S. State Department is studying and drafting various options on extending China's most favored nation [MFN] trading status, to be presented to Clinton for him to choose. The United States does not have much time or options to resolve this question. The best way is to extend China's MFN status unconditionally. This will benefit the two powers as well as the stability and prosperity of the world at large. When meeting with former Secretary of State Henry Kissinger the day before yesterday, Chinese President Jiang Zemin said that Sino- U.S. relations are not just limited to the two sides, they are closely linked to the international situation. China and the United States should maintain good relations for the sake of world peace and stability. As two big powers which can influence the world, China and the United States shoulder great responsibility for the future of the world and the destiny of mankind. We should do something for people of the world. Jiang Zemin looked at Sino-U.S. relations from the macroscopic viewpoint of the developing situation of the world as a whole. This should give U.S. policymakers much food for thought. Following the end of the Cold War, some people in Western political circles thought that the Soviet Union had disintegrated and that it was no longer necessary to attach importance to ties with China. They even thought that the 1989 Beijing disturbance could be used to force China to take the same road of the Soviet Union. Facts have totally shattered these people's dreams. China was not and will never be a card to be played by any country. China has its own weight. As a big country with the largest population and quickest economic growth in the world, China's stability and development will nurture the development of the Asia-Pacific region and also maintain peace in the region. Peace and stability are inseparable major issues which the world is now facing. China has made tremendous efforts and contributions to the solution of these two issues. The IMF, World Bank, and UN Food and Agriculture Organization and Population Organization, as well as industrial and commercial tycoons from the developed nations and the transnational corporations, have all affirmed China's achievements and are optimistic about China. The United States is the largest developed nation and the only superpower in the world. Nixon's visit to China 23 years ago opened up a new chapter in Sino-U.S. relations. Thanks to the reform and opening up introduced by China, Sino-U.S. economic and trade ties have developed rapidly and the two countries have become important trading partners. Economic and trade ties have become increasingly important in bilateral relations. This conforms with the trend of contemporary international relations whereby economic interests constitute the main factor. The increasingly close economic and trade ties between the most populous country and the most developed nation in the world will naturally be conducive to world peace and stability. The ideal option should be cooperation and construction rather than confrontation and war. A relatively open market is one of the characteristics of the U.S. economy, which has benefited the United States tremendously. The flow of cheap and good products from China, Hong Kong, and Taiwan to the U.S. market is welcomed by U.S. consumers. Meanwhile, large numbers of U.S. industrial and agricultural products have also entered China along with the country's modernization program. Many large U.S. companies have discovered that China is the irreplaceable and last big market which can offer them enormous opportunities. Under such circumstances, if the Clinton administration insist on taking the question of human rights, which has nothing to do with economy and trade, or even the question of Tibet, as bargaining chips and link these issues with the MFN status, this will never be accepted by the Chinese Government and people. It is true that China's economy will be affected without MFN status but the United States will also suffer great losses. More importantly, it will produce an impact on the situation in the Asia-Pacific region and the world as a whole. If the United States chooses the option of confrontation with China, its interests affected will not be limited to the field of economy and trade. The United States tried to hinder and block New China for a long time during the Cold War and this did not benefit it in the slightest. It was President Nixon who changed U.S. policy toward China and this is a lesson to remember. The number of people from various U.S. circles supporting unconditional extension of China's MFN status is increasing, far exceeding those who are against. Since taking office, Clinton has not made any achievements in domestic affairs or diplomacy. If China's MFN status is revoked, the grave consequences will not help him running for another term of the presidency, still less the U.S. manufacturers and consumers. The United States should unconditionally extend China's MFN status. We believe that the United States, in consideration of the long-term interests and proceeding from the overall situation, will make a rational choice.
