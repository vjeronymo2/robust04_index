May 6, 1990, Sunday, Home Edition

THE CHILDREN'S BOOKSHELF: TAKING PLEASURE FROM THE PAST;

THE DRAGON'S ROBE BY DEBORAH NOURSE LATTIMORE (HARPER & ROW: $14.95; 32 PP., ILLUSTRATED; 0-06-023719-8);

THE LADY WITH THE SHIP ON HER HEAD BY DEBORAH NOURSE LATTIMORE (HARCOURT BRACE JOVANOVICH; $14.95; 32 PP., ILLUSTRATED; 0-15-243525-5)

For the life of me I'll never understand why history is so often regarded as a dull subject, an endurance test -- that teacher and student alike must trudge through until the final date is memorized and the final grade given. If only we could all capture the spark of excitement that has so ignited Deborah Nourse Lattimore's love for it.

That love is richly displayed, in quite divergent ways, in her two new works, "The Dragon's Robe" and "The Lady With The Ship On Her Head." Lattimore expresses an affection that could only come from wandering mentally for countless hours through historical material for the sheer joy of it. She reveals herself most clearly to a fellow historiophile through her meticulous attention to accuracy and detail.

"The Dragon's Robe" is an original tale that the author has extracted from historical events in 13th-Century China. It concerns Hui Tsung, who was at once artist, poet and emperor.

A young, orphaned weaver girl named Kwan Yin after the Chinese Goddess of Mercy (a constantly recurring subject in Chinese art) leaves her village to seek work at the imperial palace. In her travels she comes upon the aging and ailing caretaker of a shrine to the dragon rain god. Taking pity on the old man, who has offered her food and shelter, she decides to stay and care for him and his beloved shrine.

They are visited in succession by two untrustworthy vassals of the emperor: first, Lord Phoenix, overseer of the emperor's rice fields, and then Lord Tiger, general of the emperor's army on the Great Wall. Through their greed and irresponsibility they manage to incur the wrath of the dragon-god, and havoc erupts.

With rice fields burning and Tatars rampaging, the little weaver desperately sets to work to appease the dragon-god by weaving a magnificent imperial robe and laying it before the shrine. Just as the great Khan, leader of the invading Tatars, seizes the newly completed robe, the great dragon springs to life, spewing forth torrents of rainwater -- nourishing to the rice fields, fatal to the enemy and transformational to Kwan Yin and the old man. His true identity is now revealed as the wiser, if wetter, emperor, who invites the good-hearted girl to live in his palace.

Lattimore has wisely chosen the art of Chinese screen painting as her inspirational source here. The style, to which this book is virtually one extended homage, generously accommodates both text and illustration. Her paintings are sumptuously rendered with a reverence for authenticity that bespeaks the limitless time spent poring over the source material. Nothing else could motivate such a skilled and sensitive adaptation. How I envy her hours of research looking for the right tree or the right waterfall or the master of this painted mountain or that painted face.

She has extended the feeling of this stylization quite effectively to the text as well. Its clarity and formal simplicity evoke a wisdom-of-the-ages grace while making it accessible for young readers.

From the sublime to the ridiculous is a tempting and almost unavoidable cliche at this juncture, as "The Lady With the Ship on Her Head" comes into view. Whereas "The Dragon's Robe" evokes the silken serenity of Eastern thought, "The Lady With the Ship On Her Head" plays for the belly laughs. Despite the dramatic mood swing, the common bond here is history and the pleasure of it.

I remember once as a child thumbing through an encyclopedia and stumbling upon a survey that could have been titled "Hair Styles Through the Ages." There in the 18th Century was a fancy French court lady with a ship on her head. What wonderful, insane society could possibly have provoked this lady to such an absurd extreme?, I wondered. My guess is that Lattimore asked herself the same question, for she has come up with a delightfully absurd answer.

Madame Pompenstance is in a dither, for she hasn't figured out a "theme" yet for the headdress she'll wear for tonight's fancy-dress ball. In desperation she scours the beach for something marvelous, or at least adequate, to wear in her hair. As she stoops for a particular shell, a miniature three-masted ship, complete with crew and captain, sails into port and onto her head. Unaware that her problem has solved itself, she notices the onset of a headache which she attributes to her worry over the headdress. The subsequent events of her day and evening are hilariously affected by this uninvited ornament. I still laugh when I think of the sequence when her headache has grown unbearable due to the crew dancing and singing sea chanteys on deck after reaping the benefits of a glass of rum dumped on Madame Pompenstance's head by one of her jealous rivals.

Although Lattimore's painting style here lacks the finesse of "The Dragon's Robe," it serves well for this send-up of the very notion of style itself. Any era that would define elegance and sophistication by dressing its ladies as birthday cakes is surely ripe for lampooning, and Lattimore doesn't spare the ammunition. The vanity and social customs of a time only slightly more exaggerated than ours are cheerfully skewered here by someone who truly understands the importance of historical silliness. Of all human characteristics, nothing is more lovable than the ability to laugh at ourselves. And isn't it fun to think that our ancestors could have been just as silly as we are?

History's magic happens when we're moved enough by what we see of ourselves in those who've gone before that we begin to appreciate the heroism, wonder, and (on occasion) the silliness in all of us. Lattimore's books are a loving nudge in that direction.
