940519 FT 19 MAY 94 / Accounts body presses ahead on disclosure The Accounting Standards Board is considering lobbying the government and the Stock Exchange in efforts to force greater disclosure of directors' share options. The action follows a legal opinion which has scuppered attempts by the board -which sets accounting standards - to introduce compulsory additional information on remuneration. The board's urgent-issues taskforce yesterday unveiled a diluted set of voluntary guidelines for companies to highlight the number and value of share options granted to directors. It began considering the topic last year at the suggestion of a number of auditing firms following recommendations for greater disclosure in the Cadbury report on the financial aspects of corporate governance. It was planning to introduce a standard with which all companies would have to comply. This comes as similar tough measures are being debated by the US Financial Accounting Standards Board. However, a recent legal opinion obtained by the board said that the wording of existing legislation does not require additional disclosure unless there is certainty over the value of the options. Members of the taskforce are now debating whether to put pressure on the Department of Trade and Industry to modify the law to force greater disclosure as part of its review of companies legislation. They are also considering suggesting to the Stock Exchange that it modifies the listing requirements for quoted companies in order to boost disclosure. The taskforce suggests disclosure of the prices that apply to directors' options overall and to individual directors, together with market price information at the year-end and at the date of exercise of the options. A separate abstract published yesterday clarifies the treatment of benefits used as incentives to encourage companies to sign leases - known as reverse premiums. It proposes that these should be spread on a straight-line basis - by the same amount each year - over the term of the lease, or over the period to when the rent is first adjusted. The ruling follows heavy losses reported by Pentos, the speciality retailer, last year. In the previous year it had included reverse premiums in profits of Pounds 6.3m. Without these it would have reported losses. In a third abstract, the taskforce proposes that when a debt instrument includes a call option that can be exercised only by the issuer, the payment required on exercise of the option should not form part of the instrument's finance costs.
