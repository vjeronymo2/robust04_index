October 10, 1989, Tuesday, Orange County Edition

JAZZ REVIEW;

BRANFORD MARSALIS, THE ORIGINAL, SHOWS HIS STUFF AT THE COACH HOUSE

"Those of you who want to hear tunes from my last album should go home and play it," Branford Marsalis announced at the beginning of his show Saturday at the Coach House in San Juan Capistrano. "We're not going to play any of them."

The mostly new originals that followed used the famous Miles Davis quintet of the mid-'60s as their touchstone, the songs showing the same involved style of composition and soloing that Wayne Shorter, Ron Carter, Tony Williams and Herbie Hancock brought to Davis' band on albums like "ESP" and "Miles Smiles." But where Miles moved on to electricity and funkified bass lines, Marsalis has headed out in an acoustic direction, extending Davis' cool posing with emotionally charged solos and heads-up rhythmic interplay.

Though Marsalis' band (Kenny Kirkland, piano; Robert Hurst, bass, and Jeff (Tain) Watts on drums) used Miles as a structural reference point, its inspiration came from another '60s band: the John Coltrane quartet.

Much of this association is based on the drumming of Watts, whose circling onslaught recalled the polyrhythms laid down by Elvin Jones for the Coltrane band. Marsalis' deep, dark-roasted sound on tenor was similar to Coltrane's large, open tone and some of the late saxophone giant's abundant technique was also apparent. But where Coltrane would cut loose with screams, squawks and high-end antics to get his point across, Marsalis stuck to the middle register, delivering his story lines with all the smart timing of a good stand-up comic.

The saxophonist announced the set's first three numbers then proceeded to work through them without interruption.

"Mr. J. C." featured a ringing solo from Kirkland while Watts leaned into his drum set to get the smoke going. Bassist Hurst used double stops and tight, playful pauses to get a rise out of the crowd, who seemed to appreciate his silences as much as what he plucked.

After a big Coltrane-like ending with Watts crashing away on his cymbals, the group moved into Hurst's "Dark Night," a sultry theme that would make an appropriate sound track for a Raymond Chandler novel. Marsalis began at the tenor's bottom range, then worked his way up with rippling trills and quick, greasy lines before handing off to Kirkland who built his solo on a mountain of block chords.

Marsalis' "Wrath of Tain," a vehicle for the group's drummer and the only previously recorded composition heard this night, gave Watts a chance to show his melodic approach to a drum solo. Much like Max Roach, Watts suggests a tune's theme and rhythms while building a solo that is rich with wit and the use of dynamics. Switching to soprano for the number, Marsalis showed a considered approach that avoided fireworks in favor of introspection.

The saxophonist's reserved soprano style backfired on Kirkland's "Revelation," as his smooth flow seemed to lag at times, lacking direction and excitement. Kirkland came to the rescue, plugging the gaps with chordal echoes and embellishments that held the piece together.

A Monkish number entitled "Spartacus" found Kirkland and Marsalis, still on soprano, stating the quirky theme in unison. The saxophonist seemed more at ease with the quicker tempo, dropping in a couple of lines from Monk's "Brilliant Corners" before turning the proceedings over to Watts and his rolling tom-tom attack.

Hurst picked out a little Michael Jackson riff ("Bad") before the group moved into a Coltrane dedication called "Mr. Steepy." Marsalis wisely avoided any overt reference to the late saxophonist, which seems to bolster the contention that he's now developing his own direction.

The territory he explored at the Coach House guarantees the journey will be an exciting one.
