BFN [Text] Vienna/Seoul/Pyongyang -- In the future, North Korea wants to discuss its nuclear program only with the United States and no longer with the IAEA [International Atomic Energy Agency]. This was confirmed on the sidelines of the meeting of the Board of Governors of the IAEA in Vienna today by the head of the North Korean delegation, Embassy Counselor Yun Ho-chin. The communist-ruled country is being accused of building atomic bombs. However, Pyongyang has not permitted the IAEA to carry out comprehensive inspections in its nuclear center at Yongbyon. Thus, the United States, South Korea, and Japan are considering supporting UN sanctions against North Korea. Pyongyang has reacted with the announcement that sanctions would be considered a declaration of war. South Korea's head of state, Kim Yong-sam, who sees a threat to world peace, called a meeting of the country's National Security Council, which will convene in Seoul on Wednesday [8 June]. In view of possible sanctions, the body wants to explain the attitude of the United States, of Russia, China, and the UN Security Council on the nuclear issue. It has so far become clear that Moscow and Beijing would reject sanctions. As the South Korean state news agency YONHAP reported today, Kim described North Korea's nuclear program as a threat to world peace. He called on the neighboring communist state to give up its "ruthless development program for nuclear weapons." Prior to the meeting of the Board of Governors of the IAEA, the director general of the North Korean Atomic Agency, Pak Yong-nam, stressed in a letter to IAEA head Hans Blix that his country no longer feels bound by the conditions stipulated by the IAEA if the IAEA should continue to exert pressure on North Korea. "We really doubt whether the nuclear issue can be solved through further talks with the IAEA," the letter reads. "We no longer want to settle the problems within the framework of the nonproliferation treaty, but in the third bilateral round of talks with the United States," Pak Yong-nam added. The diplomat stated that it would be better to resolve the technical problems first and then the political problems. In the letter that is available to DPA, Pak Yong-nam wrote that, contrary to earlier statements by Blix, it is naturally possible to find out whether North Korea has removed the plutonium required for the production of nuclear weapons when it exchanged the fuel rods in the five-megawatt reactor at Yongbyon. He also reaffirmed North Korea's view that it wants to maintain a special position within the Nuclear Nonproliferation Treaty, after it announced its resignation about one year ago and later suspended this move. If the IAEA should refer the North Korean nuclear issue to the UN Security Council, "we no longer feel bound by the IAEA and cannot but initiate the next step of our peaceful nuclear activities," Pak concluded his letter. The crisis that broke out one year ago exacerbated recently as a result of North Korea's unauthorized disposal of fuel rods from its reactor. Thus, the IAEA cannot ascertain whether plutonium was removed for the construction of atomic bombs during the disposal.
