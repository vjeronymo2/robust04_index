July 23, 1989, Sunday, Bulldog Edition

TIAN AN MEN SQUARE;

IN THE TRADITIONAL VIEW IT IS CENTER OF BEIJING, CHINA AND THE UNIVERSE

For Chinese citizens, few places mean as much, in history or in symbol, as the Gate of Heavenly Peace (Tian An Men), the massive vermilion wall that guards the southern entrance to Beijing's Forbidden City.

Hundreds and perhaps thousands of protesting students died in the shadow of the gate early in June, before the tanks and rifles of the 27th Army. It was no accident the youths chose Tian An Men Square, which lies before the gate, as their stage.

Even before the creation of Communist China 40 years ago, Tian An Men was known as the place where government and the governed met. After the Ming emperors built their capital at Beijing in the early 15th Century, Tian An Men became the focus of the dynasty's most elaborate public ceremony, "The Gold Phoenix Issues an Edict," in which court officials announced imperial policy from a rostrum on the gate, long before written copies of the mandate were conveyed to the provinces.

The emperor's benevolent power, subjects believed, flowed southward from the throne room, past the walled fortress of the Forbidden City, and through Tian An Men like a calming river that branched into the far reaches of his empire.

When the People's Republic of China was founded Oct. 1, 1949, this was the place where Communist Party leader Mao Tse-tung raised the country's new flag.

"In the traditional Chinese view, Tian An Men was not only the center of the capital, but the center of the country and the center of the universe," says Anthony J. Kane, director of the China Council of the Asia Foundation, a New York-based educational institution committed to the study and understanding of Asian culture.

The gate remains a symbol of central authority. An image of the long, low building, with its sweeping roof upturned to fend off evil forces, is stamped on Chinese coins, currency, airline tickets, and policemen's badges. Schoolchildren learn to sing "I Love Tian An Men Square," much as others learn "America" or "La Marseillaise."

Tian An Men Square is, at 98 acres, the largest city square in the world, a pancake-flat expanse of paving stones inspired by Moscow's Red Square. Tian An Men was created by the Communist government, which cleared away a rat's maze of gray houses, walls and alleys that used to squat outside the imperial compound.

Thousands of tourists from the provinces flock to the square to see museums and monuments to the revolution and to have their pictures taken by vendors in front of the old gate. In normal times the atmosphere is easy, with vacationers gawking, licking flavored ice, and pointing at the sights, while staid Beijing residents drift to and from work on their black Flying Pigeon bicycles, accompanied by the festive tinkling of thousands upon thousands of bike bells.

Mao Still Haunts

Although more than a decade has passed since Mao's death, the late chairman still haunts Tian An Men Square. At the plaza's south end, hundreds of the reverent and the curious line up daily, hats in hand, to visit the Chairman Mao Tse-tung Memorial Hall, a gray mausoleum built in the same heroic proportions as the emperor's quarters. Here the founder of modern China lies on view under glass.

At Tian An Men Square's northern end, Mao's portrait peers down from Tian An Men Gate, where he liked to review thousands of marching Red Guards, the radical youths who held his little red poetry books aloft and greeted the Great Helmsman with a chant once reserved for emperors: "Ten thousand years! Ten thousand! Ten thousand and ten thousand!"

Site of Testing

Along with the worshipful, Tian An Men has also drawn the disaffected from all parts of China. Traditionally, the capital gates have been a forum for testing the limits of political speech.

Even before there was a China, a legendary king named Yao encouraged his subjects to carve their criticisms of government officials on wooden pillars outside his palace, so he would know of commoners' concerns. The wooden columns are gone now, but they are recalled by two pairs of white marble huabiao that support the gate at Tian An Men.

Perhaps with that tradition in mind, Chinese gathered before the columns of Tian An Men to press the Qing emperor for reforms in government and education between 1895 and 1900. It was here the last dynasty collapsed in 1912 and here that guards shot and bayoneted 47 students to death in 1926, while the youths were protesting what they considered undue foreign influence in Chinese affairs.

Even Mao's Cultural Revolution, which began in Tian An Men, finally ended there. A million students poured into the capital to mourn the death of Premier Chou En-lai in 1976, and their mourning swiftly turned into a riot, aimed at the repressive Maoists then in power. Police brutally beat hundreds of the youths and made mass arrests, but the students eventually prevailed.
