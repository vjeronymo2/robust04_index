BFN [Article by krzem, irem, PAP: "premier@urm.gov.pl; Mr. Prime Minister on Computer"] [Text] If you have a computer, a telephone, and a modem and pay 1.5 million zlotys [Z] to open an account on the Internet and Z1.5 million a month in subscription fees, from today on you can send your opinions to Prime Minister Pawlak. It will be enough to put the following address: premier@urm.gov.pl. You can also write it in English--primeminister@urm.gov.pl.--and it will reach the addressee as well. The prime minister gave these addresses yesterday in the government's holiday center in Lansk, during the Forum of the Association for the Development of Open Systems, which was held in Lansk. Pawlak is the second high-ranking politician who has revealed his address on the Internet. The other one is U.S. President Bill Clinton, who made his address available on the Internet a year ago: president@whitehouse.gov. The first message Prime Minister Pawlak sent was greetings to President Clinton. How does it work? All of the computers in the Office of the Council of Ministers [URM] (including the ones that are on the desk of the prime minister) are linked to one another by a so-called network. Their users can send messages to each other. Yesterday, URM joined the world Internet system, a huge computer network that already includes 65 countries (such as the U.S., Canada, the countries of Western Europe, Japan, and, for over a year--Poland). In America, increasingly more companies give not only their fax numbers but also their addresses in the computer mail (so-called e-mail). Where did the Internet come from? More than 10 years ago, the Pentagon tasked research institutes and armament enterprises with creating a system of computer communications. One university after another joined the system so the network became available to all scientists. Recently, it has even become available to every owner of a personal computer, a modem, and a telephone. In Poland, the users of this network are mainly universities and institutes--35,000 people in 220 institutions. The installation costs are covered by the Scientific Research Committee. Other users (a few dozen, including GAZETA) must pay Z1.5 million to open an account in the network, and a monthly subscription fee of Z1.5 million. The system is installed by the Warsaw-based company ATM.
