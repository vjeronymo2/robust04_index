February 26, 1989, Sunday, Home Edition

TRAVELER'S DIARY: TAKING A TRIP TO THE RESORT LAND OF YOUR DREAMS

If most travelers are given a choice they would take off for a dream world that exists beyond the daily workaday life and beyond the borders of an ordinary holiday.

In an effort to meet those desires, developers -- stretched from Walt Disney's "Imagineers" in Florida and in California to the far-out dreamers in Hawaii -- have ready-to-serve realities.

Americans are taking about 400-million trips a year, with vacation excursions accounting for a 16% rise in the last five years and resort travel jumping 5% every year.

Hyatt, which has 32 resorts around the world, expects to spend $3 billion in the next five years, putting up extravagant shelters at Wailea and on Kauai in Hawaii; in Aruba; at Beaver Creek, Colo., and at the new resort complex at Nusa Dua in Bali, where former President Reagan and Nancy stayed.

Hyatt has attached the word "fantasy" to these resorts, and none is more of a dream world than the $360-million, 1,241-room extravaganza it operates at Waikoloa on the island of Hawaii.

The resort, which is traversed by a fleet of launches as well as by a tubular tram, is a product of the quintessential fantasy fancier Christopher Hemmeter, who has another playland called Kauai Lagoons, managed by Westin.

Occupancy at the Waikoloa fantasy, a month after the September opening, ran 15 points over the industry average. It is often sold out on weekends and fantasy fanciers can spend up to $1,000 a couple a night.

Drive a Race Car

Among the offerings by the fantasy desk there are learning how to drive a race car; holding a private dinner party at Hulihee Palace, a landmark of Hawaii's monarchy days (printed personalized menus included); a helicopter picnic at the edge of a 300-foot cliff; spending a day as a Hawaiian cowboy learning how to herd sheep on a motorcycle or a horse.

Then one can have dinner for two at a secluded beach reached by a 45-foot sailboat chartered just for you, and joining the dolphins for a play period after learning how to "talk" to them and give hand signals. That little number costs $55 for adults, $45 for children.

On the books in Las Vegas is a $1.2-billion resort called Pharaoh's Kingdom. It will have rooms for 5,000, a gaming area that measures 280,000 square feet, a convention center and a $100-million spa.

But that's only for starters. In the middle of the desert they'll create a jungle-fringed lagoon with its own beach, an Egyptian city as it may have appeared in Tut's time -- but underwater, this time -- and an underwater seafood restaurant. Add to all that an amusement park put together by Disney.

Children, to be sure, are allowed to have fantasies too. In the middle of Indianapolis the old railroad station has vintage railroad cars that have been rebuilt as hotel suites -- two to a car. The train car suites occupy 13 Pullman car sleepers all resting on their original tracks.

Linblad's tours have had their Antarctic customers swimming in the seas at the bottom of the world. Try that for dinner-party conversation.

Or tell about Finnair's eight-day excursions to Lapland beyond the Arctic Circle -- snowmobiling to Lapp villages or living through the nightless summer nights and dining in a tepee, with visits to Helsinki and Stockholm and free-of-visa sailings to Leningrad.

Fantasy Weekends

For those with little time but big dreams there are even fantasy weekends. The Hilton at Short Hills, N.J., runs a 24-karat weekend that starts when a chauffeur picks you up at Newark Airport, then offers a bottle of Dom Perignon champagne in the limousine.

A dozen roses await in the deluxe suite, where a chef and serenading violinist attend the couple at dinner in the suite.

Gift Certificate

A choice of Japanese or Swedish massages awaits, as does a $100 gift certificate for shopping at a nearby mall. All that for $1,500.

Last year the Grand Hyatt in New York City offered -- steady now -- a $10,000 weekend . . . without air fare.

It included the presidential suite, chauffeurs and limousines, caviar and a continuous flow of champagne, as well as tickets to "Phantom of the Opera," a limo to the theater, Regine's for dinner and over to Christie's to get measured for a mink. Yes, madame got to keep the coat. There were two takers.
