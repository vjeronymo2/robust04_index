August 6, 1989, Sunday, Home Edition

KEEPING HER HEAD ABOVE WATER;

THE BODY'S MEMORY BY JEAN STEWART (ST. MARTIN'S PRESS: $16.95; 272 PP.)

"I remember the moment I discovered, at age 10 in 1957, that not everyone in America had freedom of choice when it came to such commonplace acts as riding buses and trains. (My parents had just bought their first television set and I, awed, sat glued to the screen for weeks.) I knew that such things were possible, that freedom could be taken away, having pored through Anne Frank's diary. But that was Germany, and history. It seemed unimaginable that some Americans were made to ride separate buses, attend separate schools and churches, sit in separate sections way in the back of the movie theaters, and enter through separate doors. A special kind of anger, a receptivity, was born in me then."

The speaker is Kate, a teacher in her 30s who lives on Long Island, the protagonist of Jean Stewart's "A Body's Memory." She is a bright, independent woman who now -- 20 years after first learning of the civil rights movement -- has herself become the target of discrimination. Race, however, is not the issue; she is discriminated against for a different reason, because she is recently disabled, dependent on a wheelchair for mobility.

Confronted with insurmountable steps and curbs, narrow doorways, broken elevators, inquisitive stares and pitying looks that establish her as separate, Kate understands what it means to be denied access to the world, to be set apart, to be deemed unemployable and lose a much-needed job, to have to accept public welfare, to be different and experience a particular kind of isolating loneliness because of that difference. Each day that one lives with any kind of discrimination, she comes to see, is a day turned into a battle against anger and despair and numbing self-doubt.

Page after page of this book chronicles that battle in its various forms and stages. In a preface, Stewart stresses that this book is a work of fiction related "only obliquely" to events in her own life. Clearly that oblique slice of the story is a big one. The novel rings terribly true and is all the more affecting for it.

Many of the characters in "The Body's Memory" are disabled, and it does become a very specific topic, presenting a character with a singular -- albeit terribly important -- crisis, and though the emphasis occasionally threatens to maroon the story on a single track, the author ultimately succeeds in writing about many things endemic to that crisis -- friendships, for instance, and courage, and self-discovery, in very strong and confident prose. It is a first novel, and like many first novels, the story of a rite of passage.

The novel unfolds through journal entries and letters and opens with Kate in the hospital, facing surgery for the second time. A cancerous tumor has reappeared on her hip:

"Cancer . . . I say it often . . . though my friends tiptoe around the word. . . . I say it and listen to its reverberations. I roll it around my mouth like a grape, to taste it, let it burst upon my senses."

The surgery destroys muscle and bone, leaving her unable to walk unassisted. Initially Kate refuses to consider a wheelchair. A wheelchair seems the final humiliation. Forearm crutches, however, aren't much better. The metal bands encircling her arms pinch her flesh, and the attempt to walk with them leaves her exhausted and in constant pain until the wheelchair begins to look like a more endurable alternative.

Determined to live alone, Kate refuses offers to move in with friends and begins the long, arduous process of adapting to her disability. "Nighttime is particularly confusing," she writes to a friend. "When sunlight scatters from the rooms, and my leg is dizzy with pain from trying to pretend it can do anything, and I crawl between the sheets and the vastly empty bed unfurls around me, I feel that other darkness encroaching on the sturdy, safe daytime world. It's hard not to panic, then. . . . I do seem to be proving what I set out to prove: that I can live alone. The question begins to reshape itself: At what price?"

The price, as it turns out, is high. Disability takes its toll in many eroding ways. Kate loses her job, her lover, her sense of herself as attractive and capable. "Men seem particularly bemused by the sight of me," she writes, "as if they expect disabled women to be asexual."

Sometimes her very sanity seems endangered as Kate goes through the cycle of illness, anger, acceptance, adaptation. She endures radiation treatments, a word that recalls the bomb shelters and air raid drills of the '50s. In fact, her world has gone slightly mad. Panic, grief, despair cannot be dismissed by Kate or her friends Ellen, Peter, Jeremy and Beth, much as they try to help her. In a climactic and horrifying scene, Kate's new lover, Carl, informs her that he's tired of carrying her upstairs to his bedroom and having to act the part of a nurse. Her reaction is to throw her wheelchair down a flight of stairs, and then herself, only to be brutally sexually assaulted even as she is lying face down with broken bones from the fall. What can happen to this woman? a reader might ask. What can she find for herself, and how will she find it?

The answer is that she can get angry enough to dissolve the self-pity blocking her path. She can seek the company of other disabled people, people who will understand her emotional status, and she can assert herself in many ordinary situations, just as her civil rights predecessors did. Kate undergoes a transformation from victim to activist, from an angry, suffering woman to a crusader working on behalf of all people's understanding. It's a thoroughly credible development, and it gives the novel a fine resolved ending, even if the reader does get the telegraphed and slightly sentimentalized sense that it was the only possible ending.

The novel concludes on a particularly strong note with a description of Kate confronting the sea, at the same beach where she once attempted suicide with pills. She now crawls into the brine like some prehistoric sea beast making its return journey, in order to take one of her now-frequent and long solo swims, and recalls an earlier incident.

"She was swimming out toward open sea one day when she came upon a small fishing boat. In it sat a lone Greek who stared down, open-mouthed. He turned and peered in all directions, looking for her boat, but his was the only one in sight. At last recovering speech, careful not to offend, he ventured, polite, his voice thickly Greek: 'Are you . . . drowning?'

"Kate smiled up at him. 'No. I'm swimming,' she said." Here is the evidence of the triumph so notable in this work.
