January 5, 1989, Thursday, Home Edition

STOCKS' GAIN BEST SINCE OCTOBER; DOW UP 33.04

A late burst of program trading, coupled with a sharp rise in the dollar, combined Wednesday to give Wall Street stocks their biggest one-day advance in more than two months.

The Dow Jones index of 30 industrials, down 23.93 on Tuesday, climbed 33.04 to 2,177.68.

Advancing issues outnumbered declines by more than 3 to 1 in New York Stock Exchange trading, with Big Board volume rising to 149.70 million shares, up from 128.50 million Tuesday.

"It's a good, broad, impressive rally," one trader said. "I think it's got some sustainability to it."

The Dow's rise was the largest since Oct. 20, when it gained 44 points, and leaves the index just a few points below its post-crash high of 2,183.50.

Stock analysts attributed the market's strength to a sharp rise in the dollar, which added nearly 2 pfennigs against the West German mark on news that U.S. Navy jets shot down two Libyan fighters over the Mediterranean Sea.

"We had very strong buy programs kicking in," said Jack Barbanel, senior vice president at Gruntal & Co. Traders said futures-linked buying helped the Dow double its early gains in the last hour of the session.

An early rise in bond prices also helped the stock market, as did a 28-cent drop in the price of U.S. crude oil to $17.08 a barrel.

In foreign trading, the Tokyo stock market started off the new year with a bang on Wednesday, climbing to a record close. The 225-share Nikkei index ended an abbreviated half-day session up 84.66 at 30,243.66.

London shares ended near the day's highs, boosted by Wall Street's early advance and a stronger dollar. The Financial Times index closed up 10.2 at 1,793 after a 10.3-point drop Tuesday.

CREDIT

Bond prices were mixed Wednesday, reacting favorably to a firmer dollar but anticipating that Friday's unemployment report might show a big increase in jobs, boosting inflation fears.

The Treasury's 30-year bond fell 1/16th of a point, or 62.5 cents per $1,000 face amount, while its yield closed at 9.08%, unchanged from late Tuesday.

Jay Goldinger, of the investment banking firm Capital Insight Inc. in Beverly Hills, said the bond market was anticipating Friday's release of federal jobless figures for December.

The federal funds rate, the interest on overnight loans between banks, traded at 9.125%, down from 9.625% late Tuesday.

COMMODITIES

Prices of copper futures rose sharply on the New York Commodity Exchange on supply worries rekindled by the apparent bombing of three locomotives in Peru and a decline in the exchange's warehouse stocks of copper.

On other markets, precious metals futures retreated, grain and soybean futures surged, cattle futures rose while pork futures fell and stock index futures advanced.

Copper settled 3.1 to 6.3 cents higher, with the spot contract at $1.58 a pound and the more actively traded contract for delivery in March at $1.426 a pound.

The bombing of the locomotives in Peru, the world's sixth-largest copper producer, served to remind traders that the settlement last month of a 57-day Peruvian miners strike did not defuse the country's internal political tensions or the potential for disruptions in the copper pipeline.

Meanwhile, the Commodity Exchange reported a 301-ton decline in its warehouse stocks of copper to 7,964 tons. It was the first daily decline after more than a week of steady or rising supplies, indicating that copper consumers have returned to the marketplace after a holiday break.

Precious metals prices fell on the Comex in response to the dollar's strength, which was in turn a reaction to the U.S. downing of two Libyan fighter planes off the Libyan coast, analysts said.
