July 18, 1989, Tuesday, Home Edition

THE DRUG CLINIC: IF YOU DON'T DRINK, IS THAT HAZARDOUS TO YOUR HEALTH?

Is being a nondrinker hazardous to your health? That's what many recovering alcoholics, along with those who are abstinent for other reasons, have asked me over the years. Their concern continues to be fueled by articles that say that moderate drinkers have a longer life expectancy than abstainers or heavy drinkers.

This trouble started in the 1970s when scientific journals began to report that moderate drinkers had fewer heart attacks than abstainers. This was thought to be due to the fact that moderate drinkers have higher levels of HDL cholesterol than do nondrinkers. HDL became known as "good" cholesterol because it was thought to protect the blood vessels.

The media version of the scientific rationale went like this: Alcohol in the bloodstream causes an increase in HDL. Serving as a coating agent, HDL protects the coronary artery walls of drinkers against atherosclerosis. From this it followed that moderate drinkers lived longer because they had less atherosclerosis and fewer heart attacks.

Recovering alcoholics and other nondrinkers drew no comfort from the fact that heavy drinkers, those who exceed a safe limit of about two drinks a day, seem to lose this alcohol-related HDL benefit. Plotted on a graph, the scientific findings suggested that the relationship between mortality and drinking was a U-shaped curve. Abstainers and heavy drinkers alike were dying at the tips of the U, while moderate drinkers safely snuggled at the bottom.

In the mid-1980s, several studies revealed that these arguments were not conclusive. It was true that moderate drinking increased HDL. But the increase was in the HDL-3 fraction, a subtype of HDL which, it turned out, does not protect blood vessels. HDL-2, another subtype, is what protects arteries, we were told. One study reported in the December, 1988, issue of Lancet shed still more light on the subject. In a follow-up study of almost 8,000 men over a period of 7 1/2 years it became clear that many of the nondrinkers on the U-shaped curve were actually patients who "already had heart disease" when they entered the study.

The implication was that these abstainers were actually patients who, in AA vernacular, would be known as "white knucklers." They were people who had "given up" alcohol either because they were worried about their health or because they had proof that they had already developed heart disease.

The new thinking was that hard drinkers may die early because of alcohol-damage to their health in general; and abstainers may die early because many of them are "white-knuckling it," which means they are leading highly stressed lives.

But that's not all. Other similar studies suggested that a number of the deaths occurring in the abstainer group had nothing to do with coronary artery disease. Such deaths often occurred in recovering alcoholics who, during their period of hard drinking, had caused irreparable damage to organs other than their heart, and that damage ultimately caused their statistically early death. Like high mileage automobiles, some of them wore out before their time.

Thus, welcome news that it would be to moderate drinkers, and fatal news that it has been for recovering alcoholics and other abstainers, moderate drinking appears not to be protective against coronary artery disease. HDL-2 is now the good cholesterol. And HDL-2 is not produced by alcohol but rather by exercise. But bending your elbow and tilting your head is not enough. It takes a more vigorous type, such as jogging.
