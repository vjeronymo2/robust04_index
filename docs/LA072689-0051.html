July 26, 1989, Wednesday, Home Edition

STANDARDS URGED ON DISPOSAL OF MEDICAL WASTE

A year after used hypodermic syringes washed up along U.S. shores prompting howls of protest and a spate of new government regulations, the nation's ability to safely dispose of medical wastes has improved little, a House subcommittee was told Tuesday.

To better protect the environment from potentially infectious wastes, Rep. Ron Wyden (D-Ore.) said that the federal government should set new national standards for handling and disposal of medical castoffs.

Wyden, chairman of a House Small Business subcommittee that is investigating the issue, said that the "crazy quilt" of laws and regulations adopted after the incidents last year has created unintended disincentives to the proper disposal of medical waste.

Urges Incinerators

Wyden stressed the need to build government-operated regional incinerators to take the place of private facilities. Currently, about 90% of U.S. hospitals burn their own waste, but tougher emissions laws are expected to make many incinerators obsolete.

Some of those who testified at a hearing before Wyden's subcommittee on regulation, business opportunities and energy said that the government needs to become more active in dealing with the problem, but they offered few specific recommendations.

Recent private and government reports have noted a lack of consensus on what constitutes infectious waste and the extent to which it poses a public health threat. Some states have no regulations, while others have rules that sometimes work at cross purposes.

Tracking Act Passed

So far, the federal government's response to the problem has been limited. Last year, Congress passed the Medical Waste Tracking Act, a measure designed to chart the course of waste from hospitals to disposals.

Several officials testified that the act has been successful, but they said its scope is too restricted. They said that better state coordination and national waste standards focusing on the larger problem of dumping might be more effective.

Most of the waste products that washed up on beaches last summer did not come from hospitals but were illegally dumped, often by drug users, several experts said, acknowledging that a solution to that problem will be difficult to devise.

"I think there's a whole lot more to the problem than adding up the sum of the muck that washes up," Wyden said.
