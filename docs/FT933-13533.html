930719 FT 19 JUL 93 / Insurers could face Pounds 100m bill INSURERS face a potential bill of more than Pounds 100m over the next few years following a ruling by the House of Lords. This came in a long-running case stemming from the failure in 1990 of London United Investments, the insurance and property group. The case concerned the liability of the Policyholders' Protection Board for claims from non-resident policyholders. The board meets 90 per cent of all claims from private policyholders when UK insurers go out of business. Payments are funded by UK insurers, with the size of levies varying according to each company's share of the market. The annual amount payable by companies is limited to a maximum of 1 per cent of premium income. The Lords ruled that several thousand US policyholders could claim compensation even though they were not resident in the UK. However, it reserved judgment as to whether a number of professional partnerships could be considered as private policyholders. Those benefiting from the ruling bought insurance from five LUI subsidiaries -Kingscoft Insurance, El Paso Insurance, Lime Street Insurance and Mutual Re, collectively known as the KELM companies - and Walbrook Insurance. The Lords' decision upheld rulings in the lower court which define a UK policy as one issued by a UK authorised insurance company operating out of the UK, irrespective of where the risk covered by the policy is located. Mr Philip Rocher of Wilde Sapte, the firm of solicitors which represented policyholders in the case, said: 'This is not a defeat for the UK insurance industry. It reaffirms the security of their policies.'
