November 25, 1990, Sunday, Home Edition

MARKETS: A NOTE OF CAUTION: TIMING UPSWINGS ISN'T EASY

If you believe that the recession you've been hearing so much about is a typical one, you had better hurry and give your broker a call. Stock prices should start to rally this winter.

But if you are one of the growing number of people who feel deep down that this recession will be longer and more severe than most postwar downturns, hold off making that call.

"The rule of thumb has always been that a new bull market begins halfway into the recession," says Allen Sinai, chief economist for Boston Co.

That's because the stock market always tries to anticipate events. In this case, Wall Street is attempting to figure out when the Federal Reserve Board's loosening of interest rates will begin bolstering the economy.

So if you believe the conventional wisdom that says the U.S. entered a recession on Aug. 2, the day Saddam Hussein invaded Kuwait, and will start pulling out of a recession in late spring, you'll want to start loading up on stocks before you start loading up on New Year's champagne.

Of course, this time around the rule of thumb could turn out to be just a dumb rule. For one thing, only with hindsight have experts learned that stocks start rallying halfway through a recession.

When the nation is actually in a recession, the experts usually can't even agree on when the downturn began, much less when it will end. And the peculiar problems this time around -- like the 1980s surge in personal and corporate debt, the fragile banking system and the unstable Middle East situation -- are making it even more difficult for reasonable economists to agree.

Making the situation even tougher to scope out is the fact that a recession hasn't even officially been declared, even though most experts would now agree that one is under way.

A recession is commonly defined as having occured when the nation's output of goods and services (gross national product) declines for two straight quarters. So far there hasn't even been one quarter in which the gross national product has declined.

Critics of the government's data-gathering system say the GNP numbers can't be trusted. H. Erich Heinemann, chief economist of Ladenburg, Thalmann & Co., recently told his clients that the 1.8% increase in GNP that the government reported for the third quarter of 1990 was "as phony as a $3 bill -- artifacts of an arcane algebra that bore no relation to economic reality."

That 1.8% gain followed a 0.4% improvement in the second quarter, a 1.7% gain in the first quarter and a gain of just 0.3% in the fourth quarter of 1989. That fourth quarter figure reflects a sharp downward revision by the government.

Some experts believe Washington will eventually adjust all of this year's GNP numbers lower -- maybe enough to show that a recession began in early 1990.

In trying to calculate the mid-point of the recession that hasn't been declared yet, keep in mind that the average post-World War II recession has been 11 months long. But the 1970 and 1982 recessions each lasted 16 months. And according to the National Bureau of Economic Research, a private group, recessions during this century have lasted as long as two years (excluding the Great Depression of the 1930s).

The middle of an 11-month recession that began in August would be mid-January. But if an investor is trying to pick the mid-point of an 11-month recession that began last January, he should have been buying stocks about the time Saddam Hussein was causing trouble.

But what if this recession turns out to be one of the 18-month variety? Or maybe the excesses of the 1980s together with recent tax increases and the deliberate inflation-fighting style of the Fed will make this recession a record breaker? Or maybe . . .

You get the picture. Picking the midpoint of a recession is about as difficult as determining what Bo Jackson will do for a midlife career change.

But Wall Street experts say that's just what a lot of professional investors have been trying to do. They contend that some of the stocks market's bounce in recent weeks can be attributed to aggressive investors trying to anticipate the economic recovery and events in the Middle East.

Gene Jay Seagle, director of technical research at Gruntal & Co., is one of those bulls. Seagle isn't trying to time the recession, but he does have another angle on this whole "when-to-buy" question.

Stock prices have generally fallen 28% to 30% during post-World War II recessions. In the past year, says Seagle, more than half of all stocks listed on the New York Stock Exchange are down at least that amount.

"The market, for all intents and purposes, has hit bottom," claims Seagle. So, this bull says, buy stocks now.

The bulls, of course, are playing in dangerous territory. Only four months ago, blue chip stocks were at record levels. Is it possible that the stock market could be at its bottom after so short a time? Or is it possible that, like last spring's fake rally, this recent upturn could be a trap that will cost investors dearly?

"All of a sudden we have a lot of bulls coming out of the woodwork," says Peter Eliades, who writes the newsletter Stock Market Cycles.

"If someone thinks the recession is going to end in the first quarter of next year, you'd want to be buying here," says Eliades.

But Eliades does not concur with that assessment, although he does agree with the rule of thumb. "You should buy (stocks) three to six months before the end of a recession. I just disagree when it will end," says Eliades, who thinks this recession is going to be deeper and more severe than any postwar recession. So no sense buying stocks just yet.

Adds Boston Co.'s Sinai: "Myself, I think the safer time to invest in stocks is when 1992 is in sight."

As far as I'm concerned, I agree with a trader friend named Bill, who says: "What's the hurry? Stocks are too dangerous right now. People aren't buying stocks rationally."

Meaning of NCR Talks Is Unclear

Wall Street has paid Charles Exley the ultimate compliment. No matter what type of deal Exley's company, NCR Corp., happens to be negotiating with AT&T Corp., traders say they are certain that it will be beneficial to NCR shareholders.

That confidence, however, may be based on a faulty interpretation of some past Exley statements. If that is so, Wall Street could be in for another big disappointment.

NCR's stock began rising a few weeks ago when the Wall Street Journal reported that NCR and AT&T were in some sort of negotiations. The paper said the talks might involve AT&T buying NCR.

But the Journal also mentioned the possibility that the two companies might only be discussing a merger of their computer businesses, or some other less-desirable transaction.

As would have been expected, NCR's stock rose on the idea that the company would be acquired. But, ordinarily, NCR's shares might have retreated as investors mulled the possibility of one of these lesser transactions occuring. But NCR's stock has generally remained strong. Why?

Traders said it is because of the reputation of Exley as a proponent of what he calls "stakeholder rights." The feeling on Wall Street is that whatever the 60-year chairman decides, it will benefit NCR shareholders.

Stephen K. Smith, the computer analyst at Paine Webber Inc., says Exley "wouldn't be doing it unless it was good for stakeholders."

But Exley has said on numerous occasions that NCR has several stakeholders -- the company's customers, suppliers and employees, as well as its shareholders.

What if the best interest of NCR's customers, suppliers and employees doesn't jibe with the interests of its stockholders? In other words, is Wall Street being too cocky by placing so much faith in Exley?

On the whole, Wall Street doesn't really think AT&T wants to buy NCR. But nobody would be surprised if AT&T somehow put its troubled computer business into the hands of NCR in some sort of joint venture.

And while that prospect would ordinarily worry Wall Street, Exley's presence seems to have calmed everyone's nerves.

"He does have an excellent reputation," adds Wendy Abramowitz, who follows NCR for Argus Research. "He's a good financial man. He's very different from the other computer executives. Some of the others say, 'I don't care what Wall Street thinks.' "

If Wall Street is reading Exley's mind correctly, it can make a lot of money. But if it isn't -- watch out. Exley wasn't available for comment.

Utility Shares May Yet Prove Electric

Want a hot investment idea? Buy shares of electric utilities.

Sorry, this isn't the takeover-crazy 1980s. The investment motto that'll get you furthest these days is: Stay conservative.

You can't get much more conservative than investing in electric utilities. They don't usually get taken over. But they also don't make a product that goes out of fashion. They aren't, in short, prone to highs and lows like other companies.

But experts say electric utilities can be pretty sexy when you look at them in a certain way. Falling interest rates could boost utility stocks. In fact, Prudential-Bache Securities figures that every 50 basis point drop in interest rates (that's one half of 1%) would push the average utility stock up 7%.

The experts also say that utility stocks are still cheap. And several electric utilities may actually be raising their dividend in the coming months.

Barry Abramson of Prudential, in fact, says the electric utility industry is in its best shape in the past 25 years.

Arnold Belosic, of Bear Stearns & Co., says: "We've been recommending utilities because of their defensive characteristics. They tend to do well in this type of market environment."

Despite uncertainty over the state of the economy, Belosic expects electric utility dividends to rise 2% to 3% annually for the next few years.

Specifically, Belosic is recommending New England Electric Systems, Scana Corp. (formerly South Carolina Electric and Gas), Florida Progress Corp., Dominion Resources, Cilcorp (formerly Central Illinois Light), and Kentucky Utilities.

The only thing that could go wrong, the analysts say, is a sudden pickup in the economy and a sustained stock market rally. Even though electric utilities will actually be helped by a healthier economy, investors will start ignoring these conservative havens again. And their stocks might go down.
