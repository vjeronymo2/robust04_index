Language: English Article Type:BFN [Text] Tokyo, March 25 KYODO -- More than 70 percent of the young Korean people in Japan surveyed last summer said they feel a sense of attachment toward this country, the Korean Youth Association in Japan said Friday [25 March]. The association affiliated with the pro-Seoul Korean Residents Union in Japan suggested in the report that the younger generation of Koreans born in and living in Japan consider their permanent residency here as a matter of course. The results of the report were brought to the public's attention as South Korean President Kim Yong-sam visits Tokyo, stressing the importance of future-oriented relations between the East Asian neighbors. Many Korean youth living in Japan today are descendants of Koreans forcibly brought to this country under its harsh colonial policies before and during World War II to labor in Japanese factories and mines. After the war, many retained their Korean nationality and today an estimated 700,00 Koreans are living throughout Japan as permanent residents. The survey conducted between June and September last year polled 800 Korean young men and women between the ages of 18 and 30 who were born in Japan but hold South Korean citizenship. The results were released in February. In addition to the more than 70 percent who said they felt a sense of attachment to Japan, more than 50 percent of the survey respondents also said they felt a sense of attachment to the Korean community in Japan. Roughly 40 percent said they felt attachment to South Korea, while less than 10 percent had similar feelings of attachment toward the Korea of old before it was divided following World War II. In response to a question about returning to Korea, just short of 30 percent of the respondents said they wished to return while another 30 percent said either Korea or Japan would be fine. More than 40 percent of the respondents, however, said they had no desire to return to Korea. Of the young people surveyed who had visited South Korea, more than 40 percent said they felt a sense of attachment to their ancestral homeland while visiting, while 30 percent said they had "mixed feelings" and 20 percent said they felt indifferent.
