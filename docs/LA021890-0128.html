February 18, 1990, Sunday, Bulldog Edition

THEY'RE THE SPURS OF THE MOMENT

There is hope for the hopeless, life for the lifeless, maybe even a home for the homeless, at HemisFair Arena these days. The San Antonio Spurs are living proof that the 98-pound weakling with braces, acne and a lisp can grow up to get the girl. The day cannot be far away when any self-respecting gym rat wears a wristwatch, black socks and generic sneakers.

Think of the possibilities, now that the most-improved basketball team on this planet owes its success in part to:

An ordained minister-keyboard-player-songwriter who takes medication to ensure that his heart beats regularly;

The world's tallest known Navy Lieutenant, who is as likely to practice on the keyboard as on his jump shot;

One failed trumpet player;

A guy who answers to Brick and, for fun, collects guns;

A 22-year-old rookie whose heroics once allowed his ninth-grade team to get beat "only" 100-18;

And a little old guy named Cheeks.

From the ruins of 21 victories and 61 defeats in 1988-89, Spur Coach Larry Brown and assistant to the chairman Bob Bass have built a team that ranks among the National Basketball Association's elite.

The Spurs return from the All-Star break with the sixth-best record in the league (32-15) and are on pace to go 57-25. Such a turnaround would be the biggest in NBA history, surpassing the 32-game improvement the Larry Bird-led Boston Celtics made from '78-79 to '79-80.

Imagine how high the Spurs can climb once they finish learning each others' names and games. Only three current Spurs were on the active roster at the end of last season. Two rookies, David Robinson and Sean Elliott, are accounting for 31% of the scoring, 34% of the rebounding and 52% of the shot-blocking. And here the Spurs are anyway, within dunking distance of catching the Lakers in the race for the best record in the NBA.

"This," Brown admits, "is a shock."

This is a far cry from last year, when Brown was about ready for shock treatments. Brown, remember, came to the Spurs last season fresh off having guided the Kansas Jayhawks to the 1988 national championship. He had a five-year, $3.5-million contract and visions of taking a 7-foot-1 David and building a Goliath of a team. The Navy sunk those plans two weeks before the season, ruling Robinson had to serve another year before joining the Spurs.

Without Robinson, the Spurs suffered a team-record, 13-game losing streak en route to the worst season in franchise history. Brown clashed with the best player he inherited, All-Star Alvin Robertson. Brown clashed with the best player he brought in, 6-7 rookie swingman Willie Anderson. Brown, in short, crashed and burned to his first losing season in 17 as a head coach.

With Robinson -- plus veteran reinforcements Terry Cummings, Maurice Cheeks and Caldwell Jones -- home attendance is at a franchise high of 14,071 per game. The HemisFair crowd usually gets what it comes to see, too. The Spurs are tied for the best home record in the league at 21-2.

"Things have definitely turned around," Anderson says. "A year ago, I wouldn't have believed it if someone had said we would be 32-14. But then if someone had told me the team would consist of the guys it does now . . . "

The Spurs were a revolving door leading to nowhere last season, using a league-high 22 players -- to used the word loosely. A May 28 trade that sent Robertson to the Milwaukee Bucks and brought Cummings proved to be a step in the right direction. Exactly three months later, the Spurs acquired the 6-1 Cheeks, swingman David Wingate and center Chris Welp from the Philadelphia 76ers for guard Johnny Dawkins and forward Jay Vincent. The draft brought Elliott. Free agency brought Jones, a 39-year-old settling influence for Robinson. Just like that, Brown had purged himself of the team even he had to have been tempted to boo.

"I looked at our team at training camp," Brown says, recalling the dark days, "and I honestly thought the University of Kansas could have beaten that team because of our attitude and work habits and stuff. It was a terrible feeling going into the arena and going around town. People were upset. You know, a lot of people get pretty personal with it. I'd never been in a situation where the fans didn't appreciate what we were doing. It has always been like a honeymoon for me."

Now it's like one wedding night after another for the Spurs. A lovefest.

"The chemistry is right," Cummings says. "The attitude is right. People will do whatever it takes to win. We have people who have accepted their role."

Cummings is a proven All-Star-level player, an eight-year veteran whose 22.4 points and 8.1 rebounds per game are right in line with his career figures. He is unflinching in tough situations, fortified, no doubt, by having overcome early in his career a scare caused by having an irregular heartbeat. Anderson made the right decision to give up the trumpet, if his versatility on a basketball court (16.1 points, 4.8 rebounds, 4.1 assists) means anything. Cheeks, at age 33, is elderly by point-guard standards. He is around as much for his steadiness and unflappability as his 11.1 points and 6.0 assists per game.

Frank Brickowski is "Brick," though his shooting touch is nothing of the sort. Since taking over for Elliott as a starter at forward two weeks ago, Brickowski has averaged 10 points and five rebounds on 68% shooting.

Robinson is the anchor of it all, an ominous defensive presence Golden State Coach Don Nelson likens to Hall of Famer Bill Russell. For good measure, Robinson contributes 23.2 points, 11.8 rebounds, 3.1 blocked shots and 2.2 assists per game. Cheeks credits Robinson for making all of the Spurs' pieces fit together. The pressure defense. The passing game on offense.

"David has a lot to do with it," says Cheeks, who touts Robinson as the league's Most Valuable Player. "When you have a big man to go to, a lot of things around here get easier. He makes everyone around him better."

Robinson arguably has outshined the rookie performance of the NBA's most highly acclaimed big men, Patrick Ewing and Akeem Olajuwon. Ewing averaged 20 points, nine rebounds and two blocks as a rookie, compared to 20, 12 and three for Olajuwon. Keep in mind, also, that Robinson hasn't had the luxury -- except for a stint on the 1988 Olympic team -- of honing his skills against top-flight competition the last two years.

"I need to play defense and rebound," Robinson says. "They don't need me to score 30 points and do everything.

"Once I get into double figures in points, I don't think about it anymore. Rebounding, I think of constantly because I never know how many I have -- unless I only have two rebounds at halftime. Then they'll say, 'You've only got two rebounds.' So if I know how many rebounds I have, I'm in trouble."

Suffice it to say, Robinson isn't in trouble often. He had 33 points, 21 rebounds, six assists and five blocked shots, for instance, in a 112-111 home victory against Chicago Feb. 3. He more than held his own in Sunday's All-Star game with 15 points and 10 rebounds.

"I knew he could be great," Brown says, "but I had no idea it would come this quick."

All Robinson has going for him is intelligence (1,320 on his SAT), Olajuwonesque quickness, deceptive strength near the basket, a deft left-handed touch. He has enough respect from the referees to rank second in the league in free-throw attempts. Best of all, he expects even more from himself. All-Star as a rookie? He shrugs.

"I hope it's not the greatest thing that ever happens in my career," Robinson says. "Or my career won't be very long."

In all likelihood, the Spurs will have to wait a bit for greatness as a team. They are just getting to know each other, they lack scoring punch off the bench, and Brown considers them vulnerable on the boards. Cheeks is playing 35 minutes a night on legs that aren't 25 anymore. At times, Elliott has looked like the guy whose team got beat, 100-18, and the Spurs don't figure to be at their peak until he has established himself as a prime-time starter.

Still, the Spurs already are a light-year way away from the days of Anderson saying, "This isn't a team. This is a soap opera." Winning cures a lot of ills, including tense player-coach relationships.

"I think what coach Brown and I went through last year gave us an understanding of each other," Anderson says. "I know now that he says things negative to motivate you and improve you. I understand him now and our relationship is better because I know he's trying to help, not bring me down. That's what I thought last year. I thought I was playing against him and the other team."

Brown knows the feeling. There were times it seemed like he was taking on the world. The boos still ring in his ears of a 49-year-old man who is perpetually in between homes.

"I learned about people," Brown says. "I will never take winning for granted. I appreciate the success that we've had so much more."
