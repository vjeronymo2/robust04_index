Language: English Article Type:BFN [Report by Linda Hutchinson] [Text] Port-of-Spain, Trinidad, 25 Jan, CANA -- The Trinidad and Tobago Government will accelerate its efforts to recover money owed to it by at least six Caribbean Community (Caricom) countries, a government minister reported Tuesday. "This process was started by the Ministry of Finance in 1993. In 1994, the minister of finance will attempt to accelerate the process through direct contact with his counterparts in the defaulting countries," Junior Finance Minister Ken Valley said in the Senate. "These efforts are expected to culminate in 1994 with the full regularisation of the arrears." He named Antigua and Barbuda, Dominica, Grenada, Jamaica, St. Lucia, and St. Vincent and the Grenadines as countries not currently servicing all of their liabilities to Trinidad and Tobago. "In some instances, no terms and conditions were determined for the repayment of the advances," he reported. "It is also the case in many instances that the countries defaulted several years ago. At this point, therefore, it has become necessary to verify the outstanding balances before the full recovery can be negotiated." At the end of December last year, U.S.517 million dollars was due to Trinidad and Tobago by its Caricom partners. The money represents only the bilateral portion of Trinidad and Tobago's debt portfolio. The multilateral arrears under the collapsed Caricom multilateral clearing facility amounted to almost 33 million dollars at the end of last year. Out of the indebted countries, only Belize and St. Kitts are fully current and to-date they have repaid principal sums of TT 65,620 dollars and 1,555,914 dollars (one TT dollar = 17.5 U.S. cents) respectively on the original loan balances, the minister said. Antigua and Barbuda owed U.S.2.5 million dollars; Dominica 1.3 million dollars; Grenada over 600,000 dollars; Jamaica 97,000 dollars; St. Lucia 388,000 dollars; and St. Vincent and the Grenadines 100,000 dollars. The Trinidad and Tobago Government was expected to sign, early this year, a bilateral agreement with the Government of Guyana, whose bilateral liability to the government here and the central bank was U.S.487 million dollars or 94 percent of the total debt. The Guyana debt was restructured in 1989 but subsequently fell into arrears. In August last year, a team of government officials from Trinidad met with Guyanese counterparts to negotiate a memorandum of understanding for the consolidation of the debt. A memorandum was signed by both governments and forms the basis of a bilateral agreement between both countries. The agreement provides for the liquidation of the arrears of 34.5 million dollars which was due from August 1990 to July 1993. The agreement provides for a rescheduling of these arrears over a twelve-year period with a six-month moratorium on principal repayment. The 1993 agreement also provides for two semi-annual payments of 10.5 million dollars in 1994 and similar amounts in 1995 and 1996. "Payments falling due under this agreement may be reduced by a range of debt conversion techniques including debt-equity swaps," said Valley. In pursuit of debt-equity swap opportunities, in March this year, the government is mounting an investment and trade mission to Guyana. The aim of the mission is to identify businesses in Guyana in which the participants, both from the private and state enterprises sectors, will be prepared to make equity investments." With regard to the recovery of the amounts due to the Trinidad and Tobago Government and central bank by the government and Central Bank of Barbados, both governments last December signed a memorandum of understanding. The memorandum provides for the set-off of the government of Trinidad and Tobago's share of the liability of the cargo airline Caricargo to the Barbados National Bank against the debt of the Government of Barbados and the Central Bank of Barbados to Trinidad and Tobago. Caricargo's debt to the Bank of Barbados is Bds59 million dollars (one Bds dollar = 50 U.S. cents) with Trinidad and Tobago's share being 29.5 million dollars. The debt set-off will result in Barbados' U.S.25 million dollar liability being reduced by U.S.15.3 million dollars.
