BFN [Report by Cecilia Cayone; from the "Union Radio News" newscast] [Text] The Chamber of Deputies has agreed to condemn unanimously any U.S. military intervention in Haiti. In addition to condemning any unilateral or multilateral military intervention, through the agreement approved by the Chamber, the lawmakers also decided to reject pressure from the United States to receive quotas of Haitian refugees. The lawmakers also demanded an end to the economic blockade on Haiti because they believe it affects the innocent population instead of the government. Deputy Herman Monzon, chairman of the Lower Chamber Foreign Policy Commission, gives more details about this decision: [Begin Monzon recording, broadcast in progress] ...to condemn the possibility of an intervention in Haiti by the U.S. An intervention of this nature would be a costly operation and it would involve the possibility of loss of human lives and material losses. I would recommend this money be used to develop Haiti. This is an historic juncture. Venezuela must once more assume leadership in Latin American to restore legitimacy in Haiti and to support the Haitian people. The Haitian people need the help of the entire international community. [end recording] In addition, Foreign Minister Miguel Angel Burelli Rivas reiterated that so far our country has not received an asylum request from Haitian refugees. The foreign minister made these statements when leaving Congress after a summons by the Foreign Policy Commission regarding Haitian refugees. When asked about the number of Haitians who would enter the country, the foreign minister, who was visibly disturbed, said the following: [Begin Burelli recording] You want me to say no flatly. Obviously, a country that received assistance from Haiti at a historical moment cannot say no. But we will only receive those we deem convenient. The three, four, or five Haitians who will help us investigate specific nuclear problems in Haiti will come, just like Manigat and his wife did. But for heaven's sake, stop speculating that 150,000 Haitians will come in planeloads or shiploads. Such speculations are ill-intended. I said here many times that only 35 Haitians have reached the U.S. ships off the coast of Jamaica. Of these, only six Haitians qualified for asylum and 29 others were returned. Just imagine, we will assess these cases in our embassy in Washington, and the ambassador will call here or send us a note when a Haitian who wants to go to Venezuela gets to the embassy. [end recording] Finally, the foreign minister reiterated that the United States has not pressured our country to accept Haitian refugees.
