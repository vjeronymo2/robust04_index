January 20, 1989, Friday, Home Edition

SIZING UP PRESIDENT BUSH: EFFECTIVE, NOT A DAREDEVIL

President Bush will not be a clone of President Reagan. But how different will he be, and in what ways?

The short answer to the first question is very, and to the second, most. Not different in the basic objectives that he seeks, but different in the ways in which he pursues them.

Ronald Reagan touched a certain mystic chord in the nation's heart. A Bush presidency will be more prose and less poetry. Reagan kept his eye on the goal. Bush will focus more on the means of reaching it. Reagan had an unparalleled ability to reach out and touch millions of Americans at once through television. Bush will reach out and touch many more people individually, and will track more closely the inner workings of his own Administration.

George Bush has always had an adventurous spirit, but has followed it in a measured way. In World War II he became the Navy's youngest pilot, and a good one -- he won the Distinguished Flying Cross and three Air Medals -- but being a good combat pilot means being effective, not being a daredevil. After college he set out on his own for the oil fields of Texas, but carefully kept his ties to the banking world of the Northeast. He doesn't shrink from risks, but he prefers prudent risks.

One of the strongest influences on his life was his father, U.S. Sen. Prescott Bush, who was also a managing partner of the elite New York investment-banking firm of Brown Brothers Harriman & Co.

As a senator Pres Bush didn't make large waves. That wasn't his style. But he did win high respect from his Senate colleagues. He had a firm sense of right and wrong, and held himself to rigorous standards of personal conduct. An ardent internationalist, he helped persuade Dwight Eisenhower to get into the 1952 presidential race. He was courteous, sometimes even courtly, with a conservative's instinctive distrust of government panaceas but also a firm belief that government exists to serve the public, not vice versa.

Call it social conscience or a patrician sense of noblesse oblige, but the whole Bush family was imbued with a deeply rooted sense of public duty. To the Bush clan, public service is not someone else's responsibility. It's theirs.

George Bush is not a theoretician. He does not have an intellectual turn of mind. He habitually deals with the concrete, not the abstract. He does have a keenly honed sense of how the world works. As U.S. ambassador to the United Nations, head of the U.S. mission to China and director of the CIA, he saw the real world beyond the U.S. borders. And there is a down-home quality about his thought processes that creates real empathy with people on an individual basis while leaving his view of the bad guys and how they operate in the world remarkably unclouded.

Because of this down-home quality and his own familiarity with Washington, the snake-oil salesmen who so heavily populate the Beltway area won't find him to be an easy sell. He has heard their pitches before. He knows the words -- and also the music.

It's no coincidence that for years his closest political confidant and collaborator has been Secretary of State-designate James A. Baker III. Baker is called a pragmatist. To the rigid ideologue a pragmatist is a person without principle. In the real world a pragmatist is often one who maneuvers to get as much of what his principles dictate as he can, then moves on to the next battle. Ideologues make better copy because they make livelier drama. But mixing in some pragmatism makes better policy.

Soon, of course, the "inconsistencies" game will begin. Analysts not capable of holding two thoughts in their heads at the same time will point to separate Bush initiatives and declare them inconsistent with one another. Already the game has begun. To 90% of the Beltway crowd, kinder and gentler means one thing: fatter federal social programs funded by higher taxes. Bush doesn't believe that taxes are necessarily kind and gentle.

But if people read his lips correctly, the new President should not be a font of major surprises. By and large, he is the sort who says what he means and means what he says and who doesn't feel that he has to raise his voice to make his point. By listening more closely, those who at first find his actions puzzling may in time find them less so.
