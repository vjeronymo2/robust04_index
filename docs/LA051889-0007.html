May 18, 1989, Thursday, Home Edition

BACK TO BASICS: THERMOMETERS THAT TAKE THE HEAT

In cooking procedures such as baking, roasting, candy making and deep-fat frying, accurate temperatures are critical. Using a thermometer can mean the difference between success and failure of a recipe.

There are two basic types of thermometers -- bimetal and glass-tube. "Cooking A to Z" (Ortho Books: 1988, $32.95) explains: "Bimetal thermometers have a face, like a dial, and a pointer. The pointer moves by action of a spring made of two metals welded together, which react differently to heat. A change in temperature causes the spring to wind or unwind and move the pointer around the dial. Bimetal thermometers are less accurate than the glass-tube type, but the dials are easier to read and they hold a temperature reading longer."

The book goes on to say: "Glass-tube models register temperature by the expansion or contraction of a fluid -- mercury or a colored liquid -- trapped in a thin column of glass." It notes that "Glass-tube thermometers are very accurate and responsive, but are fragile and more difficult to read than a dial type."

Some thermometers are multipurpose and have a wide temperature range (all temperatures in this column are given in Fahrenheit). Others have a more narrow range for greater accuracy and are intended for a single purpose, such as the following:

Oven Thermometers -- Although conventional ovens have internal thermostats, they may not always be calibrated accurately and sometimes become defective. Ovens also tend to have hot and cold spots. A supplemental thermometer provides an added check that the appliance is working properly and pinpoints problem areas. Oven thermometers typically register temperatures up to 600 or 700 degrees.

In years past, oven temperatures were often expressed in words, but today the use of exact degrees is standard. Here's a translation of the terms:

Very slow -- 250 to 300 degrees

Slow -- 300 to 325 degrees

Moderately slow -- 325 to 350 degrees

Moderate -- 350 to 375 degrees

Quick moderate -- 375 to 400 degrees

Moderately hot -- 400 to 425 degrees

Hot -- 425 to 450 degrees

Very hot -- 450 to 500 degrees

Extremely hot -- 500 to 525 degrees

Refrigerator/Freezer Thermometers -- Like their oven counterparts, these thermometers are used to double-check the appliance's internal thermostat. The recommended setting for refrigerators is between 32 and 40 degrees; freezers should be kept at zero degrees.

Meat Thermometers -- Internal temperature is the best indication of doneness for meats, poultry and game. Slow-response type thermometers register temperatures up to about 200 degrees and are recommended for use in conventional ovens. Insert the probe into the meat or poultry before cooking and leave it in position throughout the cooking period.

Instant-read thermometers register internal temperatures between 60 and 220 degrees after being inserted into food for about 10 seconds. They are particularly useful for taking quick readings of foods cooked by rotisserie and microwave. Only plastic, slow-response thermometers should be used inside microwave ovens.

These are the recommended cooking temperatures for meat and poultry, in Fahrenheit:

Beef -- 125 to 130 degrees for rare; 140 to 145 degrees for medium; 160 degrees for well-done.

Lamb -- 130 to 140 degrees for rare; 145 degrees for medium; 160 degrees for well-done.

Pork -- 145 to 150 degrees for medium; 160 degrees for well-done.

Poultry -- 170 to 175 degrees for medium; 180 degrees for well-done.

Veal -- 140 to 145 degrees for medium; 160 degrees for well-done.

Candy-Making Thermometers -- An upper limit of approximately 325 degrees is necessary to provide a margin of safety above the high of 310 degrees required for some candies. Most are glass-tube types and have a clip so they may be attached to the side of the pan.

Within this classification there are also thermometers designed especially for chocolate. They measure temperatures from about 50 to 140 degrees in wide, one-degree increments that are easy to read.

As with older baking and roasting recipes, candy temperatures are sometimes described in terms of appearance rather than degrees. Here are the equivalents:

Thread -- 230 to 234 degrees

Soft ball -- 234 to 240 degrees

Firm ball -- 244 to 248 degrees

Hard ball -- 250 to 266 degrees

Soft crack -- 270 to 290 degrees

Hard crack -- 300 to 310 degrees

Deep-Fat Frying Thermometers -- To accommodate the high temperatures used for frying, these thermometers typically have an upper limit of 500 degrees. Many can be clipped to the side of the pan so they suspend in the fat without touching the bottom.

It's common to find combination candy and deep-fat frying thermometers.
