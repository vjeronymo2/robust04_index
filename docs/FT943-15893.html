940707 FT 07 JUL 94 / Bar backs system of picking QCs The Bar has come down in favour of retaining the present system for appointing Queen's Counsel in spite of criticism from barristers that the system is secretive and open to abuse. A working party chaired by Mr Michael Kalisher QC says in a report published today that the present system of appointment by the Queen on the advice of the Lord Chancellor is preferable to alternatives such as appointment by an independent commission, or by the Bar itself. The Bar favours retaining the rank of QC because it helps the public and solicitors identify barristers of a recognised high standard. But it recognises that changes would have to be made to the present system to meet the concerns of many barristers. The report recommends publication of formal criteria for getting silk, including outstanding ability as an advocate, experience and knowledge of the law, professional integrity and an extensive practice. The report says appointments should be solely on merit, with no positive discrimination in favour of women or ethnic minorities. The number of appointments should remain low to underline the importance of the rank but numbers should not be artificially restricted. Applications have risen from 204 in 1985, when 34 were appointed, to 539 in 1994, when 77 were appointed. The barristers say the high number of applicants threatens the efficacy of the system as the judges and senior barristers who are consulted on appointments cannot give every application the consideration it requires.
