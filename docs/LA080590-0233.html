August 5, 1990, Sunday, Home Edition

TAX SAVINGS ON MORTGAGE EXAGGERATED

QUESTION: My father-in-law is deceased now and my mother-in-law would like to pay off the mortgage on her home. But one of her sons insists on keeping a small mortgage on it for tax purposes.

My father-in-law, every few years, would take out a mortgage so he could claim it on taxes. I cannot believe the benefits of this outweigh the interest rate, plus the bother of making these payments, etc. Even if you deposit this money from the mortgage at 5% or 6% it still is less than your mortgage payment interest. What's your opinion on this?

ANSWER: This notion that you must have a mortgage is one that dies hard. The value of mortgage interest deductibility -- under the most ideal of circumstances -- is almost always grossly exaggerated. We're talking about X number of dollars in interest that will be deducted from your taxable income, and the resulting figure will then be taxed, probably, at about 28%.

For every dollar that he's spending on interest he's saving 28 cents. Big deal! And, of course, he's also paying for the cost of borrowing it in the first place. As you say, what's the attraction of paying a lender 10% on money that you might then turn around and invest at 8%?

And your mother-in-law is undoubtedly living on a fixed, reduced income and probably is being taxed (if at all) at the minimum rate, anyway. If she wants the peace of mind of having the home free of indebtedness, it's none of her son's business.

Giving Property Back to S&L Not Applauded

Q: Recently, you advised an elderly man from New Orleans to "tell the S&L to take the property back." You seem to think it's the S&L's fault that he has a negative cash flow.

This irks me for a couple of reasons: The first is that elderly people have enough trouble receiving credit as it is, in fact we have laws to protect them.

Secondly, part of the reason that the S&L industry is as big a mess as it is people with the "give it back" mentality. More palatable suggestions should have included renegotiating the rate or refinancing the loan.

A: The elderly gentleman (he was in his 80s) wasn't experiencing a negative cash flow, or, more importantly, rapidly exhausting his liquid resources because of any fault of the S&Ls involved.

He was experiencing this because he was voluntarily -- and steeply -- accelerating the payoff of the mortgages on both pieces of income property simply to benefit his heirs.

This 80-year-old man, at his age, simply could not afford to strap himself and deplete his irreplaceable resources by pandering to his heirs.

If immediately stopping the principal-only acceleration didn't put his financial house in order then, I advised him, let the S&L take one of the properties back. At his age he should worry about a black mark on his credit?

More Frequent Payoffs Won't Make Difference

Q: I read an article some time ago about making payments on a home weekly instead of monthly. If you made 52 weekly payments for the same total amount as 12 monthly payments on a 10-year loan of $70,000 at 12%, what would be the savings on interest? What is the formula to figure this out?

Actually, making the same annual payment on a weekly instead of a monthly basis (if you could find a lender obliging enough to go along with it -- which is doubtful) wouldn't have much of an impact.

On this 10-year loan that you cite, by going weekly would save you only $460 in interest ($50,055 instead of $50,515). The formula is loan balance multiplied by the interest rate and divided by 12, or, in this case, divided by 52.

Unless there is some acceleration involved -- another $5, $10, $20 or $50 per week or month -- frequency of payoff without acceleration doesn't make any dramatic waves. Sorry.

Why is there something less than comforting in knowing that, after paying $5,045 in monthly payments in your first year of a mortgage -- on $50,000 at 9.5% -- you have built up a fat $308 in equity? That's about 6% of your payments. The other 94% went to the lender in interest.
