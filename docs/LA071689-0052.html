July 16, 1989, Sunday, Home Edition

PULL LIGHT INTO HOME FROM ABOVE;

BETTER SKYLIGHTS MAKE INSTALLATION AND UPKEEP EASIER FOR HOMEOWNERS.

Skylights: For the past 20 years or so, they have been one of the hottest "accessories" to be added to the home. And during that time, the reasons for adding them have gotten better and better.

Not many years ago, skylights were "net energy wasters." What they gained in terms of free light and wintertime solar heating, they more than lost in terms of winter heat loss and unwanted summer heat gain.

To solve that problem, the major makers formed a committee to discover ways to improve efficiency. What they found was that most energy loss was taking place through highly conductive metal skylight frames, and through air leaks.

It didn't take them long to remedy that situation. Improved seals and gaskets were the answer to air leaks. To combat conductive losses through the frame, some makers switched from metal to vinyl or fiberglass. Others stayed with metal, but installed "thermal breaks," insulating barriers of plastic between inner and outer shells of the frame.

With those improvements, skylights turned into net energy gainers. And with the introduction in the past few years of new energy-efficient glazing systems such as Low-E glass and Heat Mirror, energy performance has gone up another notch.

Today, a good skylight can probably pay for itself in energy savings in 15 years or less, including the price of installation. And some are so easy to install that you can do the job yourself, cutting pay-back time even further.

If all this convinces you to put a skylight or two in your home, here are some tips and features to look for.

SIZING: As a rule of thumb, a skylight should have an area roughly equal to 1/23 the area of the room it will serve. If you want extra light -- perhaps for a plant room -- you should increase the size. You'll also need a bigger skylight if your ceilings are higher than the usual eight feet.

GLAZING: Single- glazed skylights are OK in the West or other regions where the climate is mild. But even there, double glazing can help fight excessive heat gain. In colder climates, single glazing just doesn't make sense. Here you should consider, in order of increasing effectiveness, double glazing, Low-E glass, and Heat Mirror systems. What about plastic? There was a time when the plastic bubble ruled the roof. But over the years, glass has begun to take over, partly because of its flat, crisp look, and partly because of the efficiency of the new glass glazing systems. Double-glazed plastic still performs well, however, and if you live in the hail belt, plastic's impact resistance is a strong selling point.

FEATURES: If you plan to try installing a skylight yourself, you'll want a self-curbing design with an easy installation system. Try to get ahold of the installation instructions for any skylight you are considering. If you don't like what you see, investigate another brand. Some makers such as Wasco actually encourage do-it-yourself installation. Others don't.

VENTING OR FIXED? Skylights that open may cost 50% more than fixed types, but that can be money well spent in some cases. Located high in the ceiling, an open skylight does a good job of venting off hot, stuffy air. I have two skylights in my bedroom and opening them both can usually get me through a hot summer night without air conditioning.

Some venting skylights are designed to open so you can clean them from inside the house, and this can be a boon if you live in an area with dusty or polluted air or heavy bird traffic. If your skylight will be located high in a cathedral ceiling, you might want to invest in a motorized opening system, controlled by a wall switch. I run my bedroom skylights by hand with a long extension crank. It's not particularly easy to handle.

SHADES: At certain times of the day or year, a skylight can let in too much light. For those occasions, a shade is the answer. Most makers offer them as options. I put a light tan roll-up shade on the skylight in my dressing room. I still get lots of light, but I keep direct sun off the clothes in the open closets, and that helps prevent fading. If you want to block off light almost completely, a shade in a dark, opaque color will do the trick.
