November 23, 1989, Thursday, Home Edition

DR. JOYCE BROTHERS: A DOG FOR DAD? ASK HIM FIRST

DEAR DR. BROTHERS: My father, who's 75, always wanted a pet, but my mother was allergic so they never had one during their marriage. Unfortunately, my mother died six months ago, and my father is having a difficult time adjusting. My husband and I are wondering about buying him a dog. Is this a good idea?

-- B.L.

DEAR B.L.: It might be great, but I certainly wouldn't do anything until you've talked it over with him first. It's important to find out if he can have a pet where he lives and also to consider if he's able to give an animal the care and attention it needs.

Having a dog is a responsibility, as well as a joy. Just as with humans, it takes time for relationships to build. Puppies always need to be housebroken. Even well-trained, older dogs may make mistakes for the first night or two while getting adjusted to a new person and a new home.

If your father is able to walk the dog, this would probably be good for him, as well as for the animal. It would get him out of the house and give him a new interest. You might want to think about getting an older dog, one who's gentle and trained and one whose disposition and temperament would blend well with your father.

Studies show that good pets help to alleviate loneliness, and stroking pets can lower the blood pressure. If your father is contemplating a move, however, it might be better to wait until he's settled.

DEAR DR. BROTHERS: I think my 34-year-old son is making a terrible mistake, but there's not much use of my talking to him because he never listens to his father. He's about to pass up a promotion in order to have more time to stay at home with his two children. He tells me this isn't as unusual as it was in my day. It seems absurd to me. This isn't a man's role. He has a wife, and even though she works, it seems to me he's trying to do her job.

-- L.C.

DEAR L.C.: Your son is correct when he reports that more and more young fathers are choosing to be good fathers first and are placing their families ahead of their jobs. Many of these men do this because they feel they didn't get to see enough of their own fathers in their childhood and are determined to be closer to their children than their fathers were to them.

There's no question about it, we're living through a social revolution when it comes to sex roles. Men used to define themselves and their role in society as the breadwinner, but men are no longer the only breadwinners. Many children are being raised in two-career families, such as your son's, and when this is the case, both partners frequently find themselves making some sacrifices in furthering their careers in order to be better parents.
