July 14, 1990, Saturday, Orange County Edition

ACTIVISTS SHOW SUPPORT FOR NEA, WARN OF BACKLASH;

ART: SUPPORTERS OF THE NATIONAL AGENCY MEET IN HUNTINGTON BEACH TO CRITICIZE WHAT THEY CALL A RIGHT-WING MOVEMENT IN THIS COUNTRY TO INHIBIT AND CENSOR THE ARTS.

In the first major public show of support in Orange County for the National Endowment for the Arts, about 150 people crowded into City Council chambers here Thursday night to listen as artists and arts activists spoke out in defense of the embattled federal agency.

"The political right wing has awakened a sleeping giant, and they're going to live to regret it," offered Michael Hudson, vice president and general counsel for the organization People For the American Way, which is actively engaged in a range of censorship issues (the group is backing choreographer Bella Lewitzky in her suit against the NEA, filed Thursday).

The formal part of the three-hour program was closed by Tim Miller, one of four performance artists who had been recommended for NEA fellowship grants by a peer review panel, but whose grants were turned down by agency Chairman John E. Frohnmayer, apparently to head off criticism from conservative critics.

"I'm one artist who has fallen prey to the far right wing of this country," said Miller, who often tackles gay-rights issues in his work. "I'm extremely frightened at what is happening."

Miller, now a Santa Monica resident, grew up in Orange County and told the audience of his youth here, including his first performances at Cal State Fullerton and Fullerton College. He attributed the denial of his NEA grant to "a fearful, reactionary impulse" on the part of the "white male power structure," and said it is part of a larger societal attack on people outside the mainstream.

Thursday's meeting took place within the district of Rep. Dana Rohrabacher (R -- Lomita), who, along with Sen. Jesse Helms (R-N.C.), has led congressional attacks on the NEA. The freshman congressman, who representions portions of Orange and Los Angeles counties, came under frequent attack during the forum. Jeffrey Chester, a media consultant, announced the formation of a Political Action Committee that will work specifically to defeat Rohrabacher when he comes up for reelection in November.

In addition, campaign supporters said they will circulate flyers today that denounce Rohrabacher's record on a number of issues in several Long Beach and Orange County shopping malls.

Thursday's lone spoken dissent came from two members of the Bieber family of Huntington Beach: son James, a national director of the conservative Young Americans for Freedom, and father Dale, who identified himself as an artists' agent. Both argued from the Libertarian perspective, saying that artists are not entitled to public funds and that taxpayers should not be forced to pay for works that may offend them.

The dispute between conservatives in Congress and the NEA began last year after protests from conservative groups over federal support of art shows containing controversial photographs by Andres Serrano and Robert Mapplethorpe. Last fall, Congress enacted a provision requiring 1990 grant recipients to sign an oath vowing not to produce "obscene" works.

In late June, Frohnmayer approved grants for 14 solo performance artists, but denied grants to Miller, Karen Finley, Holly Hughes and John Fleck, all of whom deal with political and sexual issues in their work. The action was roundly condemned by the arts community and has helped to escalate the battle over the NEA.

Long Beach-based video artist Bill Viola, reading from a 20-page written statement, offered a detailed defense of the agency, calling it a "shining example of an arts patronage system" -- one that costs the average individual about 68 cents a year to support.

In its 25-year history, Viola said, the agency has handed out more than 80,000 grants. "That a few individuals can turn around and, because less than 20 of these projects have been overtly offensive to some, cry out for the abolition of the agency is beyond me and beyond any exercise of common sense and rationality," the artist said.

Thursday's forum was organized by the nascent Orange County Committee of the Long Beach/Orange County Coalition for Freedom of Expression, with help from Fullerton-based Poets Reading Inc. The event, which opened with performances by local musicians and a poet, was billed as a "call to action" for local artists and other citizens to support a reauthorized NEA.
