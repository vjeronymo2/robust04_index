BFN [Text] Beijing, May 20 KYODO -- North Korea's ambassador to China said Friday [20 May] that the International Atomic Energy Agency (IAEA) can inspect and measure spent fuel rods from their nuclear reactor only after bilateral talks with the United States are resumed. Ambassador Chu Chang-chun told reporters the dialogue with the U.S., which was suspended after two rounds last July, should be continued if the nuclear issue on the Korean peninsula is to be resolved. Technicians at North Korea's 5-megawatt Yongbyon experimental nuclear reactor began replacing spent fuel rods Saturday without inspectors from the IAEA present, a move which violates the Nuclear Nonproliferation Treaty (NPT) and other agreements between North Korea and the IAEA. Three IAEA inspectors now at Yongbyon have not been allowed to take samples or make tests on the spent rods, reports said. "We will contain the spent fuel rods in a safe place and display them before the inspectors. Then the inspectors can verify that our nuclear materials have not been diverted to nonpeaceful purposes," Chu said. "This will be the inspection for the continuity of the safeguards, this is to show the transparency of our nuclear activities," he said. "When the third round of talks between the DPRK (North Korea) and the U.S. are held and a package solution is realized, then the agency (IAEA) can take the measurements at any time," he added. The Vienna-based IAEA has insisted that the spent rods must be sorted and examined to see whether any spent fuel has been diverted to North Korea's suspected nuclear weapons program. Chu claimed that North Korea unilaterally suspended withdrawal from the NPT last summer when talks with the U.S. began and now has ''a special status'' within the treaty which releases it from its NPT obligations. ''We cannot allow the agency to conduct the routine and ad hoc inspections because it is contrary to the special status of our country,'' Chu said. The ambassador blamed the U.S. for fabricating the North Korean nuclear issue as a means to slander his country, while demanding that the U.S. honor its commitment to provide Pyongyang with a light-water nuclear reactor. Chu also accused Japan of nuclear ambitions, while continuing to maintain that up to 1,000 nuclear devices are on the Korean peninsula and they are all U.S.-made. "That is why we insist that talks be held between the DPRK and the U.S.," he said. The 15-month-long dispute has become central to North Korean relations with the U.S. as well as the eventual easing of tension and possible reunification of the Korean peninsula.
