May 19, 1989, Friday, Home Edition

SWITZER FINDS NFL MORE APPEALING;

OKLAHOMA COACH CITES PROBLEMS WITH COLLEGE ATHLETES

Oklahoma football Coach Barry Switzer says he would be interested in joining the National Football League because of problems he's experienced trying to handle the college athletes of the 1980s.

"Coaching collegiate football (in the 1970s), you didn't have the problems you do today," Switzer said during his radio show, which aired Thursday on the Oklahoma News Network. "Obviously, it was more fun than it is today because of all the problems."

In December, the Oklahoma football team was placed on three years' probation by the National Collegiate Athletic Assn. for recruiting violations, and several Sooner players have had recent brushes with the law.

"You've got the restrictions at NCAA. You've got the drug-alcohol abuse problems that didn't exist at the magnitude that they do today," Switzer said. "There's so many things that would make it much more enjoyable and easier to coach in professional football.

"I enjoy still what I'm doing. There's only a few jobs in professional football. There's hundreds of college jobs and there's only 28 NFL jobs. Of those 28 NFL jobs, there's only a few really good jobs."

Switzer said during the show taped Wednesday that he had an opportunity for a professional coaching job in the 1970s, but such a position would be more attractive than in the past.

"Obviously, I've got some friends that have gone on and accepted head coaching positions (in the pros)," Switzer said. "I would be more attuned today to entertain any opportunity than I have been in the past."

What would it take to make him leave the Sooners?

"I think, obviously, the job opportunity," he said. "You always listen. (Oklahoma basketball Coach) Billy Tubbs and I both have talked about the professional ranks and the opportunities."

Switzer said he liked the thought of disciplining players with financial penalties rather than unscheduled workouts.

"You don't want to worry about making a kid run stadium steps at 6 o'clock in the morning if he cuts a class or is late for a meeting," Switzer said. "(In the NFL), you just dock him 500 or a thousand bucks. It gets their attention. If they want to continue to do that. That's great."
