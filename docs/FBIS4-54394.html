BFN [From BTA press review] [Text] Sofia, April 29 (BTA) -- Yesterday's resignation of Deputy Prime Minister and Minister of Trade Valentin Karabashev is in the highlights of today's press. Most dailies carry frontpage reports commenting on Mr. Karabashev's motives. The fate of Deputy Prime Minister Karabashev was decided by his Alternative Social Liberal Party (ASP) as early as last week, reads an editorial in DEMOKRATSIYA, the daily of the Union of Democratic Forces (SDS). It goes on to say that Mr. Karabashev's resignation had to do with the privatization by businessmen close to the ASP of two hotels in the Sunny Beach resort on the Black Sea. Minister of Industry Rumen Bikov, the other ASP representative in the cabinet, will stay on for the time being. However, the private STANDART NEWS writes that Mr. Bikov was likely to resign, too. He had told the daily's report that he considered doing so. 24 CHASA carries an interview with Mr. Karabashev. "At first I was offered to stay as a deputy prime minister or a minister without portfolio, but in the course of the conversation I had with Mr. Berov it did not become clear to me what my responsibilities were supposed to be. Using other sources, I found out that he himself was unclear about it," he said. In his opinion, what the prime minister intends to do would be disastrous to the government. OTECHESTVEN VESTNIK writes that "after Valentin Karabashev's resignation (backed by the parliamentary New Union for Democracy, which withdrew its support from Lyuben Berov's cabinet) the Movement for Rights and Freedoms (DPS) will now find itself in the spotlight and will hardly muster the courage to accept the conditions of the Bulgarian Socialist Party (BSP).... Facing the threat of ultimate discredit if they do so, it would be no surprise if the DPS decide to follow suit and withdraw their support from the cabinet, thus prompting procedures for the formation of a new cabinet. KONTINENT comments on the same issue under the headline "Struggle for Ministerial Posts." "The resignation of Mr. Karabashev as deputy prime minister paves the way for the formal procedures for the expected structural and personal changes which the government and parliament have been using for months to fool the crisis-crushed electorate." "The cabinet overhaul has nothing to do with the work the ministers have or have not done for the nation; it has to do with the work they have or have not done to satisfy certain private interests," the daily comments. Newspapers hurry to prognosticate on the future members of the cabinet. STANDART NEWS mentions the names of Emil Khursev, ex-vice governor of the central bank, Aleksandur Tomov, leader of the Civil Alliance for the Republic (GOR, a centrist formation which split off from the BSP), and Chavdar Kunchev, head of the Bulgarian Foreign Trade Bank, as possible candidates for the post of deputy prime minister on the foreign debt. According to DEMOKRATSIYA and STANDART NEWS, the next minister of trade would be Bulgaria's Ambassador to Japan Konstantin Glavanakov, a friend of ex-Prime Minister and MP of the BSP Andrey Lukanov.
