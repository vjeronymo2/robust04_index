Language: Bulgarian Article Type:BFN [Commentary by Evgeni Gavrilov: "Cabinet Sets New Record in Governing"] [Text] On 30 December Prime Minister Lyuben Berov and his ministers festively marked the first anniversary of their tenure at the head of the executive. This is noteworthy because no other government since 10 November 1989 could match this achievement. Foe a whole year the government based itself on a "dynamic parliamentary majority." Indeed, this was the government's foundation. In the final analysis the cabinet's longevity was a result of Lyuben Berov's ability to balance interests, which at first glance seem to be contradictory. This is because the interests of the Bulgarian Socialist Party [BSP], the Movement for Rights and Freedoms [DPS], and the Union of Democratic Forces [SDS] are rather different. This resulted in certain incomplete decisions that disturbed rather than furthered reform. The cabinet, which survived four no-confidence votes, had succeeded in strengthening its positions only by the end of the year. It was then that its two major successes came -- the deal with the bank-creditors on remitting 50 percent of Bulgaria's foreign debt, and signing a provisional trade agreement with the European Union. Other governments also contributed to those successes, but it was Berov's government that reaped the rewards. However, the successes were preceded by the three great failures of Berov's team -- privatization, land reform, and the budget. In his program speech, precisely one year ago, the prime minister announced that his team would be the government of privatization. The lack of privatization deals was replaced by actions that in the past belonged to the category of organized enterprises. In January the government adopted a decision to prepare a long-range privatization program by the end of May. The program has still not been carried out. In February a group of experts led by Emil Khursev, deputy chairman of the Bulgarian National Bank, submitted a proposal to the Council of Ministers on the participation of the banks in privatization. At a special meeting in March the government adopted a number of decisions related to accelerating privatization, but they remained only on paper. In May there was an inadequately considered proposal by Berov on accelerating mass privatization through promissory notes. Deputy Prime Minister Valentin Karabashev did not like the proposal. Two months later he proposed a draft mass privatization program through investment funds. This time Berov disagreed with the proposal. Finally, in August the Berov-Karabashev hybrid proposal was accepted. Prior to this it had been rejected by everyone, but, nevertheless, it was adopted without any debate at a government meeting. In November the professor told REUTER that he was contemplating the establishment of a ministry of privatization. In December the government admitted that the greatest privatization deal -- the one with the "Balkan" Air Company -- had fallen through. Throughout this entire period the government four times dismissed the members of the Privatization Agency Supervisory Board, appointed on a quota of the government basis, but the Supreme Court restored their membership. The words of the minister of agriculture that in 1993 land will be returned to its owners still hang in the air.
