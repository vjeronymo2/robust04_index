Language: English Article Type:BFN ["Envoy's Remarks Touch Off Confusion on Seoul's Ties With Beijing"] [Text] Beijing, March 30 (YONHAP) -- An envoy's comments touched a raw nerve with the [ROK] president and his aides Tuesday, just when they thought they had finished the tension-packed duties of their visits to Japan and china. South Korean Ambassador to Beijing Hwang Pyong-tae, a former lawmaker, apparently made two big mistakes -- he disclosed the contents of the Korea-China [summit] talks, and what he chose to reveal ran counter to what the president said. President Kim Yong-sam refused to answer questions on the specifics of his talks with Chinese Premier Li Peng at a press conference Tuesday, saying only that his visits to Tokyo and Beijing had helped form a trilateral cooperation channel among the United States, Japan and China. He said he hoped to include Russia in this chain to bring aboard all four regional powers in solving the North Korean nuclear problem. Then came Ambassador Hwang, who arranged an evening press conference at the request of South Korean TV journalists. He suggested that while South Korean-U.S. coordination supplied the impetus for dealing with the nuclear question, it would be South Korean-Chinese coordination from now on. "The discussion on the North Korean nuclear issue between South Korea and China has outgrown the past practice, where South Korea and the United States would only notify the results of their consultations (to others), changing to where South Korea and China would hold deep discussions from the beginning and take joint steps," the ambassador told the press. Asked about Japan and Russia's role, he said Seoul would simply brief Moscow on the results of the negotiations, and "it's realistically difficult to expect Japan to play a role." He went on to disclose the details of the Kim-Li meeting. "Premier Li Peng told President Kim that China would continue to try to solve the nuclear problem through dialogue if North Korea has no nuclear weapons. But if North Korea does have nuclear weapons, China would counter it ever strongly," he said. The envoy then predicted that North Korea would soon resume negotiations with the International Atomic Energy Agency (IAEA) on additional inspections. The news later reached Senior Secretary for Diplomacy Chong Chong-uk and Spokesman Chu Ton-sik, who belatedly tried to cork the story. Chung, Chu, and Hwang came by the press center twice around midnight, saying the facts had been misrepresented and pleading that sensitive parts of the story not be published. A close presidential aide insisted that Hwang's remarks do not reflect the Seoul government's official position: Nor is it true that the South Korea-China channel has reached the same level as negotiations between Seoul and Washington. "All it means is that the channel with China was strengthend through the Beijing visit," the aide said. "It's also not true that North Korea will resume talks with the IAEA soon." President Kim personally refuted Hwang's remarks in a breakfast meeting with the press Wednesday, emphasizing South Korea's affinity to the United States, its close cooperation with allies and Foreign Minister Han Sung-chu's "hasty" trip to Washington. One official at the presidential office [Chongwadae] openly criticized the envoy, saying, "I don't know whether Ambassador Hwang is a diplomat or still a politician."
