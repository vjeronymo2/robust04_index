BFN [Editorial: "China's Goodwill Gesture"] [Text] The Chinese ambassador, Mr Cheng Ruisheng's statement, that Beijing is soon likely to officially recognise Sikkim's merger with India, is further good tidings for New Delhi and indicates that India can now look forward to the dividends of last year's agreement which marked a departure from past hostility between the two countries. Ever since Indira Gandhi's decision to make the "Protectorate" of Sikkim a State of the Republic in 1975, Beijing has steadfastly questioned the legality of the annexation. More than the technicality of the Himalayan kingdom's merger with India and whether or not a sovereign nation had been subjugated, Beijing was bothered by the disappearance of a buffer between India and China. After all, Sikkim had been the gateway to Tibet and its merger with India meant pushing the Indian frontier that much forward. Indeed, Chinese unhappiness with the turn of the events was reflected in Beijing's refusal to acknowledge Sikkim as a part of the Indian Union, a view which has been repeatedly expressed over the years. It was Rajiv Gandhi's visit to China that led to a softening in Beijing's stand on Sikkim insofar as any official mention of the protectorate's merger with India was concerned. It is possible that the Chinese political leadership remained unhappy with the merger, but it refrained from striking public postures on the issue. Two factors have since contributed to the removal of doubts in Beijing about India's motives behind granting Sikkim statehood: First, the Prime Minister's visit to China last year which led to the Sino- Indian agreement on maintaining peace and tranquillity along the Line of Actual Control; and, second, China's interest in forging a regional front to prevent the U.S. from meddling in Asian affairs. Both the factors have contributed towards an upswing in Sino-Indian relations to the detriment of America's hegemonistic designs in the region, an added spinoff being the weakening of the Pakistan-China axis. True, Beijing's official recognition of Sikkim's merger will not necessarily mean the removal of all irritants in Sino-Indian relations; neither should it be seen as indicative of a smooth road to solving the vexatious border dispute. At the same time, the benefits of narrowing down differences in other areas by stepping around what is apparently an intractable problem cannot be over- emphasised. Some problems will continue to plague Sino-Indian relations and both New Delhi and Beijing will have to learn to cope with them if a lasting relation is to be forged between the two Asian giants. For instance, Beijing will have to stop doubting New Delhi's official position on Tibet -- that it is an autonomous zone of China -- and accept the inevitability of pluralism in a democratic society. By cavilling at views on Tibet's future expressed by individuals at seminars and rallies, like it did recently, Beijing will be running the risk of setting the clock back. A better option would be to hasten the reopening of the silk route which once connected two civilisations.
