October 4, 1990, Thursday, Home Edition

ERMA BOMBECK: EMPTY NEST GETS FAST REDECORATION

For a long time sociologists had us believing that the "empty nest" was like some dreaded, uncontrollable disease, symptomatized by crying, long periods of depression and an inability to function.

We'd hear stories about mothers who converted their grown children's rooms into shrines and fathers who called up their married sons and begged them to "come over and shoot a few baskets after dinner."

Rumors circulated that mothers automatically set the table for three for the rest of their lives and dads gave up their bowling nights for fear they'd miss a call from the "kid."

In my mind, I put the empty nest right up there with menopause and gingivitis.

When our last child left home, a friend called and said, "You need company to get you through the first night alone?"

"Actually," I said, "you caught us as we were leaving. We're going out to dinner."

"You're going to have to face that empty house sometime," she warned.

She was right. We filled in all the small holes left by darts and nails and had his room painted and made into a small TV room. We had the rugs cleaned and the oil spots removed from the driveway. After that, we went on our first vacation that wasn't planned around a paper route.

I'm not saying there weren't adjustments. It was weeks before we stopped looking for the TV tuner in the bathroom, months before our hearing returned to normal, and I still haven't gotten over the shock of going into the garage and seeing my own car parked there.

In retrospect, we have learned to live with phones that ring only when they're for us, two loads of laundry a week and popcorn for dinner. Our friends marvel at how well we are able to deal with the trauma of just the two of us entering the twilight years alone. They say we are very brave. We smile and say, "Thank you."

Recently the University of Nebraska researched the empty-nest syndrome and discovered that when children leave home, the marriage of the parents not only improves, but that they may even experience a mini-honeymoon.

Some say this change of attitude is due to the independence of women who go from child raising to a second career that is just as fulfilling. I say there was a whole generation of parents who were lying to us through their teeth just to keep the prophecy alive.
