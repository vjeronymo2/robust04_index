September 24, 1989, Sunday, Home Edition

HOW TO DOFF A SUIT OF ARMOR;

PHYSICAL CULTURE BY HILLARY JOHNSON (POSEIDON PRESS: $16.95; 105 PP.; 0-671-67818-3)

"Physical Culture" is a novella about five days in the life of a middle-aged man named John, the retiring accountant for a mattress factory in an unidentified suburban town, who is an active masochist. John has a young acquaintance named Stephan, a procurer, whom he knows from the "health" club he's frequented in the city, Physical Culture. As the book unfolds, he also develops a relationship with a young woman named Liann, an alcoholic who works at the mattress factory and is the daughter of the woman he drives to work each morning.

The novel is narrated in the first person by John, but, especially at the beginning, it's an opaque, elliptical first-person, almost entirely preoccupied with physical states and processes. In several sentences, for example, the author assumes the narrative perspective of the male sexual organ, which, sans sexual play, is something this reviewer can't remember seeing before in expository prose.

This seems even more provocative in light of the fact that the author, Hillary Johnson, as the publicity kit for "Physical Culture" announces with some pride, is a 23-year-old woman born in Burbank, Calif., who now lives with her husband in Portland, Ore., and Calcutta, India. If the author were a 45-year-old ex-convict from Lompoc, would the reviewer have found the sentence more plausible? Hopefully not. Still, there is an admirable boldness in this first novel, which contains a grimly vivid portrait of the subterranean S&M world. John reports:

"The first person Stephan ever introduced me to was a man six-foot five, with an orange beard that I remember distinctly because the hairs were twice as thick as any human hair I had ever seen. They were curly, too -- tight, wide curls. He took off my shirt and wrapped my upper body in barbed wire, under my armpits and down my arms, then dressed me in a padded vest, a starched white dress shirt, and evening jacket, and took me out dancing. . . .

"(Dancing) I could see little dimes, quarters, and pennies of blood fall to the floor, as if through a hole in my pocket, and get scuffed into streaks by a herd of feet."

At her best, Johnson commands a forceful, evocative prose ("a restaurant where I can hear a whole flat of glasses shivering against each other in the kitchen"), and she possesses a sure sense of how to play a scene for maximum effect and then move on. As John and Liann, each with their separate shame, grow closer, her touch is especially graceful, and these scenes, which might be set into a far more conventional story, indicate a writer of real promise. On the other hand, a stylistic "innovation" in which a line ends with a period before the meaning is clear, and then is concluded in the first line of a new paragraph, never grows into anything beyond distracting mannerism.

The jacket copy and several endorsements from writers promise a denouement that will reveal the deeper personal reality that underlies John's masochism, and it's certainly to Johnson's credit that she's written a novel on so sensationalistic a subject that for the most part keeps its focus trained on the minutely human, the quotidian. The emotional climax occurs when John allows his naked body's visible history of abominations to be glimpsed by Stephan and Liann, and finally, a little later, alone in front of a mirror, looks himself.

It's a powerful scene that promises further growth and change, but the book begins to feel somewhat simplistic as it rounds into its final stretch. John goes back in memory to being 9 years old in "a family full of wits and buffoons and practical jokers enjoying the hideousness of the day. I wondered why my body felt uncomfortable, as if I were clanking around in a suit of armor." Yes, one wants to interject, welcome to the human race.

If physical pain can overwhelm emotional pain, Johnson indicates, then perhaps the pain is less. Then too, there is the control exhibited in writing one's own experience, decreeing the atrocities oneself. In the end, of course, it's a closed circuit in which life, in any larger sense, has been edited out. What is affecting in "Physical Culture" is that while John may have all but forgotten it, even as he advances toward its graces, Hillary Johnson for the most part keeps her eye steadily on that larger life.
