January 20, 1990, Saturday, Home Edition

LOS ANGELES CITY HALL'S PILOT PROJECT TO RECYCLE WASTEPAPER

As the sponsor of the City Hall pilot recycled paper program, we feel compelled to correct the many misstatements of fact in your article ("China Recycling Program a Paper Tiger," Metro, Dec. 3) and to affirm the excellent efforts of both the City of Los Angeles General Services Department and the city employees in making this project successful.

This 6-month pilot was funded at considerable expense entirely by China Paper Partners. It tested a unique concept of separating, collecting and recycling mixed paper and represented the first phase of a project to build a de-inking pulp mill in Tianjin, China.

The intent was to finish the feasibility study in June, break ground in August, and be able to ship Los Angeles wastepaper to the mill when it was finished. Unfortunately, the difficulty in relying on foreign markets for recycling that the United States should be undertaking itself was dramatically underscored by last summer's political events in China.

Nevertheless, Los Angeles made a good faith commitment to explore an innovative and needed means of recycling mixed paper. Neither party walked away from the program. I would like to correct several points:

1. Conservatree Paper Co. and China Paper partners did not "lose interest in" or "abandon" the program in the spring of 1989. Mayor Tom Bradley notified employees about the program by letter in late February.

Several meetings were conducted with the entire custodial services staff in April and May. China Paper Partners launched a follow-up employee education campaign throughout City Hall in early May, 1989, with a second letter of instruction from the mayor.

With the assistance of the Los Angeles Conservation Corps, practically every employee work station in City Hall was visited, with program materials distributed and program operations explained on a one-to-one basis. This was supplemented by decals on all recycling and refuse bins, as well as posters in every elevator lobby.

2. City's Hall's existing white paper and newspaper recycling programs were never discontinued. Rather, our mixed paper recycling program was added to the system.

3. China Paper Partners was not in the business of buying and selling wastepaper. This would occur only if the pilot project succeeded and a mill were established in China.

4. The collection program resulted in more than two-thirds of the waste stream being collected for recycling from both Transamerica and City Hall, more than twice the collection rate of programs previously judged successful.

China Paper partners briefed the mayor's office, the General Services Department, and Councilwoman Ruth Galanter's office, as well as your newspaper, in October and November regarding the status of the program and the uncertainty of the situation in China.

Innovative ventures such as this are complex, particularly when international political and business interests are mixed. The Chinese government remains deeply interested in this project.

It is only after our November visit to China and time to review the complicated situation with planned investing institutions that we have had the information necessary to determine the next steps. Unfortunately, this includes the realization that our ability to proceed in China will be delayed. Therefore, we have laid the groundwork to extend the program to other countries, if necessary.

Despite the current political problems hampering the program in China, we remain deeply committed to designing and implementing environmentally sound and effective business solutions to a serious environmental problem.

ALAN DAVIS

Chairman

China Paper Partners Inc.

San Francisco
