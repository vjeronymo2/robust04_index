May 20, 1989, Saturday, Home Edition

TEACHER STRIKE TALKS HANG UP ON PAY ISSUE

Negotiators in the 5-day-old Los Angeles teachers' strike resumed talks Friday in hope that schools could be back to normal by Monday, but there was no sign of a breakthrough and officials said pay hikes remain the big sticking point.

After about six hours of talks, Dick Fisher, chief negotiator for the Los Angeles Unified School District, said his side was "not too happy with the progress of the negotiations." Asked to characterize the talks, he said: "Disappointment, that's a fair word for it."

Goldberg Not Optimistic

Despite growing expectations around the district that a settlement might be near, school board member Jackie Goldberg said she "was not particularly optimistic" for a quick settlement if the union continues to insist on a 10% retroactive raise for this year. The board, she said, would not go any higher than a total 24% raise over three years, while the union is seeking 26%.

Goldberg spoke after a four-hour, closed-door meeting of the board. Later, at about 4 p.m., negotiating teams gathered in a room on the 28th floor of the Westin Bonaventure Hotel downtown, and huddled late into the night. On Thursday, they had met there for 10 hours.

At a large and enthusiastic rally in Exposition Park at noon Friday, United Teachers-Los Angeles President Wayne Johnson told his membership to be prepared to go into a possible second week of the strike. "Hold on, hold on," Johnson said in a passionate address. "If they don't settle this weekend, we will force the contract from them. We are going to win; make no mistake about that."

In an interview at the park, the union leader described the negotiations as "friendly, cordial and frank." But he said he would abide by an agreement for a news blackout on specifics of the talks.

"We will meet until this thing is resolved," Johnson said. "As long as we're talking, there is hope."

Entering the Bonaventure, district Supt. Leonard Britton estimated that about two-thirds of the remaining disagreements center on pay. He also said he is worried about the long-term effects of the strike on the school system. "Everybody is tarnished by it," he said.

Participating in the talks were, for the district, school board President Roberta Weintraub, negotiator Fisher and Britton. On the union side were UTLA Vice President Helen Bernstein, negotiator John Britz and Johnson. Refereeing was state mediator Draz Mrvichin, who handled talks earlier this year.

According to Britton, a settlement would have to be reached by noon Sunday in order to have schools in full operation on Monday. The school board will remain ready for an emergency meeting, officials said.

The union's House of Representatives is on call to meet this weekend if a contract is ready for a ratification vote, Johnson said. If a contract is approved by those representatives, teachers would go back to work on Monday and hold a full membership ratification tally soon afterward, he said.

Police estimates of the crowd at the noon teachers' rally ranged from 3,000 to 8,000. But there were no doubts that the teachers' spirits were high.

"We will not go back to school unless we solve our problems," Jaime Escalante, the famous calculus teacher from Garfield High School told the gathering. "The strike energizes us. The strike puts together the whole team as a unit. And I'm proud to be a teacher."

The presence of Escalante, whose work with Latino youngsters was the basis of the hit film "Stand and Deliver," gratified union leaders who feared that he might not support the strike because of his conservative political beliefs.

Other speakers included Assemblywoman Teresa P. Hughes (D-Los Angeles) and school board candidate Mark Slavkin, who is strongly supported by the teachers' union against incumbent Alan Gershman in a runoff election next month that could determine the majority on the panel.

In a parody of the old Chuck Berry rock song, "Johnny B Good," entertainers and the teachers sang "Go, Go, Lenny Go, Back to the Swamps." That was a musical suggestion that Supt. Britton return to Florida, where he formerly headed Dade County schools. Someone also strung up from a tree branch a dummy with a drawing of Britton's face.

'Prepared to Hold Out'

'We'd rather be inside teaching but we are prepared to hold out," said Barbara Goodson, an English teacher at Fremont High School in San Pedro. She was carrying a sign that said: "Rita Walters, We Won't Forget," referring to school board member Walters' opposition to some union proposals.

Jerry Gediman, a fifth-grade teacher at Justice Street Elementary School in Canoga Park, said strikers will continue the picketing if necessary. "There are just too many reasons why teachers are fed up," said Gediman, who has worked for the district for 31 years. "There is something for everybody to latch onto."

Meanwhile, the district reported that school attendance on Friday continued to drop sharply and that the strikers' numbers held. However, district spokeswoman Eva Hain suggested that many students and teachers saw Friday as a chance to have a long weekend.

According to Hain, only 259,128 of the district's 594,000 students were at the morning attendance roll call Friday. That was 24,492 fewer than on Thursday and 70,192 fewer than on Monday when the strike began.

Of the nearly 26,259 regular teachers in the district, 8,642 came to work Friday, only 18 more than on the day before, according to district figures. On Monday, 6,740 teachers worked. Strikers also include school nurses, counselors and librarians.

'It's Reasonably Quiet'

Wesley Mitchell, chief of school district police, said no arrests related to the strike were made Friday. "It's reasonably quiet, probably the quietest day we've had all week," he said.

The schools, with the help of 2,453 substitute teachers, continued efforts to offer classes, although films and games were again the mainstay at some campuses.

At Stephen M. White Intermediate School in Carson, Principal Carolyn Bohm, reported that about half of her 1,200 students attended Friday, and that about 40 of the 75 teachers were on strike. "I hope it ends soon, so we can get back to normal," she said.

Tamotsu Ikeda, principal of Gardena High School, said that only a third of his usual 1,900 students came to school Friday. And, he is worried about the effect on the academic achievement and grades of the absentees, especially since the youngsters who are coming to school tend to be the better students, he added.

In another development Friday, nine teachers and aides who continue to work at Capistrano Avenue Elementary School in Canoga Park held a press conference at a Sherman Oaks hotel to protest their treatment by picketers.

"We've had people spit on us. We've been called scabs. And there have been obscene gestures," complained Richard Mason, a fifth-grade teacher who is president of a group called Professional Educators of Los Angeles. The group, which he said has about 3,000 members, was formed by teachers who opposed the previous school strike in 1970.

Mason also denounced a union proposal for an agency shop fee, under which all teachers would be required to pay some form of dues.

Uncertainty remains on how much of an enormous state tax windfall over the next two years will be available to raise teachers' pay. However, the union continues to say that the district can afford much of the raises even without any new state funds.

Starting pay for Los Angeles teachers is $23,440 and the current top salary is $43,319, although some teachers earn more for extracurricular duties and summer school. A state fact-finder's report released this week and denounced by the union described the current pay range as among the best in the state.

Other issues include the elimination of schoolyard duties for elementary school teachers and the formation of school governing councils on which teachers would have a big say over schedules and curricula.

"We are education professional people and we want some control over what we do every single day of our lives," Johnson told teachers at the rally.
