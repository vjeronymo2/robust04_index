July 30, 1989, Sunday, Home Edition

TRAVELER'S DIARY: VACATIONERS' LOVE COAST

We have had the Cote d'Azur and the Costa Brava, to say nothing of the Costa Smeralda, playground of the Aga Khan. Now it's the Cote d'Amour -- the Love Coast.

The Cote d'Amour is the name given to the sandy stretch that rims the southern coast of Brittany barely five miles from St. Nazaire, the shipbuilding port where the Nazis maintained a nest of U-boats that preyed on Allied shipping during World War II.

It has a museum now, and the submarine pens are still intact, but otherwise it is all sunshine and sailing on this stretch of coast that somehow has been overlooked by North Americans. All that is certain to change by early fall when the TGV, the fast French train, will trim the travel time from Paris to two hours (from four).

If Americans have overlooked this sparkling strand, the French and a few other Europeans have not. Its three-mile beach snuggles the Atlantic Ocean and is lined with condominiums.

Hotels With Style

Hotels of style and personality are also in evidence, among them the fashionable L'Hermitage, the small Castel Marie-Louise and the broad-shouldered Royal, connected by a passageway to the glittering glass box that encloses the curative spa called Thalgo La Baule.

Opened just a year, the Thalgo is all glass pyramids, glass elevators, stainless-steel trim and pale green walls. It treats rheumatism, cardiovascular problems, circulatory problems, stress and sports injuries year round and even touts a cure for smoking.

In front of the glass-block Thalgo sits a casino that opened this summer.

Built before the turn of the century when La Baule emerged as a resort, the Royal casino, set in pine trees, has 103 rooms. They rent for a minimum of $50 per person double, except in July and August when the rate rises to $62. The price includes breakfast and, like other Barriere-group hotels such as L'Hermitage and the Marie-Louise, it includes golf, tennis, horseback riding and wind-surfing.

The tiny Castel Marie-Louise, which is also a member of the Relais-Chateau, has 31 rooms for $50 per person, except mid-June to mid-September when the minimum garden-view room is about $80 per person.

Breakfast here is an extra $10. The Castel has had a Michelin star for more than a dozen years. Its menu includes: paper-thin slices of marinated bass and salmon, a scallop of trout with grapefruit sabayon and, its signature dish, a fricassee of lobster in a tomato cream sauce.

Seawater Pool

L'Hermitage also has a seawater pool sitting between its back door and the beach. Meals are served on shaded terraces, in the bar and restaurant and in a formal dining room.

Approximate cost is a minimum of $50 per person a day in May, June, September and October, and a base rate of $75 per person in July and August. Breakfast is an additional $10. For demi-pension or modified American plan, add another $62 per person a day.

All the action is by no means at the seaside hotels.

By day, the open market dispenses everything from Brittany lace ($125 for a cloth about five feet in diameter, six-inch doilies at $6 each) to spider crabs, succulent langoustines, wild strawberries and kuign aman, a Breton upside-down cake. An eight-table restaurant, the Duo, serves a terrine of crab with avocado mousse and mushrooms stuffed with duck liver.

The Tour de Pise (Tower of Pisa) prepares pasta in assorted colors.
