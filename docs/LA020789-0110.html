February 7, 1989, Tuesday, Home Edition

YOUR BODY: COLD-WEATHER EXERCISERS NEED TO EASE INTO THEIR ROUTINES

Cold-weather exercisers should increase their warm-up time to decrease injury risk, being careful to warm the entire body before stretching major muscle groups, exercise specialists say.

Colder weather makes muscles more prone to injury, according to Dr. Ronald Mackenzie, medical director of Centinela National Athletic Health Institute in Culver City. The traditional "tree lean" -- in which exercisers lean against a tree or wall to stretch leg muscles -- isn't sufficient pre-exercise warm-up and should never be done first, he added.

Spend time warming up the entire body, advised Cindy Baily, a Santa Monica physical therapist. "Shift your weight from side to side, for instance, and pump your arms." She recommends the overall body warm-up in any weather and suggests lengthening it from one minute to two in cold weather "to get blood flowing to major muscle groups before you stretch them." Mackenzie's alternate suggestion for an overall body warm-up: "Step into hip-deep Jacuzzi water for five minutes and walk around."

Next, concentrate on slowly stretching specific muscles, said Bailey, paying particular attention to those on the front and back of the thighs and the buttocks. In all, spend seven to 10 minutes warming up, he advised.

Latchkey Kids, Health

Latchkey kids aren't ill any more often than their counterparts in adult-supervised care, nor is their self-esteem usually lower, researchers in two separate studies have concluded.

Dr. Rickey Williams of the University of Arizona Health Sciences Center in Tucson and Dr. W. Thomas Boyce of the UC San Francisco Medical Center studied 503 Tucson fifth-graders over a two-year period and found latchkey kids no more likely to be obese than other children, nor more likely to miss school or visit the school health office.

The study, reported in the American Journal of Diseases of Children, was prompted by medical reports indicating that latchkey kids may be more stressed and that stress, in turn, might affect their physical health.

In a separate study, Dr. Brad D. Berman of Children's Hospital in Oakland and an assistant clinical professor of pediatrics at UC San Francisco studied fourth- and sixth-graders in three Bay Area schools, separating them into four groups: self-care, home-care by parents or other adults, older-sibling care, or out-of-home day care. "Kids in self-care didn't have any lower self-esteem than kids in adult care. But unexpectedly, kids in older-sibling care did have lower self-esteem compared with the other three groups," said Berman, who presented his findings Monday at the western regional meeting of the Ambulatory Pediatrics Assn.

The researchers acknowledged that the optimal arrangement of adult-supervised care is not always available or affordable. If children must stay alone, Boyce said, "it's important for kids to know how they can get in touch with a parent and to know safety procedures such as how to answer the door and the telephone."

Said Dr. Keitha Scott, a pediatrician at Kaiser Permanente Los Angeles Medical Center: "Kids in self-care (at age 11) probably aren't sicker. But I worry more about their emotional health and physical security than about obesity or visits to the nurse."

Travel and Pregnancy

Healthy pregnant women can usually travel safely if they follow some common-sense precautions, conclude two physicians writing in the current Journal of the American Medical Assn.

"Try to get immunizations up to date before pregnancy," said Dr. Frank Bia, an associate professor of medicine at Yale University School of Medicine who co-authored the report with Dr. Michelle Barry, also a Yale associate professor of medicine. "And stay out of areas where there is chloroquine-resistant malaria," Bia advised, citing Thailand and the Amazon region as locations to avoid.

Among their other advice:

-- Don't plan air travel late in pregnancy. "Domestic airline regulations stipulate that air travel for pregnant women after 36 weeks' gestation is not allowed and most foreign airlines have a cutoff of 35 weeks' gestation," according to Bia and Barry.

-- To reduce the risk of phlebitis (vein inflammation), walk around as frequently as possible during air travel, Bia suggested.

-- Forget high-altitude trekking. "Prolonged exposure of pregnant women to the high altitudes beyond (15,000 feet) achieved on many commercial treks has not been studied, and caution should be exercised," the doctors write.
