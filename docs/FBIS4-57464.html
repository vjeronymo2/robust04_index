BFN [Editorial Report] The following is a compilation of reports on economic developments monitored through 17 May. Finance Minister Julio Sosa Rodriguez has announced that Venezuela became a member of the Multilateral Investment Guarantees Agency (MIGA) on 6 May by paying the $3,088,038 that corresponds to the 20-percent subscription fee. By joining MIGA, Venezuela acquires more guarantees in the flow of foreign investment. (Caracas EL GLOBO in Spanish 10 May 94 p 12) The Venezuelan Central Bank (BCV) reported on 10 May that the people have no need to fear an unstable bolivar, because the drop in international reserves has been curbed. BCV President Antonio Casas said, "There is no reason not to have faith in the bolivar and in the country's economic future." The dollar experienced a setback on 29 April when the bolivar rose from 118.30 per dollar to 123.50. On 10 May, the bolivar closed at 131.50. (Madrid EFE in Spanish 0112 GMT 11 May 94) Foreign Trade Institute President Alberto Poletto has announced that the abrupt break in trade talks between Colombia, Mexico, and Venezuela to form a trade zone under the Group of Three "does not mean the end or failure of the negotiations." The foreign trade ministers of Mexico, Colombia, and Venezuela will strive in the coming days to overcome the impasse created in Bogota when Mexico tried to impose conditions the other two countries did not accept. (Caracas REPORTE DE LA ECONOMIA in Spanish 11 May 94 p 20) Finance Ministry sources have reported that imports have experienced a 40-percent decline. It is estimated that in 1994 exports will reach 10.2-10.5 billion dollars, compared to 1993's 10.9 billion dollar figure. According to the same sources, in 1993 the country sustained an economic reduction of 1 percent, which is expected to reach 1.5 percent in 1994. (Caracas EL NACIONAL in Spanish 11 May 94 p E-2) During the first three months of 1994, Venezuela experienced a slowdown characterized by spiraling inflation and declines in the basic economic indicators. This is reflected in an economic report prepared by the Presidency's Office of Coordination and Planning. The price of oil, which is a touchstone used to assess the country's revenues, was $12.33 per barrel, showing a 4.8-percent decline when compared to prices recorded in the last three months of 1993. (Caracas Union Radion Network in Spanish 1100 GMT 17 May 94)
