May 28, 1989, Sunday, Home Edition

ON AGING: SUPPLEMENTING THE BENEFITS OF MEDICARE

How many private health-insurance policies does it take to supplement Medicare benefits? Answer: one. And only one, because by law so-called Medigap policies must provide coverage for what Medicare does not: the $564 deductible for hospital costs, the $75 deductible for physicians' services and the 20% of Medicare-approved doctor charges. A second policy duplicates coverage.

Says Robert Hunt of the National Insurance Consumer Organization: "Anyone who tries to sell a person more than one (Medigap policy) is trying to take the customer." So far, about 5.6 million older Americans have been taken, according to a 1987 study by the Health Insurance Assn. of America. That many people own two or more Medigap policies.

Often they've been misled. It's illegal to knowingly sell a supplemental policy duplicating the purchaser's existing coverage, so unscrupulous sales persons may not ask if you already own a Medigap policy. They'll just try to sell you theirs.

Here are some common sales pitches and facts for deciphering them:

-- A Social Security or Medicare worker comes calling to sell you a Medigap policy. Don't believe it. Representatives of these federal programs don't sell or service such insurance.

-- A salesman explains that Medicare does not cover extended stays in a nursing home, then pitches a Medigap policy. His statement is true as far as it goes: Medicare helps pay for care in a skilled-nursing facility for only 150 days and does not cover stays of any length in intermediate- or custodial-care nursing homes. But the agent's misled you by implying that Medigap policies cover lengthy nursing-home stays. They don't. Consumers can purchase insurance policies that cover long-term care, but these are currently distinct from Medigap policies and do not supplement Medicare hospital and physician benefits.

-- A Medigap insurance agent asks if you'd be interested in a policy that helps prevent you from ever becoming a burden to your spouse or children. With your worst fears running rampant, you have difficulty resisting the sales pitch. Again, the implication is that Medigap insurance offers coverage for long-term care. Once again, it doesn't.

If you're worried about what will happen if you ever need extended care, discuss your concerns with family members and explore such options as long-term-care insurance or buying into a continuing-care retirement community.

-- An insurance rep asks if you'd like coverage for prescription drugs. You would, because your current Medigap policy doesn't provide this benefit.

In addition to paying for items required by federal and state laws, some higher-priced Medigap polices provide extra insurance for prescription drugs, physician fees in excess of Medicare-approved charges or other benefits. Monthly premiums for these policies pay for the standard Medigap insurance plus the additional coverage.

If you want broader coverage than your current Medigap policy provides, exchange it for one that meets your needs. Don't buy an extra Medigap policy.
