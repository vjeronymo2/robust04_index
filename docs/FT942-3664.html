940614 FT 14 JUN 94 / Minister promises protection for rural post offices in sell-off A regulatory framework to ensure the survival of rural post offices would form part of any privatisation package introduced by the government, the House of Lords was told yesterday. Lord Strathclyde, trade and industry minister, assured peers who expressed concern about the effect of privatisation on rural communities that they would find a 'great deal of comfort' when the promised consultative green paper was published. He said the document would be published soon. Lord Strathclyde said: 'In the same way that we protected rural telephone boxes when it came to privatisation of BT, there's absolutely no reason why we should not do the same thing for rural post offices.' He refused to comment when pressed to explain why, if the Post Office's counters business could be accorded greater commercial freedom while remaining in the public sector, the same approach could not be adopted in the case of the Royal Mail. Lord Strathclyde reaffirmed, however, that the maintenance of a uniform postal rate, a universal service, and a nationwide network of services, was 'non-negotiable'. Lord Clinton-Davis, a Labour spokesman, said that public opposition to the privatisation of the Post Office had contributed to the government's dismal showing in the European elections and last week's crop of by-elections for seats in the House of Commons. He noted with satisfaction that the government was paying a very high price at the polls for such acts of 'political lunacy'. Lord Strathclyde insisted that the privatisation programme had proved successful, and added: 'Labour policies would have provided no benefit, and substantial costs.'
