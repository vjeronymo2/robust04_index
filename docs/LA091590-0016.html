September 15, 1990, Saturday, Home Edition

DEFACEMENT SUIT AGAINST PRIEST

A recent article by Bob Secter ("Priest Fights Liquor, Cigarette Billboards," Part A, Aug. 18) calls into question The Times' longstanding policy of printing balanced, unbiased news stories.

Secter presents the case of Father Michael Pfleger who will stand trial for vandalism and battery.

Father Pfleger has been accused of defacing outdoor signs and throwing paint on the owner of a billboard company.

The article cites his actions as "an echo of a civil-rights theme from the days of lunch counter sit-ins and bus boycotts." Nonviolent protests of the '60s are far from the premeditated defacing of property and battery charges served on Father Pfleger.

With all due respect for Father Pfleger, the fact that he is charged with defacing public property and battery must not be downplayed nor made to seem as acceptable behavior. Both cigarettes and alcohol are legal products and companies have the right to advertise. One may not use or even like the product being advertised, but that doesn't excuse criminal conduct.

Protest is one thing, but extremist activities displayed by Father Pfleger sets the wrong kind of example for communities to follow. What has happened to the process of negotiation, of sitting down with the involved parties and reaching a solution?

JOHN R. NELSON

Vice President, Corporate Affairs

Philip Morris, New York
