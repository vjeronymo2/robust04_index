March 5, 1989, Sunday, Home Edition

IN CRITICAL CONDITION;

PHYSICIANS SAY INCREASING NUMBER OF PATIENTS AND MOUNTING FINANCIAL LOSSES ARE BEGINNING TO PLAGUE THE EMERGENCY-CARE SYSTEM THROUGHOUT THE VALLEY

The crisis in emergency medical care that has shaken Los Angeles has begun rippling through the San Gabriel Valley.

Temporary closures of emergency rooms and tense waits by paramedics to find available beds once were unheard of in suburban hospitals.

But in recent months, troubles have hit the region in ways few could have imagined as recently as two years ago.

Consider these incidents in the San Gabriel Valley that occurred in early January:

Covina's Inter-Community Medical Center diverted paramedic ambulances carrying critically ill patients for all but 48 hours during a 12-day period because its intensive care and coronary care units had no available beds.

Ten of the 16 hospitals in the San Gabriel Valley were simultaneously closed to critical patients for three hours on Jan. 10 because there were no empty beds.

And for the first time that anyone can remember, Huntington Memorial Hospital in Pasadena, the largest hospital in the San Gabriel Valley, had to temporarily close its emergency room to paramedic ambulances on Jan. 9 because it was overrun with patients.

Approaching Limit

The flu season has been a major reason for the number of emergency room closures in the San Gabriel Valley. Even so, conditions have not approached the snarling disarray that has become the norm at County-USC Medical Center in Los Angeles.

But most doctors agree the San Gabriel Valley's emergency system is approaching its limit, and the first signs of stress appeared well before winter.

The troubles in the emergency medical care system, which some have described as a "brownout," have brought a bounty of changes, some routine, others ominous.

When Arcadia's Methodist Hospital of Southern California noticed its emergency room consistently filling with patients 18 months ago, it ordered all emergency physicians to install car phones so they could be summoned at any time.

Dr. John A. Mardones, head of the emergency room at Monterey Park Hospital, said he has just begun noticing a new phenomenon among patients that he calls "emergency room shopping."

"It's like looking for a good restaurant," he said. "They'll go to one place and then another to find one with less waiting."

At Santa Teresita Hospital in Duarte, the crowding and increased costs of maintaining an emergency room have led the hospital to threaten to close it to paramedics at the end of March.

Most emergency physicians at San Gabriel Valley hospitals say patients have not been seriously affected in the suburbs, and they know of no deaths attributed to the problems. But those doctors are now casting an eye toward Los Angeles, which they see as a harbinger of their own future.

"It's like a snowball coming down the hill," said Hallie Hetzel, chief operating officer of San Gabriel Valley Medical Center. "It's going to hit us sooner or later."

Tracing the cause of the brownout is like trying to piece together a jigsaw puzzle that has been strewn on the floor. There are no single sources, but several interlocking problems.

The growth of the San Gabriel Valley as well as the aging of the population and the shortage of skilled nurses have all contributed to overloading the system.

Most doctors, however, say the critical link in the chain of events was the skyrocketing cost of providing care for Medi-Cal, Medicare and uninsured patients.

Medi-Cal and Medicare reimburse hospitals for their services, but the payments have failed to keep pace with the rising cost of health care, the doctors say. Medi-Cal provides health insurance to the poor and Medicare to the elderly and disabled.

The losses have created a financial drain that has deprived hospitals of the funds and incentive to expand their emergency rooms, doctors say.

Consider the case of a 5-month-old baby who was brought to the emergency room at San Gabriel Valley Medical Center with seizures. The child was stabilized, tested and then transferred to Huntington Memorial. The bill came to $1,187. The hospital received $157.

"I could find 30 cases in a row just like that," said Dr. W. Richard Bukata, head of San Gabriel Valley's emergency room. "The hospitals are losing money left and right."

Insurance Study

Compounding the problems is the growing number of patients who have no insurance. In the Los Angeles metropolitan area, one in four people under the age of 65 is uninsured, the highest proportion of any area in the country. Just a decade a ago, the figure was one in five, according to a recent study by two UCLA public-health professors.

With nowhere else to go, these patients have turned to emergency rooms where they cannot be turned away.

The losses for hospitals have been enormous. In 1982, unreimbursed costs for treating Medi-Cal, Medicare and uninsured patients at the 16 hospitals in the San Gabriel Valley reached $36.5 million. Last year, the figure topped $73.5 million.

The problems have hit inner-city hospitals the hardest, but they have spread outward toward private suburban hospitals linked with the large county hospitals in Los Angeles.

County-USC Medical Center has the prime responsibility for caring for the county's poor. So private hospitals in the San Gabriel Valley have long transferred most indigent patients there once they are stable enough to move.

Those transfers, however, have become much harder in the last 12 to 18 months because of the lack of space, said Dr. J. Paul Holland, director of the emergency room at Queen of the Valley Hospital in West Covina.

Chain Reaction

Last year, almost 60% of the potential transfers from Queen of the Valley, or 150 patients, were rejected by County-USC.

The result frequently is a chain reaction. Critical-care patients brought to Queen of the Valley are diverted to other hospitals, which then fill up and must divert patients to yet other hospitals.

"We run at near-capacity almost all the time, and it takes just a small change to saturate us," Holland said.

The most publicized victim of the crisis has been the county's trauma-care system, which was set up in 1983 to guarantee victims of serious injuries a maximum 20-minute ambulance ride to a specialized emergency room staffed 24 hours a day by a team of emergency physicians.

Hospitals competed fiercely for the prestigious designation of being a trauma center.

But staying in the system was more expensive than many imagined, and eight of the 23 original trauma centers have dropped out, including three in the San Gabriel Valley: Queen of the Valley, Methodist Hospital and Pomona Valley Community Hospital in Pomona.

Delays in Treatment

Today, Huntington Memorial is the only trauma center left in the San Gabriel Valley.

The number of trauma cases has always been small. For example, out of the 4,786 patients seen at Pomona Valley's emergency room in January, just 42 were trauma patients

But the loss of three trauma centers in the San Gabriel Valley has meant occasional delays in treating the seriously injured. Trauma patients are now taken to the nearest hospital, but sometimes must wait for a surgical team to be summoned, a process that can take half an hour or more.

For the most part, the San Gabriel Valley hospitals have been able to adjust to the new load of emergency room patients.

To begin with, the system has several protections to avoid outright disaster.

No hospital, no matter how crowded, can turn away a patient when it is a matter of life or death. Emergency rooms always accept walk-in patients or those who have been transported by ambulances that do not have paramedics on board. And in the worst case, when every emergency room in the area is closed, the county requires every hospital reopened to prevent a total blackout of emergency care.

But recently, rumors have begun surfacing that some hospitals are not playing by the rules and are closing to lessen their workload or avoid treating indigent patients.

Last month, the county warned Greater El Monte Community Hospital and Inter-Community Medical Center for closing even though beds were still available.

Inspectors visited both hospitals in late January and found three out of six emergency room beds empty at Greater El Monte and 10 of 13 empty at Inter-Community.

But officials at both hospitals say their emergency rooms were reported closed because of misunderstandings, and the county agrees that neither case was a deliberate attempt to break the rules.

But some paramedics and doctors believe instances of abuse have probably occurred because the temptation is strong.

"People are people," said Mardones of Monterey Park Hospital. "There's no doubt the temptation is there."

Even with a system that functions properly, some doctors worry that the load has reached the point where it is only a matter of time before a patient is harmed because they had to be diverted to a more distant hospital.

"I don't care how good you are, you're not God, and you make mistakes," said Dr. Russell Maatz, head of the emergency room at Methodist Hospital. "You have to go by the information the paramedics give you and you make the best guess you can. Now, something unexpected doesn't happen 99% of the time, but it does happen."

The predictions of a collapse in the system are only part of the picture. Some hospitals have actually welcomed the increase in emergency room patients.

Although emergency rooms are generally money losers, they also are an entryway for paying patients who end up staying in other parts of the hospital.

"I'd be thrilled to death to get more patients," said Dr. Keith J. Rosing, head of the emergency room at St. Luke Medical Center in Pasadena. "Its a problem I'd love to have."

St. Luke has always played second fiddle to its much larger cross-town cousin, Huntington Memorial, which receives most of the emergency patients in the city.

In the past eight years, the number of emergency patients at St. Luke has dwindled from 24,700 to 16,500.

"We're not in a poor area and we a have a nice mix of patients," Rosing said. "More patients wouldn't hurt us at this point."

High-Priced Care

But even hospitals like St. Luke worry that as Medi-Cal and Medicare reimbursements fall farther behind hospital costs and the indigent population increases, they too will feel the pinch.

"Even if we wanted to take care of these patients for nothing, you can't do it without the facilities," said Maatz of Methodist Hospital. "The nurses, the equipment, the space, everything about emergency care is high-priced."

Looming on the horizon is the possibility that some hospitals will ultimately find it too expensive to maintain their emergency rooms and simply close them.

In Los Angeles, French Hospital and Linda Vista Hospital downgraded the status of their emergency rooms last year. They no longer accept patients brought by paramedic ambulances. In January, Queen of Angels Medical Center closed its entire facility to merge with Hollywood-Presbyterian Medical Center.

No emergency room in the San Gabriel Valley has moved to ban paramedic traffic, although Santa Teresita Hospital in Duarte is threatening to be the first.

The hospital originally announced that it would close its emergency room to paramedic ambulances March 1 unless the county approved a request to add 23 beds to the hospital's nursing home. The hospital decided last week to extend the deadline to the end of the month and has left open the possibility of extending it yet another 30 days. Santa Teresita officials expect their proposal to add the beds to be approved. The emergency room lost $400,000 in 1987 and $600,000 last year. By enlarging its nursing home, the hospital estimates it could make an extra $800,000 a year, which would offset its emergency room losses.

Closing Santa Teresita's emergency room to paramedics would have a limited effect on hospitals outside of its immediate area, health officials say.

But many doctors worry that other hospitals may eventually consider downgrading their emergency rooms because the problems are so widespread and the rationale to close, at least on an economic level, is so compelling.

The loss of one emergency room could be absorbed by other hospitals, but how many more would it take to begin affecting care throughout the San Gabriel Valley?

"If things continue to go the way they are going with no new funding, you're going to see other emergency rooms close or downscale," said Jeffrey P. Winter, administrator of Greater El Monte Community Hospital. Then clearly it's just a matter of time, he said, before the tangled congestion in inner-city hospitals becomes a reality in the San Gabriel Valley.
