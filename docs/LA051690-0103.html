May 16, 1990, Wednesday, Southland Edition

VAN GOGH PAINTING SELLS AT RECORD $82.5 MILLION;

ART: 'PORTRAIT OF DR. GACHET' IS AUCTIONED TO A JAPANESE BUYER. THE PREVIOUS HIGH PRICE WAS $53.9 MILLION.

Vincent van Gogh's "Portrait of Dr. Gachet" stunned the art market Tuesday when it was auctioned for $82.5 million, shattering the world record price for a painting by more than $28 million.

The price was double the estimate by Christie's, a spokeswoman for the auction house said.

"This is by far the world's most expensive painting," surpassing the previous auction record of $53.9 million bid by Australian businessman Alan Bond for Van Gogh's "Irises" in November, 1987.

The money for Bond's bid was largely borrowed from Sotheby's, the house that handled the auction. The debt-ridden Bond was eventually forced to sell the painting for an undisclosed price to the J. Paul Getty Museum of Malibu.

The unidentified Japanese bidder was in the room during the about 10 minutes of bidding that opened at $20 million, Britman said. Toward the end of bidding, just two people were vying for the painting, she said.

"Portrait of Dr. Gachet" was painted six weeks before Van Gogh committed suicide in 1890. Dr. Paul-Ferdinand Gachet tended Van Gogh in the weeks before his death.

The record price quashed all reports of an art market in recession after last week's disappointing contemporary sales. More than 35% of the works had gone unsold last week.

The crowd gasped when the bidding reached $50 million.

It had been expected to fetch between $40 million and $50 million at a sale of major works by Impressionist and Modern artists.

"It's a great painting," said Manhattan art dealer Richard Feigen. "It's by a rare artist. It's in fine condition. There are practically no paintings like it in private hands."

After the bidding, which was tensely conducted between the Japanese dealer and a telephone bidder, the jam-packed gallery broke into a standing ovation, prompting auctioneer Christopher Burge to pound his gavel to control the house.

The painting was lent to the Metropolitan Museum of Art in 1984. It is from the collection of Siegfried Kramarsky, a German-born New York banker who died in 1961. It was put on the block by a trust created under his will.

In a letter to artist Paul Gauguin, Van Gogh wrote on June 16 that he had painted Gachet "with the heart-broken expression of our time" and that it was "not destined to be understood."

Gachet, a practitioner of homeopathic medicine, was Van Gogh's friend and physician. He was also close to other Impressionist painters of the time.

Van Gogh painted a second, slightly larger version of the portrait, which hangs in the Musee d'Orsay in Paris. It lacks the details of the Kramarsky painting and its colors are flatter.

Another Van Gogh from the Kramarsky collection was auctioned in 1987 for $20.24 million. It too had been lent to the Metropolitan.
