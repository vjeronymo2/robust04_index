Language: English Article Type:BFN [Text] Tokyo, March 4 KYODO -- Japan on Friday voiced concern about U.S. President Bill Clinton's decision to revive the "Super 301" trade retaliation mechanism through an executive order. "We have to express concern about the U.S. Government move to build up the strength of its procedures that would clear the way for unilateral action in resolving trade disputes with other countries," Chief Cabinet Secretary Masayoshi Takemura told a news conference. The top government spokesman made the comment after Clinton reinstated Thursday the trade measure that gives the U.S. Government broad legal authority to impose sanctions unless Japan meets U.S. demands on greater access to Japanese markets. Takemura argued that taking unilateral action as a means of resolving bilateral trade rows is "implicitly" prohibited under the General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade or the yet-to-be-created World Trade Organization. Saying both Japan and the United States shoulder heavy responsibility for smooth management of the world economy, Takemura urged the U.S. Government to "take sensible action." The chief cabinet secretary also reiterated Tokyo is determined to do its utmost to further open its markets to foreign competition on a voluntary basis. Super 301, passed by the U.S. Congress in 1988, gives the President power to retaliate against countries which the U.S. deems to be engaging in unfair trade practices. Prime Minister Hosokawa received a phone call from Clinton shortly after midnight Thursday Japan time notifying him of the decision to resuscitate Super 301, Takemura said. Clinton was quoted as saying the revived provision is not intended to target any particular country and that the U.S. and Japan can resolve the brewing bilateral trade spat through dialogue. Under the provisions spelled out by U.S. Trade Representative Mickey Kantor on Thursday, the U.S. Government will not identify any potential target country until September 30, and will seek to negotiate a settlement over a period of 12 to 18 months before taking any punitive action. Kantor said, "It should be clearly understood we are not designating or identifying any practice of any country today." During the phone call, Hosokawa told Clinton that Tokyo is scheduled to devise a package of measures to further open its markets by the end of March and said both countries should show restraint, Takemura said. Asked if Japan will file a complaint with GATT if the U.S. imposes trade sanctions against it, the chief government spokesman only reiterated that Tokyo is making voluntary efforts to formulate a market-opening package.
