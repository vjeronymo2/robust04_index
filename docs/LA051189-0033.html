May 11, 1989, Thursday, Home Edition

LOW-CAL RABBIT DISHES;

THE MAKING OF A CULINARY CLASSIC

Chefs are cooking rabbit in some of the country's top restaurant kitchens because rabbit dishes are culinary classics that are high in prestige and low in calories.

Europeans have long been rabbit fanciers with the French consuming more than 200 million rabbits a year, or about 13 pounds per person. Italy, Germany and Spain consume about the same amount, with England right behind.

Health-conscious dieters should note that rabbit has fewer calories per pound, more protein and less cholesterol than chicken or veal.

Traditionally, rabbit has been fried, stewed, baked, broiled, roasted, curried, hashed or eaten as a pie or ragout. Today's cooks serve rabbit in coconut milk, in sour cream and mustard sauce, barbecued, boiled, cacciatore, en casserole and Creole style.

Angus Cameron and Judith Jones, authors of "The L.L. Bean Game and Fish Cookbook," maintain that "rabbit can be prepared with any recipe you use for chicken. There is only one difference," they said. "Rabbit is better."

New York writer Charlie Rubin orders rabbit at business lunches -- he finds it a power play as well as a culinary choice.

"It knocks out the guys you're with," he said. "People think you're a serious eater."

Rubin ranked rabbit this way: "Rabbit is as good as alligator tails, less good than frog legs."

Michel Jean, owner of New York's Provence bistro, said most restaurants served only the filet, which is tender but too bland. The French use more flavorful cuts.

"I'm trying to make it more appealing," Jean said. "I want to include more traditional Provencal dishes such as rabbit Brignolaise (cut in pieces, stuffed with rosemary, garlic and thyme, wrapped in bacon and roasted with garlic cloves) or a rabbit stew, which is tastier and gamy."

Rabbit is served in a number of New York restaurants, as well as sophisticated eateries in Chicago and Beverly Hills. "In L.A., rabbits are rampant," said Mickey Ziffren, a novelist and wife of Paul Ziffren, chairman of the 1984 Olympics.

Rabbits are traditional in regional cooking. In New Orleans, Cajun-Creole restaurants prepare rabbit in time-honored as well as innovative ways. In North Carolina, professional cook Carrie Lyerly uses a simple century-old family recipe. She says people today do too much to rabbit. "They put in so many things that the rabbit loses its distinctive flavor," she said.

In New Mexico, the Pueblo Indians go on rabbit hunts in spring and fall.
