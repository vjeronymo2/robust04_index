May 16, 1989, Tuesday, Home Edition

CYCLAMATE LIKELY TO BE EXONERATED -- 20 YEARS LATER

Federal health officials, who banned the artificial sweetener cyclamate 20 years ago in the midst of one of the biggest health scares of the 1960s, are now widely expected to reapprove it, possibly this year. Once accused of causing everything from bladder cancer to birth defects, cyclamate is now widely thought to be harmless.

"I have no reluctance in saying that with cyclamate we made a mistake," said Robert Scheuplein, acting director of the office of toxicological services for the FDA's Center for Food Safety and Applied Nutrition.

A group of monkeys at the National Cancer Institute provides evidence of the mistake. Each of them has been fed the amount of cyclamate that would sweeten 30 cans of diet soda every day, five days a week, for the last 17 years.

They're Doing Fine

In February, the institute issued its first progress report: The monkeys are doing just fine.

The agency's reversal on cyclamate promises to add to the growing public debate about how government health officials evaluate potential health risks. According to scientists and industry officials involved in the controversy, the evidence exonerating cyclamate was available years ago and the fact that the agency took two decades to concede its mistake shows how large a role politics can play in decisions presented as scientific.

Cyclamate was one of the world's most widely used artificial sweeteners when, in the late 1960s, its safety became a matter of public concern.

In 1969, after the completion of a study showing that some rats with cyclamate pellets implanted in their bladders were developing tumors, the FDA restricted the sale of the chemical. The following year, it was banned entirely.
