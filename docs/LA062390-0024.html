June 23, 1990, Saturday, Home Edition

PANEL'S RECOMMENDATIONS ON VITAMINS FOR PREGNANT WOMEN

The headline ("Skip Vitamins, Pregnant Women Told") and the lead in your front-page article were not fully in sync with all the information presented in the rest of the article -- including legitimate viewpoints favoring supplement use by pregnant women because of deficiencies in their diets.

Many dietary surveys show a high frequency of deficient diets in the United States, with young women some of the worst offenders.

Indeed, supplement use prevents certain birth defects. A recent signed editorial comment in the Journal of the American Medical Assn. stated, "The biggest challenge . . . about the benefits of taking vitamins is to get women of childbearing age to hear and follow this simple advice."

The National Academy of Sciences committee members whose report you cited are not omniscient. Nutritionists with credentials equal or superior to theirs differ greatly over the benefits of supplement use by pregnant women. The committee is not even consistent -- concern with possible nutrient interaction (iron's interference with zinc absorption) was not reflected in its advice to take iron supplements during pregnancy.

Even though the committee admits to "considerable variation in nutrient requirements among individuals," its report failed to identify how physicians can determine their patients' requirements. In fact, no method that is both inexpensive and reliable is available.

BURTON KALLMAN

Director, Science and Technology

National Nutritional Foods Assn.

Costa Mesa
