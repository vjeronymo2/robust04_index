940513 FT 13 MAY 94 / Watchdog clears FT's on-line database service The Monopolies and Mergers Commission announced yesterday that the monopoly position of Profile, the Financial Times on-line database service, does not operate against the public interest. On-line financial and business database services deliver information, including the text of newspaper articles, to subscribers' personal computers or terminals via telecommunications links. Profile has a share of almost 40 per cent of the UK market in such services. The year-long commission inquiry centred on the FT's copyright licensing practices: the FT does not generally allow material from its publications to be used by other database service providers. Some competitors argued that this practice gave FT Profile an unfair advantage and some users, who needed access to information from the FT, complained that they were unable to choose their preferred service on the basis of cost and ease of use. The commission found that the FT's licensing practice was 'a legitimate competitive action intended to differentiate FT Profile from its competitors'. It concluded that, while the FT enjoyed a special position as a business and financial newspaper, almost all the factual information it contained was also available elsewhere. The inquiry also covered a second database provider, Data-Star Dialog, which is owned by the US company Knight-Ridder and which has a one-third share of the UK market. No complaints were received about Data-Star Dialog but the commission was bound to investigate its practices because, under the terms of the Fair Trading Act 1973, the company was also considered to hold a monopoly position. The commission found the monopoly 'does not give cause for concern'. Mr Fred Perkins, the FT group's director of electronic publishing, said: 'It is a well-established principle that ownership of copyright automatically bestows a monopoly advantage which allows companies to recoup their investment costs and generate income.'
