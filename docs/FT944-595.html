941224 FT 24 DEC 94 / Weekend Money: Finance Week BUSINESS Italian prime minister Silvio Berlusconi, whose seven-month-old government was toppled after a revolt by his Northern League coalition partner, resigned rather than face defeat in a no-confidence drive in parliament. President Oscar Luigi Scalfaro is left with the delicate decision of finding a new government or dissolving parliament. The Home Office has asked the Court of Appeal to review the convictions of the four original Guinness trial defendants because of allegations that material evidence was withheld from the defence. Britain's current account balance of payments moved into surplus for the first time in 7 1/2 years in the third quarter. John Major said that he would make peace with the Conservative party's rebels over Europe only on his own terms. The UK's 6m part-time workers are to have most of the statutory employment rights of those in full-time jobs, the government announced. The decision represents a climbdown for the government which two weeks ago vetoed a draft European Union directive on part-time workers. The Halifax Building Society plans to cut buildings insurance premiums by an average of 20 per cent from January 1. London Life Association, part of London Life Group, has been fined Pounds 50,000 by Imro, the self-regulatory body for the fund management industry, for rule breaches that left client money at risk. Serious breaches of regulatory rules emerged in the announcement of fines imposed by Lautro, the life insurance watchdog, on two friendly societies - Homeowners and Ideal Benefit. Cruise operator Cunard blamed sub-contractors for delays in completing a Pounds 30m refit of the liner Queen Elizabeth 2, which resulted in 500 passengers being turned away from its Christmas cruise. Cunard said passengers unable to travel on this cruise could take two others next year - should they want to. The government will receive nearly Pounds 960m from the sale of British Coal's mining assets. This emerged as details were announced of the sale of the south Wales region for Pounds 94.5m. Michael Heseltine, trade and industry secretary, decided not to refer the proposed acquisition by the Halifax Building Society of Leeds Permanent Building Society to the Monopolies and Mergers Commission. US investors have provided the bulk of new capital committed to Lloyd's of London for next year, partly filling the hole left by exiting Names - individuals whose assets traditionally have supported the insurance market. COMPANIES The first contested take-over bid in the UK utility sector got under way as Northern Electric, the regional power company for the north-east of England, rejected a Pounds 1.2bn offer from Trafalgar House. Trafalgar House decided to offer a Pounds 20 rebate to each of Northern Electric's 1.3m customers. This, it claimed, was at the prompting of the electricity industry regulator, Professor Stephen Littlechild. Labour questioned whether Littlechild had shown 'proper conduct' in asking Trafalgar representatives what customers would gain from the bid. Shares in troubled UK developer Stanhope Properties were suspended after the breakdown of negotiations with banks owed Pounds 140m. Unilever's problems with its revolutionary new detergents appeared to have disrupted the orderly succession to the group's top job. The company announced a postponement of previously announced boardroom changes amid mounting evidence that Persil Power and Omo Power, the detergents launched this spring, have failed to meet their targets. UK bioscience company Zeneca is to pay up to Dollars 485m (Pounds 310m) for Salick Healthcare, a Los Angeles-based operator of specialist cancer centres. Mercury Communications, the UK's second largest public telecommunications operator, cut international call charges for its largest users in reaction to intensifying competition. SG Warburg has beaten other investment banks, including Morgan Stanley, the bank with which it planned to merge until last week, in the competition to advise the government on the privatisation of Railtrack. A Pounds 1.5bn offer to buy the St Martins Property group, the leading property business owned by the Kuwaiti government, has been made by Barings, the London merchant bank, on behalf of an investment consortium. Mars, the privately owned US confectionery and pet foods group, has launched a review of the bulk of its Dollars 1bn (Pounds 640m) world-wide advertising, in response to Maurice Saatchi's removal from the board of advertising group Saatchi & Saatchi. Intel, the world's biggest computer chip-maker, offered to replace free of charge the flawed Pentium microprocessors that are the 'brains' of millions of high performance personal computers. Shares in East Midlands Electricity rose by 5 per cent after the group announced substantially better than expected interim results and a surprisingly large dividend increase. Peter Ward is to quit as chief executive of Rolls-Royce Motors, which has signed a collaboration agreement with BMW of Germany. The move is believed to reflect a disagreement with the parent Vickers board on the future of the luxury car builder. Robert Montague finally conceded defeat in his attempt to stave off bankruptcy, resigning as chief executive of Tiphook, the trailer rental company he founded 16 years ago. UK security printer De La Rue's Pounds 682m offer for Portals was accepted by the specialist paper-maker's board. Shareholders in British Assets Trust have approved a capital re-organisation but raised questions about the trust's relationship with Ivory & Sime, its fund manager. Private investors have taken up fewer than a sixth of the shares available to them in RJB Mining's sale of shares to fund the purchase of British Coal's English regions. MARKETS Mexico's stock market fell heavily and short-term interest rates rose sharply following Tuesday's 15 per cent devaluation of the peso, pulling down other Latin American markets. The currency was driven to new lows and the Mexican government was forced to float the peso - abandoning its fiscal policy of keeping the currency in a specified target zone. Sales of uncut diamonds remain near record levels, but the market is still in turmoil and cutters continue to complain about lack of profitability. The US stock market attempted a tentative year-end rally as the Dow Jones broke through the 3,800 mark on Wednesday for the first time since November 18. The present bull market in base metals probably will end in the third quarter of 1995, according to analysts at the Merrill Lynch financial services group. The world's two major nutmeg producers, Indonesia and Grenada, have suspended negotiations on a new joint marketing agreement amid concerns about the involvement of a European marketing company. They will resume their discussions in March.
