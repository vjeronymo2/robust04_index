July 4, 1990, Wednesday, Orange County Edition

MUSIC;

PRESERVATIONIST HAILE PLANNING RHAPSODY IN RED, WHITE & BLUE;

THE PACIFIC SYMPHONY WILL PLAY SELECTIONS BY SOUSA, GERSHWIN, VICTOR HERBERT AND OTHERS AT IRVINE MEADOWS AMPHITHEATRE. IT WILL END WITH A FIREWORKS DISPLAY.

Many conductors are worried that the niceties of playing Baroque music are properly vanishing into the mists of time. Evans Haile has similar fears about the American musical.

"There is a lot of wonderful material -- shows by Gershwin, Victor Herbert and others -- that is not being done," Haile said from his home in New York. "I do not think these are museum pieces or can only be done one way. But I believe very strongly there are valid reasons for hearing them."

An example is Richard Rodgers and Lorenz Hart's 1937 musical, "Babes in Arms," which Haile resurrected in its original form last year in New York and has recently recorded. Haile will conduct selections from the show on a program of American music played by the Pacific Symphony today at 8 p.m. at Irvine Meadows Amphitheatre. The program will also include music by Sousa, Gershwin, Victor Herbert and others, and will end with a fireworks display.

A graduate of the Juilliard School of Music in New York, Haile said that, in his case, "necessity was the mother of invention."

Haile and a friend formed a company to produce a series of concerts at Town Hall in New York in 1981-86.

"When you first get out of school, you want to work," he said. "It was a practical decision from that end. Our idea was to re-create these shows the way the creators had envisioned them.

"There were a lot of shows where the original orchestrations and original material had been lost and had to be reconstructed. It's not an unwise thing to take a look at these. The orchestrations give evidence of the period. They were so clearly done and so wittily done. They can clue you into why a lot of these shows did work."

Other discoveries included changes in the "original routing of the numbers, or what key someone is singing in, or even what characters do the numbers."

"Don't forget that a lot of these shows in (later) productions, after the composers died, were given in cut-down productions," Haile said. "The point of our series in Town Hall was to do them with the full resources as they originally had thought them out. If the chorus was 45, we had them."

Of course, shows changed in the tryouts given in other cities before moving on to the Big Apple and Broadway.

"If a song was cut from a show, that doesn't mean you should put it back in," Haile said. "Theater pieces are living, breathing animals that you mold and you shape. But I think opera is the same way. Like Verdi's 'Masked Ball' or Wagner's 'Tannhauser,' there are different editions. You have to make decisions (which to use). It's the same with these pieces. I do think there is a validity to see what's going on."

At the Pacific concert, Haile will be both the conductor and the piano soloist for Gershwin's "Rhapsody in Blue." In keeping with the patriotic theme -- and also as a touring artist for Baldwin pianos -- Haile will be playing on a red, white and blue piano.

"It's more tasteful than it sounds," he said. "The piano is red-and-white striped, with a blue interior. . . . Actually it is quite handsome, for a red, white and blue piano. It's very befitting for an outdoor concert."

Still, despite his fame in American musicals, Haile also has a "serious music" side to his career. He, for instance, conducted a Wagner "Ring" cycle for the Seattle Opera in 1984, 1986 and 1987.

"I hate (being called) a 'specialist in music of the American musical,' " Haile said. "I don't read just one author's books or eat one type of food. Nor do most people. And I think that anyone who is aware of the world is going to be open to the world and aware of the whole variety, open to what's going on in the world.

"Good music is good music, whether it's by Rodgers and Hart or Wagner. It's music that has withstood the test of time."

Evans Haile will conduct the Pacific Symphony in a program of music by American composers today at 8 p.m. at Irvine Meadows Amphitheatre, 8800 Irvine Center Drive, Irvine. Judy Balzer will be soprano soloist. The concert will end with fireworks. Tickets: $10.50 to $37.50. Lawn seating: $8.50. Information: (714) 474-4233.
