BFN [By Silvio J. Mendiandua: "Visit by Carlos Salinas Will Strengthen Cuban-Mexican Relations"] [Text] Havana, 6 Jun (PL) -- Mexican Ambassador to Havana Carlos Tello said here today Mexican President Carlos Salinas' visit to Cuba will strengthen relations between the two countries, and as a result, new forms of cooperation may emerge and current programs of cooperation may be strengthened. In an interview with PRENSA LATINA, the Mexican diplomat said: Salinas is expected to arrive on 13 June and will stay on the island for 24 hours, and his presence here will be further evidence of the strength and solidity of our relations. Tello, who began his mission in this capital this year, affirmed: The talks between Salinas and Cuban President Fidel Castro will produce new ideas and initiatives for promoting bilateral exchanges. The ambassador said the two leaders will discuss in general political, economic, cultural, and commercial relations during their dialogue as well as programs of scientific and technological cooperation, all of which have been developing in a firm and cooperative manner. He said: No specific agreement is expected to result from this encounter, because negotiations between the two countries on issues of economic or commercial interest are so fluid and dynamic that agreements may be signed at any moment, not necessarily during a visit. He recalled that in recent years Mexico's presidents had visited the island during their respective administrations: Luis Echeverria (1975); Jose Lopez Portillo (1980); Miguel de la Madrid (1988); and now Carlos Salinas de Gortari. To reciprocate, he said, Cuban President Fidel Castro had visited Mexico on three occasions during the past five years. These exchanges at the highest level are a direct and straightforward way of manifesting the solidity of relations, he said Tello also mentioned the recent visit to Mexico of U.S. Democratic Representative Robert Torricelli, who has meddled in the domestic affairs of Cuba and Mexico and who was told during his visit to Mexico of the independent nature of the foreign policy of the country of the Aztecs and Mayas. Torricelli is the author of the bill, which the U.S. Congress later approved, that establishes a tightening of the economic blockade against the largest of the Antillean islands. The Mexican ambassador said his country's foreign policy is a state, not a government, policy and one of its main permanent principles is recognizing the people's right to self-determination. In another part of the interview, Tello revealed the Mexican Government team that will organize Salinas' visit with Cuban officials will arrive in Havana today. Ambassador Ricardo Ampudia, director general of the Mexican Foreign Secretariat Protocol Office, will head the Foreign Policy Cabinet, which the Mexicans call these advisers, which will include a group of individuals from various backgrounds that will decide between today and 8 June the agenda of the visit. Salinas' visit on 13 June, the day before the beginning of the meeting of the Ibero-American presidents, is a way of manifesting solidarity with Cuba, Tello said. Tello also revealed that during the summit, the chiefs of state of Mexico, Colombia, and Venezuela will sign a free trade agreement. Mexico has signed similar documents with Chile, Costa Rica, Canada, and the United States, and it is negotiating others with the Central American countries.
