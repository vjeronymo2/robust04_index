930701 FT 01 JUL 93 / International Company News: Tighter US employee options rule nearer A CONTROVERSIAL accoun-ting rule change proposed by the US Financial Accounting Standards Board (FASB), which would force companies to disclose the value of options granted to employees and subtract them from profits, yesterday moved one step closer to becoming reality when FASB published its exposure draft of the new rule. FASB, a private sector body which sets standards for the accounting industry, came under fire from business groups in April when it set out its proposal to force companies to recognise stock options and other stock-based compensation rewards as an expense. Yesterday's draft was essentially unchanged from the board's earlier proposal. FASB has asked to receive comments on the proposal by December 31. It will then hold public hearings on the issues. A final ruling will be issued after the public hearings. Although companies will not be required to take charges against earnings until 1997, they will have to provide note disclosures starting in 1994, including disclosure of the effect on net income and earnings per share as if the new recognition requirements had been adopted. Mr Dennis Beresford, FASB chairman, said the delay should help employers become familiar with using option-pricing models and for financial statement users to become accustomed to the new information. 'It will give employers time to consider whether to change their stock-based compensation plans, the design of which may have been heavily influenced by current accounting requirements.'
