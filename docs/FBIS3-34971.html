Language: English Article Type:BFN [By Mona Salim] [Text] Taba, Egypt, Feb 16 (AFP)--PLO and Israeli negotiators worked here Wednesday [16 February] to draw up a timetable for the release of Palestinian prisoners after making headway on plans for Gaza and Jericho to have their own police force. The chief Palestinian negotiator Nabil Sha'th said he was optimistic that an overall accord on the implementation of the September 13 autonomy accord was within sight. "If the negotiations go on at the same rhythm as the last two days, we will not take long," he said. On the third day of negotiations in Egypt's Red Sea resort of Taba, his Israeli counterpart General Amnon Shahaq said: "The paper on the police is almost completed. There are some things that can wait a few more days." Israel and the PLO drafted two documents on Tuesday, one on the size and strength of the force and the other on coordination with the Israeli army, Sha'th told reporters. However, differences remain over "how large the force should be and how many weapons it should have," said Hasan 'Asfur, another PLO negotiator. The Palestine Liberation Organisation wants a force of 9,000 police for the Gaza Strip and the West Bank district of Jericho, while Israel has set a ceiling of 6,000. Israel's Police Minister Moshe Shahal said the Palestinians would be allowed to use the same light weapons and armoured cars as their Israeli counterparts, but a request for tanks was rejected. The PLO also wants speed boats to patrol parts of the Mediterranean coast. The negotiators turned Wednesday to the key issue of prisoners, with the Palestinians seeking the phased release of all 11,000 detainees within four or five months. Colonel David Agmon, an adviser on the intifada to the Israeli army command, and Palestinian deportee Muhammad Dahlan headed the sub-committee in charge of drawing up a timetable for the releases. Israel has said it would not pardon Palestinians who carried out attacks since the autonomy accord was signed in Washington. A separate timetable is under discussion for the withdrawal of Israeli troops from Gaza and Jericho, the launch of which is already two months behind schedule because of a deadlock on security issues. PLO leader Yasir 'Arafat and Israeli Foreign Minister Shim'on Peres finally signed a security agreement last week in Cairo. Economic talks are to resume next Monday in Paris. A committee on the hand-over of civilian powers also met Wednesday to hammer out deals on electricity, archaeology and communications, including the launch of Palestinian television and radio stations. Israeli Prime Minister Yitzhaq Rabin insists his country will take as much time as necessary for a detailed, overall agreement on autonomy. In Washington, the wider Middle East peace talks resumed on Tuesday with the heads of Israeli, Palestinian, Jordanian, Syrian and Lebanese delegations meeting away from the cameras after a two-week break. The Israeli-Palestinian negotiations were focused on elections in the self-rule areas set for July. US State Department officials said privately that Secretary of State Warren Christopher was planning to return to the Middle East in late March to try and advance the Israeli-Syrian track of the peace process.
