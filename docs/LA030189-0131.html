March 1, 1989, Wednesday, Home Edition

HEALTH FILE: SMELLING TROUBLE AFTER RADIATION TREATMENTS

Question: The husband of a friend of mine had radiation treatments, and now my friend says that things don't smell the same to him any more. Could this have been caused by the treatments, or is it just a coincidence? Does this usually happen in such cases?

Answer: The use of radiation in cancers of the head and neck has been reported to affect the sense of smell. Many patients experience some improvement a few months after treatment, but some residual impairment is often the rule.

Q: Two of my friends have had to have hysterectomies, and their reactions to it have been as different as night and day. One of them is acting as if the world has come to an end, while the other one has told me that she looks on it as if she had to have a tooth extracted. How can you account for this difference in attitude?

A: A number of factors may help determine a woman's reaction to the need for a hysterectomy. For example, a 25-year-old childless woman may be more concerned about having a hysterectomy than a 55-year-old mother. The reason for the procedure and any complications associated with it may also influence the woman's attitude and reaction.

Cultural factors also appear to play a role. The results of various studies have shown that women from different cultural and ethnic backgrounds show different degrees of anxiety and depression both before and after surgery. In addition, women who were better educated appeared to tolerate the procedure better than did less well educated women.
