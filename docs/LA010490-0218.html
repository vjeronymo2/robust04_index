January 4, 1990, Thursday, P.M. Final

PEOPLE'S PHARMACY: MORE EXCITING DRUG BREAKTHROUGHS FORESEEN FOR 1990S

The drug breakthroughs during the last decade have been extraordinary, but the outlook for the 1990s is even more exciting.

Looking back over the past 10 years, we have seen a revolution in ulcer therapy. The most successful drugs of the '80s were Zantac (ranitidine) and Tagamet (cimetidine) -- both topped annuals sales of a billion dollars, the first drugs in history to do so.

Blood pressure control became easier with the development of two new classes of medicine. The ACE enzyme inhibitors Capoten (captopril) and Vasotec (enalapril) along with the calcium antagonists Cardizem (diltiazem), Procardia (nifedipine) and Calan and Isoptin (verapamil) have given physicians valuable new tools in their fight against hypertension.

Clot busters like Activase and Streptase are reversing heart attacks that would have killed or crippled people 10 years ago. And new research showed that an old drug, aspirin, could dramatically reduce the risk of heart attacks.

Sexually transmitted diseases captured headlines during the decade just past. First herpes, then AIDS stimulated drug researchers to develop Zovirax (acyclovir) and Retrovir (zidovudine or AZT). Although these drugs aren't cures, they proved that antiviral drug development has promise.

New antidepressants like Prozac (fluoxetine) and Wellbutrin (bupropion) offered relief without the usual side effects. And non-sedating antihistamines like Seldane (terfenadine) and Hismanal (astemizole) allowed allergy victims relief without drowsiness.

Finally, the baby boom generation woke up to discover that it was aging. Rogaine Topical Solution (minoxidil) disproved the dermatological dogma that baldness was irreversible. And the acne medicine Retin-A undid excess sun exposure by reversing wrinkles.

But as exciting as the '80s were for pharmaceutical innovation, the 1990s offer even greater breakthroughs.

Millions of men with enlarged prostate glands may benefit from Proscar (finasteride), a drug that may also prove beneficial against male pattern baldness and acne.

Drug companies are aggressively pursuing treatments for Alzheimer's disease and other brain disorders of aging.

Lazaroids (named after the biblical character Lazarus who rose from the dead) represent an exciting new class of compounds that may preserve the heart and brain from injury during a heart attack or stroke.

Other conditions that may yield to new therapies include osteoporosis, migraine headaches, schizophrenia, arthritis, asthma and heart disease. If researchers are able to deliver on a fraction of the promising agents now in development, a new age of drug breakthroughs lies ahead.
