HOME IMPROVEMENT;

GET EDGE ON QUALITY IN BUILDING WITH PLYWOOD;

FINISH: THERE ARE SEVERAL WAYS TO DISPLAY WORKMANSHIP MAKING THE ROUGH EDGES OF BOARDS LOOK SMOOTH.

If you're building a piece of furniture, ripping some shelving from a sheet of plywood or fabricating a countertop for plastic laminate or ceramic tile, the way you finish the edges will reflect the quality of your workmanship.

Plywood got its name because thin layers, or plies, of wood were glued together at right angles to each other, which provided stability and rigidity, in a manufacturing process that yielded more usable wood from each log. However, the process also resulted in a panel with a rough edge. Chipboard -- made by mixing and compressing small chips of wood in an adhesive -- also has a rough edge that requires special treatment.

You have many edge-finishing options that you can exercise, depending on the project, your skill and your tools.

A very popular way to conceal the end grain is to miter the corners, as illustrated in Figure 1. However, this requires a table or radial arm saw, absolutely flat material and some skill on your part.

Probably the mostly widely used edge treatment -- because it's easier and more economical -- is to fill the edge grain with a paste wood filler, sand the filler smooth and repeat the application until the grain no longer is visible, as illustrated in Figure 2.

Another technique that is faster but slightly more expensive is to glue and nail decorative molding to the front (see Figure 3). This is an especially fine technique when making a coffee table or decorative shelving.

If you are working with expensive hardwood panels, such as birch or mahogany, and plan to show the natural grain and color of the wood, your edge treatment should be to adhere a strip of the same type wood to the edge, as shown in Figure 4. A contact adhesive is best, and the veneer should be at least one-eighth inch wider than the thickness of the plywood. When in place, the protruding edge of the veneer can be easily cut away with a plane, razor or even sanded so it is flush with the top.

If you are building a kitchen countertop or making shelves that require additional reinforcement, the edge treatment illustrated in Figure 5 is best. Screw and nail straight white pine pieces, sand smooth and you are ready for covering the surface and edge with ceramic tile or the most widely used countertop material, decorative plastic laminate.
