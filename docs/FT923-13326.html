920714 FT 14 JUL 92 / Home loans face threat BUILDING SOCIETIES may have to cut lending to first-time buyers because of pressure from insurance companies, the chief executive of a leading building society warned yesterday. Mr Martin Armstrong, head of Norwich & Peterborough Building Society, said insurance companies were trying to impose terms on mortgage-indemnity insurance that would depress the market. 'There is a strong likelihood that the maximum percentage on loans we can offer borrowers will be reduced from 95 per cent to 90 per cent, harming the first-time-buyer market,' Mr Armstrong said. Insurers have been renegotiating contracts with mortgage lenders since the end of last year. Most existing contracts for mortgage-indemnity insurance, a one-off payment insuring high loan-to-value mortgages, finish on August 31. Insurance companies have made heavy losses in the mortgage indemnity business in the last two years because of the squeeze on the housing market and the fall in house prices. Royal Insurance, one of the market leaders, faces losses of more than Pounds 400m from domestic mortgage indemnities. It says it is trying to develop a proper trading relationship with its clients. Legal & General, another large insurer, said it had stopped dealing with several small building societies to concentrate on larger lenders. Two small societies, Buckinghamshire and Kent Reliance, confirmed yesterday that Legal & General had told them it would no longer offer them mortgage-indemnity insurance. Both say their experience of bad loans has been very low and they are negotiating with other insurance companies. Mr John Wriglesworth, a housing market analyst at UBS Phillips & Drew, said the insurance companies were operating a cartel in dealing with mortgage lenders. 'All the new terms for the contracts are exactly the same wording,' he said. 'The insurance companies risk shooting themselves in the foot.'
