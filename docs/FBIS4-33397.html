BFN [By Hugo Sergio del Aguila] [Text] Caracas, 2 Jun (NOTIMEX) -- Venezuelan President Rafael Caldera will ask his colleagues, who will participate in the Fourth Ibero-American Summit, to seek urgent solutions for the demands of millions of poor people in the region, Foreign Minister Miguel Angel Burelli said today. The Venezuelan foreign minister said the Ibero-American Summit will be a good opportunity to find solutions "for social disorders and urban overcrowding," which have become common and serious problems for Latin America and the Caribbean. "Latin America and the Caribbean have common problems such as poverty, social disorders, and overcrowded cities because of people moving in from the countryside and becoming disorderly masses," Burelli said. In an interview with NOTIMEX, the foreign minister said Venezuela regrets to see that the development of the last few decades has not kept man as its center of attention with his various needs, such as education, health, food, and housing. He confirmed that President Caldera will participate in the summit in Cartagena de Indias, Colombia, on 14 and 15 June, which will be attended by 21 heads of state and government, including Spain and Portugal, because "Venezuela will fulfill its obligation once again." It will be Caldera's first trip abroad since he was sworn in on 2 February. According to the Venezuelan foreign minister, Ibero-American summits are of great importance far beyond their agenda points, because of the opportunities they represent and because they provide a greater awareness of reality. "The Ibero-American Summit will also be a good opportunity--without ruling out other forums such as the OAS--to clearly establish the desire to give greater importance to human development," Burelli said. The minister said work is still being done on the summit's agenda, but noted that, in addition to the discussion of social problems, it is necessary to discuss "the Haitian tragedy, one of the most difficult topics because of its lack of solutions." He said it is important to "exchange ideas" about the Summit of the Americas convened by U.S. President William Clinton for Miami, Florida to which all countries of the hemisphere have been invited, except Cuba. According to Burelli, the Miami meeting will be an opportunity for Latin America to talk to the United States about topics of extreme importance to the region. Venezuela will reiterate at the forum the proposal it made during the recent Rio Group Summit in Sao Paulo, Brazil, on the urgent need to create an instrument to fight corruption, such as drug trafficking, in a drastic and expeditious manner. The foreign minister explained that the Venezuelan proposal coincides with a law being drafted by the Ecuadoran Government to curb influence-peddling and corruption. "We seek to merge both initiatives so as to propose an agile and useful instrument to stop the wave of corruption in our countries," he said. Burelli said we must take advantage of the Ibero-American Summit and other forums to insist on this serious problem because democracy "must involve proven honesty." As for the topic of continental security, the foreign minister pointed out that, in view of the failure of the Inter-American Security System, which was dismantled following the Islas Malvinas War [Falkland Islands War], a mechanism to detect elements of destabilization and insecurity in the region is necessary. "There are sufficient reasons to undertake an Ibero-American action on the security issue. Some of these reasons include drug trafficking, guerrillas, extreme poverty, human rights violations, hunger, and injustice," he said. According to Burelli, the agenda for the Ibero-American Summit will include topics such as culture, education, and the exchange of technology as a mechanism to free ourselves from our dependence on huge industrial centers. "We are going to seek mechanisms that can allow universities and industries at a hemispheric level to make innovations that can contribute to the reduction of the high costs of social development," Burelli said. The minister reiterated the importance of the so-called Group of Three's (Mexico, Colombia, and Venezuela) signing an agreement to create a free trade zone, which is to take place in Cartagena de Indias on 13 June. Burelli said the trade agreement, which will open up a market of 245 million potential consumers with a gross regional product of $370 billion, will greatly contribute to the regional integration process and will promote the economic development of the region.
