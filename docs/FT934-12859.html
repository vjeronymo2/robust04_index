931025 FT 25 OCT 93 / Hurd challenged on Malaysia aid LABOUR last night demanded that Mr Douglas Hurd, foreign secretary, spell out whether a Pounds 234m aid package for Malaysia had been linked to sales of British military equipment. Mr Jack Cunningham, shadow foreign secretary, said the Commons should investigate whether the aid - criticised last week in a report from the National Audit Office - had been a quid pro quo for a Pounds 1bn arms order. The public-spending watchdog's report said that Pounds 56m of public money had been wasted in the funding of a hydro-electric project on the Pergau river. Mr Hurd is to be called before the public accounts committee to explain his decision to overrule official advice that the aid should not be granted. Last week he defended the 1991 decision on the basis of an 'understanding' reached some years earlier with the Malaysian government that the aid would be given. He said that it would have been 'very damaging' to Britain's commercial and political interests if he had broken the agreement. The undertaking was given by Baroness Thatcher, the then prime minister. In 1988 she personally negotiated the sale of Pounds 1bn worth of arms with Mr Mahatir Mohamad, the Malaysian prime minister, during a visit to Kuala Lumpur. The aid package was announced within weeks of the military deal and both were discussed in the talks between the two leaders. Labour has long argued that the price for the arms sales, which included 12 Tornado ground attack aircraft, was the promise of assistance for the Pergau project. Last night Mr Cunningham said: 'Mr Hurd appears to have clearly breached his fiduciary duty over a very large amount of taxpayers' money. His action is all the more questionable given the controversial nature of the Malaysian project.' He added: 'The House of Commons will want to investigate the reports that a British aid decision was contingent upon a major British arms sale to Malaysia.'
