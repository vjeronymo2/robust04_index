November 11, 1990, Sunday, Home Edition

GARDENING;

FAST-GROWING TREES TO GUARD YOUR PRIVACY;

EVERGREENS: THE TRICK IS TO PLANT TREES THAT DON'T GROW TOO TALL, TOO WIDE OR MAKE A MESS.

It's familiar quest for many Southern California gardeners: the search for an evergreen tree that will quickly soar above a six-foot fence to block a view of the neighbors. But then it must stop before it towers over the rest of the back yard and casts shadows everywhere.

Fifteen to 25 feet of fast growth will do, with a leafy canopy about as wide. In smaller urban gardens, that's probably all the space to be had.

The trouble is, many young trees that look so appealing in their nursery cans become too tall, too wide, too messy or too lacy to provide a solid block.

But there are a few winners. Most are readily available for fall planting. Because of their smaller scale, some are commonly grown as large shrubs. But they're easily trained as trees. Or you can get a head start by buying or ordering "standards," which have already been pruned into tree form.

Bronze Loquat

Ideal for cramped gardens or filtered shade, bronze loquat (Eriobotrya deflexa) tops out at about 20 feet. Long, new leaves emerge a rich, reddish-brown, then gradually change to dark green. In spring, fragrant white flowers similar to apple blossoms appear clustered on branch tips.

The small, yellow-green fruit is not edible, but does attract birds. (The larger Eriobotrya japonica produces edible fruit.) Bronze loquat does well along the coast or inland with regular, deep watering.

Fraser Photinia

This popular street tree (Photinia fraseri) tolerates a host of urban woes: smog, dust, heat, even sprinklers. Mature leaves are dark green, while new leaves and stems are bright red. Plentiful clusters of white flowers top the red tips for a splendid, spring display.

Although it adapts well to espaliering or shaping, fall pruning does limit spring flowering. Photina prefers full sun and frequent, deep watering early on to develop drought resistance. Provide good drainage -- in constantly wet clay, leaves may yellow and drop.

Hopseed Bush

Native to the harsh, Arizona desert, hopseed bush (Dodonea viscosa) tolerates everything from salt spray to winds and dry heat. Its long, narrow, upright leaves form a dense, willow-like screen.

Cold weather causes the rusty-purple leaves of 'Purpurea' to turn bright purple (it reverts in spring). Full sun helps sustain the distinctive color. Tiny spring flowers are barely noticeable, but clusters of pale pink seed pods in late summer are interesting and rustle in the wind. With regular water, hopseed bush grows fast. With less, it remains compact.

Lemon Bottlebrush

Hummingbirds descend on lemon bottlebrush (Callistemon citrinus) whenever a flush of bright red "brushes" of flowers appear. Bloom peaks in early summer, although some flowering occurs all year. Left unpruned, it forms a round, 12-foot-tall bush. But with shaping, its gnarled branches grow into a graceful, slender tree to 25 feet. Lemon bottlebrush tolerates heavy soil, smog, wind and heat, and may become drought-resistant over time.

Pineapple Guava

One of the prettiest flowering and fruiting small trees, pineapple guava (Feijoa sellowiana) pumps out showy, edible, red-and-white flower clusters in early summer, followed by edible, gray-green fruit. Its clean, oval leaves are glossy green on top and pale-gray underneath. In coastal areas, it requires full sun. Inland, provides filtered shade.

Shiny Xylosma

Commonly seen as a hedge, shiny xylosma (Xylosma congestum) is also a fine, quick-growing tree. Glossy, apple-green leaves are tinged with red when they emerge.

Left to their own devices, the branches droop, crisscross and dangle in odd directions. But with direction and pruning, it grows into a graceful, multi-trunked tree to 25 feet. Shiny xylosma tolerates most soils and weather conditions, but may drop leaves after a severe frost.
