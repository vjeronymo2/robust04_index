August 6, 1990, Monday, P.M. Final

PEOPLE'S PHARMACY: HOME REMEDIES COULD SOOTHE THE PAIN OF COLD SORES

Question: I get cold sores whenever we go to the beach. They are extremely painful and the scars last quite a while. My doctor tells me there is no cure. Is there a home remedy that I could use on my vacation this month?

Answer: Over the years we have received lots of home remedies from readers. We cannot vouch for any of them, but here they are.

One man swears by buttermilk. He writes, "After suffering for several years I asked a doctor about treating fever blisters. His reply was not very encouraging. My pharmacist said to drink buttermilk. I tried it. It's easy and cheap and it has really cut down on the number of fever blisters I have suffered."

Another reader suggests douching powder applied to the sore. She says dabbing a little Massengill or Bo-Car-Al on the blister overnight helps speed healing.

An alternative would be the prescription medicine Zovirax (acyclovir). It is available as an ointment or in pill form. Although it is not a cure it can also speed healing or perhaps even prevent an outbreak.

You should also use a lip balm containing an effective sunscreen. This may eliminate the problem.

Q. My wife and her mother both have high cholesterol. Even eating a very low-fat diet has done little to lower it.

They both take Dyazide and have for many years. I have read in the paper that Dyazide can raise cholesterol. How much does it affect cholesterol readings? Is there a good substitute for Dyazide that does not increase the cholesterol count?

A. Many people find it difficult to lower cholesterol even with a strict low-fat diet. Some medicines may compound the problem, though. Thiazide diuretics such as Dyazide may make it a little harder to achieve the desired results.

Fortunately, there are other blood pressure medications that are less likely to raise blood fats. Only your wife's physician can decide which drug is most appropriate for her.
