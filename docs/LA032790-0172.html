March 27, 1990, Tuesday, P.M. Final

COMMENTARY;

OWNERS' LOCKOUT SHOWED ORIOLES' BRADLEY A NEW GAME

Rest easy, folks. Whatever you might have supposed, baseball is the same as it ever was.

Of course, at Bobby Maduro Miami Stadium, that's a mixed blessing. The crack of the bat was muffled by the roar of planes flying overhead, and the elevator was in mid-season form, briefly trapping one unfortunate. There was a fire sighted somewhere in the distance, a dark cloud befouling the blue skies, and it seemed just like home.

Only 1,959 hardy souls braved the 80-degree temperatures (it was slightly overcast) for the Baltimore Orioles' spring opener. The small crowd was almost certainly less a statement on the recent labor strife than it was one more reason why the Orioles would rather be playing their spring games elsewhere.

Eager to begin, the Orioles played two Monday, starting with a "B" game, featuring mostly minor leaguers. In the fourth inning of the first game, Orioles Manager Frank Robinson leaned over from the dugout to pronounce his team "unbeaten and unbeatable."

As it turned out, they lost both games, by identical 5-3 scores. Not that it mattered. Spring games are public rehearsals that don't merit serious review. The idea is to learn your lines.

For Phil Bradley, it is the 10th such rehearsal, but this one differs significantly from the others. He prepared himself for the 1990 baseball season by sitting through labor negotiations in the face of a lockout, arguing the players' cause, helping to sort out an agreement that took longer than anyone should have guessed.

"They told me to be patient," Bradley was saying Monday, "but I didn't understand what they meant. Now I do."

It wasn't the same kind of patience demanded of a batter facing a breaking-ball pitcher. Lawyers understand this kind of patience, wherein delay is a weapon and obfuscation is the name of the game. If you ask Bradley what he did in his off-season, he'll tell you he learned a whole new ballgame.

First, he volunteered to be elected to serve on the executive board of the players' association, knowing full well that it meant he'd have to become a labor negotiator, for good and ill. He said that the choice wasn't as difficult as it might seem.

"I like to know what's going on," he said. "The only way you could know was to be a part of it. From early December, I was involved in almost every negotiation that took place. I was in L.A. and I was in Tampa. I must have made eight or 10 trips to New York.

"The frustrating part was that so many of the issues seemed so direct, but they were never dealt with directly. Eventually, I figured out this thing wasn't going to end until the owners wanted it to end, no matter what I did. When I understood that, it took a lot of pressure off me. They were the ones who were going to decide when we were going to get things resolved."

And so, Bradley was involved, up to his numbers. He's that kind of person anyway, one who takes life a little more seriously than the rest of us do. If you were a member of a union, he's just the person you'd want negotiating on your side.

For a ballplayer, though, there is a downside to that degree of involvement.

For today's player, at least for those players who don't have to drive a truck to support themselves, the off-season is a time to prepare for the on-season. Players take better care of themselves than they used to in the supposed good old days, when the only wintertime conditioning in which your average player partook was to pay someone to shovel the snow off the walk. Today, a player comes to camp in shape. He has stretched out his arm and probably taken some swings in a batting cage.

"Up until March, I was working out three times a week," Bradley said. "After that, I didn't get as much work in. Am I behind? I don't know. I feel good. I know I haven't had a good start since 1985, no matter what I've done over the winter. I told myself when I got involved in this that I wasn't going to blame a slow start on the negotiations. If I did that, how was I going to explain the other years?"

Last year was not everything Bradley wanted it to be. Although it was the first year in six big-league seasons he had played on a team to have won more than half its games, he hit only .277 -- 16 points below his lifetime average. A big second-half hitter for all his career, Bradley slumped after the All-Star break, and he may have been pressing during his first pennant race.

For this season, he has changed the number of his uniform from 16 to 1, and maybe that will help. But he wouldn't change anything about his role as a labor negotiator, except its duration.

"If they want me to do it again the next time, I'd be happy to do it," he said.

In the meantime, there was baseball to be played. Bradley, batting leadoff in Monday's spring opener, had a single and a walk and reached base on an error in three trips to the plate. It didn't seem so bad, and the best part was nobody tried to talk him out of it.
