September 17, 1989, Sunday, Home Edition

A JUNGLE OF OUR IMAGINATION;

PRIMATE VISIONS GENDER, RACE, AND NATURE IN THE WORLD OF MODERN SCIENCE BY DONNA J. HARAWAY (ROUTLEDGE: $35; 472 PP., ILLUSTRATED; 0-415-90114-6)

"Primate Visions" is about the ways in which scientific accounts of the lives of animals -- specifically those most closely related to us -- are intertwined with our concepts of gender, race, and nature. Depictions of monkeys and apes tell us much about ourselves, or, to paraphrase Pogo: "We have met the primates, and they are us." Both scientific accounts and popular images of monkeys and apes reflect sometimes-disturbing projections of human problems.

Haraway precedes from the assumption that monkeys and apes occupy the crucial border zone between culture and nature; in the book we see them catapulted into space, taught to communicate with humans, or portrayed as symbols of "lost innocence" in a Third World devoid of indigenous human actors. In this mythological terrain, a lone white girl named Jane meets the innocent ape and both are transfigured. Through Haraway's lens, we see that the National Geographic vision of Africa bears a stronger resemblance to Disney World than to the African reality -- it is a theme park of the late 20th-Century industrial West's imagination.

Haraway is chairman of the history of consciousness program at UC Santa Cruz, and a leading figure in post-modern deconstructionism. "Primate Visions" is about the way in which scientific accounts reflect and create our natural cosmology. Haraway is at her best when she monkeys brilliantly with some of our most precious symbols, at her worst when her language is inaccessible to a layperson not steeped in post-modern criticism. This is an important book that deserves the widest possible audience.

The first part of the book historically contextualizes the modern view of primates. In a chapter entitled "Teddy Bear Patriarchy" we are led through the great halls of the American Museum of Natural History. Here, Teddy Roosevelt's patriarchal ideals of manhood are reflected in the African Hall dioramas in which stuffed animals are mounted in family groupings to symbolize the "purity of nature." African Hall was the work of Carl Akeley, the artist, scientist and hunter who collected and stuffed African animals beginning in the 1880s. "From the dead body of the primate Akeley crafted something finer than the living organism; he achieved its true end, a new genesis. Decadence -- the threat of the city, civilization, machine -- was stayed in the politics of eugenics and the art of taxidermy."

The magnificent silverback male gorilla of which Haraway writes, was killed in 1921, the same year the museum hosted a large international gathering of eugenicists. She connects the vision in the dioramas to the obsession with "genetic hygiene" of many early 20th-Century Americans and Europeans. Like the dioramas, eugenics contrasted the woes of civilization with the goodness of nature. By showing us African Hall as "simultaneously a very strange place and an ordinary experience for literally millions of North Americans" Haraway points out how strange our ordinary reality is. No one who has read "Teddy Bear Patriarchy" will ever experience a trip to the Museum of Natural History in quite the same way as before.

Haraway devotes later chapters to modern primate field and laboratory studies. She connects the portrayals of white women watching apes in the Third World to First World anxieties about African decolonization. We read what is left out of the National Geographic accounts: Jane Goodall and her mother making 2,000 Spam sandwiches for Belgian troops fleeing their former colony; a cage constructed for Goodall's son, to protect him from the chimpanzees. National Geographic omits the African political context in which Goodall's work took place.

In a final section of the book, Haraway presents biographies of four modern primatologists -- all of them women. It is probably not coincidental that primatology is the only branch of evolutionary studies with a predominance of successful women practitioners. Women are identified with nature and figure importantly in the mythological construction of the primate Garden of Eden. Women primatologists have contested male-centered origin stories by placing female monkeys and apes center stage in their accounts, and emphasizing female primate agency.

Because Haraway is interested in reading primatology as text, she avoids questions about what is scientific fact versus poorly supported theoretical speculation. One primatologist whose work Haraway discusses is a leading proponent of sociobiology (the theory that animals and humans follow strategies that insure that the greatest number of their own genes will survive in future generations). The primatologist speaks of female monkeys, apes and humans as competitive, assertive, dominance- oriented and looking out for their own genetic advantage. It seems that female primates of the 1980s have taken their places in the board room of primate troops that resemble corporate culture.

Haraway points out the ways in which this new image of female primates mirrors late 20th-Century capitalist society. But sociobiology has been criticized for its failure to document actual behavior across the primate life cycle. Because a researcher seems to tell a good "feminist" story (i.e. that female primates are strategists for their own genetic advantage) does not mean that the work is good science. Sociobiology is genetic reductionism. It implies that gendered behaviors are controlled by genes. There is much evidence that this is not the case for humans, apes or monkeys. While all scientific stories may be read as myth, some are also poor science.

Challenging and engaging, "Primate Visions" holds up a mirror to some of our favorite myths about animals. It shows us that we are not innocent; a Sunday at the museum or an evening in front of television are laden with meanings and deep ambiguities at the heart of our ideas about human and animal.
