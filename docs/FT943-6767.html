940826 FT 26 AUG 94 / (CORRECTED) Performance of Tecs shows much improvement Correction (published 31st August 1994) appended to this article. Big improvements in the performance of individual training and enterprise councils were revealed yesterday by the Department of Employment. Figures showed more unemployed people gaining qualifications, as well as more obtaining jobs. Seven performance indicators were used to assess the relative performance of the 82 employer-led Tecs in relation to their delivery of publicly funded training in England and Wales. Tecs, the oldest of which is four years old, have been put in the spotlight recently by speculation that Mr Michael Portillo, the new employment secretary, wants to slash their Pounds 1.8bn training budget. Ms Ann Clwyd, the acting shadow employment secretary, yesterday wrote to Mr Portillo calling for him to 'categorically confirm' that he was not seeking to halve Tecs' budgets. It was difficult, she said, for Tecs to operate successfully when such rumours existed. The indicators show a substantial, if uneven, reduction in the cost incurred by Tecs to get jobs and/or qualifications for those on Youth Training, which guarantees a training place for young people, and Training for Work, the programme for long-term unemployed people. TFW, which is probably the programme more vulnerable to cuts, showed a particularly strong improvement. The number of qualifications gained per hundred leavers rose nearly 50 per cent on last year's performance, with an average 40 per cent fall in the cost of points towards a qualification. The Tec National Council, which represents Tecs, said they had delivered efficiency improvements every year since taking over provision of the programmes from the government's Training Agency in 1990. While their budgets had declined, they had served the same number of clients with increased performance. Mr Chris Humphries, policy adviser to the council, said he hoped that the improved performance would be rewarded by maintenance of budgets, not cuts. Mr James Paice, the employment minister, said the indices showed the better value for money that the taxpayer was receiving. But there should be 'no let up' in the effort the government made to help Tecs improve their performance. The indices measured the cost incurred by each Tec in gaining individual qualifications, as well as the number of national vocational qualifications per 100 leavers. In the case of TFW, the number of people in jobs on a specific date was also measured. The department has not prepared a list of the best and worst Tecs. It is understood that this is because there are no consistent patterns and it would be difficult to reflect improvements in individual performances over the previous year. A crude league table places Cumbria, Barnsley/Doncaster and Hereford and Worcester Tecs at the top.
