Language: Slovene Article Type:CSO [Report by Branko Jokic: "Business Is Business"] [Text] (From our correspondent) Podgorica, 28 January -- According to an article published in the Novi Pazar magazine SANDZAK, Montenegrin and Albanian authorities concluded a secret agreement on the export of gasoline from Albania to Montenegro, from where it is delivered to Pale [capital of the Srpska republic] to be used by the Bosnian Serb military. According to the article, Montenegrin Albanians living along the Montenegrin-Albanian border are involved in the deals. Some of them have been licensed by the Montenegrin Government to "import" gasoline. Gasoline, which costs one German mark per liter in the Albanian town of Scutari, is transported in large quantities across Lake Skutari to Montenegro. The Yugoslav Army provides specially equipped boats. The citizens of Montenegro involved in this business earn one-half mark per liter. Almost all local residents participate in this trade (actually it is a state-sanctioned smuggling operation) because of the high earnings. Montenegro and the Srpska republic are well supplied with gasoline. The Pizon Petrol Enterprise, owned by the former Crvena Zvezda soccer player Vladimir Petrovic Pizon, transports gasoline from Lake Scutari to Pale. As reported, the Albanian authorities have recently tightened control over gasoline sales in Scutari, but it is a well-known secret that gasoline stations in Scutari work at full speed and sell almost half of all the gasoline sold in Albania. We are talking about very large quantities, knowing that last year Albanians registered several thousand vehicles that they had purchased at European junkyards. According to the article, Pale receives five to seven tanker-truck loads of Albanian gasoline each day. In addition to Albania, Macedonia is also accused of sanctions violations. At the border crossings near Ohrid and Rozaj (on the Macedonian-Albanian and Montenegrin-Albanian borders), aluminum is exported from Montenegro and raw materials are imported for Montenegro and Serbia without hindrance. Heavy trucks and trailers wait for hours on both sides of the border crossings, which in the past were almost without traffic. If all this is true, we could conclude that this type of border trade, uncontrolled by representatives of the international community, was agreed to by Momir Bulatovic and Sali Berisha when the Montenegrin president visited Tirana. At the same time, Montenegrin Prime Minister Milo Djukanovic paid a visit to Skopje. It is a fact that we can only guess now how Albania benefits. Maybe it is alleviation of discontent in the western part of the country, where at least some hundred local residents are earning good incomes trafficking in gasoline.
