Language: Dutch Article Type:BFN [Article by Hans Buddingh' and Marcel Haenen: "Justice Department Unaware of DEA Infiltration"] [Excerpt] During the 1980's, agents from the U.S. Drug Enforcement Administration (DEA) organized, from the U.S. Embassy in The Hague, the transport of chemicals to the Netherlands Antilles, Suriname, and Colombia for use in the manufacture of cocaine. The DEA used barrels equipped with a transmitter in order to locate the laboratories for which the chemicals were destined. These activities, in 1985 and 1986, took place in the greatest secrecy and without the knowledge of the Netherlands Justice Department. The CRI [Central Information Research Agency] had made it known to the Americans during the preparatory stages that such infiltration methods were to be rejected. For the deliveries, the Americans used the Netherlands criminal informer Dick Stotijn, who was in contact with drug cartels in South America. He was appointed director of a company, Berg Trading Services, which was based in the Belgian town of Putte, just over the border from the Netherlands. In the Netherlands Antilles Stotijn found drug dealers ready to take chemicals purchased from the French company Blondeau. With the knowledge of the French company, direction finders were included with the ether and acetone in the barrels. According to DEA agent D. Laverty, who worked in The Hague in the 1980's, two major operations were conducted in any event via the export from Belgium in 1986. One shipment located a laboratory in Colombia which was finally put out of operation. The former head of the CRI narcotics bureau, G. de Gooyer, said that his department was not "actively involved" in the controlled dispatch of chemicals. He said he could not remember whether his American colleagues had told him what they were doing. [passage omitted]
