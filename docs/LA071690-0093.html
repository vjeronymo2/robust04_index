July 16, 1990, Monday, Home Edition

NO FUNDS, MUCH FURY

Donna Reed she's not. But Holly Hughes, that frisky femme of the performance world and author of plays whose titles you can't print in this newspaper, is cooking plenty.

With her poet's ear tuned to the ironies of gay life, her life, and the ins-and-outs of waiting tables, Hughes is on the road to set some records straight.

The New Yorker is one of four performance artists who were recently recommended for NEA support by a peer panel of theater experts and then denied that funding in an unprecedented action by NEA Chairman John E. Frohnmayer.

Despite these trying times, Hughes -- a Grove Press-published playwright, Jerome Foundation grantee and recipient of a MacDowell Fellowship -- refuses to let what she sees as the "open season on artists, whether they're publicly funded or not," stop her from making art.

The world premiere of her performance "Dead Meat" ("I didn't know how prophetic my title would be," she quips. "I didn't plan to live up to it this way") comes to Highways on Tuesday for a benefit performance. The run continues Thursday as part of "Ecce Lesbo/Ecce Homo," Highways' second annual gay and lesbian performance festival. Later this month, Hughes reprises her solo, "World Without End."

Tired from repeated sessions with the press and legal counsel, Hughes spoke about the Frohnmayer action from Seattle, where she was taking part in the National Gay and Lesbian Theater Festival. (Frohnmayer declined comment for this article.)

"I'm furious," says Hughes. "All four of us are appealing the decision. Not only did we get our grants turned down, but before the overturnings were announced, (John) Fleck, Karen (Finley) and I were getting our careers demolished by misrepresentation in the press."

Hughes has been recommended for three NEA grants in different categories during the last three months. Before last year, when Congress installed a clause that requires grantees to sign a pledge to comply with restrictive language in the NEA's appropriations bill, Hughes was awarded a 1989 Inter-arts grant.

In March, after the new language was in place, she won a 1990 playwriting grant. "I smeared the word lesbian all over my application," says Hughes. "I wanted to make it perfectly clear; I have yet to receive that money."

After that award, says Hughes, the situation got much worse. "It was leaked to me in the spring that I'd been recommended for funding in the solo performance-artist category," she says, "but the solo fellowships had been deferred.

"There was a gag order, but obviously somebody in the NEA was talking and misrepresenting the work," recalls Hughes. "I contacted Frohnmayer's office in early June when these distortions first started coming out in the Washington Times and said, 'What are you going to do about these leaks?'

"I hadn't committed any crime other than applying to the NEA," says Hughes. "I was offered all sorts of assurances that (Frohnmayer) was concerned and wanted to protect the artists."

Yet, says Hughes, those promises apparently didn't matter next to what she describes as Frohnmayer's desire "to make sacrifices to the right wing.

"I suspect the one thing that politicians do all the time is leak something and see what the public response to it is," she says. "It's not impossible that these leaks were coming from Frohnmayer's office."

"I have this fantasy: 'A Day in the Life of John Frohnmayer.' He wakes up in the morning and crawls on his belly to the door of (North Carolina Sen.) Jesse Helms. And he scratches on Helms' door and says, 'Ohhh, ohhhh, what can I do for you today? Take some more trash out of the NEA -- those lesbians, those feminists, those gay people -- and demolish the entire performance-art category and establish in its place maybe a bake-off?' "

Kidding aside, Hughes says that Frohnmayer isn't doing his job. "Funding is supposed to be based on artistic merit alone," she says. "The chairman's job is to insulate (the process) from political realities, meaning Jesse Helms and (Lomita Rep.) Dana Rohrabacher."

The reason for the four "sacrifices," says Hughes, is politics. "It diverts attention," she says. "It's easier to de-fund four performance artists and to look like you're doing something, than to indict Charles Keating or the President's son" (both of whom are involved in savings and loan scandals).

"The total of all four of our grants was just $23,000, which is just a weekend at Kennebunkport for the Bushes -- if they don't bring Millie along."

Nor, says Hughes, does the assault on the arts stop with the defunding of the four performers. "Various organizations from the NEA to city governments are going after spaces that present us," says Hughes.

"The Kitchen in New York is the subject of three grueling audits from the IRA. The WOW Cafe is getting eviction notices. Franklin Furnace was shut down, ostensibly for fire reasons, but really because they presented Karen (Finley). And Sushi in San Diego -- which has presented all four of us -- has been getting hate mail and phone calls because the religious right in San Diego has been complaining to the city council.

"We have to remember other repressive cultures," Hughes continues. "It always begins with the censorship of artists, the lack of tolerance for dissent and the persecution of a minority."
