Language: English Article Type:BFN [Report by Linda Hutchinson] [Text] Port-of-Spain, Trinidad, 22 Jan, CANA -- Trinidad and Tobago's Prime Minister Patrick Manning on Friday night announced a mini cabinet reshuffle which saw additional responsibilities for some senior ministers. The cabinet reshuffle followed the resignation of Trade, Industry and Tourism Minister Brian Kuei Tung who at the beginning of the month said he wanted to pursue other career options. In the reshuffle, Junior Finance Minister Ken Valley, who is considered to be one of the most productive ministers in the government, will now have additional responsibilities for trade and industry. The local government ministry which was handled by Valley was shifted to the minister of works and transport, Colm Imbert. Finance Minister Wendell Mottley was given the additional portfolio of the tourism ministry. In addition, Manning announced the creation of a Ministry of Social Development which will be handled by Senator Camille Regis- Robinson, who will continue as minister in the office of the prime minister. In a wide-ranging address to the nation, in which he raised concerns about the upsurge in violent crime and gave an up-date about the state of the economy, Manning said in the rebuilding of the country's economy, government has to devote time to the details of tourism and agricultural promotion. "As we make this shift, we found it necessary to make some corresponding organisational adjustments in the government," he said. "Mr Brian Kuei Tung, the minister of trade, industry and tourism leaves the government (on Friday). I would like to thank him for the tremendous effort that he has exerted in the discharge of his responsibilities over the last two years and to wish him well in his new pursuits. "In accordance with the government's priorities for the development of the tourism sector in the context of job creation and the need to accelerate the process of streamlining administrative procedures to attract greater levels of investment, there will now be a separate Ministry of Tourism." Expressing concern about crime in the country, which he described as "callous brutality," the prime minister announced that from Monday, members of the Defence Force will be brought out onto the streets on foot patrol. "The government feels justified in taking this course of action in the light of the anxiety of citizens over the situation." Manning also said that in seeking to make the operations of the police more efficient, cabinet has accepted proposals which envisage the establishment of a nine-person police service board of management appointed by the president after consultation with the government and opposition. The minister of national security, the police service commission, and the commissioner of police in recent times had become embroiled in an issue involving the discipline of police officers. Manning said that opposition leader Basdeo Panday was informed of the proposal and government now awaits a reply. "The board will have the authority to manage the police service and to deal with the recruitment of all levels of police personnel from the rank of constable to the rank of assistant commissioner," said Manning. "The commissioner of police will be responsible for the discipline of officers in the ranks of constable to inspector. The police service commission in this model will become an appeal tribunal and will also have the power to discipline the commissioner of police and deputy commissioners of police." The proposal further envisages that both the police service board of management and the police service commission will be accountable to a committee of parliament. "In addition to all of this, the proposals call for an appointment of an inspector of police service whose responsibility would be to keep the minister of national security up-to-date on the performance of the police services," said Manning. He added that disciplinary proceedings could be instituted against individuals in the service, based on reports from the proposed inspector. Speaking on the economy of the country, Manning described 1993 as being a "difficult year," which saw a double blow from low oil prices and falling oil production, while an unprecedented level of debt repayment had to be sustained. "All of this translated into money worries which forced the government to cut back expenditure in many areas," he said, adding that through the sacrifice government had been able to lay a solid foundation on which to build in 1994. He told the nation that government was successful in diversifying the economy away from the exclusive dependence on oil. He said that in 1984, government's revenues were 42 percent dependent on oil, while in 1993, it was only 27 percent dependent. "So that unwelcome though the present oil prices are, we are infinitely better able to sustain them than we are in 1984. Even so, the repercussions of developments in the oil industry remain too significant to be left to the vagaries of the international oil market. Whereas we cannot control price, we have some influence on our production," said Manning. He said government has been working with several mulitlateral petroleum companies in Trinidad and the local petroleum company to offset the fall in oil prices.
