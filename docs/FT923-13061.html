920715 FT 15 JUL 92 / An elite ranging from Branson to Papa Doc AS LIFE president of British Airways, Lord King will be joining a select band of British executives and Third World politicians. But if precedent is anything to go by, he cannot count on undisturbed enjoyment of his new role. For politicians such as Dr Hastings Banda of Malawi or Papa Doc Duvalier of Haiti, the title has not guaranteed popularity. Corporate life presidents have been caught up in feuds with their successors; some have had to return to sort things out. Last year, Sir Laurie Barratt, life president of the house-building company he founded, returned to the chair after the company suffered in the hands of new managers. Mr David Ogilvy, life president of Ogilvy & Mather, was so distressed by the bid for his advertising agency by Mr Martin Sorrell that he called the WPP chief executive 'an odious little jerk'. Mr Ogilvy went on to serve as WPP chairman. Lord Kissin, life president of Guinness Peat, had a series of rows with Mr Alastair Morton, whom he left in charge. Lord Kissin won, and Mr Morton moved on to Eurotunnel. A warning for Lord King came from Mr Alec Horsley, 89, life president of Northern Foods. He said: 'I founded the firm. So many people knew me that I couldn't really have been ignored. I don't think there will be quite the same goodwill with Lord King.' Other life presidents include Lord Bernstein of Granada, Sir Reo Stakis and Lord Forte of the eponymous hotel chains, Lord Laing of United Biscuits and Mr Richard Branson, life president of Virgin Music, now part of Thorn-EMI. Mr Branson - whose other company, Virgin Atlantic, is suing BA - said yesterday: 'It's probably sub judice to say it, but I wish Lord King well in his retirement.'
