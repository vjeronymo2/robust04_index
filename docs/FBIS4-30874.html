BFN [Text] Podgorica, May 31 (TANJUG) -- It has been officially estimated that the Yugoslav republic of Montenegro has sustained a loss of over five billion dollars over the past two years of the international blockade. Montenegrin Prime Minister Milo Djukanovic has said that this is the rough estimate of both direct and indirect losses caused by the sanctions. He added, however, that there were also losses which cannot be measured by statistical figures. According to the federal government, the international blockade has over the past two years inflicted on the Yugoslav economy a direct loss of 35 billion dollars, which, together with those caused indirectly, amount to 100 billion dollars. On May 30, 1992, the U.N. Security Council introduced draconic economic sanctions against the Federal Republic of Yugoslavia (Serbia/Montenegro) because of its alleged involvement in the civil war among Moslems, Serbs and Croats in neighbouring Bosnia-Herzegovina. Most Montenegrin firms are operating at intervals and below their rated capacities. It is estimated that one-third of the total 130,000-strong workforce has been laid off with token pay due to the slashed production rate. Montenegro's most vital and profitable industries, such as tourism, maritime economy, iron and steel industry and non-ferrous metal industry, have been severely crippled by the international blockade. Montenegro's fleet, which formerly accounted for about 20 per cent of the annual foreign currency revenues and for about five per cent of the total revenues, has found itself on the verge of collapse. The fleet's 37 ships, with 977,000-tonne cargo capacity, is blocked in harbours throughout the world, for which Yugoslavia has to pay a lay-over tax amounting to over 50 million dollars a year. The sanctions have especially aggravated the humanitarian situation in the republic, as the impoverished economy bears the brunt of providing not only for its 615,000 citizens but also for more than 50,000 refugees in Montenegro. Over the past two years, Montenegro has on several occasions urged the U.N. Sanctions Committee to approve exports of alumina, which would enable the purchase of necessary foodstuffs and medicines, but none of the requests have so far been approved.
