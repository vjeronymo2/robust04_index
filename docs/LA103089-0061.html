October 30, 1989, Monday, Home Edition

L.A. SCHOOLS EXPLORE ERA OF SHARED POWER

At Bell High School, they're working on ending "squawk box" interruptions over the public address system. At Micheltorena Elementary, they drew up a new lunch and recess schedule. And at Norwood Street Elementary, they tackled the irksome question of who gets to use the copying machine.

In about 100 schools throughout the Los Angeles Unified School District -- and soon on the mammoth district's 500 other campuses -- newly elected local councils of teachers, parents and administrators are beginning to change the way decisions are made regarding the basics of daily school operations.

Despite some initial complaints, the councils are producing surprising results.

"I love it that they (the councils) are making decisions that would be dictatorial if they came from me," said Norwood Principal Angie Kasza.

The new system is called "shared decision-making," a method of decentralizing power that is being tried in an increasing number of school districts across the country. It is taking shape in many forms.

In Chicago, an arrangement put into effect this fall gives parents significant power over local schools. In other cities such as Los Angeles, Santa Fe, N.M., and Rochester, N.Y., teachers are being given the dominant voice.

In Los Angeles, educators say the councils signal a badly needed revamping of public education for the nation's second-largest school system, which has 610,000 students. Educators across the country are watching how Los Angeles fares as it enters a largely uncharted area of educational reform -- one that says schools will not improve unless teachers have a major hand in running them.

Giving more power to teachers sounds good, said Stanford University education professor Michael Kirst, "but there's not much basis for predicting how it will work."

So far, the experiment has not been trouble-free. In several Los Angeles district schools, allegations of electioneering and failing to provide adequate information have marred the elections of parent and community representatives.

For example, at Belvedere Elementary School in East Los Angeles, district and teachers' union officials delayed counting ballots until charges of ballot tampering and intimidating voters could be resolved.

Voter turnout at many of the year-round schools was abysmal; in some instances there were more candidates than people who showed up to cast ballots.

Once elected, many parents have shown only spotty attendance. And after only a few meetings, some parents, feeling outnumbered and harboring years of frustration over low-performing schools, say they already are disillusioned.

"It's power for principals and teachers," said Janice White, a parent representative at Main Street School in South-Central Los Angeles. "It alienates the parents and community at our school."

Under the terms of the contract with United Teachers-Los Angeles, forged after last May's teacher strike, principals no longer can make unilateral decisions in five specific areas: continuing education programs for teachers; student discipline guidelines; scheduling of final exams and other school activities; spending of certain monies, such as lottery funds; and use of school equipment, such as the copier. Those decisions will be made by the council through a consensus process, with a vote being taken only as a last resort.

The councils range in size from six to 16 members, depending on school enrollment. Half of each council must be made up of the local UTLA chapter chair and other teachers, while the remaining seats are filled by the principal, parent and community representatives and other school employees.

Teachers at all schools chose their representatives over the summer. District and union officials said the election of more than 2,000 parent and community representatives will be completed in November.

Campuses that operate year-round because of crowding were the first to form councils. These schools started their academic year in July. Councils at the district's other schools are expected to begin meeting soon.

At Norwood, a year-round school near downtown Los Angeles, one of the first items on the agenda was to review the use of the school's only copying machine, which is operated by clerks and administrators. Teachers raised the issue because they were frustrated about a school policy that said they had to submit copying requests 24 hours in advance.

The council reviewed the cost of running the machine, which Principal Kasza said amounts to $18,000 a year for the lease, maintenance and supplies. It decided to start a system for filling emergency copying requests so teachers could have copies made of lesson materials in the morning for use the same day. But the council imposed a limit, allowing no more than a daily total of 60 copies per teacher.

"I found that when they analyzed the cost of operating the machine, they were horror-stricken. They decided to restrict the use of it rather than open it up," Kasza said. "I wouldn't have had the nerve to make that decision. And you know what? It's working."

Michael Romo, a Norwood second-grade teacher and council co-chair, described the new policy as an important morale booster. Teachers have long complained that they are victims of bureaucratic tyranny, with their powerlessness to control a piece of equipment as mundane as a copying machine a symbol of their lowly status.

"I know when the average person reads in the newspaper about teachers wanting access to the Xerox machine, they think it's ludicrous. . . . But we won the right in our contract to make these decisions," Romo said, and teachers will demand a voice in setting

more momentous policies in the future.

"We'll never go back to the point of accepting things from the top blindly. We're going to question things," he said. "(Teachers) keep coming up to me and asking, 'Is this the way things are supposed to be done? Can we change things and do things better?' I think it's great. It shows we are professionals."

At Bell High, a year-round school in the city of Bell, the council has turned its attention to alleged abuses of the school's public address system.

Bell teachers said the P.A. system too often is used to make "inane" announcements, such as reporting prom queen election results or the license plate of a car left with its lights on in the school parking lot. This disrupts classes, which are too short to begin with, they say.

"Why should 2,400 students be jolted out of what they are doing to hear someone say 'Will so-and-so please call the office,'?" music teacher Ken Salter said at a recent council meeting.

After a lengthy discussion, the council agreed to turn the matter over to a subcommittee to draft guidelines. Principal Mary Ann Sesma agreed on the condition that a student would be included in the group.

Then the council took up the matter of student discipline, a topic that provoked critical comments from several of the parent and community members. One member told the council of her children's complaints that school was boring. Another asked why the school hadn't contacted her about her son's truancy problem.

"The outside community has the feeling that this (shared decision-making) isn't going to work," said Pat Quiles, a retired maintenance administrator for the city of Los Angeles whose four children attended district schools. "After all these years, there has been no (improvement in) achievement. So why do this now?

"The record shows that our schools are down in the bottom as far as achievement levels go," Quiles said in an interview.

Social studies teacher and council co-chair Beverly Cook said winning the parents' trust is going to be a challenge. "They blame us and want us to cure it all," she said.

Principal Sesma agreed. "I'm trying to get people to think about where the school is now -- and where they want it to go. They have to create a vision of what they want to see in the school five years from now."

Some say that it is hard to envision profound gains in school performance when the councils' authority is limited to such concerns as copying machines and when final exams should be given. But the councils will have a chance to demonstrate their need for more sweeping powers next year when district and union officials will begin inviting proposals for "school-based management," the next phase of the restructuring plan outlined in the teachers' contract.

In Miami, changes undertaken by school-based management teams have included reorganizing the school day to create longer instructional periods, for instance, and hiring Berlitz instructors to teach foreign languages.

Helen Bernstein, a UTLA vice president who is a co-chair of the districtwide central council on school-based management, said Los Angeles teachers are beginning to talk about such changes as combining primary grades into cooperative learning groups, personalizing large schools by breaking them up into several mini-schools, and providing child care on campus for students who are already parents.

School-based management is the revolution that will turn around low-performing schools, Bernstein said.

For now, teachers, principals and parents say, they are struggling to learn the basics: how to set up a school calendar, how to read a budget and boning up on district policies. And they are trying to get along without the adversarial roles to which many of them had grown accustomed.

"It's a little awkward now," said Lucille Derrig, principal of Miles Avenue School in Huntington Park. "I'm used to making decisions sometimes without having to consult other people. Now I have to think twice about moving too fast in certain areas.

"And it's a little awkward for teachers, too," she added, "because they need background information" before they can make decisions.

For many parents, the challenge is to become equal partners with administrators and faculty.

"I feel that shared decision-making is a great idea," said Larry Galvan, a Bell High School parent member. "But you know what? It's not going to succeed unless the others remember the parent. There are some of us who do know what we are doing."

BACKGROUND

School districts across the country are looking for new ways to decentralize authority and raise academic performance. One promising strategy is "shared decision-making," which involves giving greater power to teachers, parents and administrators in individual schools. Some experts say it could lead to dramatic improvements in the way schools operate.
