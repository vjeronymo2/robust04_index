March 18, 1989, Saturday, Home Edition

MANAGUA FREES 1,894 SOMOZA GUARD VETERANS

Nearly 10 years after their army collapsed in defeat in the Sandinista revolution, 1,645 former national guardsmen walked out of a prison Friday in a mass pardon aimed at ending the U.S.-backed rebellion against Sandinista rule.

In a ceremony resembling a high school commencement, the prisoners stood in formation on a hot, wind-swept basketball court at the Modelo Prison as their names were called. One by one, they stepped forward to collect certificates attesting to their freedom with full rights of citizens.

Then, while loudspeakers blared recorded folk music, ropes separating the guardsmen from their families dropped, setting off a scramble of tearful reunions. Some of those freed dropped to their knees in prayer.

'Shout With Joy'

"Finally, this nightmare is over," said Hazel Maraz Soto, 26, one of the youngest prisoners, embracing his mother. She exclaimed, "I want to shout with joy that my son has returned!"

Counting 249 prisoners under house arrest or home on furlough, the pardons covered 1,894 people imprisoned for service in the army of the Somoza family that ruled Nicaragua for 43 years until the Sandinista takeover of July, 1979.

Thirty-nine guardsmen remained behind bars, judged by the Sandinistas to be guilty of serious war crimes or defiant prison behavior. The pardon did not touch any of the 1,400 or more jailed Contra suspects, whose freedom has been promised when the Contras lay down their weapons.

The prisoner release, the largest in Nicaragua in two wars spanning more than a decade, was the first step taken to comply with President Daniel Ortega's commitment under a month-old Central American peace accord, which also promises elections in Nicaragua by next Feb. 25.

In return, the region's four other presidents agreed to help draft by mid-May a plan for closing the Contra military camps in Honduras, a step that would further cripple the idled rebel army.

Ortega has said the Bush Administration is "acting against" the regional accord by saying it will seek as much as $50 million in non-military aid from Congress to hold the rebels together in Honduras for one more year as a potential threat to force Sandinistas to live up to the accord.

To demonstrate his compliance, Ortega brought the secretary general of the Organization of American States, Joao Baena Soares, and foreign diplomats to the prison ceremony 14 miles east of Managua.

In a later gathering in the capital, Ortega told diplomats and opposition party leaders that he will soon send the National Assembly a series of proposed changes in the electoral law and the law governing the news media, with the aim of assuring open and fair elections.

He said the changes will be based on suggestions offered by 14 opposition parties in talks with him in recent weeks, but he gave no details.

Calling the prisoner release "a transcendental act" that shows "the generosity of the revolution," Ortega appealed to Washington to limit any new Contra aid to the advancement of the regional peace pact -- the rebels' disarmament and repatriation to Nicaragua or relocation in third countries.

Rescheduled Date

The prisoner release had been scheduled for Sunday but was moved forward on two days' notice when Sandinista officials realized that international attention would be distracted by Sunday's elections in El Salvador.

Opposition leaders and some former guardsmen said the government seemed more concerned about the public relations impact outside Nicaragua than the needs of those freed to feel accepted as useful members of society.

Indeed, debate over the pardons did little to heal the wounds of the Sandinista uprising that left an estimated 10,000 people dead in 1978 and 1979.

Trepidation Voiced

One guardsman on the pardon list, Ramon Valle Arancibia, was the object of a last-ditch citizens' campaign to keep him locked up. A mothers group accused the ailing, 59-year-old prisoner of having taking part in a June, 1979, massacre in a Managua neighborhood and driving the police bulldozer that scooped the bodies into a mass grave.

"If I see that man on the street, I'll stone him, I assure you," declared Angela Hernandez de Lorio, 55, whose son died in the incident. Valle was freed anyway.

As they left prison Friday, most guardsmen claimed to be victims of unjust prosecution for guilt by association. Some expressed varying degrees of trepidation over returning to a society still tightly ruled by the one-time guerrillas who defeated them.

Less than an hour after his release, Gustavo Mendoza, 32, looked nervously over his shoulder and said he was afraid that Sandinista agents were following him. "There is no security for us," he said.

A more typical outlook was expressed by Rodolfo Salmeron, 28, a former National Guard officer from the mining town of Siuna, who said he was glad to be alive.

"This is historic," he said. "I think it's hard to find this kind of pardon in other countries. I never thought I would get out because first they told us that all the guards were going to die. But God never abandoned us, and now we're free. Since I never did nothing to anybody, I don't feel the slightest fear of going home."

In random interviews with 20 freed guardsmen, eight said they might leave Nicaragua because they feel uncomfortable under Sandinista rule or want better job opportunities. All but one said they were interested in moving to the United States. The U.S. Embassy said there is no special consideration for freed guardsmen in its strict immigration rules, although many guardsmen who escaped before the Sandinista take-over or were freed later now live in the United States.

The latest batch of guardsmen is being thrown onto a shrinking job market amid a severe economic crisis and sharp cutbacks in public spending. Many said they were worried more about finding work than about avoiding reprisals.

"I think about leaving, but if they give me a chance to work, I would rather stay," said Juan de Dios Herrera, 33, who was trained in prison as a carpenter. "I would feel much better with a place in the new society."

The newly freed prisoners will have several sources of assistance.

Mario Arevalo, an official at the Modelo Prison, said 220 of them will continue working there, as they did as prisoners, in such jobs as making boots for the Sandinista army, repairing government vehicles and building public housing.

The International Red Cross gave every freed prisoner a modest wardrobe to go home in -- the first civilian clothes many had worn in a decade. It also arranged their bus transport home, temporary lodging for about 40 guardsmen with no known relatives in Nicaragua and food deliveries for four months.

The Church of God, a Pentecostal sect active inside the prison, will offer former guardsmen job placement in rural cooperatives, according to the Rev. Pedro Pablo Castillo, a former National Guard lieutenant colonel who left the jail in a 1985 pardon. Seven inmates who left Friday will work for the church as pastors, he said.
