February 7, 1989, Tuesday, Orange County Edition

DORNAN CREDITS THE PEOPLE WITH KILLING U.S. PAY RAISE

Members of Orange County's congressional delegation Monday applauded Speaker Jim Wright's decision to let them vote on an unprecedented pay raise. And all five said they intend to vote against the measure when it reaches the House floor today.

"It was a great victory for representative government," proclaimed Dana Rohrabacher (R-Lomita), whose 42nd District includes a portion of west Orange County. Rohrabacher, like the other four members in the county delegation, all Republicans, credited the public with forcing today's House vote on the pay increase.

"The American people raised their voices to the point they could not be ignored," he said in a telephone interview from Washington. "It's just too bad that the American people have to scream until they are hoarse."

Rep. C. Christopher Cox of Newport Beach said his staff in recent weeks has received more than 1,000 phone calls and letters opposed to the pay raise.

The proposal calls for raising the annual salaries of members of Congress from $89,500 to $135,000.

The pay raise proposal had stemmed from recommendations made by a presidential commission. Besides the raise for members of Congress, it would increase the pay for federal judges and top executive branch officials. Rep. Ron Packard of Carlsbad, whose 43rd District covers much of south Orange County, said he would have preferred that Congress allow the salary increase to take effect and then rescind it so judges and top federal officials could have "gotten a pay boost that's long overdue."

But now, "the whole thing is dead," he said.

Packard also contends that a defeat of the pay raise will "harden the resolve" of congressional members to keep lucrative honorariums received from outside speaking engagements and appearances. With no chance of improving their base pay, Packard said, members will be reluctant to give up outside sources of income.

"We didn't deserve the pay raise," said Rep. Robert K. Dornan (R-Garden Grove). "But don't limit our right to moonlighting. . . ."

Dornan defended honorariums as a legitimate way "to make ends meet." He challenged the public and press to determine whether a lawmaker is abusing his office by accepting too much outside money.

Cox was sharply critical of how the entire pay raise issue was handled. A former White House attorney who won election in November to the 40th District, Cox said he already had a "low opinion" of Congress when he took office in January, and the attempt to push the pay raise through without a vote "knocked that opinion down a few more notches."

"People were definitely exercised over this," Cox said. "I took one call from a woman who complained that we have our mistresses and we have our bribes and now we want more of the taxpayers' money."

Dornan said, "The boss simply wouldn't go for it."

In this case, the "boss" is the American people, who, Dornan said Monday, became fed up with watching "the employees try to give themselves a raise" and created a furor that has all but defeated the controversial pay package.

Though crediting the public's role in forcing the issue, Cox also praised the efforts of Rep. William E. Dannemeyer of Fullerton. Dannemeyer, using his experience and understanding of House rules, introduced a resolution that unexpectedly called for the vote on Monday, a move that caught Wright by surprise. It forced the House Speaker to set the vote for today.

Dannemeyer could not be reached for comment Monday night, but a spokesman for his office, Paul Mero, said the lawmaker had contemplated taking Monday's action for several days.

"He just believed that someone has to take a stand and get this into the open," Mero said. "It's a sad commentary on Congress that this thing was sailing through without a vote."
