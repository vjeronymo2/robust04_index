BFN [by Taib Mahjoub] [Text] Doha, 3 May (AFP)--Israeli and Arab negotiators clashed here Tuesday [3 May] on the first day of talks on Middle East disarmament aimed at halting the spiralling billion-dollar arms race in the region. More than 40 states, including Israel and most Arab countries, were taking part in three days of multilateral discussions behind closed doors on a draft arms limitation deal for the region. But trouble flared almost immediately as the Arab and Israeli delegations differed over how the talks should proceed, officials said. A Western delegate said the head of the Israeli delegation, defence ministry director-general David Ivry, insisted "straight away that the session should adopt the draft, provoking Arab reticence." An Arab delegate said: "Certain Arab participants, including Saudi Arabia, saw the proposed text as a political statement which has nothing to do with arms limitation." The text specifies that arms limitation "must in no way come at the expense of one nation or provide advantages to another," and it must be approved "with the unanimous support of all parties concerned." Under the terms of the 1991 Madrid peace conference which launched the disarmament talks, the draft declaration of principles on disarmament also includes a reference to "a reduction in the arsenals and personnel of the armed forces" in the region. And it stresses that the Middle East should become an area where "weapons of mass destruction will be eliminated." UN and EU envoys, as well as representatives of the United States and Russia, the co-sponsors of the Middle East peace process, were also attending the conference in the Qatari capital, held under tight security. A Western delegate said: "The Arabs believe we must first agree on the elimination of weapons of mass destruction, while the Israelis insist the priority is to establish confidence-building measures in the region." The Arab countries first want Israel to agree to practical measures on arms control, which should cover Israel's nuclear weapons capability. Israel has vehemently denied that it possesses nuclear weapons, but Western experts have estimated it probably has up to 200 warheads. "The fact that a state in the region possesses a nuclear strength perpetuates tension," said Qatari Foreign Minister Hamad Bin-Jasim Bin-Jabbur Al Thani. He urged Israel to submit its nuclear potential to international supervision, and called on it to sign the Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty. "Political stability in the region depends on an arms balance between Israel and the Arabs," Al Thani said. A Western delegate said Israel "considers its technological superiority in defence matters is designed to ensure a balance in the region, as the Arabs have such great advantages in terms of population and territory." In a bid to narrow Arab-Israeli differences, Western countries have proposed setting up collective structures including a crisis prevention centre to improve understanding and promote cooperation, a delegate said. Russia has also suggested opening a regional military data bank "to exchange information on the state and position of forces and military manoeuvres in the region," another delegate said. Ivry is heading a 21-strong team, the first to visit Qatar since the creation of Israel in 1948. Informal talks between Arab countries and other participants are due to be held Wednesday on the sidelines of the main meeting.
