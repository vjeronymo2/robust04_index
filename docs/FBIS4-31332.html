BFN [Text] Bucharest ROMPRES, 2/6/1994 -- Appreciative statements about Romania's position to the crisis in the Yugoslav space made by several high-ranking officials of the Federal Republic of Yugoslavia have been clustered by the daily newspaper DIMINEATA in its today's issue. Radoje Kontic, prime minister of the Federal Republic of Yugoslavia, said that Romania's position relative to the complex situation in the area of former Yugoslavia, including its stance in the question of the economic sanctions instated against Serbia and Montenegro are remarkable by their being "poised, objective, and principled." Romania, he said, "has and is playing a realistic and constructive role in the crisis affecting former Yugoslavia," by making itself available any time to be one of the initiators of negotiated settlements pursuing the suspension of the economic sanctions and as a country which, after Yugoslavia, has been most affected by these. Momir Bulatovic, president of the Republic of Montenegro, showed that between the Federal Republic of Yugoslavia, of which Montenegro is a constituent part, and Romania the relations have always been friendly and cooperative in various fields. "We are happy that now, in the difficult situation we are confronted with, we have found in Romania and its people feelings of true friendship and full understanding. We hope for an as fast as possible settlement of the crisis in our zone, so that we can resume these relations," Bulatovic said. The prime minister of Montenegro, Milo Djukanovic, assessed that once the sanctions suspended, the conditions would be put in place for a direct Romanian-Montenegro cooperation, for the mutual benefit of the sides, in sectors like tourism, machine-building, ecology, and agriculture. Under the current circumstances, as Milan Grubic, director of the press department of the Yugoslav Foreign Ministry remarked, in its relationships with all states, Romania included, Yugoslavia makes efforts to reach a balance between each country's role and interest in the zone of former Yugoslavia. The best experience gained to this effect was in relationship with Romania, Grubic said, the ties with this country being "an example of communication between two good neighbors. We have never been in conflict, we have been bonded by true friendship. In our relations with Romania, we started from the principle that `ideologies are transient, peoples permanent.'"
