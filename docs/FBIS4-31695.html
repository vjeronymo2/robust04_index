BFN [Interview with Nedim Gendzhev, leader of the Democratic Party of Justice, by Nataliya Vuchkova; place and date not given] [Text] [Vuchkova] Dr. Gendzhev, is the Democratic Party of Justice ready with its program and, if elections are held, among which parties will it seek a coalition partner? [Gendzhev] Our party will have a leftist orientation. We can work in coalition with both the Aleksandur Stamboliyski Bulgarian National Agrarian Union and the Bulgarian Socialist Party [BSP], despite the fact that some people will not like this. The party's program is social democratic in nature and close to the BSP program. The Union of Democratic Forces, which claims to be a democratic party, has nothing to show the people apart from a pronounced primeval anticommunism. However, this will not help us emerge from the economic crisis, which can only be achieved by national consensus reached by making compromises. If anyone analyzes the activities of the Bulgarian Parliament during the last two years, he will reach the conclusion that Bulgaria has no Bulgarians who think about their state, only anticommunists and sodomists. [Vuchkova] Dr. Gendzhev, do you think your party will capture votes from the electorates of the Movement for Rights and Freedoms [DPS] and Mehmed Hodja's party? [Gendzhev] We address ourselves to all people, regardless of their party affiliations or official position. We want to develop an economic program for the regions that are in the most wretched situation. The present government is a DPS one, but not one that represents the DPS electorate; it is a government of Ahmed Dogan, Osman Oktay, Yunal Lyutfi, Kadir Kadir, and a handful of others. More than a half of the electors who voted for the DPS at the last elections will now cast their votes for the coalition that we will form. However, one thing is clear -- namely that from now on we will not enter any coalition with the DPS or Mehmed Hodja. [Vuchkova] How will you react to possible accusations that you formed a party on an ethnic basis? [Gendzhev] One should not make such speculations if a Turk or Muslim stands at the head of a party. I believe that, in this case, one cannot say that our party is religiously or ethnically based. [Vuchkova] Would you like to comment on the recent practice of identifying religious with ethnic features. [Gendzhev] One cannot identify the Muslim religion with Turkish nationality. Armenians, Bulgarians, Turks, and Romanies may all be Muslims. Jean-Jacques Rousseau, who was both a Frenchman and a Muslim, did not identify himself as a Turk, did he? It must be made clear that Islam is a world religion, not a Turkish one. A degree of Turkification may indeed be taking place in certain regions, but the more attention you draw to a problem, the more it comes to the forefront of public attention. When they made the Muslims change their names and closed the mosques, did this turn them into Bulgarians?
