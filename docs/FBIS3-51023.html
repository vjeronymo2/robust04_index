Article Type:BFN [Editorial Report] The following is a compilation of reports on Mexican economic developments monitored through 11 January. Source is cited in parentheses following each item. The Secretariat of Commerce and Industrial Development has imposed a 323-percent tax on the importation of shoes from China. This concludes the investigation into unfair trade practices in China, which determined dumping practices were in effect. (Mexico City Radio ACIR Network in Spanish 1624 GMT 5 Jan 94) According to the Mexican Association of Banks, discount rates on businesses affiliated to credit charge companies have been reduced. The discounts will be based on the annual sales of establishments and credit card sales amounts. Those establishments with higher sales will be benefited the most. Commercial establishments with annual sales of over 300,000 new pesos will pay a 2.25 percent rate, on condition that average sales amount to more than 500 new pesos. (Mexico City Radio ACIR Network in Spanish 0200 GMT 6 Jan 94) The agreements Mexico signed with the United States and Germany to avoid double taxation on income taxes went into effect on 1 January, the Secretariat of Credit and Public Finance has reported. (Mexico City XEW Television Network in Spanish 0430 GMT 6 Jan 94) The Central Bank of Mexico reported on 9 January that Mexico registered an 8-percent inflation rate in 1993. This is the first time in more than 20 years that the inflation rate in Mexico is a one-digit figure. According to the Bank of Mexico, inflation totalled 11.9 percent in 1992, 18.8 percent in 1991, and 29.9 percent in 1990. (Hamburg DPA in Spanish 0058 GMT 10 Jan 94) The Bank of Mexico has reported that 1994 began with good prospects for economic growth in Mexico. The slowdown in the United States, Mexico's main trading partner, has ended. Therefore, Mexican exports are expected to increase. Interest rates have recently declined, thereby promoting a greater level of economic activity. In sum, national finances are clean following the problems in the 1980's. The public debt only represents 32 percent of GDP. (Mexico City Canal 13 Television Azteca Network in Spanish 1300 GMT 11 Jan 94)
