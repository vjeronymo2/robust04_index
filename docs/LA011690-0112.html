January 16, 1990, Tuesday, Home Edition

PANEL TO HEAR WAYS TO CHANGE THE AHMANSON;

STAGE: THE COMMITTEE WILL MEET TODAY TO CONSIDER PROPOSALS TO RENOVATE THE THEATER AFTER 'PHANTOM' CLOSES.

With the box office booming for "The Phantom of the Opera," the Ahmanson Theatre may not look as if it needs to be fixed.

Today, however, a committee will listen to proposals for how to make the Ahmanson a better theater, after "The Phantom" has become a mere phantom.

That might not happen for a long time. A "Phantom" spokesman said "there is no sign of its slowing down" in Los Angeles and no date for its departure. Still, the theater's managers want to be ready when the big day arrives.

The theater has been criticized for years -- by actors and audience members as well as critics -- for the effect of its size and acoustics on spoken drama.

The Ahmanson Foundation contributed $100,000 to pay for the first formal proposals of what to do with the theater. Three architectural firms and one London-based theatrical consulting firm will make presentations today, and a London theater manager also has been asked for his advice.

If renovation plans proceed, the ultimate goal is not quite clear.

Actor and committee member Charlton Heston, who has appeared in eight shows at the Ahmanson, is so adamant about improving it for spoken drama that he wants to remove the orchestra pit altogether -- "I don't think we'll ever do musicals again in the Ahmanson," he said.

The spoken-drama advocates would like to see the 2,071-seat auditorium reduced -- and all of the proposals, said Heston, would provide for a degree of convertibility, so that the theater could change its seating capacity, depending on the occasion.

Within the proposals, said Heston, are plans that could shrink the hall to as few as 800 seats, or expand it to 2,400. He favors a capacity of 1,000-1,200 -- assuming, that is, that a larger house isn't required for the blockbuster musicals.

Yet committee member Charles Schneider, chairman of the Music Center Operating Company (MCOC), cautioned against assuming that musicals will disappear along with "The Phantom," pointing out that the next London musical spectacular, "Miss Saigon," probably could bring a lot of money into the Music Center.

"We've got people coming to the Music Center (for 'Phantom') who have never been there before," he said, "and while they're there, they might buy a ticket to the Taper, the Chorale, the Philharmonic." The main problem with leaving the Ahmanson open for long-running "Phantom"-like extravaganzas, he said, is figuring out where to put the Ahmanson subscription audience, which has been diverted to the Doolittle Theatre in Hollywood for the duration of the "Phantom."

The committee that will review the proposals is led by TV producer Douglas Cramer, a member of the Center Theatre Group (CTG) board. It also includes five other members of the CTG board, two other officers (besides Schneider) of the MCOC, and Robert Ahmanson of the Ahmanson Foundation.

"We have no commitments for financing" beyond the $100,000 spent on the proposals, said CTG President Lawrence Ramer. "We couldn't go out and seek money until we knew how much the ballpark figures are (in the proposals)."

Said one of the architects who has prepared a proposal: "The question we all have is: How real is it? There is no commitment that anything will happen." Another of the architects, however, predicted that "big, exciting changes" are in store for the Ahmanson.

Those who will present their ideas at today's meeting include:

Ellerbe Becket, the architectural firm that is descended from the firm of the late Welton Becket, the Music Center's original architect. None of the individuals who worked on Welton Becket's original plans will be involved in the firm's latest proposal, and the company's design principal, Lou Naidorf, said he is open to the idea of changing the original design: " A lot of improvements need to be made, and we think they can be." However, he added, "for the most part, the city has been pleased with the Music Center, and it's natural that we should come back in" for a remodeling proposal.

Hardy, Holzman, Pfeiffer -- a New York-based firm with a Los Angeles office which has done work for Los Angeles County Museum of Art, the Los Angeles Public Library, and theaters in Eugene, Ore., Anchorage, Alaska, Denver and Brooklyn.

Barton Myers, a Hollywood-based architect who worked for 20 years in Canada and recently designed the Cerritos Community Arts Center. "I understand I am still controversial with some Music Center people," said Myers, because of his preliminary study for what was to become the Music Center's new Disney Hall. Myers suggested placing the building at the top of the Civic Center Mall, to the east of the Center, instead of the location that was selected to the south of the Center. If Myers' Ahmanson proposal is selected, he plans to do the work in conjunction with Theatre Projects, a London-based theater design consulting firm, which also was invited to submit its own independent proposal to the committee.

The above-mentioned Theatre Projects, which has advised on the planning of theaters in 28 countries, including seven national theaters, Cerritos Community Arts Center, Whittier College Performing Arts Center and the theaters within the new Escondido civic complex. President Lou Fleming said his company offers "specialist expertise that isn't available in most architectural firms. In the final analysis, we would end up advising one of the other three (architects)."

In addition, advice was solicited from Ian Albery, the managing director of London's Shaftesbury Theatre and past president of the Society of West End Theatre. Albery said he was asked because of "the work I've done in adapting existing large theaters." He worked on the rehabilitation of eight West End theaters, he said.

None of those who will submit proposals, each of whom will be paid $20,000, would discuss the details of their proposals before the meeting.
