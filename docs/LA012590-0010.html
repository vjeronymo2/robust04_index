January 25, 1990, Thursday, Home Edition

FOOD AND COMMON SENSE: UNEXPLAINED HEADACHE COULD BE TRIGGERED BY MANY NUTRITIONAL FACTORS

It might be a dull, diffuse sensation or a brain-splitting migraine, but nearly all of us suffer from the occasional headache.

Chronic or severe pain could signal a serious problem and requires medical evaluation. Fortunately, most minor headaches disappear spontaneously or respond to non-prescription pain relievers. Sometimes, though, neither scenario applies: No medical cause can be found, yet the ache recurs.

If you're plagued by unexplained headaches, one place to look for clues is your diet. At first glance, the list of possible food factors appears long. In fact, however, food holds the answer in very few cases.

That said, it's true that nutritional factors can precipitate either type of headache, more commonly the dull, diffuse kind. When such pain occurs regularly in late morning or late afternoon, the most likely cause is simple hunger. Some people, particularly children and pregnant women, are more sensitive than others to the minor drop in blood sugar that is normal between meals. Skipping meals can make the headaches worse.

The solution? Eat regularly, and keep meals and snacks balanced. Too many refined starches and sugars to the exclusion of lean-protein sources leads to rapid release of the hormone insulin. Insulin normally works to move nutrients from the blood into the cells. But when it's released too quickly, the brain may not receive enough nutrients, and a headache can result. Low-fat cottage cheese and fresh fruit or part-skim mozzarella on whole-grain crackers are excellent snack choices for people susceptible to this sort of pain.

Another source of headaches is iron-deficiency anemia. Women and children are the likeliest victims, since their iron requirements are higher and deficiency is more common. In anemia, oxygen-transport by the blood is impaired, and insufficient oxygen to the brain translates into headache. If anemia is suspected, a blood test can verify the diagnosis.

Remember, though, that iron supplements can be dangerous and should not be taken without medical supervision. In most cases, anemia can be avoided by eating foods naturally rich in iron, such as lean red meats, chicken and dried beans. Consuming foods rich in Vitamin C (oranges, peppers or broccoli, for example) along with a meat-containing meal improves absorption.

You might have noticed that many over-the-counter pain relievers contain caffeine. Migraine headaches are often caused by dilation, or relaxation, of blood vessels to the brain. Caffeine causes these vessels to constrict, thus relieving many headaches, particularly migraines.

Unfortunately, the body can become dependent on caffeine. Any change in coffee-drinking habits -- either more or less than usual -- can trigger a headache. If you suspect caffeine is the culprit, try weaning yourself away from coffee, tea and colas. Decaffeinated teas and coffee provide an alternative and are probably safe in reasonable quantities.

For a few people, reactions to certain foods and food additives can start that horrible hammer tap-tapping in the skull. Preservations such as sodium nitrite and nitrate, commonly used in packaged meats, may affect sensitive individuals. Others may react to a food dye called tartrazine, or yellow No. 5, found in many yellow and green foods, among them cake mixes, gelatins and candies. Also, the flavor-enhancer monosodium glutamate (MSG), best known for use in Chinese foods, can produce symptoms nicknamed "Chinese restaurant syndrome," which include a dizzy, throbbing headache.

If you consistently notice a headache after eating foods with these additives, you can easily avoid them. According to Food and Drug Administration regulations, all three must be listed by name on food labels.

Besides food additives, certain foods contain naturally occurring compounds to which some people are sensitive. Foods aged or "ripened" through bacterial action may contain tyramine, a substance related to the building blocks of proteins, which the body converts to a potent blood-vessel constrictor. Like caffeine, vasoconstrictors sometimes relieve headaches but can also cause them. People taking certain drugs to treat depression are especially vulnerable to the effects of tyramine, but it may trigger headache in others who aren't on medication.

Foods high in tyramine include aged cheeses such as Cheddar and Brie, wine and beer, yeast extracts, breads high in yeast, figs and eggplant. Another compound, phenylethylamine, found in chocolate, cheese and alcoholic beverages produces vasoconstriction that can lead to headache.

Keeping a record of food intake and headaches can help you and your physician determine whether diet plays a role. Bear in mind that dietary factors are not a major cause of headaches: Unless you can find a clear link, don't burden yourself with needless diet modifications.

All that will do is create more stress -- probably the last thing you need.

Duck Registers With More Calories Because of Saturated Fat Content

Question: Over the holidays I prepared duck for the first time, and everyone welcomed the change. But now that the applause has died down, I'm wondering how duck compares to chicken in terms of saturated fat.

Answer: Duck has more. For example, 3 1/2 ounces of dark-meat chicken without the skin contains between 9 and 10 grams of fat, depending on whether it's a boiler or a fryer. Of that, about 2 1/2 grams is saturated fat. The same portion of duck, by contrast, contains about 11 grams of fat, of which over four grams is saturated. In other words, the two types of birds provide roughly comparable amounts of fat, but the amount that is saturated is quite different.

Here's another way of looking at it. In the chicken, 43% of the calories come from fat, compared to 50% in the duck. And of the total calories, 12% of those in the chicken but more than 18% of those in the duck are from saturated fat.

Goose is even fattier than duck, but contains about the same proportion of saturated fat. Three-and-a-half ounces have over 12 1/2 grams of fat, of which 4 1/2 grams -- accounting for 17% of the calories -- are saturated.

Q: Will eating a high-fiber diet prevent diabetes?

A: Scientists are trying to find out, but so far the evidence is unclear. Several studies of the relationship between fiber intake and prevalence of diabetes have found the disease less frequent in groups on high-fiber diets than among those who have consumed little. Examining changes in dietary patterns revealed that as fiber intake declined, diabetes became more common. However, other studies have found no such effect.

Why the inconsistent results? Perhaps the answer lies in differences -- dietary, environmental, genetic and socioeconomic -- among the various groups that have been studied.

Some studies have looked at the effects of fiber supplements on those who already have diabetes. It is clear that some water-soluble fibers can help control blood-sugar levels in diabetics. But how these observations relate to preventing the disease in the first place is uncertain.

Results of most, but not all, studies in which high-fiber foods rather than supplements have been used have demonstrated benefit too. But because these diets differed in other aspects, it is difficult to separate the exclusive effect of fiber. And once again, it's impossible to determine how the observed effect ties in with prevention.

On the other hand, studies of animals who are genetically susceptible to diabetes show that fiber helps delay onset of the disease, giving support to several links between diabetes and fiber intake in humans.

The bottom line is that even without more proof of the fiber-diabetes connection, there are plenty of good reasons to consume whole grains of various types, and generous amounts of raw and lightly cooked fruits and vegetables, and dried beans.

Q: Recently I entered the hospital for some minor surgery. As I lay in bed recuperating, I found myself watching the intravenous fluid drip and wondering how the procedure originated. Can you tell me?

A: Intravenous therapy was first used in Great Britain during the cholera epidemic of the early 1830s. Physicians of that era generally did not understand fluid balance. They treated their already dehydrated patients with blood-letting and other methods that only aggravated the problem and hastened their demise.

The man thought to be the first to suggest that adding fluids would be a more rational treatment was William Brooke O'Shaugnessy. Dr. O'Shaugnessy was a native of Limerick and a graduate of the University of Edinburgh. He originally reported his ideas on the restoration of fluids to the Westminster Medical Society in December, 1831.

A year-and-a-half later, the earliest report of the use of injections of water with added salts appeared. Of the 25 cases recorded around that time, eight recovered. Today we might not call this a medical breakthrough, but at the time it was remarkable, especially since it was used only in patients who were dangerously ill.

Yet it took decades before the treatment came into widespread use. One stumbling block was that unsterile and chemically impure water caused serious problems. In a recent article in Lancet, Dr. J.E. Cosnett observed that while intravenous therapy was a rational idea, it appeared before our understanding both of the body's chemistry and of microbiology were sufficiently advanced to use it well.

Ironically, neither the names of Dr. O'Shaugnessy nor of Dr. Thomas Latta and Robert Lewins of Leith, who were the first to try the treatment, are found in any major work on the general history of medicine.
