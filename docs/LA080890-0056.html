August 8, 1990, Wednesday, Home Edition

DR. NEIL SOLOMON: DORMANT VIRUS

Question: My brother has come down with shingles. I realize this is not major news, but he never had chickenpox, and I'm positive I've been told that you're not supposed to be able to get shingles unless you first had chickenpox.

Is there an explanation for this? And why does one depend on the other?

Answer: It is true that a person can't have shingles (herpes zoster) without first having had chickenpox. In your brother's case, I can only guess that he must have had chickenpox, but no one realized it at the time. This would be possible, for example, if he had the virus but no clinical symptoms.

Once a child is infected with the varicella zoster virus, which causes both chickenpox and shingles, he remains infected with it. Following a case of chickenpox, the virus lies dormant. Why it remains latent and why it becomes reactivated are not known. What is known is that the incidence of shingles increases with age, and seldom occurs before middle age.
