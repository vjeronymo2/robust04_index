BFN [Text] Kingston, 2 Jun (PL) -- Jamaican Prime Minister Percival Patterson has confirmed, here before Parliament, that his government will allow the United States to use Jamaican territory to process Haitians intercepted on the high seas. The next group of Haitians detained on the high seas will be taken to Jamaica, where U.S. vessels anchored in the ports of Kingston, Port Antonio, and Montego Bay will be converted into floating platforms to conduct interviews and to provide temporary shelter for those seeking asylum. Reports from Washington note that the U.S. Navy hospital ship "Comfort," with a capacity of 1,000 beds, has sailed from Norfolk, Virginia, for Kingston and that another vessel, flying the Ukrainian flag, may also leave Tampa en route to Kingston. Labor leader Edward Seaga voiced opposition to the government's decision. Although he stressed he supported Bill Clinton's change in policy of not returning the Haitians without a preliminary hearing, he said it was unacceptable to use Jamaican territory to do this. Patterson disclosed that on Wednesday, U.S. and Jamaican specialists worked out a bilateral memorandum of understanding to outline each side's responsibility and in which the White House has promised to pay all operational expenses for the six months Jamaica has granted. Some observers believe strong pressure has been exerted on Jamaica, which opposed this idea at the beginning, but the presence of a high-level delegation proved that the report was not merely media rumors. The delegation included U.S. Under Secretary of State Strobe Talbott; William H. Gray, President Clinton's special adviser on Haitian affairs; and eight other officials. Several analysts believe there is a consensus among members of the Caribbean Common Market (Caricom) on the commitment they must make as a regional organization to resolve the Haitian crisis and to help President Jean-Bertrand Aristide return to his post as well as to cooperate in reestablishing democracy in the neighboring country.
