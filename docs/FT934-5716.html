931129 FT 29 NOV 93 / More than a conduit, insists Adams: Adams calls Mayhew a liar Sinn Fein yesterday rejected the British government's contention that secret contacts with Sinn Fein had been made through intermediaries in response to an IRA wish to end the conflict. Mr Gerry Adams, the Sinn Fein president, said at a news conference in West Belfast yesterday that the contact amounted to 'more than a mere conduit'. He said that meetings had been approved by Mr Major, were known about at cabinet level, and involved several senior civil servants. Mr Adams disputed Sir Patrick Mayhew's explanation that the IRA had considered the conflict over. He said: 'At no point was there any communication from the IRA that the conflict was over.' Mr Adams accused Sir Patrick of lying about the contacts between the government and Sinn Fein. He said outlines of policy had been exchanged and discussed. He indicated that he was preparing to publish material exchanged between the two sides, saying that it was of the utmost importance to point out that the issue of talking to Sinn Fein was a distraction. Talks at this time were not necessary to move towards a lasting peace. 'This required the British government to fulfil its responsibilities. John Major knows what he needs to do. He knows that the Irish peace initiative deals with the substantive issues and includes the principles, the dynamic and the process required to bring peace to Ireland. To use the words of John Hume, 'Mr Major holds the key'.' Mr Adams said the government was only interested in protecting its own position and that Mr Major had no serious interest in conceding to the Irish people their right to a peaceful future. He insisted the contact was between Sinn Fein and the government and not the IRA and the government. He confirmed it pre-dated February but said, contrary to what Sir Patrick Mayhew said: 'This particular phase of the contact was initiated by the British government. Mayhew is telling lies from beginning to end.' He said Sir Patrick and Mr Major had denied knowing anything that was in the Hume-Adams initiative, denied on numerous occasions that there were any contacts at all between the government and Sinn Fein and insisted there could be no contact without pre-conditions while they were at the same time actively engaged in contacts. 'The record shows quite clearly all of these things are lies and are proved to be lies.' Mr Adams added: 'They have lied to their own parliament, to James Molyneaux (Ulster Unionist party leader), and to the Irish and British people about the existence and nature of their contact with Sinn Fein.' He said the government had for some time set its face against a real peace process. It had refused to make the change of policy required to 'move all of us forward into a negotiated settlement. In the course of our contact with their representatives they rejected and walked away from a peace process'. A Sinn Fein spokeswoman said yesterday, however: 'The main point is not about who said what and when. This is a distraction from the main goal of laying a basis for getting this war over and done with.'
