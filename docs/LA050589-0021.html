May 5, 1989, Friday, Home Edition

CLOTHESLINES: WEAVING OLD JACKETS INTO NEW WARDROBE

Question: Now that women's clothes are less mannish, what do I do with my two blazer jackets that have served me so well? One is a navy Anne Klein II and one is a beige Mary Ann Restivo. Both are in good-to-excellent condition. I'm a 39-year-old working mother with lots of demands on my paycheck and can't afford to throw out good clothes. -- O.P.

Answer: If you can give your jackets vest-like closings, as illustrated here, you'll be right in step with spring/summer fashion. The jacket in our sketch is Vogue 7422, in case you need a pattern. If you sew or have someone who sews for you, check out Very Easy Very Vogue 7335, which offers three versions of vests, with and without lapels. These are real, sleeveless vests, and they can be worn in lieu of jackets over shirts, blouses or turtlenecks or, when summer strikes, over nothing but skin. A lot of people in the fashion industry believe that the vest will replace the T-shirt this summer as the top top for hot weather.

Q: I will graduate from college this June and go immediately to work in a law office. My college wardrobe is totally unsuitable, and I need advice on how to start building a work wardrobe. Please tell me how to start from scratch. -- E.M.K.

A: If you can get by for the first week with college leftovers, wait to begin your new work wardrobe until you've seen what other young women in the office wear. Or, better yet, if your boss is a woman, follow the lead of Melanie Griffith in "Working Girl," who worked her way up by mimicking her boss (Sigourney Weaver).

Your first major purchase should be a jacket -- one with a vest-like hemline, as mentioned above -- or any jacket without a collar. Cardigans look especially fresh right now. If you choose a fabric such as a beige washed silk, you will be able to wear it with any color skirt, and you'll soon find that dark skirts are much more practical than light ones.
