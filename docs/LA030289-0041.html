March 2, 1989, Thursday, Home Edition

ERMA BOMBECK: FOR SALE: ANYTHING AND EVERYTHING

Nowhere in the world will you find people with the same creative work ethic as Americans.

At the age of 4, one of my sons was going through the neighborhood dragging a little red wagon with our canceled checks in it. He was selling them for a penny a package.

In the years that followed, all three of our kids went from selling watered-down lemonade to baby-sitting to delivering newspapers. As teen-agers, they were paid an allowance to breathe and to put gas in our own car, which they had driven to E in the first place.

I personally taught them how to drop money each week into a ceramic pig on their nightstand. They taught me how to get the money out of the ceramic pig by inserting a kitchen knife in the slot and sliding the coins out to pay the paperboy. The message was set: You are never too young or too rich to make a few bucks if you use your imagination.

We may have overdone it.

Everything in this country is for sale. (What am I saying? The country is for sale.) You can rent out your bull for a stud fee. You can sell your seat at the Super Bowl and take an early retirement. You can pay someone to send your daughter cookies at college or write her a letter on her birthday. There's a man in Syracuse who will scoop dog droppings from your yard for $5 a week. If you have a need for $21,000, you could sell Napoleon I's toothbrush. A farmer in Oklahoma has offered the Soviet Union $1 million worth of wheat in exchange for a seat on a Soviet space flight. The name of the game is supply and demand.

I didn't realize how far we had drifted until I read about a letter that appeared in the New York Times. A guy was standing in a line at the fish counter holding number 997. Number 793 was being served. As he stood there, a guy in a black leather jacket approached him and asked him what number he had. "For 10 bucks, I'll give you 873," he said. When the writer refused, he said: "How about 25 bucks for 840?"

"You're scalping tickets at the fish counter?" the shopper asked in shock.

"Hey, pal," Mr. Black Leather Jacket said, "time is money."

The story brought to mind a crusty old editor I used to work for in my early reporting days in Ohio. He stormed into the women's department one day and demanded to know why I had used a trade name for a soft drink in a story I had written. The tirade lasted an eternity. As the entire newsroom strained to listen, he ended by shouting, "Don't give it away when you can sell it."

I didn't realize so many people heard him.
