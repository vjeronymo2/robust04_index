BFN [Text] Tokyo, May 8 KYODO -- Prime Minister Tsutomu Hata appointed a lower house member Hiroshi Nakai of the Democratic Socialist Party [DSP] as justice minister early Sunday morning, replacing Shigeto Nagano for his controversial remarks over the 1937 Nanjing massacre and Japan's role during World War II. Emperor Akihito is scheduled to attest Nakai's appointment at 10 A.M. Sunday at the Imperial House. Prime Minister Hata explained his views on World War II to the new justice minister, saying that Japan's act of aggression as well as its colonial rule inflicted great pains on a number of people, according to Chief Cabinet Secretary Hiroshi Kumagai. Hata asked Nakai to conform to the prime minister's position, Kumagai added. At a press conference early Sunday morning, Nakai said, "it is an undeniable fact that (the former Japanese Imperial Army) conducted cruel acts," when he was asked about the Nanjing massacre. Political observers said that Prime Minister Hata apparently picked the new justice minister from the DSP in a bid to appease mounting criticisms against the Shinseito [Japan Renewal Party]- controlled cabinet in the wake of Nagano's controversial remarks. Nagano is a member of Shinseito, a key political party of the coalition. The observers added Shinseito intends to show its remorse and responsibility over party member Nagano's remarks by handing over the ministerial post to the DSP. Nagano, 71-year-old former chief of staff of Ground Self-Defense Force who served for only 10 days as justice minister, submitted his resignation to Prime Minister Hata shortly after he returned from a six-day tour of four European countries. Hata accepted Nagano's resignation and named a successor from the DSP, a party in the ruling coalition, after about three hours of consultations with Chief Cabinet Secretary Kumagai and other members of the cabinet. The 51-year-old Nakai, deputy secretary general of the DSP, is the youngest justice minister in postwar Japan. Nagano apologized to Hata for the trouble caused by his remarks and vowed personal action to show that Japan will not start a war again, Nagano said after meeting Hata. At a press conference following his resignation, Nagano reiterated his apology, saying "(My remarks) caused great shocks and angers among peoples in neighboring countries." Yoshiro Mori, secretary general of the largest opposition Liberal Democratic Party (LDP), called Nagano's resignation a "matter of course," saying the remarks spoiled the diplomatic efforts the LDP made to improve ties with neighboring nations as the ruling party for 38 years until last August. Wataru Kubo, secretary general of the Social Democratic Party [of Japan -- SDPJ], former largest force in the coalition, said Nagano's resignation is only natural as it damaged the Japanese Government's international integrity. Both the LDP and the SDP are ready to question the Hata cabinet over Nagano's war remarks in Diet deliberations slated to begin on Monday. Nagano said in an interview published Wednesday in the major daily MAINICHI SHIMBUN, "The Nanjing incident was a fabrication." He also said, "I think it is wrong to define the war as a war of aggression (by Japan)... Still, I doubt the Greater East Asia War, as it was called in Japan, was aimed at aggression." The remarks immediately drew harsh criticisms from China, North and South Korea and other Asian countries, making Nagano to retract his remarks and apologize at a news conference Friday. The Nanjing incident refers to the massacre of a great number of Chinese by the defunct Imperial Japanese Army troops following the invasion of the then Chinese capital in December 1937.
