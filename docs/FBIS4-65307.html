BFN [Text] Vienna, May 14 KYODO -- The International Atomic Energy Agency (IAEA) will send a five-member inspection team to North Korea on Sunday [15 May] to conduct a weeklong inspection of a radiochemical laboratory in Yongbyon starting Tuesday, an IAEA spokesman said Saturday. The IAEA needs the additional inspection now that North Korea has started preparatory works for exchanging spent fuel rods at its 5-megawatt nuclear reactor in Yongbyon, some 90 kilometers north of Pyongyang, the IAEA spokesman said. North Korea notified the IAEA of the start of preparatory works in its letter on Friday, he said. If North Korea commits a serious violation of the nuclear safeguards agreement, such as peeling seals off the fuel rod container, the team may suspend inspections, the spokesman said. IAEA sources said the international nuclear watchdog could determine if North Korea has exchanged fuel rods by monitoring records from cameras and counters installed at the nuclear reactor. The Yongbyon complex has been the focus of allegations that North Korea is pushing a clandestine nuclear weapons program. In February, the United States and North Korea agreed to resume high-level talks on condition that Pyongyang allows full inspections of its seven declared nuclear sites. But North Korea in March denied IAEA inspectors' access to the laboratory in Yongbyon, considered crucial in verifying whether fissionable radioactive material had been diverted to make nuclear bombs. On March 1, the United Nations Security Council adopted a presidential statement calling on North Korea to allow full international inspection of its nuclear facilities. This time the IAEA inspectors will conduct necessary checks at the nuclear facility, including radiation measurement, the spokesman said. The IAEA has repeatedly insisted that if North Korea goes ahead with the fuel rod exchange at the nuclear reactor without the supervision of IAEA inspectors, it would constitute a violation of the nuclear safeguards agreement. South Korea's YONHAP NEWS AGENCY said in a dispatch from Seoul that North Korea told the IAEA it is regrettable that Pyongyang had to start changing fuel rods without IAEA inspections. North Korea, however, made clear that there is room for negotiations, saying that there are a lot of opportunities as North Korea has just recently begun replacing the fuel rods, according to the YONHAP report. The U.S. has also said that it would suspend a third round of high-level negotiations with North Korea if it changes the fuel rods without U.N. inspectors present. North Korea said Thursday that the replacement of the uranium fuel rods in the Yongbyon reactor, which was started up in 1986 and is almost exhausted, is a very urgent matter that cannot be deferred indefinitely. The IAEA maintains that the sampling and analysis of the fuel rods would provide information on the North Korean nuclear program and any past activities in retrieving plutonium from spent nuclear fuel.
