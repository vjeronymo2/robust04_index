March 18, 1989, Saturday, Home Edition

AMERICANS SPOKE THEIR MINDS IN 4-DAY 'SUMMIT' AT THE VATICAN;

LACK OF CLOUT IN VATICAN PUTS U.S. CHURCH AT A DISADVANTAGE

Pope John Paul II excused himself briefly from a meeting last week with American archbishops to accept the credentials of the new Ethiopian ambassador to the Holy See, and to deliver a 3,000-word speech of encouragement to six visiting Chilean bishops.

During John Paul's four days with the Americans, the gigantic institution he heads announced a major economic reform package and proclaimed a breakthrough for religious freedom in Lithuania. As the American bishops headed home, the Pope met with 7,000 relatives of Italian missionaries and visited a Roman parish. While the returning American prelates prepared for a press conference in Washington, he was receiving bishops from Burkina Faso and Brazil.

As the papal focus shifts away from the extraordinary meeting with American church leaders, it is left to the Vatican bureaucracy to follow up on the issues. Veteran Vatican observers say that over the next few months the church's Curia, or administrative arm, will seek to accentuate trends already under way to assert greater central authority over the maverick Americans.

Some Curia moves will be apparent and others unseen, the observers believe, but all will come in the context of the way the Vatican views the independent-minded American church.

As seen by the European-dominated Curia, the church in the United States is both wealthy and important -- often lamentably so -- as an international trend setter. It is, to be sure, large, with 53 million members. But it is also small: It amounts to only 6% of the world's Catholics.

In asserting its positions against Vatican traditions of patient jockeying and unwavering respect for hierarchical authority, the observers say, the American church will be at a disadvantage because of its disproportionately weak clout at the Vatican.

Little Power

One ailing American cardinal heads the Vatican equivalent of a ministry. Three American bishops direct lesser dependencies, but the most powerful among them, the head of the Vatican banks, is being ousted.

The scarcity of Americans within the Curia to translate the peculiarities of their outspoken countrymen to the glacial Vatican bureaucracy contributes to the mutual cultural incomprehension that was the hallmark of the summit, the observers believe.

"America is a 'marketplace society' where ideas have to sell themselves on an intrinsic merit. That this would conflict at times with the hierarchical nature of the church is not surprising," noted St. Louis Archbishop John L. May, president of the National Conference of Catholic Bishops.

In the aftermath, issues that triggered brotherly differences of opinion at the meeting will likely be the focus of Vatican housekeeping initiatives, the observers believe. The sources, who requested anonymity, expect the Vatican to seek:

* A more rigorous selection of American bishops to assure support for basic doctrines such as the Vatican ban on contraception. The Vatican believes that some of the current bishops may be better administrators than pastors. It is also concerned about what it considers the theological inadequacy of some of them. Although cardinals and bishops are expected to have advanced degrees in some theological sciences, many American prelates lack them. Theological expertise will weigh more heavily in a selection of future bishops and cardinals, the Vatican observers believe.

* A tighter rein on theologians to ensure orthodoxy. Vatican critics blame iconoclastic Catholic theologians unchecked by their bishops for divisive crosscurrents within the American church. The Vatican has made clear that it will not tolerate theological dissent on central issues and expects the bishops to correct theological aberrations. In recent years, the Vatican believes, some American bishops have virtually ceded teaching authority to their theologians. The Vatican wants that stopped. The bishop, above all, says the Pope, must himself be a teacher and must know what he is talking about.

* Better theological and philosophical preparation in American seminaries. There is no controversy on this point: Americans and Curia cardinals agree with professors at church universities here and in the United States that American seminarians are generally far behind their European peers in philosophical preparation.

* A slowdown in the granting of marriage annulments. American marriage tribunals grant about 75% of the annulments worldwide, a figure that sticks in the craw of a Curia foursquare behind the indissolubility of marriage. The American bishops vigorously defended their tribunals at the summit but in the process invited greater Vatican inspection of their operations.

If he is distressed by some of what he finds in the American church, and by the materialistic society in which it preaches, there is also a lot that John Paul admires about the dynamism of American Catholicism.

Still, it is but one church among many churches in a global constituency of 850 million Catholics. And, as Vatican loyalists are quick to note, John Paul has equal responsibility for all of the churches, and all of the people. Indeed, one of the most challenging parts of the Pope's job is that he must teach different things to different peoples with equal authority.

Unyielding Preacher

For some Americans and Europeans, John Paul may seem principally a stern disciplinarian, a moralist supreme and an unyielding preacher of unpopular doctrines. Simultaneously, though, he is an outspoken crusader, the poor man's friend who demands decent living standards for African peasants, social justice for Brazilian slum dwellers, with freedom of worship for Lithuanian shipbuilders, and an end to racism everywhere.

That this Polish Pope of all the Catholics has reservations about some practices of the American church, so rich, so free, is more predictable than exceptional; so, too, that the bishop of Rome and the heir of St. Peter will strive relentlessly to put his house in the order he prescribes.
