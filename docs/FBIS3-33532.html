Language: Spanish Article Type:BFN ["Exclusive" article by Carlos Fazio: "Drugs: Over Those Roads in the South"] [Text] On 9 February the U.S. Government disclosed part of its new antidrug strategy. It stressed the importance of treatment and prevention of drugs at home, as well as alternative economic development and strengthening of the institutions charged with combating drug trafficking abroad. In theory, and regarding the international component of the new strategy, there seems to be a change of course. Robert Gelbard, U.S. assistant secretary of state for international drug trafficking affairs, has said that while in the past a special effort was made to intercept and seize drug shipments destined for the United States, the current plan is aimed at cocaine-producing countries, combining eradication of coca plants with self-sustained development. In principle it sounds good. The only catch is that this is the strategy's publicity portion. During the past few years the drug trafficking issue has been at the core of relations between Washington and Latin America. In its development this issue has been defined by the White House as a national security problem and in fact has gradually become a threat to the internal and external security of the rest of the area's countries. During the two most recent Republican administrations (Reagan- Bush) the concept of "drug trafficking" occupied the central spot in the new sociological language on drugs. Stress was put on the external nature of this "malignant" problem. The core of the problem was thought to consist of narcotics trafficking and transportation. In other words, the danger was thought to stem from the supply, which the Reagan-Bush administrations regarded as a threat to U.S. social and even institutional structures. Since the first Reagan administration, political-strategic logic ruled out the drug phenomenon's economic and commercial rationale. In the cause-effect diagnosis, ideology replaced pragmatism. With the hobbyhorse of "morals," the emphasis on solutions was expressed in words like "combat, war, and crusade." The Pentagon said it was necessary to combat the problem "at its origin" -- production and processing centers -- with highly coercive and repressive methods. This argument, which failed to underline the importance of demand as the cause of the supply -- and which therefore concealed the situation's commercial and financial reality -- evidently had a political objective, that is to say, to transfer the cost of the fight to the producing countries. Roughly, this has been the story of the failed "war on drugs" of the past three Republican administrations, with three additions. Drugs have also invaded a very specific area -- counterinsurgency. Pentagon and Department of State publicity fanned the notion of "narcoterrorism" in the framework of a counterinsurgency policy, which has to do with the new standard that the U.S. Department of State seeks to impose on the subcontinent at the end of the Cold War. During the Reagan and Bush administrations, the most prominent U.S. proposal to the Latin American drug-producing countries was to create a coercive regional counternarcotics force, with extraterritorial jurisdiction which could operate without the limitations imposed by sovereignty at local borders. In view of the "coca for development" Andean strategy -- encouraged by Peruvian President Alberto Fujimori and Bolivian President Jaime Paz Zamora -- Washington implemented a policy stressing military aspects. Bush, both president of the world's largest narcotics-consuming country and the epitome of contradiction, avoided facing the problem at home and pressed to mobilize the Armed Forces of the "drug countries" into carrying out essentially police tasks. In addition, the Pentagon succeeded in establishing some posts in area countries, particularly Peru and Colombia. Two examples are Santa Lucia military base in the Upper Huallaga in a Peruvian coca- producing zone controlled by the Shining Path, and a base in Leticia, Colombia in the Amazon area of the border with Peru, where 500 U.S. soldiers are stationed. Although Bill Clinton's speech now emphasizes the antidrug struggle at home and the budget allocation for the external front has been reduced, part of what he failed to say when he explained his strategy makes it possible to conjecture that the new president will continue to apply some of the former policy's central components through the militarization of producing countries. It is no mere chance that there are U. S. military engineers in Colombia, Ecuador, Bolivia, and Guatemala in the framework of the "Caminos del Sur/94" exercise. Their alleged tasks hint at the nature of their specific assignments, such as training in Guatemala, training and radar site installation in Colombia, and participation in the local fight against drugs in Bolivia, Ecuador, and Colombia. These are functions usually assigned to the police. In reality, the Pentagon has spent a great deal of time and money on these actions and has succeeded in involving the CIA and DEA. For this reason, we can assume that President Clinton was carried away by the momentum of the circumstances. While he says one thing in his speeches, in practice he follows and improves on previous strategies in Latin American countries.
