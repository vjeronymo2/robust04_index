August 5, 1990, Sunday, Orange County Edition

COUNTY PLANNING: PATTERN OF BLUNDERS;

UNSEEMLY ACTIONS, LAND-USE FUMBLES ARE DISTURBING

This is turning out to be a year when the public is learning some very disturbing things about how business is done with and by the Orange County Planning Commission.

We've heard about planners riding in the golf carts of developers, and seen the district attorney investigate allegations that some commissioners had a hand in a fund-raiser for a political candidate. There is the continuing saga of the Laguna Niguel land transfer, a kind of summer mystery novel whose recent chapters strongly suggest that it was county planners who dropped the ball.

All of this inevitably affects public perceptions, raising doubts about the competence and fairness of the process for all. Just ask the new homeowners in Laguna Niguel who now find that they have bought houses on 96 acres supposedly set aside for open space.

Can others like them make real estate choices confidently when they know that county commissioners are taking freebies from developers and have fumbled away public land? The Laguna Niguel case adds questions of staff thoroughness to those already raised about commissioners' cozy relations with developers.

The land transfer is not all the county's fault, of course. There are problems at the local level that the district attorney is investigating. The questionable stroke of the pen by James F. Krembas, formerly Laguna Niguel Community Services District vice president and now Laguna Niguel councilman, is inadequately explained by his statement that he believed it was part of another transaction.

But it has also become apparent in the weeks since the scandal broke that county planners fell down much earlier in their obligation to the public. This failure unquestionably invited the current mess, with or without the compounding error of Krembas.

The quality of communication between county planning staffers and county planning commissioners on matters of vital interest to the public is a central point in the Laguna Niguel fiasco, as far back as 1985. We now know, from the curious explanations offered by key players, that Planning Commission staffers recommended that the commission approve a revision proposed by Taylor Woodrow Homes, without pointing out that the houses would be built on designated open space, and without requiring a new environmental impact report.

The former manager of that staff work has offered the lame explanation that because there were no rare or endangered species on the site, and because traffic and the number of units were projected to be the same, there was no need to raise the open-space question with commissioners.

But don't just blame the planning staff. It's the responsibility of approving commissioners to anticipate problems that may be raised by staff reports on previous agreements, such as the one that was in place for the Laguna Niguel land. The public could have been spared all the trouble by a skeptical commission that asked whether there were any problems with the land. But commissioners appear to have waved the revision through.

This year's revelations raise important doubts about the approaches of Orange County planners to land-use questions. Until the public is assured of better communication between commission and staff, and until there is some sense that commissioners keep their distance from developers, homeowners and those who care for Orange County's environment are right to rest uneasily.
