May 6, 1990, Sunday, Home Edition

ENDING U.S. AID TO EL SALVADOR

The editorial "El Salvador -- a Big Question" (April 17) goes a long way toward recognizing the futility and the immorality of U.S. intervention in El Salvador. Yet in the end it accepts the logic that has kept U.S. arms aid fueling the war for the last decade.

"Without outside aid," you say, "El Salvador's war-ravaged economy would collapse." But for most Salvadorans, with the notable exception of the oligarchic and military elite, the economy already has collapsed. More than half the adult work force is unemployed, and living standards have plummeted.

Against the aid cutoff proposed by several congressional leaders, you argue that such a move might tempt "leftist rebels to try and strike a knockout blow." But it is clearly the hard-line right in the government and the army, and not the Farabundo Marti National Liberation Front (FMLN), that would prefer a military solution at this point.

A cutoff of U.S. aid would help produce progress toward a negotiated political settlement, with concessions made by both sides.

But even if, as seems unlikely, an aid cutoff were to bring about the outright collapse of the Cristiani govennment, its replacement would most likely be a coalition of all the democratic opposition forces on the center and left, including the Christian Democrats, the social democratic parties, and the FMLN. Such a reformist government would be a vast improvement over the nightmarish regime now in power.

Until we back up our professed concern for human rights by halting all arms aid, the war will continue and the negotiations under way now in Geneva will achieve little.

MICHAEL J. DAVIS

Executive Director, El Salvador

Institute for Democratic Development

San Francisco
