BFN [Text] La Paz, 28 Apr (EFE) -- Bolivian President Gonzalo Sanchez de Lozada has ordered military troops to the streets and national highways to control public order and guarantee free transit, in view of possible road blockades by peasants. The Bolivian Labor Federation (COB) has urged peasants to block roads, highways, and railroad tracks as of today to increase pressure on the government to yield to their salary demands. Bolivian main cities were today under military control, while several military units were patrolling highways to stop peasants from complying with the COB appeal. Although the government has expressed its willingness to negotiate with union leaders, it has warned that it is very difficult to amend the budget law which provides for an 8 percent salary increase because "there is no flexibility in the allotment of resources." Military and police officials have reported that all roads were cleared during the first 12 hours of the day; but they admitted that several roadblocks were set up at different locations. However, Radio Fides in La Paz reported that peasants have complied with the COB instructions to block roads and railroad tracks. Peasant Leader Juan de La Curz Villca asserted that about 30,000 members of the coca leaf farmers union in the El Chapare region have set up roadblocks on the main highway linking Cochabamba and Santa Cruz. Villca warned that the producers of coca leaves, a traditional product for Bolivian indigenous groups that is also the basic ingredient for manufacturing cocaine, have reported "they will not lift the roadblocks, not even under machine gun fire." Union leader David Herrada asked the members of the Armed Forces and the police to join the blockade in Cochabamba. He said: "If we take another piece out of this neoliberal government it will also benefit the police and the Armed Forces, who also have low salaries, like the workers." The prefect (governor) of Chuquisaca, Omar Montalvo, confirmed that the military unit patrolling Sucre and the access roads will break up any demonstration and prevent any road blockade. A Sucre radio station reported that about 3,000 peasants closed roads in five of the 10 districts of Chuquisaca. In addition some buses going to Potosi had problems in arriving in that city due to the blockades set up by the peasants. Several military units have been deployed to other regions of the southern part of the country, where roads and railroad tracks were closed. The FIDES news agency reported that in Camargo, in the southern part of the country, about 500 peasants prevented about 100 cargo and passenger vehicles from passing, and that because of this a two-year-old girl "died from the very cold weather early this morning." Some cargo and bus companies have suspended services while the strike ordered by the COB continues. Despite the tense situation created by the presence of military troops on the streets and the road blockades, government officials and COB leaders are holding talks behind closed doors in an effort to find a solution to the crisis. The appeal made by the COB to the Catholic Church to mediate in the conflict was rejected by Apostolic Nuncio Giovanni Tonnucci, who said that the church cannot mediate "among people who turn a deaf ear to dialogue."
