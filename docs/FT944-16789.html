941012 FT 12 OCT 94 / The Conservatives in Bournemouth: Health officials resist pressure from Treasury The Department of Health is coming under strong Treasury pressure to reduce capital spending on hospitals and community health projects in spite of drastic cuts in the amount allocated for these schemes in recent years. Amid signs that Mr Kenneth Clarke, the chancellor, is preparing big cuts in public expenditure in the Budget, the Treasury has told Department of Health officials to fund more capital spending on the National Health Service with private-sector money. In recent years the Department of Health has had considerable success obtaining capital investment under the government's private-finance initiative for the provision of dialysis centres, food and laundry services. This has led to a cut in state spending on NHS capital expenditure from Pounds 1.093bn in 1993-94 to Pounds 600m in this financial year. As negotiations over this year's spending round come to a close, Department of Health officials are resisting a further cut. They have told the Treasury that the department has already made efficiency savings through proposals to abolish regional health authorities and by keeping pay increases very low. 'We are in favour of private finance,' said a Department of Health official yesterday. 'But we are also telling the Treasury that there are hospitals with leaky roofs and poor facilities which need increased spending by Whitehall.' The pressure on health service spending comes amid signs that Tory activists are increasingly concerned about the impact of some of the reforms to the NHS. Mrs Virginia Bottomley, the health secretary, yesterday ran into tough criticism from constituency representatives attending a fringe meeting. In previous years Tory activists have listened respectfully to Mrs Bottomley's comments at fringe meetings. But this year they issued a range of complaints about management expenditure in the NHS, the closure of an accident-and-emergency department and a cut in medical services for the elderly. Ms Uma Fernandez, a community nurse from Brent in north-west London, told Mrs Bottomley that too many nursing jobs were now being done by people who were not qualified. She said: 'We feel threatened by some of these changes.'
