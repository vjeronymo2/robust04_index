Language: English Article Type:BFN [Text] New Delhi, March 19 (AFP) -- Calls for recognition of the Dalai Lama's government-in-exile and support for the Tibetan freedom struggle resounded at a global parliamentary conference on Tibet here Saturday. Delegate after delegate voiced support for the cause of Tibetan independence from Chinese rule on the second day of the three-day convention, under way at a venue on the outskirts of the Indian capital. Lord Ennais, a former British minister for home and overseas development and a member of the all-party parliamentary group for Tibet in Britain, urged the international community to extend recognition to the exile government. He also called upon world governments to use their influence in the United Nations to help the Tibetan cause and end what he called the "last vestiges of colonialism." It was the main theme of Saturday's proceedings at the conference, being attended by nearly 40 MPs from 23 countries and an equal number of Indian deputies. Gonzalo Facio, a former foreign minister of Costa Rica and his country's ambassador to the United States, supported the call for recognition of the government-in-exile, based in the northern Indian town of Dharamsala. "Such a recognition by governments will not immediately lead to the freedom of the Tibetan people but it will help them achieve the objective," said Facio. "I am all in favour of it. I think governments will start doing it," he said. The Dalai Lama, supreme spiritual leader of six million Tibetans, has lived in exile in India since fleeing his homeland in 1959 after an abortive uprising against Chinese rule. His government-in-exile is presently recognised by no country. His host India, which is home to some 100,000 of his followers, also treats Tibet as a part of China. But the sentiments expressed at the parliamentary conference reflected the growing world support for recognition of the exile government, said Tashi Wangdi, a Tibetan leader. Delegates also said complete independence was the only solution to the Tibetan issue. "The root of all problems (in Tibet) is the Chinese colonial rule in Tibet," said Norwegian MP Harald Ellefsen. "Bringing an end to that colonial rule will be the only fair and just solution." Latvian representative Jurif Sinka said: "I hope China will find the courage, humility and generosity .. and set the Tibetan people free." Polish delegate Henryk Wujec urged the Tibetans to struggle like the Polish Solidarity group had done against the Communist regime in Warsaw. Leif Bergdahl of Sweden suggested that the convention send a delegation to Tibet and compile a report on the situation in the Himalayan region, overrun by Chinese troops in 1949. The conference is being held in the shadow of the Dalai Lama's warning to China earlier this month that he may be forced to bow to hardline elements within the Tibetan movement if China does not respond to his moderate approach. Tibetan delegate Karma Chophell said the Tibetans wanted nothing less than total freedom. "We are not seeking any autonomy. this should be very clear to everyone," he said. Chophell said the Tibetans had realised after 14 years of contacts between the Dalai Lama and the Chinese authorities that Beijing was only biding time and was not interested in a solution. "The final solution for all problems in Tibet is the complete independence of Tibet," he sad. Deputies on Saturday also broke up into separate groups to discuss alleged human rights abuses in Tibet, environmental degradation and a perceived threat to Tibetan culture posed by a Chinese influx.
