BFN [Text] The prime minister has arrived in Washington for wide-ranging talks with President Clinton. During his stay in the U.S. capital, Mr. Narasimha Rao will meet the decision makers and exchange views on bilateral and international matters. He will address the joint meeting of the U.S. Congress today. The Rao-Clinton talks tomorrow are expected to break new ground in bilateral relations. This will be their first meeting and also the first high-level contact between the two countries since the end of the cold war. Expansion of economic cooperation and better appreciation of each other's stand on issues like nuclear nonproliferation are likely to be the focus. Indian officials say that the Clinton Administration wanted the summit meeting to cover the situation in Russia, the role of the United Nations in peacekeeping, and the Nonaligned Movement. Shortly before leaving for Washington, Mr. Narasimha Rao said in Boston that an important purpose of his current visit is to invite America to join the Indian efforts for speedy industrialization and infrastructure development. Delivering the (Joe Deddy) memorial lecture at the Harvard University, the prime minister said true North-South cooperation demands collaboration between the developed countries which have a huge research and development base and the developing countries. He also called for development of new technologies that can result in creation of maximum number of jobs at minimum cost. The (Joe Deddy) memorial lecture established in 1955 is for the promotion of tolerance, understanding, and goodwill among the nations, particularly the East and the West. Rajiv Gandhi and V.S. Naipaul were the prominent personalities who delivered the lecture in the past. The prime minister spoke at length about the Indian scene and the economic reform process. He said globalization of economies should be on the basis of partnership and should be a smooth affair. Amid lusty cheers from the audience, Mr. Rao declared that despite occasional clashes and agitations India will remain united with its unmatched attractions. The former U.S. ambassador to India and noted economist, Professor Galbraith, introducing the prime minister to the 1,000 strong student audience said India is America's most enduring friend. Prof. Galbraith said the bilateral relations are deep rooted and the so-called concerns belong to the realm of bureaucrats.
