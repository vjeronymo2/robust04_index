November 25, 1989, Saturday, Home Edition

FACES: A STRENUOUS WEEKEND

Can Brian Kerwin survive this weekend? It's still up in the air.

By Sunday, the actor will have prepared two Thanksgiving dinners, finished memorizing his lines for the one-man "A Poster of the Cosmos" (for the Dec. 11 "Because We Care" AIDS holiday benefit at the Taper), and put in eight 3 1/2-hour performances of "Who's Afraid of Virginia Woolf?" at the Doolittle -- one on Tuesday, two on Wednesday, two on Friday, two on Saturday and one on Sunday.

"After the first rehearsal, I just shook," says Kerwin, who's Nick to John Lithgow and Glenda Jackson's George and Martha.

"For 3 1/2 hours, I'm either being berated or berating someone else. It's also a progressive situation of drunkenness. I walk in the door already loosened up and go downhill from there. By the second show, I really do feel hung-over, a little woozy. I went to a doctor once who said, 'Your body doesn't know you're faking up there.' It's true."

"Cosmos" offers its own Angst.

"Lanford Wilson wrote it to be done at AIDS benefits," Kerwin says of the 25-minute monologue, a man's statement to the police after the death of his AIDS-stricken lover. Although he had been introduced to the piece some time ago by director David Galligan, the call to do it for this benefit came out of the blue -- and right in the middle of his Doolittle run. "I said, 'Sure, I've got all the time in the world.' "

If he survives his theater commitments, the actor can relax and look forward to "Challenger," ABC's upcoming three-hour TV-movie about the 1986 shuttle explosion.

"Of course, there was a lot of sadness," he says of his experience playing pilot Mike Smith.

"But you can't play that tragedy. For them, there was no foreboding. Our movie ends with the countdown, the engines starting -- and the astronauts' faces beaming as they go into outer space." JANICE ARKATOV
