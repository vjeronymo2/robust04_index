November 28, 1989, Tuesday, Home Edition

DR. JOYCE BROTHERS: OK FOR KIDS TO BE AFRAID

DEAR DR. BROTHERS: Recently, our 7-year-old has been watching the evening news with his father, and I've noticed that he's become much more fearful. He's worried about robbers, fires, earthquakes -- you name it. It is normal for children his age to have fears? How many are too many? -- S.S.

DEAR S.S.: Everyone needs a certain amount of fear to protect us from danger. If your son has been watching the news, he's become more aware of what's happening in the world around him. This isn't necessarily a bad thing. While parents need to take time to explain the dangers to the child, they should also let him know that there are lots of good things happening in the world that don't get reported on the evening news because people take them for granted. It's the bad and the unusual that make the news.

If parents are calm, controlled and in command of their lives, children are more likely to feel curious rather than anxious. Children's fears are sometimes a reflection of their parents' anxiety levels.

Dr. Leon Rosenberg, child psychologist at the Johns Hopkins Hospital Children's Center in Baltimore, says being afraid or worried about strangers, bad people, fire and robbers isn't unusual for 7-, 8- and 9-year-olds. If the child is spending any significant amount of time with these worries, if it's interrupting the child's play during the daytime, then the anxiety may be unhealthy. If he's only a little anxious before bedtime, take time to talk about his fears and explore the source, but chances are this is healthy, normal fear.

DEAR DR. BROTHERS: I love my wife despite the fact that she's convinced she is sick, when her doctors and I know that this isn't the case. I have to screen newspapers and magazines before leaving them around the house, because if there is an article about an illness, she's got it and nothing will persuade her that she hasn't. What causes this preoccupation and how can I get her off the subject of her health? -- L.R.

DEAR L.R.: Some hypochondriacs develop the problem as children, often as a way of getting attention from parents or friends. Children discover that the only time a mother or father shows affection is when they have a headache or when they complain about some physical ailment.

Hypochondriacs often come from homes where one parent has been seriously ill for a long period of time or when their life has been threatened by invalidism in the home. A parent may pass on his or her anxieties about illness to the child so that the youngster grows up with a persistent preoccupation with disease.

As for the treatment of this imagined illness, it isn't easy. Most psychiatrists agree with Sigmund Freud that intense, long-term psychoanalysis isn't especially effective in treating hypochondria. Group therapy, behavior modification and drugs to treat the accompanying depression are generally what is advised today. Treating the depression helps the patient feel better. Doctors usually try to manage the patient's anxiety, as this is the dominant emotion with hypochondriacs. This will help your wife.
