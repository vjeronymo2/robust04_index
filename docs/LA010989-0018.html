January 9, 1989, Monday, Home Edition

A YIDDISH TERM EVOLVES

In "The American Jewish Experience" (Dec. 22), Elaine Woo referred to a common but mistaken explanation of the Yiddish term bobe mayse.

First, a bobe mayse is not merely an anecdote about the past but rather an old wives' tale, often of doubtful veracity. Moreover, while most Yiddish speakers believe that the expression derives from the Yiddish bobe (grandmother), it actually has a more literary antecedent: an early 16th-Century Yiddish romance called the "Bove-bukh" ("Bove-Book"), written in Italy. Its author, Elye Levita, borrowed from an earlier Italian source, which was in turn based on the escapades -- in English -- of one Bevis of Hampton. Via the Italian, Bevis became Bove.

Once the Bove-bukh was no longer regular reading among Yiddish speakers, the term bove-mayse (Bove story) acquired its present folk etymology.

JANET HADDA

Associate Professor of Yiddish

Los Angeles
