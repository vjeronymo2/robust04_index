April 28, 1990, Saturday, Home Edition

SHAMIR SEEKS LIKUD GOVERNMENT BUT WON'T RULE OUT A LABOR ROLE

Caretaker Prime Minister Yitzhak Shamir said he is trying to assemble a narrowly based government controlled by his hard-line Likud Party, but he declined Friday to rule out another coalition with the rival Labor Party.

Shamir, who was given the task of forming Israel's next government by President Chaim Herzog on Friday, immediately ran into trouble.

Yigal Bibi, a legislator of the National Religious Party, said he will back Likud only if the center-left Labor Party joins the coalition. The surprise statement brought the two major parties back to a 60-60 deadlock in the 120-seat Knesset (Parliament).

Herzog had turned to Shamir after Shimon Peres, the Labor leader, announced Thursday he had failed to assemble a coalition after 35 days of effort.

Shamir, 74, head of the interim government, said he hopes to obtain a majority within the next three weeks, as required by the presidential mandate. Speaking to reporters, Shamir said prospects for a new Likud-Labor coalition "at the moment look unrealistic."

"I would never rule out the possibility" of a coalition with Labor, he said, "but my efforts in this period won't be aimed at a national unity government."

Likud and Labor ruled for 15 months in an uneasy partnership before their government was brought down March 15 by a Knesset vote of no confidence.

The crisis was prompted by Shamir's refusal to accept, as Labor demanded, a U.S. formula for arranging Israeli-Palestinian talks.

Herzog urged Shamir to act quickly to form a government.

"These days, the country is in a state of political and public confusion such as it has never known since its founding," the president said. "There is a heavy and true sense of depression in the public."

Shamir hopes to lure three right-wing factions and three ultra-Orthodox parties into a majority coalition in the Knesset.
