November 13, 1990, Tuesday, Orange County Edition

KEATING GRAND JURY CONSIDERED OTHER S&L INDICTMENTS

The state grand jury that indicted Charles H. Keating Jr. and three others on securities fraud also considered charging several other employees of Lincoln Savings & Loan and its parent firm with wrongdoing, recently released grand jury transcripts reveal.

But to keep the complex case manageable, prosecutors advised the 22-member panel to limit the indictment to the top officials of Lincoln and Phoenix-based American Continental Corp.

"We want to go after the people truly responsible for this behavior in a way that promises some possibility of success in a time frame soon enough to be meaningful to the victims," Los Angeles County Deputy Dist. Atty. Thomas Papageorge told the grand jurors.

The result was a 42-count indictment in mid-September for alleged securities fraud against Keating, former American Continental President Judy J. Wischer and Robin S. Symes and Ray C. Fidel, both former presidents of Irvine-based Lincoln.

On Friday, Los Angeles Superior Court Judge Lance A. Ito set aside 22 counts of the indictment because many of the charges did not clearly state the alleged wrongdoing. The judge gave the prosecutors until Nov. 19 to rewrite the indictment.

The indictment charges the defendants with falsely stating and failing to state the true risks associated with the sale of $200 million in American Continental bonds to more than 17,000 investors. Those bonds became worthless after the company filed for bankruptcy protection in April, 1989, and regulators seized Lincoln the next day. Regulators estimate that Lincoln's collapse will cost taxpayers more than $2 billion.

The grand jury transcripts, which were released Friday, give a picture of the prosecutor's strategy. The state wanted to keep the indictment narrow to make it understandable to a trial court jury.

Toward that end, prosecutors decided to drop their original effort to seek charges of conspiracy and grand theft by false pretenses because the charges were too complex and difficult to prove, the grand jury transcripts show.

At the closing arguments before the grand jury on Sept. 11, prosecutors came with a proposed indictment in hand naming Keating and three others. They argued against expanding the case to include more defendants or more charges.

One of the grand jurors, Glenn Buchanan of Sherman Oaks, confirmed in an interview Monday that the panel considered including several other Lincoln and American Continental officials in the indictment but finally agreed with the prosecutor's approach.

Buchanan said grand jurors considered bringing charges against American Continental executives Robert J. Kielty, Andrew Ligget and Jack D. Atchison and Lincoln executive Dariush Razavi, a top aide to Fidel. The grand jury discussed indicting at least two others.

While none of the people identified by Buchanan have been indicted, their names appear often in the 4,500 pages of grand jury testimony publicly released. They could not be reached for comment.

Before joining American Continental at a salary of nearly $1 million, Atchison had been a partner at the accounting firm of Arthur Young & Co. in Phoenix and was head of the audit team that produced the last two American Continental financial statements.

Those statements, much criticized by thrift regulators, were largely what the state Department of Corporations relied on in approving the sale of American Continental bonds, even though the state agency's staff had developed a strong case for denying approval of the bond sale.

The grand jury also wanted to delve more into the role of state regulators in the sale of nearly $200 million worth of debt securities at Irvine-based Lincoln's 29 Southern California branches, he said.

Buchanan said a minority of grand jurors wanted to bring in more regulators to determine the state's role in approving the sale of the bonds, but proposals to subpoena them were defeated. A separate investigation by the Sacramento district attorney's office is looking into possible wrongdoing involving state officials.

The grand jury also narrowly voted down a proposal to subpoena U.S. Sen. Alan Cranston (D-Calif.), he said, to testify about his relationship with Keating, former chairman of American Continental.

In August, near the end of the investigation, the grand jury came close to issuing a subpoena for Cranston, Buchanan said, but instead sent a letter simply requesting him to appear. The senator's lawyer responded at the time that Cranston, who was out of the country at the time, was too busy and could not provide meaningful information.
