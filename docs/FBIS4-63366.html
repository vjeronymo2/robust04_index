BFN [By ITAR-TASS diplomatic correspondent Aleksandr Krylovich] [Text] Moscow, 20 May -- If Russia makes its proposals about an international conference on security and the nuclear-free status of the Korean peninsula more concrete, North Korea is ready to study them and reply in concrete terms, Son Song-pil, DPRK ambassador to Moscow, told journalists today. He said: "The attractive side of the Russian initiative is the desire to resolve the nuclear issue in the region through talks, without the use of force and threats, based on the principle of equal rights." According to the ambassador, only methods based on the use of force and pressure have been applied to his country so far. The United States, the International Atomic Energy Agency (IAEA), and certain states have frequently used such methods. Son Song-pil said: Regrettably, Russia has also been unable to refrain from using them, and for a while it toed the line of the U.S. Administration with respect to the issue of nuclear safety on the Korean peninsula. This has caused relations between North Korea and the Russian Federation to cool in the past few years. Now some aspects of their bilateral relations have started to inspire hope, and the present Russian initiative reflects this hope. Answering an ITAR-TASS correspondent's question about the obstacles preventing the nuclear problem from being solved immediately, Son Song-pil spoke primarily about the IAEA's so-called "double standards" when estimating the level of nuclear activity of different states. He said conclusive proof of this is the 70 kg of plutonium that is unaccounted in Tokaymura, Japan, and only recently made public. Given the well-developed and efficient system of monitoring the security of nuclear installations by this international organization, this incident can only signify that the IAEA is "far from being impartial." The North Korean ambassador does not rule out that the plutonium found in Japan is "merely the tip of the iceberg" and that thorough and unbiased inspection could lead to the discovery of other serious Japanese violations of the Nuclear Nonproliferation Treaty.
