Language: English Article Type:BFN [Text] Tokyo, Nov. 30 KYODO -- Japan's plutonium safety standards are so strict that even an unforeseen major accident at the nation's fast-breeder reactor Monju would not endanger nearby residents, the 1993 White Paper on nuclear safety said Tuesday [30 November]. The Director General of the Science and Technology Agency, Satsuki Eda, presented the paper to the cabinet Tuesday morning. The paper includes a special section on plutonium use in advance of Monju's scheduled April 1994 criticality, the moment when the reactor's fuel achieves a chain reaction. The reactor, at Tsuruga in Fukui Prefecture, uses plutonium, which is 260,000 times more radioactive than uranium. The paper states that since plutonium is a byproduct of the burning of uranium, there is no intrinsic difference between the two substances. But the paper notes that the nation's standards for acceptable irradiation levels also pertain to bone and lungs, which absorb plutonium easily. At Monju, for example, the paper said that even if a major accident occurs -- an event it said is technically impossible -- safety measures are so tight that nearby residents would receive less than the allowable levels of radiation.
