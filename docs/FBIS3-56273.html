Language: Russian Article Type:BFN [Pavel Shinkarenko report: "Russia and United States -- Guarantors of Peace. Russian-U.S. Summit Talks Begin in Kremlin"] [Text] The official meeting ceremony between the Russian and U.S. presidents was held in the Kremlin's St. George's Hall yesterday morning. Boris Yeltsin and Bill Clinton then moved to the Green Hall of the Great Kremlin Palace for their first talks with few officials present [v uzkom sostave]. Apart from the heads of state, those taking part in the talks were Dmitriy Ryurikov, aide to the Russian Federation president, and Strobe Talbott, special adviser to the U.S. secretary of state. But before this the two presidents nonetheless stated their attitude to the coming talks. In the opinion of both of them, the dialogue between Russia and the United States will help to strengthen peace and tranquillity worldwide and to deepen cooperation between our countries. The first round of talks lasted 90 minutes -- 20 minutes longer than scheduled. The second round started after a brief recess, which Boris Yeltsin used to show the guest the sights of the Kremlin. This time the talks were held on an enlarged scale. According to information obtained by ROSSIYSKIYE VESTI from both delegations' entourages, the presidents discussed a wide range of issues. In particular, they discussed the results of the 12 December parliamentary elections in Russia, the progress of Russian reform, and problems relating to international security and nuclear nonproliferation. In this regard the sides stressed the importance of Ukraine's readiness to sign a document with Russia and the United States in Moscow 14 January on the withdrawal of nuclear warheads from its territory. The situation relating to the Russian radar station in Latvia was also examined. As Viktor Glukhikh, chairman of the Russian Federation State Committee for the Defense Sectors of Industry, put it, there is quite a clear trend in relations between our countries away from pronounced technological confrontation toward technological partnership. There are 108 joint projects covering the widest range of basic and applied research, development, production, and marketing that are now at various stages of implementation. For instance, a memorandum of understanding was recently signed with the National Aeronautical and Space Administration, and this has the potential to become one of the most promising projects. In the afternoon Bill Clinton held a cocktail party for representatives of Russian political and business circles. The day ended with a select dinner hosted by the Russian Federation president.
