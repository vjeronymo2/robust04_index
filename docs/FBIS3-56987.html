Aleksandr Panov Ready To Give Helping Hand to Russian Businessmen for Asian Market"] [Text] Russian politicians of the highest rank have repeatedly underlined that broader cooperation with the countries of Asia could plug some of the gaps in Russia's relations with the West. This equally applies to trade and economic relations, as well as political ones. Their invigoration will be discussed in the course of a tour of the countries of Central Asia, the Russian Far East, and China by Andrey Kozyrev, head of the Ministry of Foreign Affairs. He is setting off on this trip on 24 January. Aleksandr Panov, who has recently changed his job from ambassador to Seoul to that of deputy minister of foreign affairs, kindly agreed to comment on the state of Russia's relations in the Asia-Pacific Region in an interview with your KOMMERSANT-DAILY correspondent.

Russia Faces Asia
In Mr. Panov's opinion, Russia has till this moment been paying its main attention to broader relations with the West. This can partially be explained by the fact that the more economically developed part of the Russian territory is in Europe. However, the diplomat believes, relations with Asia are extremely promising only if because of the current rapid economic growth in the Asia-Pacific region. Of no minor importance is also the fact that Asian countries are not refusing to buy finished products from Russia. Mr. Panov thinks that Moscow has been able to do much in recent years to expand trade and economic relations with the Asia-Pacific region: far from falling, the volume of trade has grown. Russia's political ties with countries in the region are also picking up: Boris Yeltsin's visit to Japan took place after all, and he also went to South Korea, India, and China. This years opens with a visit to the PRC by Andrey Kozyrev. In Moscow, they are awaiting South Korea President Kim Yong-sam, and the head of Japan's cabinet, Morahiro Hosokawa.
Foreign Ministry Offers its Services to Russian Businessmen
Mr. Panov believes that a stronger Russian position in the Asia- Pacific Region will be helped by its participation in regional organizations. Russia already today takes part in two of them: the Pacific Economic Council, which brings together private businesses; and the Pacific Economic Cooperation Council. Membership in these structures helps Russian entrepreneurs keep abreast of all important events in the region. According to the diplomat, entrepreneurs of the Russian Far East began to trade independently on Asia's markets in recent years. Today they account for nearly 50 percent ($600-700 million) of Russia's trade with South Korea. The business contacts, however, are not always successful owing to unfamiliarity with the region. Mr. Panov therefore advises that Russian businessmen increasingly seek assistance from Russian embassies and trade missions which, in his words, are ready to help them find partners. Mr. Panov thinks that membership in regional structures would help Russia avoid competition with countries like the United States and Japan that traditionally wield strong influence in the region and are naturally reluctant to yield ground to Moscow. The diplomat noted with regret, however, that real competition is a long way off because U.S. trade with Asia-Pacific countries is around $300 billion, while Russia's trade with the Asia-Pacific region has never exceeded $10-15 billion. As for boosting these figures through arms exports, one should not expect contracts worth billions in the near future. Russia, he believes, lacks sufficient experience in this business. Besides, Asian markets, arms markets in particular, are traditionally geared to supplies from the United States, France, and Great Britain.
To Draw Borders With China Is Easier Than With Japan
Mr. Panov thinks that, for the first time in decades, relations between Russia and China are simply normal: "In the past, we either fraternized or argued." Problems do exist, of course, but they are solvable. One is territorial. There is a widely held opinion that Russia is handing over its territories to the PRC unilaterally. What really is happening, according to him, is that Beijing also makes concessions. The diplomat also terms as promising Russia's recent proposal on the joint economic use of these border territories. The other problem is the transparency of the Russian- Chinese border. There too, the Russian Foreign Ministry is taking measures to limit no-visa entry by the Chinese. As for Japan, in the wake of Boris Yeltsin's visit to Tokyo, the sides have in full measure come to realize the entire complexity of the territorial problem. Mr. Panov believes that one should not expect an appreciable progress in its solution in the near future. According to him, Russia and Japan will start a new round of talks in February, but a complete settlement of this question should be preceded by the process of rapprochement in the sides' positions on the entire set of both political and economic problems.
