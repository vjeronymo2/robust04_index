September 15, 1990, Saturday, Orange County Edition

NUTS AND BOLTS;

FORMICA NOT JUST FOR THE KRAMDENS ANYMORE

Formica, traditionally, has had about the same effect on Americans as jail. We'll concede that it's probably necessary in the public sector, but we wouldn't want it anywhere near our house.

When it first appeared in the 1950s, Formica was the interior lineman of building materials: functional, durable and nobody's idea of subtle beauty, able to withstand heroic amounts of grime and still come clean. It was the roadside diner's shield against the world, covering countertops from coast to coast with low-maintenance -- but nonetheless ghastly -- colors, reeking of fried onions and Pine-Sol.

In the home, however, Formica was strictly for the Kramdens. It was the table top of choice for people with children who threw food. You could hose it down, but you didn't want to put the Waterford on it.

Today, however, you can use that veteran laminate material in your kitchen and bathroom without risking turning them into a knockoff of every beanery in the world named EAT. Formica has gone uptown.

Don't worry; it's still Formica. It's still resistant to most of the slings and arrows of outrageous fortune, like strained beets and certain low-impact kitchen explosions. But instead of looking like the centerpiece of a cartoon nightmare, with swirls here and whirls there, all on a background of thundering yellow, today's Formica can be had in such incarnations as Nile Dorian Marble, Bronze Lacque Metallique and Vicuna Milano Stripe.

"The patterns and the colors have been so expanded that now you have a rainbow of things to work with," said Mary Knott, a residential space planner and owner of Mary Fisher Designs in San Marino.

Knott, who will speak about the uses of Formica at the Orange County Interior Design and Home Decorating Show at the Orange County Fairgrounds on Sept. 22 and 23, said the popularity of the original Formica was diluted not only by the limited designs available at the time, but by cheap imitations.

"The patterns they came out with at the time, like the boomerang pattern, for instance, were very Art Deco, with blues and pinks and yellows, and the appliances then followed suit in that color range," Knott said. "They were quite limited and they didn't have the subtle color changes they do now. And, as with many other things, mass merchandisers get knockoffs that are made from cheap materials and use them in a way that those materials were never meant to be used."

What can you use Formica for now? Just about anything except a floor covering, Knott said.

"So many people think of laminate materials strictly as countertops," she said. "But one of the best surfaces to use it on is cabinetry. That and wall surfacing is where I think you're going to see more and more builders and contractors using it."

Unlike the plain vanilla days, this doesn't mean simply slapping a sheet of Formica on a surface, said Knott. The Formica Corp. has come up with a laminate material they call Colorcore that is "solid color all the way through. You can literally layer these materials and make all kinds of incredible designs. The only limitations are your imagination and your pocketbook."

The traditional Formica laminate -- which is still available -- is bonded to a dark-colored backing that is revealed when the material is cut at, say, the edge of a counter. Colorcore is the same thickness, but the backing is eliminated. The color is solid throughout.

"You can bevel it, you can round it off, you can do any kind of edge treatment you want," Knott said. "You can take layers of it and make striping and banding and wild stuff like pin stripes and make very creative looks."

How creative? Any shape you can cut is fair game. Knott said she had heard of one application on a wall that involved layering the laminate and cutting one layer to resemble ripped construction paper.

Also, she said, it's possible to pile on multiple layers and then cut geometric designs through the layers with a router, allowing all the colors to show through at the cuts.

But kids, don't try this yourself.

"The key is to have a good fabricator who has worked with the material and is familiar with the proper tools," Knott said.

"You have to have a fabricator who's willing to take time and work with you and who knows all the wild stuff you can do with laminates. It really has to be handled with particular tools, so the best thing to do is to go through the trade."

This generally means going to a cabinet shop, rather than a lumberyard or home center.

The more generalized stores may carry some laminates, but the variety probably will be limited, Knott said.

Formica isn't inexpensive. Price depends on the type and style of the material. But, said Knott, "it's not as (expensive) as some of the natural materials around. It's about neck-and-neck with wood."

Not all Formica is doggedly contemporary, though, which ought to be good news for nostalgia buffs and people bent on re-creating Pop's Malt Shoppe in their den. In the back of the Formica color catalogue are four pages of examples with names like Neo Papercraft and Sorbet and Moonstone Papyrus.

But there, at the top of the last page, is an example called Rosetta Boomerang.

It's a deep pink shot through with intersecting yellow, blue and lavender parabolas. The kind of design you would have seen in Tomorrowland 35 years ago, a style that was known as futuristic when jet propulsion was new, the stuff that surely must have covered Buck Rogers' bathroom countertops.

Formica kept it, bless them. The classics never die.
