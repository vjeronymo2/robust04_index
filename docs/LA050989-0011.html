May 9, 1989, Tuesday, Home Edition

IF WE REALLY WANT TO COME UP AS WINNERS, WE'D BEGIN TO CURB OUR LOTTERY FEVER

With the national media feeding the frenzy, people flew in from all over America to gamble on Pennsylvania's $115-million record-setting jackpot, the biggest since Illinois set its $69-million record 12 days earlier.

"I'll be rich tomorrow," a postal worker tells a reporter after standing in line three hours to buy her tickets.

Whatever the long, snaking queues and 500 ticket-per-second sales have to say about our national condition, it isn't good. Lotteries, once the peculiar blight of impoverished Third World countries, are mushrooming across 28 states -- even as the U.S. savings rate sinks to the lowest in the industrialized world.

Why this lottery craze, now? Sheer materialism, fed by media hype? Lack of individual or national purpose? Erosion of the work ethic? There's no one answer. But players for the Pennsylvania jackpot did repeatedly tell interviewers they'd use the loot, if they won, for a house. Until the 1970s, the American dream -- and practice -- was to work hard, save weekly, then make the down payment on your own home. Now peoples' hopes get boiled down to million-to-one odds. Perhaps our national standard of living is plummeting faster than we realize.

States used to treat gambling as a faintly tainted activity, at best. No longer. Now it's the states' silver bullet, a substitute for politically dangerous taxes. States are hooked, like their gambling citizens. A decade ago, less than $2 billion worth of lottery tickets were sold nationwide; states netted $730 million. But last year, Americans bought more than $15 billion worth of lottery tickets. Net revenue soared to $5.8 billion. New Jersey and California may soon net $1 billion each.

So the money is getting deadly serious. And the lottery rash keeps spreading. A last bastion, the South, is now crumbling. Florida and Virginia recently succumbed; Louisiana and Georgia are teetering.

Lotteries have become a huge, regressive, debilitating tax -- especially on lower-income people. Statewide sales of more than $100 per adult per year are commonplace, meaning that hundreds of thousands of individuals are spending more than $1,000 on the tickets. A study in New Jersey found that more than one-third of families that live in poverty at under $10,000 a year spend one-fifth of their income on lotteries.

If lotteries were a harmless form of entertainment, who could oppose them? But they can become an addiction. "We're building a nation of gamblers," says Arnold Wexler, executive director of New Jersey's Council on Compulsive Gambling. Buying a state lottery ticket is many people's first brush with gambling -- a potentially "devastating illness that takes away your whole life," says Wexler, a compulsive gambler who kicked the habit 21 years ago.

Lottery-driven gambling might indeed become the national addiction of the '90s, as serious as alcohol, drug abuse and cigarette smoking.

So what do we do other than turn the wheel of history back to 1964 when New Hampshire unleashed the first state lottery of the 20th Century?

A huge scandal, like the corruption that killed Louisiana's lottery in 1894, might dampen, even kill off today's craze. Short of that, seven stopgap steps come to mind:

First, curb the outrageous $400-million-a-year lottery ad campaigns that states finance and countenance. A billboard in a Chicago ghetto reads: "This could be your ticket out." A New York TV ad, cited by the Citizens League in Minnesota, showed a mother telling her daughter that she should stop striving for a scholarship because Mom's buying lottery tickets. An Illinois ad showed a man scoffing at people investing in savings bonds, saying that he's investing in the lottery and that it's the only way he'll be a millionaire.

Lottery ads should be banned. The media hypes the games enough -- for free. Short of that, state-sponsored ads should conform to some minimal standards of truth telling, noting the odds against winning, and warning that gambling can become addictive.

Second, the states ought to print warnings about compulsive gambling directly on the tickets (as we now insist for cigarette packaging).

Third, state lotteries should funnel more cash into treatment programs for the addicts their own public gambling help create. Reserving a minimal 1% of New Jersey's lottery and casino revenues, for example, would create a $8 million-a-year treatment fund, compared to the measly $275,000 the state spends now.

Fourth, cap the size of jackpots. They have a spiraling effect, drawing more and more people, siphoning off more and more cash needed for family budgets and education and savings.

Fifth, limit the spread of lottery outlets. An example of today's abuse: California has started putting mini-lottery terminals in checkout lines of some supermarkets.

Sixth, stop pretending that education, old folks' programs or state economic-development programs really benefit from lottery sales. Lottery funding just supplants other necessary state appropriations. It encourages people to think that in government, as in gambling itself, you can get what you want for nothing.

Final suggestion: If you hear anyone seriously propose a national lottery, tell him the facts.
