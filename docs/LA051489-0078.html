May 14, 1989, Sunday, Home Edition

MORE FIRMS SHIELDING DIRECTORS FROM SUITS

A growing number of California and U.S. companies, finding it harder to attract directors, are continuing this year to seek permission to shield those officers from liability in certain types of shareholder lawsuits, a survey of this year's proxy statements reveals.

The result is that many shareholders may no longer be able to sue and collect damages in cases where they lost money following a company board's rejection of a hostile takeover offer.

Dozens of companies this year are joining the list of firms that last year began seeking shareholder approvals for provisions that indemnify directors and possibly other employees from monetary damages where judgment errors may have led to a loss of shareholder value, according to William M. Mercer Meidinger Hansen. The human resources consulting firm surveyed proxy statements of 260 California-based publicly held companies.

"They are called raincoat provisions because for protected officers, they insulate them from the rain of litigation," said Richard D. Greenfield, a Haverford, Pa., attorney who represents shareholders in numerous lawsuits against companies.

The provisions are intended primarily to discourage lawsuits in instances where directors may reject hostile takeover bids and then see the stock price drop, said Roger Lustberg, an attorney at the Los Angeles law firm of Riordan & McKinzie. The firm has written indemnification provisions for such California firms as Comarco, Foothill Group and Transtechnology Corp.

Covers Judgment Errors

Major shareholder actions seeking damages for fraud or misrepresentation can still go forward, Lustberg said. Also, shareholders can still sue for violations of federal securities laws, such as for failure to disclose or insider trading.

"This will assure that directors are not liable for acts that are not wrongful but merely judgment errors," Lustberg said.

"It is meant to cut down on suits from shareholders angry just because they lost some money," said Michael O. McCullough, principal at Mercer Meidinger Hansen.

"We haven't found it to be any significant impediment" to serious actions, shareholder attorney Greenfield admitted.

The moves to install these provisions started following Delaware's action in 1986 to amend its corporate law to allow such measures. The action was triggered in part by the high cost of directors liability insurance and the difficulty many firms have in attracting directors.

Larger California-based companies incorporated in Delaware, such as Columbia Savings, Great Western Financial, Hewlett-Packard and Transamerica, sought and won shareholder approvals of such provisions in 1988, according to Mercer Meidinger Hansen. Others, such as Gap Inc. and Intel, reincorporated in Delaware to allow themselves to use the provision.

Since the Delaware action, however, numerous other states, including California, amended corporate laws in similar fashion, in part to stem an outflow of companies to reincorporate in Delaware.

That has led a number of other firms incorporated in California, including Price Co., Teledyne, Oracle Systems, Marshall Industries and Flourocarbon, to seek approvals in this year's annual meeting season without having to reincorporate, Mercer Meidinger Hansen said.
