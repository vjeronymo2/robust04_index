930204 FT 04 FEB 93 / School testing prompts concern ASSESSMENT of pupils by teachers too often fails to tally with exam results, the Schools Examinations and Assessment Council said in a report yesterday. The report, based on teacher assessments of 14 year olds in maths and science, will reinforce the government's determination to press ahead with nationwide testing of pupils studying under the national curriculum in England and Wales. It comes amid growing controversy over the future of the curriculum in English, and over this summer's English tests for 14 year olds. Plans expected to be agreed today by the National Curriculum Council, another government agency, will advocate a 'return to basics' in the curriculum, with greater emphasis on spelling, grammar, punctuation and the speaking of 'standard English'. The teachers' unions think the English curriculum is too prescriptive and are threatening industrial action if this year's tests for 14 year olds are made compulsory, as intended. A survey of English teachers in 145 grant-maintained schools by the 145,000-strong Association of Teachers and Lecturers found more than two-thirds in favour of a boycott unless the government agreed not to publish the results. Mr Peter Smith, association general secretary, said: 'We are not opposed to testing; we are opposed to the government's plan for compulsory testing this year and the results being published in league tables.'
