February 7, 1990, Wednesday, Home Edition

NORTH SAYS HEARING MADE FAIR TRIAL IMPOSSIBLE;

IRAN-CONTRA: HE SEEKS REVERSAL OF CONVICTION. HIS ATTORNEY MAINTAINS THAT EARLIER TESTIMONY WAS A "COMPELLED CONFESSION."

A lawyer for Oliver L. North urged a federal appeals court Tuesday to overturn his Iran-Contra conviction on grounds that the former Marine's televised testimony to a congressional committee amounted to a "compelled confession" that destroyed his hope for a fair trial.

Attorney Barry Simon said the judge who presided over North's trial last year failed to make sure that the 1987 congressional hearings did not influence either jurors or witnesses in the criminal case.

Simon also said U.S. District Judge Gerhard A. Gesell should have permitted North to call former President Ronald Reagan as a defense witness.

Two members of the three-judge panel of the Circuit Court of Appeals, Laurence Silberman and David Sentelle, questioned prosecutors closely about why Reagan was not put on the stand. Silberman said he is convinced that Reagan's knowledge of North's case was such that he would have been compelled to testify had he not been President.

"As a matter of basic fairness," the defense should at least have been given the written answers that Reagan submitted to the Iran-Contra grand jury in response to its questions, said Silberman, who was appointed to the court by Reagan.

Sentelle, also a Reagan appointee, suggested that the former President lost his right to claim executive privilege because he provided the written answers and also gave information about the Iran-Contra affair to the Tower Commission and the independent counsel.

"It seems to me that if you have waived privilege and answered questions, you can't come back and claim it . . . ," Sentelle said. "If you waive it, it's gone."

Gesell ruled that Reagan's testimony was not relevant to North's defense because the two men never were alone together and, therefore, others could provide any information that the defense might have gotten from the former President.

Gesell's refusal to require Reagan to testify contrasts with Judge Harold H. Greene's ruling on Monday that the former President must give videotaped testimony in the trial, scheduled to begin Feb. 20, of former White House National Security Adviser John M. Poindexter. But Greene said Poindexter reported directly to Reagan and North did not.

The hearing ran more than an hour beyond its scheduled 90 minutes because the members of the panel -- Chief Judge Patricia Wald, Silberman and Sentelle -- peppered Simon and Gerard Lynch, who argued the prosecution's case, with questions. The jurists, who frequently appeared skeptical of both sides, gave no hint of their ultimate ruling.

A written opinion will be issued later. That decision may be appealed to the full circuit court and ultimately to the Supreme Court.

North was convicted last May 4 on three felonies -- altering and destroying official documents, accepting an illegal gratuity, and aiding and abetting in the obstruction of Congress. All charges arose from the Reagan Administration's secret arms sales to Nicaragua and the diversion of some of the proceeds to anti-government rebels in Nicaragua. The former National Security Council staff member and his wife, Betsy, listened to the appellate hearing but took no part in the proceeding.

North spent six days on Capitol Hill giving sworn testimony under a law that permits congressional committees to compel a person to reveal incriminating evidence despite Fifth Amendment rights by granting an immunity that is supposed to guarantee that the testimony will not be used against the person in court.

About 80% of the prospective jurors in the North case were rejected because they admitted that the hearings had influenced them. But Simon maintained that most of the others were also affected, even if they did not realize it.

"Jurors who heard a compelled confession -- which is what immunized testimony is -- cannot put that out of their minds," Simon said. He added that witnesses in North's criminal trial may have been influenced by the congressional hearings. The only way to find out, he said, would have been for Gesell to conduct a "line-by-line" review of the testimony during a post-trial hearing, which the judge refused to do.

"That would take months and months," Judge Wald said.

Simon agreed that the procedure would be time consuming but maintained that nothing less would protect North's Fifth Amendment rights.

Lynch said North's claim that the congressional testimony tainted the entire criminal proceeding "has a surface appeal, but it goes far beyond what is required by law." The former Columbia University law professor was recently named to head the criminal division of the U.S. Attorney's Office in New York and argued the case for Iran-Contra independent counsel Lawrence E. Walsh.

Lynch said Gesell was careful to ensure that the congressional testimony had no impact on the trial.

Simon devoted very little of his oral presentation to his contention that North should have been allowed to subpoena Reagan as a witness. But the issue clearly interested the judges. The court may have to face it again if Reagan appeals Judge Greene's order to give videotaped testimony in the Poindexter case.

Lynch agreed that Reagan "is treated somewhat differently because he (was) the President." But, he said, the written answers were so general that they would have been of little help to the defense.

Reagan's lawyers have not specifically invoked the doctrine of executive privilege, although they have claimed that the former President is protected by the Constitution from being forced either to testify or to turn over papers.

In defending the trial judge's decision to bar Reagan's testimony, Lynch said the evidence would have been irrelevant. Therefore, he said, there was no need to decide whether executive privilege would apply.

"I'm not talking about executive privilege," Lynch said. "I'm talking about the relevance of the testimony."

But Silberman maintained that if North had been a State Department official, the secretary of state would surely have been summoned to testify. He said Reagan's testimony might have helped North prove that he thought his actions were authorized and, therefore, not illegal.

Lynch responded that North had never claimed that he received authorization directly from the President but had, instead, inferred the authorization from what he was told by Poindexter and former National Security Adviser Robert McFarlane.
