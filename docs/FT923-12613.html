920717 FT 17 JUL 92 / Single parents lose benefits battle BRITAIN'S system of income support, which can leave single parents better off on the dole than working, is not a breach of European Community sex discrimination laws, the European Court of Justice in Luxembourg ruled yesterday. Mrs Patricia Cresswell and Ms Sonia Jackson, supported by the Child Poverty Action Group, had argued that Department of Social Security regulations made it financially impossible for almost 1m mothers with young children to work. Both mothers were challenging the Social Security Act 1986 which, unlike the Supplementary Benefit Act it replaced, does not allow child-minding expenses to be deducted from part-time earnings when assessing eligibility for benefit. Mrs Cresswell said she was Pounds 10 a week worse off by working part-time as a graphic designer. She had to pay Pounds 25 a week for child-minding for her six-year-old son but only the first Pounds 15 of her income was free from DSS clawback of allowances. The European Court ruled that the issue was not covered by EC directives on equal treatment regarding access to work and social security. Mrs Cresswell expressed disappointment at the ruling but said there was no possibility of further appeal: 'If the government just gave us a little bit of money to help us with our childcare costs we can take jobs and we are not going to be claiming income support. That will benefit everybody.'
