Language: Japanese Article Type:BFN [Editorial: "The United States Now in High Spirits for Economic Revival"; from Tokyo NIHON KEIZAI SHIMBUN 27 January Morning Edition] [Text] The annual policy speech the U.S. President makes to Congress at the beginning of the year is called "state of the union address" in English. Most of the one hour-odd speech given by President Bill Clinton on the evening of 25 January (morning of 26 January Japan time) was devoted to the economy, health care reform, education, welfare, and other domestic issues. It was literally a speech worthy of the term "state of the union address." It was a speech devoid of the pretentious rhetoric characteristic of most presidential addresses, and was a piece of pragmatic discourse focusing on policy. While some say it was not a speech becoming the president of a major power, considering Clinton's position as the President of a United States with declining power in the post-Cold War world, which is exerting desperate efforts toward economic revival, his speech was straightforward and made a favorable impression. One year ago, when the first baby boomer president took office, few, even inside the Democratic Party, believed that Clinton's economic policies would be successful. However, as Japan and the nations on the European continent moan under an economic recession, the U.S. economy is now definitely on its way to recovery. One can sense confidence in his policy direction and his own leadership from the President's speech, which puts across the optimistic atmosphere that has come back to U.S. society after a long time. The president used the word "renewal" many times. Although proud of the achievements in the past year, he said: "We know and acknowledge that our work has just begun. There is more to be done." Although Clinton is in a tight spot over the appointment of a defense secretary, and scandals have created controversies in the mass media, his popularity rating has recovered, and his administration is basically stable. Clinton plans to carry on with administrative and fiscal reforms, and reduce federal government personnel by 252,200 in five years. He stressed that "we will work hard for the American people, who pay our salaries." The President strongly advocated the reform of the health care system, where "58 million Americans have no coverage at all." In this health care reform, of which Mrs. Hillary Clinton is in charge, the president spoke of "the courage to face up to the special interests obstructing the reform." Lights did not go off in the White House until the late hours for days on end. Meetings were held with the President even late at night or early in the morning. Expensive restaurants in the neighborhood complained that they were out of business because the White House staff never came out to eat. Foreign affairs will be the task for the hardworking President from now on. "There are some who argue that with the end of the Cold War, the United States should turn its back on the rest of the world," but the President asserted that "we will not stop our contributions to the world." He expressed the intention to continue to exercise leadership in building a new world order. The word "Japan" did not appear at all in the speech. However, President Clinton has to take Japan into consideration, whether as a partner or as a competitor, in many of the things he wants to do from now on. In that sense, one cannot help being apprehensive about how the United States will behave at the Japan-U.S. summit in February, which will discuss the economic framework talks.
