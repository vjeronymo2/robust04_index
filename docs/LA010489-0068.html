January 4, 1989, Wednesday, Home Edition

JEFFERIES IMPLICATES GAF, EXECUTIVE IN STOCK CASE;

FORMER BROKERAGE CHIEF TELLS OF REQUEST TO BID UP UNION CARBIDE SHARES BEFORE SALE OF BIG BLOCK

Boyd L. Jefferies, the former chairman of the Los Angeles-based Jefferies & Co. brokerage firm, made his debut as a prosecution witness Tuesday, implicating GAF Corp. and its vice chairman in what the government claims was a criminal scheme to manipulate stock prices.

Jefferies, 58, who appeared tan and fit despite a cold, at times had trouble remembering dates and couldn't positively identify a central piece of evidence in the case, which is being tried in U.S. District Court in Manhattan. But he said he clearly recalled a telephone conversation with GAF Vice Chairman James T. Sherwin in October, 1986, in which he said Sherwin asked him to have Jefferies & Co. arrange to bid up the the price of Union Carbide Corp. stock shortly before GAF sold off a big block of Carbide shares.

"Mr. Sherwin called and asked me, if they wanted to have Union Carbide close at a specific price for several days in a row, would I be willing to do that," Jefferies testified. "I said, 'Yes,' and Mr. Sherwin said, 'Of course, we'll make up any loss to you' " incurred in the transaction, Jefferies said.

Implicated in Stock 'Parking'

GAF, a Wayne, N.J., chemicals and building products concern, and Sherwin were named in a 10-count criminal indictment returned by a federal grand jury in New York in July. They are charged with stock manipulation, securities fraud, wire fraud and conspiracy.

Federal prosecutors claim that, after an unsuccessful attempt to take over Union Carbide earlier in 1986, GAF decided to sell off some of the approximately 10 million Carbide shares that it had amassed and enlisted Jefferies in a conspiracy to drive up the market price.

Jefferies, who built his company into one of the nation's leading traders of large blocks of stock, has been cooperating with prosecutors since he was implicated by former stock speculator Ivan F. Boesky in illegal stock "parking" and securities fraud. In stock parking, one individual or firm intentionally conceals the ownership of stock that is held for the benefit of another.

Jefferies pleaded guilty to two felony counts in 1987 and is still awaiting sentencing. He was required to resign as chairman of both Jefferies & Co. and its parent holding company, Jefferies Group Inc. He has since been living at his home on the grounds of the Vintage Country Club in Indian Wells, Calif.

Jefferies' performance as a witness is considered crucial not only to this trial but to others as well, since he has implicated several individuals in allegedly illegal schemes. Among other trials in which he is expected to be the star witness is a pending case against Salim B. (Sandy) Lewis, a well-known Wall Street takeover specialist and speculator who is accused of manipulating the price of Fireman's Fund Corp. stock in 1986.

The GAF case is the first of many Boesky-related cases to actually come to trial, so it is also being viewed as an important test of how well a jury can comprehend the complex workings of the securities world, as well as its jargon.

Under questioning by Assistant U.S. Atty. Carl H. Loewenson Jr., Jefferies at times appeared confused about the precise dates of several of his telephone conversations with Sherwin in which he said the GAF official gave him specific instructions about the price at which he would like Carbide's stock to close.

Jefferies also testified that at Sherwin's request he made up a bill to cover the approximately $40,000 in trading losses that Jefferies & Co. suffered in buying and later reselling Carbide stock in late October and early November, 1986. He said he instructed his secretary to falsify the bill to GAF to make it appear, as he said Sherwin requested, that the charges were for investment banking services for a GAF subsidiary.

Confusion on Dates

But when he was shown copies of two invoices -- one bearing handwriting that said "OK" and signed by Sherwin, and another that had the name of the GAF subsidiary and was stamped by Jefferies & Co.'s accounting department -- Jefferies said he couldn't say which one was actually sent to GAF.

The existence of two invoices was brought out at the trial by Arthur L. Liman, GAF's lead defense lawyer. Liman afterward refused to comment. But the defense evidently hopes that Jefferies' inability to identify the invoices will cast doubt on his claim that he and Sherwin conspired to falsify records of Jefferies & Co.'s work for GAF.

Liman is also one of the lead attorneys for Michael Milken, the "junk bond" wizard at Drexel Burnham Lambert Inc. who is expected to be indicted soon. Junk bonds are high-yield, high-risk debt securities.

Jefferies said he had done business with GAF Chairman Samuel Heyman and Sherwin for several years before the alleged market manipulation scheme. He said he had held more than 100 telephone conversations with Heyman and many with Sherwin, whom he said he had first met in Los Angeles when Sherwin was attending one of Drexel's annual junk bond conventions.

Recounts Personal History

Heyman isn't accused of any wrongdoing in the case.

Before testifying about GAF, Jefferies was asked to lay out for the jury his own personal history, which included working as a ranch hand for several years after graduating from UCLA. He said he later started at an entry-level job at a small Los Angeles brokerage firm and left after six years to found his own company, which initially had two employees.

By the early 1980s, Jefferies & Co. had grown to six offices nationwide and an office in London. With about 425 employees, it had become a leading firm to which big investors went to trade blocks of stock off the floor of the major exchanges.

Jefferies testified how on a normal day he would arrive at work between 1:30 and 2:30 a.m. and remain at his desk at the center of the firm's big trading room until 6 p.m. He said he usually tried to get four hours of sleep a night.

His testimony is due to resume today. Prosecutors said they didn't know how many days Jefferies would remain on the stand.

The jury, made up mainly of people with blue-collar jobs, has been subjected to terms such as "brokers' cost of carry," "pretax equivalent yield" and stocks going "ex-dividend."

U.S. District Judge Mary Johnson Lowe repeatedly stopped the proceedings to remind lawyers and witnesses that "we are not economists here." As his testimony opened, Jefferies was asked to give what amounted to an elementary course for laymen on how stockbrokers make their money and on how buying and selling shares of stock is actually carried out.
