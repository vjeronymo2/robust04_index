May 25, 1989, Thursday, Home Edition

HINTS FROM HELOISE: SPICES WON'T SPOIL BUT THEY MAY LOSE THEIR POTENCY AS TIME GOES BY

DEAR HELOISE: How long can one keep spices in the cupboard? We will be moving in the near future and I was wondering if I should throw them all out and start anew since I have had some of them for a few years.

-- JEANNE, Belleville, N.J.

DEAR JEANNE: Before you throw away all those spices, check each one for its individually strong, full-bodied aroma and a fresh look. Spices will not go bad or spoil; instead, they may lose their potency as time goes by.

The oils in spices, which give them their flavor, escape when exposed to heat, moisture or air and eventually deteriorate if stored for a long time. This is why it is very important not to store any spices near or over areas of higher-than-normal heat or humidity such as over or near a stove, sink or dishwasher. Also, a good habit to get into is to replace the lid of a spice container as soon as you are through with it to keep it fresh.

If you have added a particular spice to a recipe and the food does not taste properly seasoned, the spice has probably lost its potency and should then be thrown away.

-- HELOISE

DEAR HELOISE: Here is what I do to remove any lingering odor of oven cleaner: I spread a thick layer of newspaper on the floor of the oven and with a spray bottle filled with warm water, I spray the top and sides of the oven well. After drying the inside of the oven with a clean cloth, I roll up the newspaper carefully and discard it.

This removes all of the oven cleaner residue and prevents bad odors when I first turn the oven after it's been cleaned.

-- FRANCES, Delta, Utah

DEAR HELOISE: I have heard of several ways to flour a cake pan. Some say to use cocoa powder when baking a chocolate cake and others say to substitute biscuit mix for flour for light-colored cakes. I would like to add my method that has never failed me.

After greasing the cake pan, I use the mixed dry ingredients of the recipe to flour the pan. If I am using a cake mix, I use some of the dry cake mix. When doing this, always remember to return any excess dry mix to the mixing bowl and proceed with the recipe.

I have never had a problem with a white dusty look on the bottom of any of my cakes.

-- E.L., Norwalk, Conn.

DEAR HELOISE: I have found a way of cooking pasta without overheating the kitchen.

I first boil the water, to which I have added a pinch of salt and a little olive oil, then I add the pasta. When the water begins to boil again, I cover the cooking pan with its lid and turn off the burner, leaving the pan on the same burner for the recommended cooking time without the heat.

After the recommended time I remove the lid and drain the pasta. It always turns out just right this way.

-- LORI, Sacramento, Calif.

DEAR HELOISE: I recently bought a couple of cast iron skillets.

The saleswoman gave me a tip: She said that after I wash and dry them to put them in the oven to dry completely. Moisture can make cast iron deteriorate. It's a good idea to place paper towels between the pans when stacking them so there's no trapped moisture.

-- SUSAN, Amarillo, Tex.

DEAR HELOISE: I have a hint that saves me a lot of time and irritation from chemicals of commercial oven cleaners.

I use baking soda to clean my oven and I even use it around the burners when something that was spilled around them has become baked on. I just wet the inside of my oven with a wet rag and then sprinkle baking soda around (more heavily on soiled areas) and let it set for about 10 minutes.

Then I use a scrubbing pad and all the baked-on mess comes off real easily. If there are some bad spots, use extra soda and scrub a little harder. Rinse out the oven. It will be sparkling.

-- BEVERLY, Boardman, Ohio

DEAR HELOISE: If you happen to break anything that leaves tiny fragments of glass all over the floor, here is a great tip that will help pick up all the pieces.

I was staying at a cabin on vacation. While doing the dishes I dropped a glass and it shattered all over the floor. I looked for a vacuum or broom, but couldn't find either. I remembered a tip that my aunt told me many years ago, so I tried it.

After picking up the large pieces of glass, I wet a piece of newspaper. I put the paper on the tiny bits of glass, lifted the paper and the glass clung to the paper. I did this all around to make sure I got all the pieces.

It worked. I never found a piece of glass during my stay there.

-- JUDY, Reno, Nev.
