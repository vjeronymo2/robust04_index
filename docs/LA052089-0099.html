May 20, 1989, Saturday, San Diego County Edition

PANEL AND SUPERVISORS SPLIT ON RESOURCE LAW

Urged on by environmentalists, county planning commissioners on Friday backed a stringent ordinance to protect the region's sensitive lands and criticized revisions made by the Board of Supervisors as an attempt to dilute the proposed law.

The commissioners voted, 4 to 0, with three members absent, to support the original version of the proposed Resource Protection Ordinance, which was drafted by a coalition of county officials, planners and environmentalists over the past year.

But on Monday, the supervisors proposed numerous changes to the ordinance and eased restrictions designed to protect threatened wildlife and the county's landscape.

Upon hearing of such changes, the environmentalists voiced their concerns before the commissioners. On Friday, the commission dismissed the changes and reaffirmed their support for the unadulterated draft of the ordinance. That set the stage for a possible confrontation with the board Monday, when the supervisors meet to consider the ordinance's adoption.

Particularly Alarmed

"If the board is going to make significant changes to our ordinance, I rather that they not adopt anything at all at this time," Commissioner Toni Kastelic said.

Commissioner Wright added: "If you put in all these exemptions, the whole purpose of the ordinance will be weakened."

Many of the environmentalists were particularly alarmed by changes to exempt the developer of the 22,500-acre Otay Ranch area from the restrictions imposed by the proposed ordinance.

Others were also concerned that changes made by the supervisors would make many of the county's steep slopes vulnerable to development.

"Especially in the Otay Ranch, there are a variety of threatened plants that are unique to San Diego," said Emily Durbin, a member of San Diegans for Managed Growth.

"And there's wildlife, like birds, that live in the area -- for example, the black-tailed gnat catcher -- that are on the verge of being endangered," Durbin said. "If exempted, developers will cover every hillside with houses." The stronger ordinance is also supported by the San Diego chapter of the Sierra Club and the San Diego Audubon Society.

Supervisor Brian Bilbray, however, dismissed environmentalists' attacks that the changes would let development run rampant.

"There is not one supervisor who does not have more experience dealing in land-use decisions than all the planning commissioners combined," Bilbray said. "We will pass the ordinance that we feel is best for the county."

Vulnerable to Developers

Environmentalists said the supervisors' changes make more steep slopes vulnerable to encroaching developers.

"San Diego's topography is unique because of our steep slopes," Durbin said. "To grade slopes, to fill in the canyons, will make us look flat. We need to protect the slopes, not only to save wildlife habitat, but because it provides visual relief from urban density . . . it's a scenic wonder."

Although many environmentalists expressed delight with the commission's endorsement, they remained skeptical about whether the Board of Supervisors would accept a stringent ordinance.

"I'm very pleased by the commissioners' actions," Durbin said. "It's obvious that they have a pulse on the situation and are concerned about the county's long-term interests, rather than interests that pop up from election to election."

"But I'm afraid the chances that the supervisors will accept the stronger ordinance are slim," Durbin added.
