BFN [Text] Bratislava, April 13 (CTK) -- A meeting between the Slovak and Hungarian environment ministers which included discussion of the Gabcikovo hydroelectric project ended without concrete results today. On the controversial subject of the division of the Danube waters, Slovak Environment Minister Juraj Hrasko said he and his Hungarian counterpart Janos Gyurko had agreed to recommend that their governments prepare themselves to discuss the approval of a political decision. "We did not bind ourselves to any conclusions. However, we both said the tone of the meeting was much friendlier than in the past," Hrasko said, adding that he was ready to continue negotiations with the aim of improving the state of the drying out branches of the Danube. Hrasko confirmed that in the dispute over the dividing of the Danube waters between the old river course and the Gabcikovo canal he had proposed negotiations without the participation of experts from the European Union (EU), but declined to give further details. Gyurko, however, rejected the offer of bilateral negotiations, thus sticking to Hungary's demand that talks take place with the participation of EU experts. However, he appreciated the change for the better in the atmosphere of the negotiations. If the Slovaks were to send more water into the old course than before -- and this would mean a fall in the output of electricity from Gabcikovo -- the Hungarians would support any request made of Slovakia by a third party for compensation for these losses. "We were assured that no standpoint from the Hungarian Parliament or Government had to be definitive in the search for an agreement," said Hrasko, who used to be chairman of the parliamentary committee for Gabcikovo. "A mare is never sold at a market for the asking price, but nor is the first offer paid," he added. The compromise proposal by Western experts on the division of the Danube waters was approved conditionally by Hungary, but Slovakia rejected it. However, the Hungarian Parliament then went against the recommendations of both the experts and its own government, and decided that the drying out branch of the Danube would be supplied with water by pumps, not by the building of a dam. Nonetheless, Hungary recently invited Slovakia to resume talks forthwith, to which Slovakia replied that it was still prepared to negotiate. Hrasko said today's talks also concerned broader issues of cooperation in environmental protection and improving the quality of contacts between the two countries. Concretely, they discussed the creation of a joint national park on the border area and an agreement on a common approach to the pollution of waters.
