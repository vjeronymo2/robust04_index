January 31, 1989, Tuesday, Home Edition

EXPERIMENT WITH STEROIDS PROVIDED A FRIGHTENING LESSON

For 3 weeks in the winter of 1972, while training for her fifth Olympics, Olga Connolly turned to the world-class athlete's little helper, anabolic steroids.

"I had four kids and a full-time job," she said. "The only time I had for weightlifting was at night. I was getting nowhere. I was stuck at 165 pounds.

"There's a saying in the discus, that you can throw a foot for each pound you can bench-press. I needed 186 to beat my personal best."

She said that she took about one-half the men's daily dosage of Dianabol, the most popular steroid among athletes at the time.

"After 3 weeks, I was bench-pressing 200 pounds," she said.

But she said that she quit when she began to grow hair on her knees.

"My doctor (who had not prescribed the steroids) told me that the next step would be to go bald," she said.

Connolly said that at the 1968 Summer Olympics in Mexico City, she realized for the first time that some women were using steroids. She said that by the 1972 Summer Olympics in Munich, steroid use was widespread. The International Olympic Committee did not begin testing for steroids until 1976.

"Of 12 discus throwers in the weight room, only three of us weren't on the drug," she said. "The other nine women were lifting well over 200 pounds, 220 and 230 in some cases. It was really devastating to know that we were beaten even before we competed.

"I went to the weightlifters and tried desperately to get a steroid booster shot. But they explained to me that it doesn't work that way."

In 1968, the gold medalist in the women's discus won with a throw of 191 feet 2 inches, more than 27 feet shorter than gold medalist threw 4 years later. The eighth-place finisher in 1972 had a better throw than the 1968 champion. Connolly, the 1956 gold medalist with a throw of 176-1, finished sixth in 1968 and 16th in 1972.

"I could either go on steroids or quit completely," she said. "I decided to quit completely. It wouldn't have been me throwing. It would have been a completely different organism. There would have been no personal challenge in it." RANDY HARVEY
