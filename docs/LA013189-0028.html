January 31, 1989, Tuesday, Home Edition

THE DRUG CLINIC: COPING WITH SUBSTANCE ABUSE IN SCHOOL

Question: I am a case worker in a statewide system for treating troubled students. Presently I'm working with about 40 students who are hard-core truants. They are constantly in behavioral trouble -- in and out of the school system -- and come from the academically lowest-achieving school in our system.

When I approached the school principal about this problem, the principal -- backed by the administration -- told us not to ask these students any questions about drugs or alcohol. He said, "These areas are personal, and parents won't like it. Besides, there are no drug or alcohol problems in our school."

Do you have any suggestions on how to overcome these barriers?

Answer: First, here are two givens: (1) Every school, every industry and every community has alcohol/drug problems. It's only a question of how extensive the problems are. (2) Clinically, adults and adolescents who have significant alcohol or drug problems also have trouble with their behavior and performance in school, at work or at play.

Based on my clinical work with overseas schools as well as with public and private schools in this country, I would say that the principal and the administration in your town are playing politics, probably in order to protect their jobs. The more sinister possibility is that the principal in question has an alcohol/drug problem himself, or in his family.

In my clinical experience I have found that in schools where alcohol/drug problems are rampant, one or more of the following situations exist:

-- Substance abuse problems among top officials of the school administration.

-- Widespread substance abuse among faculty members. (When I ask in such a school how many recovering alcohol/drug abusers there are among the school faculty, I get blank hostile stares, and the answer "zero." That tells me that the teachers who are addicts in that school are still sick.)

-- The school has no substance abuse program at all, or the program exists only on paper.

Your community is not unique. Recently the principal of a New York City public school was arrested while buying crack. He had been suspended three times before by the school board because of unexplained absences and late appearances at the school, but he had not been disciplined or removed.

When the media printed a news story about the principal's arrest, New York City's school chancellor criticized the press and described his errant principal as "a principal who unfortunately made a bad decision about the use of crack."

When the news caught the attention of Mayor Edward Koch, he ordered an investigation that revealed that the principal had become a troubled drinker about three years before and was often observed drunk and dozing at meetings; that he was late or absent 142 days out of 184 school days last year; that one-fourth of the teaching staff was regularly late or absent; and that the school's reading levels were among the lowest in the city, ranking 529th out of 616 elementary schools last year. The principal has since enrolled in a drug treatment program.

What can be done to overcome the problem in your community? As a professional, you have an obligation to treat your clients by using the latest effective, accepted techniques available. If you counsel troubled students, getting a drug/alcohol history, not only of your client but also of your client's family, is of utmost clinical importance.

You must also discuss the school principal's advice with your own boss. If your boss sides with the principal, maybe your mayor will want to look further into this problem.
