April 19, 1990, Thursday, Home Edition

ERMA BOMBECK: TV FAMILIES JUST DON'T GET PICTURE

A few years ago, the Cosby clan gave mouth-to-mouth resuscitation to the family sitcom and brought it back to life. TV families appeared in every conceivable combination. There is a mother raising two children alone, three children raising their grandfather alone, three guys with a house full of kids (I have no idea to whom they belong), adopted parents, laminated families from several marriages, and an aunt who raises three boys whose mother split due to contract problems. There are blue-collar families, white-collar families and ring-around-the-collar families.

I have followed closely the lives of these households and have yet to see one that parallels my own: a family that can go through 30 minutes without a single laugh. Sometimes we go for weeks! If we had a laugh-track, it wouldn't laugh.

I think I've figured out why. There are glaring discrepancies between real life and sitcoms. In a series, the parents are always wise and profound. They pass out advice as if they were passing out napkins at a picnic. In the last five minutes, the kids realize their parents are once again infallible and thank them for setting them right.

Let's talk real life. Your child buys a used car with 210,000 miles on it. You tell him it won't run. It doesn't. You predict he will spend the rest of his life visiting his car in a garage. He does. You tell him it was the biggest mistake of his entire life. He sells it for three times what he paid for it.

Sitcom parents are protected by contracts. They are not going to get killed off because they're stars with lines. If something bad happens to them, they'll recover.

In reality, parents live on the edge. When you climb into the car with a child who is on his fifth "temp" and the car radio renders you sterile, there are no guarantees you are ever coming back.

Real-life teen-agers speak only five words a year to their parents, yet on television they bond their brains out. They all sit down to the dinner table together (all on one side of the table facing the camera). When was the last time that happened in your house?

For some reason, family comedy always has the siblings at odds with one another. Insults and threats get big laughs. The reality is that it's kids versus parents. Any couple foolish enough to have three children will be outvoted for the rest of their lives. They become the great minority.

I wrote a family sitcom once that incorporated reality into the script. The powers that be said it looked like we didn't love one another and advised me to put more laughs in. It was canceled in eight weeks. My family is still playing . . . every week.
