921215 FT 15 DEC 92 / Process plant contractor sold HUMPHREYS & Glasgow, the venerable but loss-making UK process plant contractor, is changing hands in a deal that switches ownership from Dallas-based Enserch Corporation to Jacobs Engineering Group of Pasadena. The sale, for which terms were not disclosed, comes in the centenary year of H&G, which was founded by two American-trained engineers, Dr Alexander Humphreys and Dr Arthur Glasgow, to tackle the carburetted water gas business in Europe. Since 1892 H&G has developed a worldwide reputation for its process-plant contracting skills with expertise in industries ranging from refining to pharmaceuticals and power generation. Financially, it has been less successful. As a private company run for more than 40 years by Mr Ambrose Congreve, Dr Glasgow's son-in-law, it struggled in the 1970s against increasing competition and dwindling orders, and was purchased by Enserch in 1983. Terms were never disclosed - Mr Congreve said at the time: 'I always understood a gentleman never asked the price' - but were believed to be about Pounds 10m. Yesterday Enserch said H&G had been losing money for some years and 'the strategic fit that we saw 10 years ago does not exist today'. Mr Frank Edwards, former managing director, says in a book to mark the H&G centenary that the company was not able to take advantage of the process plant boom in the late 1980s and did not have a large order backlog to cushion it through the recession. H&G's offshore business, employing 650 to 700 people, was sold in February to Kvaerner of Norway. Jacobs is acquiring the remaining process contracting business, a 50 per cent stake in a successful new joint venture with Anglian Water, and a 40 per cent stake in Bombay-based H&G Consultants. Mr Robert Clement, vice-president of marketing services at Jacobs, said it had been searching for a long time for the right company to expand its presence in Europe. 'This is an excellent opportunity to shape and mould H&G into a winning organisation,' he said. 'Our perception is that the losses are manageable.'
