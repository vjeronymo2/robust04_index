December 24, 1990, Monday, Home Edition

A GENIUS FOR POLITICS, BUT WHAT ELSE?;

MARIO CUOMO: HE HAS AN EXQUISITE SENSE OF TIMING AND WON'T RUN FOR PRESIDENT UNLESS HE'S SURE TO WIN. THAT DOESN'T MEAN HE CAN GOVERN.

Will Mario Cuomo run for President in 1992? The answer is simple and will persuade anyone who overlooks its slight defect of not being an answer at all.

Mario Cuomo will run for President if he senses he will win. He won't if he senses he will lose. He will have judged correctly in either case. Those assessments of the political future that are un-useful speculations for the rest of us are dead certainties for him. He always knows.

Cuomo's peculiar gift is a genius for politics, which is not, to be sure, the same thing as a genius for governing. When his wind is up as an orator, he can more than occasionally bemuse his auditors, not to say himself, into thinking him a lonely antagonist of destiny. But what he really is, instead, is destiny's favored messenger.

On Labor Day, 1989, New York's Democratic mayoral primary was approaching its climax with most observers unconvinced that Edward Koch's official franchise for giving offense might not extend to another four years. Then Cuomo greeted the Labor Day marchers with the wickedly wistful expression of the hope that New York would find leaders of the sort who heal rather than wound. He cited David Dinkins and two other officeholders. He did not mention the incumbent mayor; it could be felt with resistless conviction that all was up for Koch.

Two weeks ago, the governor roused the New York Daily News strikers with incandescences of solidarity. He closed the question while men of good will and bad alike were still struggling to keep it open. No sensible politician could conceivably thus flame for the oppressed if he could remotely imagine that the oppressor might have a chance to pay him back.

I have one or two friends who have crossed the News picket line and many more who have refused to; and the abandonment of all hope of a happy ending for any of them is a bitter thought. But Cuomo can be trusted never to throw caution to the winds if he feels the faintest breath of a breeze in the air. There is no appealing his cool and sober judgment; he had but to strike the opening chord of destiny's overture to persuade the ear that the curtain will fall upon the death of the Daily News.

There are complaints that Cuomo's views on the Persian Gulf crisis are inscrutable. They are likely to stay that way so long as the event itself remains so inscrutable that even destiny cannot think of the lines to prompt him. Our official policy seems to have arrived at a condition of incoherence that suggests management of the sort that puzzles itself as much as it does its antagonist.

Uncertainties of this sort do not fit Cuomo's style, which depends upon exquisite subtleties of timing inapplicable to messy and unresolved situations. Economy of effort marks the genius for politics; its highest art is waiting until the candidate for disposal wanders to the edge of the abyss and then giving the unobtrusively slight shove that carries him all the way down to the coroner's verdict of suicide.

That was the fashion of Cuomo's dealings with Koch and it apparently obtains for his dealings with the Daily News. It was also the fashion of Joe McCarthy's riddance by Dwight Eisenhower, who may have been our last President to combine an acute genius for politics with a considerable talent for governing when he had to -- and who at the same time contrived to keep both secret from observers thought wiser than he.

A genius for politics never permits itself a calculated stroke without its proper disguise as the spontaneous expression of a heart that can be silent no longer. If Cuomo runs for President, he will win. If he doesn't, he would have lost. How do I know? I don't have to; Cuomo will. A genius for politics is that rarest of presences, the one that frees us from guessing for ourselves because it somehow knows for sure.
