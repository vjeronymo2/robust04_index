921210 FT 10 DEC 92 / Parliament and Politics: Prince praises World Service THE PRINCE of Wales called last night for an expansion of the BBC World Service in a speech to mark the 60th anniversary of the service. Speaking hours after the announcement that he and Princess Diana were separating, Prince Charles told a reception in London's Guildhall that 'whether we deserve it or not - and often whether we have funded it or not - the World Service has come up with a broadcasting formula that is the envy and admiration of the world.' The World Service, which has an estimated 120m regular listeners, was important to Britain's economic and political interests abroad. 'Wherever I have travelled, countless British businessmen have told me how commercial opportunities only come about when an atmosphere of understanding already exists,' the Prince said. The government's green paper on the BBC had asked whether the corporation should continue to expand its services for international audiences through the World Service and World Service Television. 'The answer should surely be yes. The troubles of the world show no sign of abating. Civil war, whether in eastern Europe or Africa, is rampant; repressive systems deny human rights to their citizens; hunger and disease and appalling corruption are rife,' Prince Charles argued. It was essential for those who strive for solutions to be able to establish the true facts and know that there is a point of reference to which everyone can turn. 'The World Service is just such a credible source of information,' he said. The Prince also praised the view of Mr John Tusa, the retiring managing director of the World Service, that a strong service could only exist within a strong BBC. 'There is no doubt that all organisations may need restructuring from time to time, as part of the process of renewal,' he said. 'We surely need to be absolutely certain in this case that the changes are actually for the best.'
