October 16, 1989, Monday, Orange County Edition

ART REVIEW ;

ART CATHY CURTIS;

LAGUNA HOMAGE TO PIONEER DAN LUTZ PROVES EVOCATIVE

One of the things the Laguna Art Museum does best is to remind us of minor but worthy Southern California artists whose vision helped shape the tone of the fledgling art scene during the early decades of this century.

One of these figures is Dan Lutz, who came to Los Angeles in 1932 from Illinois to teach at USC. In a pocket-size exhibit at the museum (through Dec. 31), a portrait by his friend, Paul Sample, shows Lutz playing his bassoon with an air of fierce concentration, his brows arched and a shock of hair working its way loose above his forehead.

Although the setting seems to be his home (a woman -- his wife Dorothy? -- can be seen cooking in the kitchen), the wiry artist is neatly turned out in a suit and tie. In a self-portrait a year or so earlier, Lutz, 26, favored the dun-colored beret and open blue work shirt of the artist-proletariat.

Lutz's mature work included scenes from everyday life, along with ecstatic, gestural paintings of gospel music-making. Although partial deafness kept him from a music career, Lutz's schooling included music-conservatory training as well as art school. A travel fellowship in Europe exposed him to contemporary art; he studied briefly in the studio of Andre Lhote, a French painter and art theoretician whose work was influenced by Cubism.

By the time Lutz settled in Los Angeles, he had developed a moody vein of realism. In his 1936 painting, "Beach Scene," the sky is a sick yellow with an angry-looking black cloud, the ocean is a threatening blackish-blue and the crowd is sparse.

A red passenger cart sits idle. On benches nearby, a pair of featureless old men prop themselves up at askew angles, and a whitefaced woman with a long nose tends to a baby who sits up in a carriage as stiffly as the subject of an American primitive painting. A tall, thin man in white stands off by himself, pinched and irresolute. This figure crops up in several of the paintings of this period, so it's tempting to see him as a self-portrait.

In "End of the Line," a watercolor from the next year, Lutz abstracts the shapes of pairs of idle trolley cars into pale-green rectangles. Three oddly miniaturized conductors trudge past their resting vehicles, heads bowed in weariness or dejection. The long shadows of the late afternoon also suggest a symbolic "shadow" -- of the cold hand of industrialization imprisoning people in routine jobs.

The exhibition brochure essay by assistant curator Susan Anderson mentions nothing about Lutz's political beliefs; it would have been interesting, in this context, to know what they were.

Toward the end of the 1930s, Lutz began incorporating carnival and circus scenes into his work. In "Carnival," broken white brushwork against the sapphire sky gives a giant Ferris wheel a magical gleam. The viewer peers from the darkness into the froth of lights and action inside the charmed circle of booths. Thick white and jewel-color snippets of paint conjure up a gay, exuberant mood without actually spelling out the details.

Again, the stark light and dark contrasts lead one to ponder possible symbolic intent. Did Lutz view such events as lone bright spots for people whose lives had been crushed by the Great Depression, or was he simply celebrating the dazzle and energy of a popular American phenomenon?

Lutz's paintings of blacks coincided with other "white man's" ventures into black culture, such as George Gershwin's 1935 musical "Porgy and Bess" (based on the play by DuBose Heyward) and Vincent Minnelli's 1943 movie musical "Cabin in the Sky."

At the same time, pioneering Supreme Court rulings were guaranteeing equal (if still separate) railroad passenger facilities for blacks (1941) and declaring the unconstitutionality of whites-only primary elections (1944). And the first Congress of Racial Equality sit-in took place in a Chicago restaurant in 1943.

It was, in other words, a time when equal rights remained a distant dream, and blacks usually were still portrayed in popular art forms as ever happy. Lutz's renderings of blacks were not free of caricature, a point Anderson mentions but does not pursue in relation to other works of the era. On the other hand, some of Lutz's white folks were caricatured too.

In any case, his interests lay specifically in the feverish transports of black spirituals. In "I Got a Harp," comma-like dashes of yellow paint sketch in the boneless arms of the harpist plying her golden instrument. Against the glowing, green-black space, a constellation of black heads wearing misty green gowns appear to be a gospel choir. They seem to be gathered at a river, where (in the lower right-hand corner) a horizontal figure is being baptized.

The painting combines hints of a real scene and real people with the ethereal aura created by the passionate swell of the music. What music? Well, we have to fill it in for ourselves. But the fever-brightness of the colors and the sight of the massed, dematerialized figures are powerfully suggestive.

In other works, Lutz conjured up a celestial three-horse chariot rising above the robed chorus ("Spiritual Chorus") and a fantastic, jewel-colored parade of indistinct figures riding on mules, curving into the glowing, aquamarine landscape ("The Glory Road").

But later in the '40s, the artist moved on to new territory. Judging -- inadequately, to be sure -- by the two examples in the show, his new fascination with mythology lacked the personality of the earlier work. "Neptune" seems little more than an excuse to paint with thicker, more impenetrable crusts of paint. "Spring Fete," with its forest revelers in various stages of undress, looks rather like a wishful libertine dream, dutifully disguised as a classical theme.

In any case, it's a pity we don't get more of a handle on Lutz, whose themes suggest there was a lot more going on in his mind than simply setting paint to canvas. The show stands in marked contrast with the museum's other exhibit -- a huge, lumbering survey of paintings by Granville Redmond, organized by the Oakland Museum and outfitted with a lavish catalogue.

Redmond, born in 1871, was another member of the seemingly endless ranks of California landscape painters active in the early 20th Century. He specialized in scenes of fields speckled with red poppies. The catalogue says the landscape appealed to him. But more important, scenes of poppy fields were what the public wanted to buy.

So here they are: "California Wild Flowers," "Pond with Poppies," "Poppies and Lupines," "Field of Poppies," "Poppy Field Landscape," "California's Flowering Hills," "California Landscape with Poppies," and on and on and on. Pleasant enough in small doses, perhaps, but tiresome in a crowd.

Redmond also painted landscapes at dusk or dawn or in fogbound moments when the atmosphere had the dreamy, misty, "poetic" quality sought after by Tonalist artists. More acutely afflicted with deafness than Lutz, Redmond is popularly supposed to have revealed his auditory solitude in these works.

A few of them escape sentimental landscape cliches and stand on their own as works of modern art. Best of all is an undated painting simply called "Landscape," from the collection of the Los Angeles County Museum of Art, in which pale lacy trees in several shades of green rise up like flat cut-outs against the sky.

This is not a neat, clean transcription of nature for people to admire in their living rooms. It's a painting about what a certain corner of the world felt like to a particular artist and how he colored it with his own fancies -- remade it, in fact, to conform to his cast of mind and sense of formal values.

But collectors of Redmond's day did not care for that sort of thing, apparently. And he had bills to pay.
