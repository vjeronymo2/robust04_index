Language: English Article Type:BFN [By ITAR-TASS correspondent Veronika Romanenkova] [Text] Moscow 11 February TASS -- The U.S. and Russian ground mission control centers applauded today as the Discovery shuttle spacecraft carrying a U.S.-Russian crew on board successfully landed today at the Kennedy Space Center. Charles Bolden, Ken Reightler, Franklin Chang-Diaz, Jan Davis, Ronald Sega and Sergey Krikalev spent slightly more than eight days in space and returned to the earth at 22:19 (Moscow time). The landing was postponed by almost two hours due to bad weather. The crew conducted 35 scientific experiments in the sphere of material science, medicine and biology with the use of the spacehab laboratory and an installation for the production of super-clean thin films. The research was made to order of scientific and industrial organizations of the U.S. and Russia. The flight is the first stage in the bilateral Mir-shuttle program, which will continue to the planned Alfa International orbiting station after several joint flights. As for the immediate plans, a flight of Vladimir Titov (whose back-up is Sergey Krikalev) with a joint crew on board the U.S. shuttle is scheduled for January 1995. Titov is training at the Johnson Center (Houston, U.S.A.). Both U.S. astronauts, whose flight is scheduled for March 1, 1995, with the 18th main expedition of the Mir orbiting station have started training this week. Bonnie Dunbar, the wife of U.S. astronaut Ronald Sega, and Norman Thagard are training at the Gagarin Cosmonaut training Center, the Moscow region.
