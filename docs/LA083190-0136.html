August 31, 1990, Friday, Home Edition

DR. NEIL SOLOMON: SANDWICHES AND DIET

Question: From time to time, you have written about a variety of different foods, including fast food, Mexican and Chinese food. What about submarine sandwiches? Can they be part of a normal diet, or are they more like cake, to be eaten once in a while but not to be made a regular part of a diet?

Answer: Today's submarine sandwich is not the same as it was when first introduced. Not only are different kinds of rolls and breads being used, but the ingredients that make up the sandwiches are different.

Traditionally, the submarine sandwich contained Italian meats and cheese. Today the fillings include a variety of salads such as seafood, tuna and chicken, as well as ham, turkey and roast beef. Moreover, a "submarine sandwich" can even be made with a pita pocket, which can be filled with a variety of fresh vegetables.

In evaluating the nutritional value of a submarine sandwich, we can consider the bread, lettuce, tomatoes, onions and peppers as acceptable components of a diet. The diet remains acceptable with the addition of such meats as turkey and roast beef. The sandwich only begins to pose a problem if you add high-fat cheeses and other high-fat, high-calorie items. Fish is usually considered a good choice as a filling, but not when it contains a large amount of mayonnaise, which adds both calories and fat.
