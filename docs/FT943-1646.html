940923 FT 23 SEP 94 / Major ends row with Thatcher on South Africa A long-distance row between Mr John Major and Baroness Thatcher over the economic prospects for South Africa is over, the prime minister said yesterday. The row blew up after Lady Thatcher told businessmen in Bombay that foreign capital was unlikely to flow to South Africa because of concerns about violence. Her comments - which cast a shadow over Mr Major's three-day official visit to South Africa - irritated the prime minister's party because they directly contradicted his repeated declarations of confidence in the ability of the government of national unity to achieve prosperity. Mr Major welcomed a clarification of Lady Thatcher's remarks - issued by her office in London after worried British officials pointed out the potential diplomatic damage. The statement, which expressed support for the South African government and confidence in the country's economic future, 'answers the point thoroughly', Mr Major said before leaving for London. He said Lady Thatcher had encouraged investment in South Africa and assistance for impoverished black townships throughout her 11 years as prime minister, which ended in 1990. 'That she wishes South Africa well, and has done so continuously, I don't think there is a shred of doubt,' he said. Mr Major's successful South African trip, which also took in short visits to Saudi Arabia and Abu Dhabi, has reinforced the growing confidence among his advisers that the government's worst problems are over. Asked at a press conference whether the government's fortunes had reached a turning point, the prime minister responded cautiously: 'No wise politician ever says that.' But he is clearly hopeful that the government's popularity will be revived by recent improvements in economic indicators, combined with progress towards peace in Northern Ireland and the unofficial truce between the prime minister and the Tory right. 'There are a series of things we have been doing for the last four years which are now beginning to come together,' he said. 'People are beginning to see the fruits.'
