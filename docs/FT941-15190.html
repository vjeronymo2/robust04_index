940117 FT 17 JAN 94 / Six-month boom for cellphone services Cellular telephones form the strongest area of growth in City telecommunications but demand for all types of services is advancing, with competition among operators tougher than ever. The last six months has seen a cellular boom. The two leading mobile telephone operators, Vodafone and Cellnet, more than 120,000 net new connections in December, easily the highest monthly total ever. About one-in-four of all cellular phones are used inside the M25, and a high proportion by City workers. One-2-One, the new network launched by Mercury in September inside the M25, has further boosted City sales. British Telecommunications and Mercury, the main fixed-line telecommunications operators, both report sustained growth in phone usage on the back of buoyant business demand. Mercury, estimated to have about two-thirds of the City's outgoing long-distance traffic, reported in the autumn that there had been a 30 per cent increase in call traffic on the previous year. Sharp competition between BT and Mercury is stimulating demand. But Mercury in turn faces competition in the City from two new operators: City of London Telecommunications, which is digging up the City streets to build its network; and Energis, a new long-distance carrier, which plans to offer direct connections to City institutions. Computer sales companies are also reporting a sharp upturn. City demand is being strengthened by the arrival of a computer 'superstore'. Specialist Computer Holdings, the private company pioneering computer superstores, opened one of its 'Byte' stores in the City's Chiswell Street late last year. Mr Neil Cox, SCH marketing manager, said there had been a 'quick build-up' of corporate customers. He added: 'All our outlets have seen increased volume but there is pressure on margins throughout the industry.'
