July 29, 1989, Saturday, Orange County Edition

FAMILY LIFE: GIRL WHO SAYS SHE'S HOMELESS ASKS IF SHE CAN LIVE WITH A NEW FAMILY

Family Life isn't an advice column, and I'm certainly no Dear Abby. But this week we have an emergency on our hands in the form of an anonymous letter we received.

"To whom it may concern," the letter begins. "I am 15 years old. . . . I have a bad family situation and a week ago I was kicked out. I have a family who will take me in; I've known them all my life. . . . This family would like to be my foster parents, but once my family finds out that I went to (them), my father will flip and he will either kill me or a member of (the other) family. He is very violent and has no common sense, obviously.

"What can be done in order for me to stay with this family legally? I want to live a normal life, go to school and be somebody, which I could never do at home. I only feel safe with (the other family) and I don't, and won't, go with anyone else. What can be done? Is it possible for them to become my foster parents? How long will it take? Who should we contact?

"Please help me. There has to be something that can be done, but everywhere we go and everyone we talk to has all the wrong answers and solutions for me and my questions regarding my problem and how I want to solve it. Thank you.

"Troubled Teen"

Dear Troubled Teen:

"To live a normal life, go to school and be somebody. . . ." It sure doesn't seem like too much to ask, does it?

You didn't tell me much about yourself or your situation. I would like to talk to you and find out more, but all I have are your initials and the post office box you gave as your return address. For your protection, I've omitted a couple of details here that could have made you recognizable to someone who knows you.

I wasn't sure what to tell you, so I called some people who could offer you expert advice. It took several phone calls, and I spent almost as much time on hold as I did talking to people. Unfortunately, says Agnes Trinchero, executive director of Florence Crittenton Services of Orange County -- one of the agencies available to help girls like you -- there is no centralized referral number for the county.

"I know it can be really frustrating," Trinchero says. But her agency can't help you, either, until you get things squared away legally.

The hard truth, T.T., is that you might not be able to decide on your own solution. So says Robert Malmberg, social services director for the Orange County Child Abuse Registry. Being a minor, you aren't free to make your own decisions about where and with whom you will live.

Under ordinary circumstances, Malmberg says, the law requires that your parents must not only know where you are but consent to whatever arrangements are made for you. But if it's a dangerous situation -- and yours sounds like it may be -- then legal steps could be taken quickly to protect you from your parents. Or if your parents still refuse to allow you to come home, then you would be considered an abandoned child, and they could be stripped of their parental rights.

Child abandonment is a misdemeanor, punishable by six months in jail or a $1,000 fine, Malmberg says. "But it's also illegal for (this new family) to have (the girl) in their home. The law says that you may not sustain an unrelated minor in your home if you are not licensed as a foster parent."

You aren't breaking the law yourself in being away from home, Malmberg says. But you need to protect your potential foster parents from possible criminal charges.

He adds, however, that you may very well be able to continue living with this family as long as the necessary steps are taken.

Malmberg wants you to call him on the Child Abuse Registry's hotline at (714) 834-5353. You can call anytime, day or night. You may have to wait on hold for a while, but please be patient. He is in the office during regular business hours, or he can be paged. "I have a beeper with me 24 hours a day," Malmberg says.

Or you can talk to one of the social workers there.

When you call, a social worker will arrange to come and talk to you about your situation. Be open and honest about what's going on, and explain why you are concerned about your father being violent. Is this something he has done before to you or someone else? "If she is, of course, in danger, we're not going to hesitate to find a protective environment for her," Malmberg says.

"As with most situations with adolescents, there are two sides to every story," he says. "But generally, when they identify themselves to be endangered, very rare are the cases we've found to be fictional.

"We like to keep families together if at all possible, but only if it's in the child's best interest."

You're fortunate, Malmberg says, to have another family to turn to. Most other kids in this kind of situation aren't so lucky. And if it's determined that it would be unsafe for you to return home, you "could keep staying with this family, but they would have to apply immediately for a foster care license."

They would have to be fingerprinted and investigated for any criminal record, and the house would have to be inspected to make sure it's safe and there is enough room for you. "Most homes could probably qualify," Malmberg says.

To apply for a foster care license, the family giving you refuge should call Carol Whelan at (714) 532-7762. Or write to her at 1517 Braden Court, Orange, Calif. 92668.

If they would like some background information on what it's like to be a foster parent, have them call Liz Lafferty at Advanced Resources for Foster Kids in Anaheim, a support organization for foster families, (714) 939-0477. Lafferty is also willing to talk to you anonymously, but she warns that she would be required by law to report the situation if she knows who or where you are.

You don't consider yourself a runaway, but if you want to relay a message to your parents without revealing where you are, you can call the runaway hotline at 1 (800) 231-6946.

Or if you want advice but aren't ready to put yourself in the hands of the authorities, call Tom Shaw at the Assessment and Treatment Services Center, (714) 756-0994. ATSC's motto is "Arrest the problem and not the child." The agency is focused on helping families and children deal with their problems without going through the juvenile justice system. ATSC can offer counseling for you and your parents, if you feel that's the way to go.

Trinchero recommends that you report either to the Orange County Probation Department at (714) 834-6611 or the nearest police station. You will most likely be dealing with the authorities at some point, and if you make the first move yourself, "then they're going to hear your side of the story first, and that could be to your advantage," she says.

It may or may not help you to know that there are a lot of other troubled teens out there. "This is a fairly typical problem," Malmberg says.

I'm sending a copy of this column to the post office box you gave me, just in case you don't see it when it comes out. I promised Malmberg and the other experts that I would let them know if I hear from you again.

But they couldn't make the same promise to me because your case, like all the others they deal with, must be kept confidential. Unless you call or write again, I'll never know what became of you.

So do me a favor. When things settle down and you have a minute, let me know how it worked out, not just because I'll be curious, but because by then you'll be an expert, too, and some other troubled teen may be able to benefit from your experience.

Until then, I'll keep my fingers crossed for you.
