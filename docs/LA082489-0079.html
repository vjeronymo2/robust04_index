August 24, 1989, Thursday, Home Edition

THE BUTCHER: A PRIME GATHERING OF MEAT PROCESSORS IN IOWA

They came from all over the country -- indeed from around the world -- to Des Moines, Iowa, for the 50th anniversary of the American Assn. of Meat Processors (AAMP). I have never seen so many butchers in one place at one time in my life. There were hundreds of us and you thought we were a vanishing breed. Many brought their families: sons and daughters who will almost certainly be the ones who do -- if indeed anyone does -- carry on the proud tradition of quality custom meat cutting and processing in the country for the next 50 years.

Dozens of them brought their hams and bacons, sausage, dried beef, jerky, bologna, salami and such to enter in the American Cured Meat Championships. Maybe the real reason so many brought the children was to help carry all those coolers full of meat products for the competition.

Same Old Names

There was not much new in the ham competition; some of the same old names came out on top. Burger's Smokehouse in California, Mo.; Wood's Smoked Meats from Bowling Green, and Mom 'n Pop's Smoked Meats from Claremont, N.C., took first, second and third, respectively, in the smoked country ham category.

Eddy Woods of Wood's Smokehouse and Fred Keller of Mom 'n Pop's no sooner sat down with those trophies when they were called back to pick up another first and second, respectively, for their unsmoked hams. The three have been winning awards regularly throughout the years. For a listing of all this year's winners (they mainly sell their products by mail), send a self-addressed stamped legal-sized envelope to Cured Meat Champs, P.O. Box 907, Tiburon, Calif. 94920.

One of the most decorated butchers in the world also was in Des Moines for the convention. Benito Plasschaert from Ghent, Belgium, a butcher who serves kings and queens, brought 13 of his butcher brethren from Holland, Germany, Austria and Belgium, along with 126 different specialty meat products from Europe. I have never seen such art work in a meat case; pates, terrines and galantines of every description; corned beef brisket layered with hazelnuts in forcemeat, beautiful and delicious; rolled herb-stuffed bacon slabs wrapped in grape leaves unlike anything we have in the United States.

An exhibit of the latest in meat processing equipment was, as it has been for 50 years, a part of the convention. This year, they also had an antique equipment display. It was interesting to note the basic similarity. We are still doing the same things to meat: mincing, chopping, dicing, slicing. The only real difference in the process is the power source. What today we do with electricity and hydraulics, great-grandad did by hand.

Innovative Packaging

The most interesting and new innovation in meat processing is in the area of packaging. It is the new packaging technology that will, I believe, most greatly affect the eating habits in the years not far ahead. Indeed, the changes have already begun. Packaging exists today that allows the consumer to keep meat fresh in the refrigerator for up to 30 days. You can also put the same vacuum-packaged meat in the freezer and keep it with no freezer burn and no loss of quality almost indefinitely.

Directly to Oven

Packaging also exists today that will go from the freezer directly to the oven or microwave and then to the table, saving not only time in the preparation process but in cleaning up (there are no extra dishes to do).

It won't be long, according to many speakers at this year's convention, before many smaller meat markets will not only offer prepared food to go, but will deliver it to the consumer's house. The technology is there, the demand is there and this is an area where the little guy can compete with the supermarkets.

So, it won't be long before you can keep food fresh almost forever; you can have anything you want for dinner delivered to your door; you can cook an eight-course dinner in the oven or microwave and not have to do dishes because everything comes in disposable containers that are attractive and durable.

I was expressing my wonder at all of this modern technology to one of the food scientists in attendance who agreed: "Yes, Merle, we've come a long way in 50 years, but we've got a long way to go. Most of this new packaging material is not biodegradable. What are we going to do with all the empty containers?"
