March 12, 1989, Sunday, Home Edition

ON AGING: A WEEK TO RECOGNIZE OLDER WORKERS

Carmen Ugalde was 68 when she accepted a human services job with the city of Montebello. After three years of widowhood, Ugalde wanted more social life and supplemental income. She credits her return to work with bringing her back into the mainstream of life.

Her supervisor, Marie Bagwell, describes Ugalde as a "dedicated, efficient person" who "accurately fields all questions from City Hall and the public."

Ugalde demonstrates the characteristics of older workers, whose contributions are celebrated during National Employ the Older Worker Week, March 12-18. Since it was first observed by the American Legion in 1959, U.S. Presidents, governors, mayors and other officials have joined in declaring this a week to celebrate the talents older men and women bring to the workplace.

In Every Sector

More than 13 million people 55 and older are in the labor force. While they work in every sector of the economy, most are in service and trade industries. Fifty-five percent work in white-collar occupations.

In California, the Los Angeles Council on Careers for Older Americans is coordinating an older-worker poster contest and reception honoring older workers at the Gene Autry Western Heritage Museum. The winning poster will be distributed to 10,000 businesses and community organizations. Says Phyllis Meltzer, chairperson for the event: "Our theme is 'Western Classics' because that is what we believe California's older workers are."

Arkansas ABLE, a senior employment program in Little Rock, plans a lunch to honor employers with strong records of hiring older workers. ABLE, or Ability Based on Long Experience, also distributes place mats announcing the week to local restaurants.

The Older Kansas Employment Program in Manhattan has encouraged a local discount variety store to pay for newspaper ads congratulating employers who have hired older workers during the past year.

Question: I am a 74-year-old nurse. Recently, the new head nurse decided that I should retire and asked me directly to resign. She then wrote a negative review of my work, which her managers refused to sign because they thought it didn't reflect my performance.

Her most recent tactic was to transfer me from the night shift to the day shift, disrupting a schedule I've had for years. I believe I'm being discriminated against because of age. What recourse do I have?

Answer: The federal Age Discrimination in Employment Act prohibits discrimination against most workers 40 and older in virtually every aspect of employment.

To pursue an age discrimination case, you need only establish an apparent violation of the law. This means showing that you are in the protected age group and were adversely affected by some employment action in which age was a consideration. The burden then shifts to your employer, who must defend his or her actions.

Age discrimination charges must be filed, in person or by mail, with the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission within 180 days of the discriminatory act. (In states with age discrimination laws, you have 300 days.)

Be aware that you may wait months for an EEOC investigator to be assigned to your case. If you want faster action, consider filing a private lawsuit or using your organization's established grievance procedures.

Q: My brother and I recently returned home to Kansas City for the funeral of our 87-year-old uncle. The only family we have left in the area is his 89-year-old widow, who has always been a free spirit and thinker. I stayed with my aunt for several days after the funeral, and she said that, should she become terminally ill, she wants no heroic attempts to save her life.

I couldn't tell if she really means this or if it was a reaction to the death of her husband of 65 years. How do I determine if she is competent to make this judgment?

A: Competence is a legal concept that can be determined only by a legal proceeding. The general assumption is that competent people can decide whether to accept proposed medical treatment. The law assumes a person is competent unless a hearing determines otherwise.

Your aunt may be reacting to her profound loss. Sometimes the death of a loved one triggers depression and a person's judgment may come into question. It's too early to judge in her case. People may verbalize wishes for their own death after such a loss, and those wishes usually changes with time.

You may want to contact your aunt's physician and ask that she be given some added attention in the next few months.
