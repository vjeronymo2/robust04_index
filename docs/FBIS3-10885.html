Language: Spanish Article Type:BFN [Article by Ricardo Gatica Trejo] [Text] It has been confirmed on-site that Guatemalan Army troops have been sent to the Peten-Belize border area where Belizean soldiers and civilians are harassing the Guatemalans who live there. The small town of Rio Blanco in Dolores, Peten Department, is the one most seriously affected by these actions. The homes of the residents -- who are mostly Quekchi Indians from Alta Verapaz -- have been burned by the invaders. This situation is nothing new in this village that was settled 15 years ago. The pressure by Belizean troops, however, began nine years ago. According to Salvador Jimenez, a man from Jalapa who has been living in this village for 15 years, a black woman came to this village several weeks ago and threatened to leave them with nothing during the corn harvest. Francisco Javier Chen, a young Quekchi Indian who is a community leader, added that the Belizean soldiers have told them they are inside Belizean territory and that if they remain there their homes, crops, and even their lives are at stake. The village, on the banks of the crystal-clear waters of the Blanco River and a little further out than Chiquibul, seems to be like the garden of Eden. There is abundant agricultural production, the weather is very nice, and the river waters are fresh. One can hear the cry of the Saraguate monkeys in the distance. Speaking in Quekchi, Defense Minister Mario Rene Enriquez Morales held a lengthy conversation with the residents of the Rio Blanco village in Dolores, Peten, who complained of the actions by the soldiers from Belize. Guatemalan peasants wearing ragged shirts and rubber boots came down nearby paths, as well as women who were wearing the native outfit from Alta Verapaz and adolescents who wore colorful necklaces and carried sweaty children. Likewise, sweaty soldiers walked down the same paths. They were wearing dirty camouflage uniforms because they have been in the area for a long time. Some of them carried light artillery weapons. Others carried M-16 rifles, while another carried radio equipment. Enriquez, speaking in their language, urged the peasants to respect the law and not cross the border. "The Army is here to give you a helping hand. If for any reason you have to abandon the area, the government will find land to give you," he said. The peasants said that they "harm no one and work in their fields." On some occasions they said they have found tombs, but they never touch them. They always leave them where they are. They complained that the Belizean soldiers accuse them of "opening the Maya tombs, but this is not true." The dialogue between Enriquez and the community leader was interrupted several times when 10 persons speaking at the same time explained that they "are Guatemalans and they hope their government will do something for them at this time -- the worst of their lives -- because there has never been anything like this before in the jungle." The troops from Military Zone No. 23, stationed in Santa Elena but with jurisdiction in Peten Department, are arriving at the border area between this department and Belize.
