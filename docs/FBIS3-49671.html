Language: English Article Type:BFN [Text] Belgrade, Jan 12 (TANJUG) -- Yugoslav Prime Minister Radoje Kontic and the premiers of the Yugoslav Republics of Serbia and Montenegro, Nikola Sainovic and Milo Djukanovic, on Wednesday evening [12 January] backed up an economic recovery programme drawn up by a team of Yugoslav experts and agreed to start implement the measures contained in it as of next week. Yugoslavia desperately needs such a programme to counter the effects of an airtight international blockade and monster inflation which in December alone reached one million percent. The U.N. Security Council imposed tough sanctions on Yugoslavia in late May of 1992 for its alleged involvement in civil war in neighbouring Bosnia-Herzegovina. Kontic, Sainovic and Djukanovic were agreed that the anti-inflation and stabilization programme, due to be launched on January 17, would ensure an economic independence to the country and open prospects for recovery on the basis of market-economy principles, the Yugoslav Information Ministry said in a statement. The Yugoslav Government on Wednesday generally upheld the programme but insisted on some corrections and amendments, the statement added. The programme focuses on curbing hyperinflation and ensuring monetary stability as a precondition for reviving production and improving the living standards of the population, the Yugoslav Government said. It also said that in the conditions of international sanctions, financial backing for the programme should be primarily ensured from domestic sources rather than from foreign ones. This should not stop actions, however, for deblocking Yugoslav assets frozen in foreign banks, settlement of claims that Yugoslavia has towards other countries and conclusion of credit and other financial arrangements to support the programme, the Federal Government said. The government of Serbia on Wednesday backed the programme, while the government of Montenegro said on Tuesday that it should be used as a basis for seeking how to surmount the present economic difficulties and suggested a series of alterations. In a letter to the Federal prime minister whose content was published on Wednesday, Montenegrin Premier Djukanovic said that the programme did not fully take into account the effects of the blockade, that it was better suited to an open economy and that it implied backing of foreign capital. Another shortcoming of the programme, the Montenegrin Government said, is that it offers no complete answer as to how to revive production and the commodities' market and how to mitigate the acute social problems.
