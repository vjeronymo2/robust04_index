Language: Spanish Article Type:BFN ["Exclusive" interview with President Gonzalo Sanchez de Lozada by Roberto Solans on 18 March; place not given] [Text] [Solans] You have said the coca crop, drug trafficking, and misery make Bolivia a time bomb. What is your strategy to defuse it? [Sanchez] We want to achieve significant growth levels, comparable with those of Chile and Argentina through capitalization, but with a significant social component. Then comes the people's participation program that makes our representative democracy a participative one through the use of a great asset of ours; a strong understanding of the community. Do not forget that two thirds of our population are Indian groups that, unlike western societies, favor the community rather than the individual. Taking advantage of this, we want to create a society capable of achieving significant employment and high social justice levels. We are seeking to restore exports. In trade terms, Bolivia lost about 90 percent over the past 14 years. We are currently exporting drugs and people. [Solans] The agreements that you have just signed with the Argentine Government seem to be aimed at achieving this goal ... [Sanchez] They promote investments by both nations [Bolivia and Argentina] in my country to establish a raw-material processing industry (the processing of national raw materials in another country and the purchase of the finished products) that will allow us to export lines in which Argentina is no longer competitive as a consequence of its high labor costs. This way we will export products and not people. This step, along with an increased access to the U.S. markets, about which we will ask U.S. Vice President Al Gore when he visits La Paz within a few days, should allow us to voluntarily eradicate coca crops. [Solans] Is Mercosur [Common Market of the South] one of Bolivia's goals? [Sanchez] We are part of the River Plate system and we want to be closer because Argentina and Brazil are our great markets. We note that Mercosur's dynamics involves expansion, but there should me much more opening and honesty. With tariff lists that have more exceptions than uses, I believe the honest approach has been neglected. If we are allowed access, NAFTA may lead us into a dynamic situation. Bolivia wants to become a bridge between Mercosur and the Andean market. [Solans] Concerning your talks with the Argentine Government, did you receive any response on the immigrants who do not have any legal papers? [Sanchez] We talked about establishing a new period for Bolivians who already are here to gain legal status. We also talked about enforcing new restrictions on the border and making entry into Argentina more difficult. Bolivia wants to give more jobs to Bolivians and this is the goal of the free trade agreement between the two countries. We are attempting to create a relationship similar to the one existing between the United States and Mexico. Deploying more guards on the border is no solution because barriers vanish with a little corruption. The solution is to create jobs in Bolivia and gain access to the Argentine market. [Solans] And the gas sales to our country? [Sanchez] Significant progress has been achieved. We have reached agreement on an indefinite, free access in both directions of gas and hydrocarbon by-products. We are very content with having defined this market. A three-year contract has also been signed in which a price list has been established for gas because Argentina is paying less than we should receive. The United States, with a deregulated economy, is paying almost double. We understand that the Argentine economic process does not permit any traumatic cost-of-living surges and we will have to follow up on this. We trust there will be a gas market here for Bolivia and that there will be a market in Bolivia for certain Argentine oil by-products. [Solans] What reaction might stem from the Bolivian Armed Forces over the impending extradition of former General [Luis] Garcia Meza? [Sanchez] None, we believe. We are enforcing a sentence. The institutions are not corrupt -- men are. The trial lasted seven years. He has been a fugitive for five years. He was discharged from the Armed Forces. It has been clearly established in Bolivia that this does not represent an attack against an institution, but against a man who has seriously damaged it. [Solans] Do the lower echelons also understand it this way? [Sanchez] I believe so. At one time it was clear that a group of officers were the culprits. As time went by there has been a natural renewal within the Armed Forces and now it is understood that this case belongs to the past and justice must be meted out. [Solans] And in the political circles? [Sanchez] We don't know. Some people are fearful of what Garcia Meza might say. I believe this is not the case, that everything was disclosed at the proper time and that, despite speculations by the press, there will be nothing new. I believe satisfaction prevails because impunity has been punished, something that is not usual in our countries. [Solans] Is it true, as some political circles in La Paz claim, there are officials in your government who closely cooperated with Garcia Meza's regime? [Sanchez] No. Thank God I have no one who was linked to drug trafficking or Garcia Meza. Our party was not involved, while other parties, for various reasons, were involved in Gen. Garcia Meza's project. [Solans] Which ones? [Sanchez] General Hugo Banzer's Nationalist Democratic Action. Nevertheless, they moved away when they realized the nature of that government.
