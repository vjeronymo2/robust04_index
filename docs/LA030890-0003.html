March 8, 1990, Thursday, Home Edition

FOOD AND COMMON SENSE: COUCH POTATOES SHOULD GET MOVING FOR BETTER HEALTH

Is moving off the sofa to fill the snack bowl during commercial breaks your major form of exercise? If so, we can officially dub you a couch potato.

Maybe it doesn't bother you to be named after a tuberous vegetable and a piece of furniture, but perhaps this will: Scientific research shows that you have a good chance of dying sooner than your more active friends.

A depressing thought, yes? But don't stop there. Despite your current sedentary habits, all is not lost. Results of an important new study in Dallas provide evidence that even moving a little goes a long way toward protecting your health.

This study differed significantly from earlier models. Instead of trying to assess physical activity -- a difficult task to accomplish accurately, especially where large groups of people are concerned -- it measured physical fitness. Fitness, the product of both genetic makeup and physical activity, can be tested easily by performance on a treadmill.

More than 10,000 men and 3,000 women, all healthy, participated. Besides the treadmill test, each subject underwent a thorough medical evaluation and provided detailed background information. After eight years, researchers were able to account for an impressive 95% of the original group. Of those, 283 (240 men and 43 women) had died.

To probe the connection between death and physical fitness, subjects were grouped into five fitness levels. Naturally, more deaths (primarily from cardiovascular disease and cancer) occur among older subjects, but this was handled by dividing subjects according to age: 20 to 39, 40 to 49, 50 to 59, and 60 years or older.

When this is done, risk of death among those who are least active is revealed as more than triple that of the most active men. Among least active women, the risk is more than 4.5 times as great. For older individuals, the difference in death rates is still more pronounced. These relationships hold true even after factors like blood cholesterol level, blood pressure, smoking, blood sugar levels and family history are accounted for.

A closer look at the data shows something rather surprising: The greatest difference in risk occurs between people classified as least physically fit and the next 20% of subjects. In other words, if you can move just one rung up the fitness ladder, you'll be able to make real progress in improving your prospects for continued good health.

So if you're starting at the bottom and you have nowhere to go but up, the next question is: How do you make it to that crucial next level? Based on their data, the researchers estimate that as little as a brisk walk of 30 to 60 minutes a day is all that's needed. In other words, you don't have to undergo a personality change and turn into a aerobic or jogging fanatic to boost your health odds. You must merely become a pedestrian instead of a lounger. And if all unfit persons became fit, the death rate among men would fall an estimated 9% and among women, over 15%.

With this incentive, you'd think more Americans would get the message. Sadly, fewer than 10% of us over age 18 meet exercise goals set by the Public Health Service as Health Objectives for 1990. Nearly one in three is estimated to be sedentary.

For the year 2000, the Public Health Service would like to see at least 30% of everyone six and older exercise vigorously enough to develop and maintain cardiorespiratory fitness three or more days a week for 20 minutes each. That seems a modest objective, and one with a major health payoff.

But will we attain it? Experts at the Centers for Disease Control suggest we'll have a better chance of doing so if we beef up health-education programs in schools, from kindergarten through high school, and provide children with the motivation, knowledge and skills to lead a physically active life. They also believe that physicians should take more initiative in promoting physical activity, prescribing it as a specific component of treatment for their patients, and in leading community drives to upgrade programs in our schools.

Physicians can, indeed, play a special role. But working to make America a physically fit nation again is a goal for us all. So couch potatoes of the world, arise. You can join in, starting today.

All you need to do is put on some comfortable clothing and sturdy shoes -- and get up off that sofa. It will still be there after you come back from a vigorous, health-giving walk.

FDA Considers Aspartame Safe, but Sets Acceptable Daily Intake

Question: I know aspartame is considered a safe artificial sweetener, but are there any guidelines defining acceptable intake?

Answer: Yes, and the margin of safety is extremely large. Based on available evidence, the Food and Drug Administration has established an acceptable daily intake (ADI) at just 1% of that which has been shown in animals and humans to have no toxic effect. The individual estimate is based on body weight.

In a man weighing about 155 pounds, it translates to the amount you'd get in seventeen 12-ounce cans of soda artificially sweetened with aspartame. Studies show this is well above the daily level usually consumed.

Q: A few years ago the subject of bulimia was constantly splashed across the cover of women's magazines. Lately I haven't seen anything about it. Has the problem subsided?

A: At least one survey has suggested that the behaviors associated with bulimia may be on the decline. Researchers sound a note of caution, however, that bingeing and purging could be replaced by excessive exercise among those who are particularly dissatisfied with their bodies.

When bulimia first emerged as a popular topic, original estimates of its prevalence may have been too high, since the criteria used to define it were overly broad. For instance, sometimes it was the existence -- but not the frequency -- of bingeing and purging that was measured.

Seeking to discover whether these behaviors were on the decline, Craig Johnson, Ph.D., and his colleagues from Northwestern University repeated a survey conducted five years earlier with over 1,200 female high-school students. The second group included nearly 1,100.

Good news first. In 1981, researchers found that 4.1% of the women met criteria for bulimia (and these criteria were somewhat broader than revised standards now used). In 1986, that figure had dropped to 2%. Significantly fewer students binged, used diet pills or reported that there were times when they couldn't stop eating.

Concern about weight and the need to go on a diet had fallen as well. Fewer of the students believed they were overweight, or that their family and friends thought they were overweight. Fewer were on a diet and they felt less pressure from family and friends to start one. Scores on a psychological test that measures drive for thinness dropped too. This is all to the good.

Now, the worrisome side. Scores on another psychological test that measures body dissatisfaction did not change. Dissatisfaction correlates with low self-esteem and personal ineffectiveness.

The researchers suggest that some young women will seek other solutions to counter their perceptions, and that exercise in the "pursuit of strength" will produce a set of side effects related to exercise abuse. Obviously, those who are most vulnerable will exhibit the most severe symptoms. Whether the researchers' prediction is correct, or whether interventions to alter their prophecy can be developed, remains to be seen.

Q: Is fiber useful to individuals with irritable bowel syndrome?

A: The jury's still out. Studies addressing the question have reached different conclusions, due both to problems in study design and to the variable nature of symptoms described as irritable bowel syndrome (IBS).

One recent study is of particular interest. The investigators, from McMaster University Medical Center in Ontario, Canada, used the so-called double-blind crossover. In this approach, neither investigators nor subjects know whether the fiber supplement or a placebo is being used, and all subjects are given supplements part of the time and a placebo for the rest of the time. This trial was extended over seven months, allowing time to examine longer term changes in symptoms.

Over a six-month period, subjects were given four cookies daily, containing either corn fiber or a placebo for 12 weeks at a time. Each trial was separated from the next by a "washout" period to allow any effects to erode. Subjects were asked to score symptoms on a questionnaire as they entered the trial and at four-week intervals thereafter. Pressure measurements to assess changes in motility were also taken.

Curiously, several problems, including pain severity, frequency and consistency of bowel movements, and other abdominal symptoms improved over time both with corn fiber and the placebo. Pain in the lower left side was the most frequent symptom reported. And in this study, the "placebo effect" (the likelihood of reduced symptoms during periods when the cookies contained no bran) was greatest for pain.

But the reduction in symptoms tended to erode over time. A drop in colon pressure with fiber intake did occur, but not to a significant degree. Perhaps one reason for the failure to demonstrate benefit is that fiber would be more effective in relieving constipation. However, only one-third of the subjects reported bowel movements less than twice a week.

This was a small study. Only nine patients completed the entire trial, which lasted seven months. More research is needed to answer your question.

Q: Recently a friend brought me a fruit called a pear-apple and I really enjoyed it. They are quite expensive and so will be only an occasional special treat, but I would like to know more about them. Are they a hybrid between a pear and an apple?

A: No. While both their shape and color suggest they are relatives of golden delicious apples, botanically they are pears. Tottori, or 20th-Century Pears as they are called, were discovered growing wild on the northern coast of Western Japan at the turn of the century, and have since been cultivated. One feature of the King of Japanese Pears, as they are also sometimes called, is the painstaking care they receive as they grow. Blossoms are hand pollinated, and once the fruit begins to mature they are wrapped in wax-treated bags to protect them against insects and disease.

Pears, to varying degrees, have a texture perhaps best described as a little gritty. It is contributed by lignin, a so-called "nonpolysaccharide" component of fiber. Tottori have a mild, aromatic pear flavor and, like other Oriental pears, a more-pronounced gritty texture than some varieties with which we are more familiar. A 3 1/2 ounce serving of pear-apple provides about 40 calories, but most of the fruits weigh considerably more than that.
