931201 FT 01 DEC 93 / Budget 93: Bringing PSBR back to balance Fiscal stance: The government aims to 'bring the PSBR back towards balance over the medium term' and ensure 'the public sector borrows no more than is required to finance its net capital spending'. Page 36 Spending squeezed: Planned general government expenditure next year is Pounds 3.8bn lower than previously forecast, Pounds 2.1bn lower in 1995-96 and Pounds 4.4bn lower in 1996-97. The control total for next year has been reduced to Pounds 251.3bn, Pounds 3.6bn below the level announced in March and a fall in real terms of 1 1/4 per cent. For 1995-96 and 1996-97 the control totals will be Pounds 1.5bn and Pounds 2.9bn lower. This should reduce total spending from 45 per cent of GDP this financial year to 41 per cent in 1998-99. Taxes up: The bulk of the fiscal tightening comes from higher taxes. Taxes go up by an extra Pounds 1.7bn in 1994-95, Pounds 4.9bn in 1995-96 and Pounds 6.1bn in 1996-97. Combined with the tax increases announced in March, total taxes will rise by Pounds 8.5bn next year rising to Pounds 16.4bn in 1996-97. Taxes as a percentage of GDP go up from 36 1/2 per cent this financial year to 39 3/4 per cent in 1996-97 and 40 3/4 per cent in 1998-99. PSBR cut: Public borrowing, including privatisation proceeds, falls from Pounds 50bn this year to Pounds 38bn in 1994-95 - 5.5 per cent of GDP - Pounds 6.5bn lower than forecast last March. The 1996-97 forecast deficit is Pounds 21bn, 2 3/4 per cent of GDP and Pounds 13bn lower than forecast. By 1998-99 the PSBR should fall to 1/4 per cent of GDP, while taxes exceed current spending in 1997-98. The chancellor suspended the full-funding rule, saying he will sell Pounds 7bn fewer gilts next year than would otherwise be needed to fund the PSBR. Pages 36, 39 Taxes: Personal taxes: No change in 20p, 25p or 40p rates of income tax; personal allowance frozen; basic rate limit frozen at Pounds 23,700. The 20p lower band to be extended by further Pounds 500, in line with March budget, to Pounds 3,000. No change to inheritance tax nor exempt amount for capital gains tax. Proposals to yield Pounds 550m in 1994-95 and Pounds 720m in 1995-96. Pages 28, 46 Married couple's allowance: Rate of relief to be cut further from 20 per cent in April 1994 to 15 per cent in April 1995 to yield Pounds 830m in 1995-96 and over Pounds 1bn in a full year. Page 28 Mortgage interest relief Rate of relief to be similarly restricted from 20 per cent to 15 per cent in 1995-96, to raise Pounds 950m, adding Pounds 10 a month for those with mortgages of Pounds 30,000 upwards. Pages 29, 24 Taxes on spending: No change in VAT. Page 24 New taxes on air travel and insurance From October 1995 a new departure tax of Pounds 5 per passenger for flights to anywhere in UK and European Union, and Pounds 10 to other destinations, to raise Pounds 330m in full year. From same month, a 3 per cent tax on most general UK insurance premiums to raise Pounds 750m in a full year. Pages 24, 29, 43 Excise duties: Duty on tobacco products to rise immediately by 7.3 per cent, an increase of 11 pence per pack of 20 cigarettes, and set to rise by 3 per cent a year in real terms in future Budgets. No increase on beer or other spirits, but wine up 2p per bottle on January 1 1994. Vehicle Excise Duty for cars and light vans up Pounds 5 to Pounds 130 from December 1. Petrol and diesel up 3p a litre, with 5 per cent increases planned for future. Page 24 Business taxation: No change to 33 per cent main rate or 25 per cent lower rate of corporation tax, but profits limit for small companies raised by 20 per cent. Prompt payment measures to be introduced. Pages 22; 24; 43 National insurance: Employers' contributions to fall 0.2 per cent to 10.2 per cent. NICs to be cut by 1 per cent for employees earning less than Pounds 200 a week. Page 43 Deregulation for small businesses: To include simpler regime for working out personal tax; an immediate rise in VAT registration threshold for small companies to Pounds 45,00 from Pounds 37,600, enabling 75,000 traders to opt out of VAT altogether; and scrapping of audit requirement for companies with turnover of less than Pounds 90,000. Page 22 Capital Gains Tax: Increase on relief for entrepreneurs when they sell a business on retirement to first Pounds 250,000 of capital gains and half-exemption on next Pounds 750,000. Page 29 VAT-on-fuel compensation package: To give extra help worth Pounds 1 1/4 bn to 15m people, mainly pensioners, by 1996-97. Pensioners all to be eligible for additional 50p a week (70p for couples) on top of normal uprating from April 1994. Same rise again in April 1995, so extra help then totals Pounds 1 for all single pensioners. Cold weather payments up 25 per cent to Pounds 7 a week next winter. All pensioners to qualify for energy efficiency grants. Pensioners Guaranteed Income Bond to be introduced. Pages 19, 28, 44 Spending: Public-sector pay: Public sector pay bill to be frozen at this year's cash level until 1996-97. Pay increases for public sector staff will have to be paid for by greater efficiency. Page 26 Unemployment benefit: New single benefit for the unemployed will replace existing unemployment benefit and income support for the unemployed from April 1996. The new benefit, known as the job seekers' allowance, will be non-means-tested only for the first six months. After that, recipients will be means-tested. Pages 19, 28 Childcare: New allowance of Pounds 28 per week available from next autumn to help those on family credit who need to pay for child care. Move designed to help single-mothers on benefit return to work. Pages 26, 28 Sick pay: Large employers not reimbursed for the cost of statutory sick pay from April next year. Companies whose national insurance bills are lower than Pounds 20,000 to be reimbursed after four weeks. Page 26 Invalidity benefit: New incapacity benefit, requiring a 'tighter and more objective medical test', will replace existing invalidity benefit from April 1995. Will be subject to tax for new claimants. Page 19 Pensions: The state pension age for women will rise to 65 from 60, phased in over ten years starting in 2010. Pages 19, 28, 45 Local authority spending: Total standard spending rises will be limited to 2.3 per cent next year after adjusting for changes in local authority functions, and excluding care in the community. Page 27 Housing: The Housing Corporation's capital programme cut from previous plans. Control total for housing will be cut by Pounds 0.9bn in real terms in next three years. Transport: Reductions in plans for the roads programme will allow some resources to shift to British Rail and London Transport investment programmes, but overall spending by the Department of Transport next year Pounds 290m lower than announced in March. Control total in real terms will be reduced by Pounds 1.3bn over the next three years. Page 27 Education: Spending on education to rise by Pounds 670m next year, an increase of 6.8 per cent, but the main rates of student grants will be cut by 10 per cent. Shortfall will be met by an increase in student loans. The new control total will increase by Pounds 500m in real terms over the next three years. Pages 27, 28, 47 Defence: Savings on defence, compared with previous plans, total Pounds 250m next year and Pounds 500m in 1995/96. Savings will be delivered through lower procurement and employment costs and through the planned sale of married quarters for service personnel. The new control total will be reduced by Pounds 2.7bn in real terms by 1996/97. Pages 27, 47 National health service: Spending to rise by 1.5 per cent in real terms next year, with more emphasis on increases in efficiency and containment of pay and the drugs bill. Pages 27, 47 Employment and training: A new apprenticeship scheme will increase work-based training and increase the number of people receiving technical and craft skills. Page 26 Home office: A reordering of priorities will allow for more prison places and victim support. Pages 27, 47 The forecast: Low inflation: Taxes increases should boost inflation in the short-term only. Inflation, excluding mortgage interest payments, is forecast to rise from an annual rate of 3 per cent in the current quarter to 3 1/4 per cent in the final quarter of next year, before falling to the lower half of the 0-4 per cent target range. Pages 21, 42 Slow growth: The economy will continue to recovery only slowly over the coming year. GDP forecast to rise 1 1/2 per cent this year, up from last March's 1 1/4 per cent forecast. Growth is expected to rise to 2 1/4 per cent next year. Pages 21, 37, 38
