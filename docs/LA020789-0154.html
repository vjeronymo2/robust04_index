February 7, 1989, Tuesday, Late Final Edition

VOLCKER BACKS BUSH PLAN TO SALVAGE S&LS;

RIEGLE, GARN VOW QUICK, NONPARTISAN ACTION ON PROPOSAL

Former Federal Reserve Board Chairman Paul A. Volcker today gave a general endorsement to the Bush Administration's effort to rescue the savings and loan industry, as members of Congress said they intend to act on a bailout package as quickly as possible.

Volcker, testifying before the Senate Banking Committee, said that while he had not had a chance to review the Bush proposal in detail he believed that it addressed all of the major problems facing the S&L industry.

"I welcome the Administration's initiative this early in the (congressional) session," Volcker said. "The President's program provides a very good base for your consideration of legislation."

Competitive Danger

Volcker said he was concerned that the deposit insurance premiums on savings and loans and the nation's commercial 13,000 banks not be raised so high that it put the institutions at a competitive disadvantage with other financial entities.

"My sense is that the increased insurance premiums goes to the edge of viability in terms of not undermining the health of the financial industry, which is under heavy competition," he said.

Volcker also said he disagreed with the Administration's proposal to abolish the Federal Home Loan Bank Board as an independent regulatory agency. President Bush's plan would retain only the chairman of the bank board, making him the head of an S&L regulatory agency that would be placed under the jurisdiction of the Treasury Department.

Would Keep Bank Board

Volcker said that instead of abolishing the three-member bank board he would prefer leaving it as an independent regulatory agency but split off its oversight of the S&L deposit insurance fund.

Committee Chairman Donald W. Riegle Jr. (D-Mich.) said he and Sen. Jake Garn of Utah, the panel's ranking Republican, plan to introduce the Administration's package as soon as it is submitted and schedule early hearings in an effort to vote the measure out in as short a time as possible.

"I am determined to see us act quickly in the committee to match the speed of the Administration," Riegle said at the beginning of the hearing. Both Riegle and Garn called on the other committee members to set aside partisan differences and work with dispatch to report a bill to the full Senate.

"This problem is serious enough that we don't have time to be partisan about it," Garn said. "I hope we can get together in the next few months to pass legislation."
