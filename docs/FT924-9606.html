921105 FT 05 NOV 92 / The Maastricht Vote: Appeals to support government APPEALS TO wavering rebels to support the government came from senior Tory backbenchers on both sides of the argument, though the moves to rally backbenchers also contained warnings for ministers about their conduct of the UK presidency of the Community, Alison Smith writes. The message from the few hardline Tory Euro-sceptics who spoke in the debate was defiant, however. Mr Michael Carttiss, the Tory MP for Great Yarmouth, said he was going to vote both against the Labour amendment and the government motion. He said unless the prime minister established a coherent economic programme, he risked going 'the same way as George Bush, only quicker. This Maastricht Bill is a monstrous irrelevance to the real issues that face this government and the country.' Sir Edward Heath, the former prime minister who took the UK into the EC, urged potential rebels to recognise that, in previous cases where the Tory party had been divided, such as Suez, dissidents had held back from endangering the government. Mr Paul Channon, a former cabinet minister, professed himself sceptical on the Maastricht treaty but argued that Tories should support the government to strengthen its hand in negotiating to achieve a decentralised EC. 'It would have saved us all a great deal of trouble if the French had voted against it,' he said.
