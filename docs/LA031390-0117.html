March 13, 1990, Tuesday, Orange County Edition

SHOWERS, HAIL AND SNOW FALL ON COUNTY

Scattered rain, hail and snow peppered a wide area of the Southland on Monday afternoon as a slow-moving storm system moved through the region.

The showers were steady and relatively light in Orange County, although several Irvine residents reported that hail pelted the city for about 30 minutes at about 5 p.m.

Malinee Churanakoses, 16, said she was hit by hail as she left a friend's house on Lindstrom Street. "It was thundering down rocks," said Churanakoses, who moved to Irvine with her parents four years ago. "It was very surprising. I thought I was back in Seattle."

Elsewhere in the county, snow dusted the Santiago and Modjeska peaks in the Santa Ana Mountains. And a water spout -- which occurs when a tornado-like column of air passes over water -- was reported near Dana Point just before 5:30 p.m. and dissipated about 10 minutes later, the National Weather Service said.

Parts of the San Bernardino Mountains recorded up to 10 inches of snow in a matter of hours, authorities said.

Light snow also fell in the Grapevine area on Interstate 5 and on California 14 in the Antelope Valley and a dusting could be seen on the San Gabriel Mountains from the Civic Center.

In addition to snow, the system dumped short-lived rain and hail showers over the Southern California foothills and valleys. Rain also was reported in downtown Los Angeles, Long Beach and Santa Ana.

While the length and severity of the rainfall varied, forecasters predicted as much as half an inch of rain in the heavier showers, along with the possibility of hail and gusty winds to 40 m.p.h.

The weather created serious traffic problems in some areas. The Simi Valley Freeway was closed for more than three hours after a tractor trailer carrying tile overturned on wet pavement about 2:45 p.m. One person was injured.

"Traffic is just screwed up all over on every one of our freeways," said California Highway Patrol spokesman Jim Wills. "The accident rate is higher than normal."

Agriculture officials canceled Monday night's malathion spraying over 47 square miles of the San Fernando Valley because of the rain.

Agriculture officials in Orange County expressed delight about the rainfall. Orange County Agriculture Commissioner James D. Harnett welcomed the showers, saying that the county's farmers "need every drop of rain they can get."

"The rain may make it a nuisance for people who are harvesting their crops now but by and large the benefits of rain overshadow the negative points," Harnett said. "There is no substitute for good rain. It does things that imported water cannot do."

Meteorologist Rick Dittmann of WeatherData Inc., however, said the showers would not significantly affect the prolonged drought being experienced in Southern California.

Staff writer Davan Maharaj contributed to this report.
