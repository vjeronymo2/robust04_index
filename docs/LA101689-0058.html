October 16, 1989, Monday, Home Edition

TO RUSSIA FOR LOVE;

MOVIES: SOVIETS COOPERATE IN FILMING OF LE CARRE'S LOVE STORY SET AGAINST A BACKDROP OF INTERNATIONAL ESPIONAGE.

The skies were leaden, the Ukraina Hotel was equally gloomy and the passers-by were already bundled against the cold wind from the Moscow River as Sean Connery stepped between two dirty black Volgas, the cars of Soviet apparatchiki, in one of those seemingly casual meetings that spies so carefully arrange.

The actor who became James Bond in half a dozen movie thrillers -- including "From Russia with Love" -- in the 1960s, Connery was again in the role of a British secret agent, but this film's theme is more "to Russia for love" than about foiling the plots of the Soviet KGB.

Connery is playing Barley Blair, the central character in John Le Carre's new novel, "The Russia House," and he was meeting Katya, played by Michelle Pfeiffer, who begins as a courier for a Soviet scientist smuggling secret military information to the West and becomes the woman for whom Blair is ready to sacrifice everything.

"Glasnost and perestroika seem to be serious," Connery said, referring to President Mikhail S. Gorbachev's policies of political openness and overall reforms. "They are letting us do this film here although it's about spies and traitors and that sort of thing, and they are going to publish Le Carre's book themselves in Russian. You have to say that the system here has changed a bit.

"Five years ago, they would not have let us in, and probably would have denounced the film as 'anti-Soviet.' Today, they are helping us all they can to do this film. I think they have gotten over their secretiveness and paranoia."

To Paul Maslansky, the film's co-producer, "The Russia House" is far more a love story in a setting of espionage than one of East-West politics merely spiced with a bit of romance.

"I think Le Carre wanted to write a love story, a story about people and what motivates them and what predicaments they find themselves in as a result of love, and naturally he set it in the world that he knows best," Maslansky said. "But it's also quite true that Gorbachev's Russia is not the 'evil empire' as people used to imagine the Soviet Union to be, and that means it is a place where this kind of romance can happen without some threat to the state.

"A Le Carre novel, however, is very difficult to make into a movie. He is very, very wordy, but he uses words to draw pictures that are hard to reproduce on the screen. He seldom writes action sequences -- there are no car crashes, no chases -- and he loves ambiguity. I hope this picture will be a tapestry of Moscow and Leningrad, one of great mood and rich feeling."

Maslansky, who has produced two other movies in the Soviet Union, said that script was reviewed by Moscow Film Studio, one of whose affiliated companies has contracted to provide some equipment and crew and arrange the scenes for filming.

"In the past, they wanted script control, and they insisted on it," Maslansky said. "This time, they just wanted to make sure there was no pornography, nothing anti-Soviet, nothing that would give the Soviet Union a bad image. But this is a story in which everyone comes out quite human."

With a screenplay by Tom Stoppard, "The Russia House" has Fred Schepisi as its director and producer and a budget of $21 million from Pathe International. Its cast includes Roy Scheider, James Fox, John Mahoney and Klaus-Maria Brandauer.

Filming began in Leningrad two weeks ago and will continue here until early November, when the cast and crew move to London, Lisbon and Vancouver. The film is scheduled for release next autumn.

Although Maslansky and Schepisi praise the cooperation they received from Corona Films, the Soviet-West German joint venture that is assisting them here, the filming is something of a study in mismatched cultures.

"For us, time is money, and the meter is running all the time," Maslansky said. "For them, there is always tomorrow, so no need to push so hard today. They get paid no more for working harder or faster, but we lose money if we don't. What we use five or six people to do -- say, lighting a scene -- they will use 11 or 12.

"On the other hand, our directors can do a lot of takes of a scene, maybe a dozen, and theirs are given much less film, enough to do perhaps four takes, making them more careful in setting up a scene."

None of this surprised Maslansky, who made one film, "The Red Tent," with Connery here 25 years ago, and then took over the production in Leningrad of "The Bluebird," with Elizabeth Taylor and Jane Fonda in 1974.

"Nothing has changed really," Maslansky said. "Time has generally stood still. Their system still constrains them in many ways. And it is tough to get people to work 14 hours a day when they get no more out of it than if they worked six or seven.

"When we did 'Bluebird,' we had the right people, and it worked. This also is working well, extremely well. As many mistakes are made by my side as by theirs. But, when you have shooting costs of maybe $10,000 an hour, the time-money ratio is severe."

The filming is a massive undertaking in logistics. More than 50 specialized vehicles, everything from catering wagons to sound trucks, have been brought from Britain and Finland. Tons of food have been imported to feed the cast and crew, about 60 from abroad and a similar number hired locally, so that there will be a minimum loss of shooting time.
