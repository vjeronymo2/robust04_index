920218 FT 18 FEB 92 / Parliament and Politics: Main parties neck and neck in poll of polls - Public opinion IF THE opinion polls are to be believed, the voters have made up their minds already. With Mr John Major now virtually committed to an April 9 general election, the latest batch confirms that the time, money and energy expended by the two main parties since the new year has been largely wasted. Rather like the opposing sides in the First World War both Labour and Conservatives have fired huge amounts of ammunition to little effect. The FT's weighted average of the last seven opinion polls shows them firmly stuck in trenches giving each a fraction less than 40 per cent the vote. Neither side has budged since the autumn. Only the Liberal Democrats, apparently benefiting rather than suffering from the publicity surrounding Mr Paddy Ashdown's admission that he had an affair with his former secretary, can claim a marginal improvement. There is potential, however, for both Mr John Major, the prime minister and Mr Neil Kinnock, the Labour leader, to claim the initiative. The prime minister can set the agenda with a tax-cutting Budget. Mr Kinnock can claim that the voters have still to fully appreciate how deep the recession has become. The present deadlock has fuelled the growing feeling at Westminster that the most likely result of the election will be a hung parliament. On their present performance the FT's computer gives the Conservatives 317 seats (against 326 needed for an overall majority), Labour 298 and the Liberal Democrats 11.
