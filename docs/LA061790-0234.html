June 17, 1990, Sunday, Home Edition

CLEANER GAS: IS IT CLEAN ENOUGH?;

ENVIRONMENT: OIL FIRMS HAVE SCRAMBLED TO BRING OUT LOWER-EMISSION FUELS. BUT STATE AND FEDERAL REGULATORS WANT THEM TO GO FURTHER. THE INDUSTRY SAYS THE PRICE WILL BE HIGH, THE SUPPLY IN JEOPARDY.

Oil refiners are in a drag race for the public's gasoline dollars, but the flag they're after isn't checkered. It's green.

Oil companies that once sold high octane, drivability or detergent additives are now touting fuels that burn cleaner and produce less smog.

Last week Chevron Corp. became the latest of eight oil companies to introduce a lower-emission gasoline since Atlantic Richfield Co. started the trend last September with its EC-1 gasoline.

Just how clean are these new fuels? Not as clean as they probably will have to be. And just how far is the oil industry willing to go in producing cleaner gasoline? Apparently, not much farther than it already has.

Ultimately, the question may become: How clean can we make gasoline and how much are we willing to pay for it?

The oil companies hope to sell more gasoline by exploiting growing environmental awareness among consumers. But as Congress and the California Air Resources Board contemplate stricter clean air rules, the oil companies also want to demonstrate that gasoline should be the cleaner-burning fuel of the future.

Arco's EC-1 may have helped boost gasoline atop the agenda of lower-emissions fuels, displacing methanol, ethanol and compressed natural gas as the clean fuel of choice among federal regulators. "We didn't come out with EC-1 to increase share of market but to increase share of mind," said George H. Babikian, president of Arco Products Co. in Los Angeles.

So far, however, none of the new gasolines would satisfy all the proposed regulations before Congress or the state air resources board.

EC-1 comes closest. Others, such as Shell SU 2000E, Chevron Supreme Unleaded and Exxon Plus, differ only slightly from ordinary gasolines. Others fall somewhere in between. Phillips, Conoco, Marathon and Diamond Shamrock are also selling reduced-emissions gasoline in various parts of the country, and Amoco, Sun and Citgo are considering bringing out their own reformulated gasolines soon.

All the new gasolines are being sold in tightly restricted markets, reflecting the limited ability of oil refiners to produce them. The fuels are being made by juggling components from other gasolines -- and refiners can take that only so far without making major investments in new refining equipment.

That may underlie the oil industry's opposition to new gasoline formulations proposed for a revised federal Clean Air Act.

Despite its rush to sell lower-emissions fuels, the oil industry -- led by its main trade group, the American Petroleum Institute -- lobbied hard but unsuccessfully against the proposed Congressional formulas, which it derisively called "Government Gas."

The API estimated that the new regulations would require $10 billion to $30 billion in capital expenditures by refiners, forcing up to one-third of them out of business. The new rules would mean retail prices could be higher by 10 to 15 cents per gallon and could even lead to gasoline shortages, the institute claimed.

The industry would prefer that Congress set targets for auto emission reductions rather than specify gasoline formulas. It would be more efficient and less costly to let refiners then devise their own formulas to meet the targets, the industry argues.

It also wants regulators to await the results of a multimillion-dollar joint research project being conducted by the oil and auto industries on various formulations of gasoline before settling on a formula. Those results won't be ready until year-end.

But air quality officials insist on specific standards.

"There's no other way to regulate the actual emissions from fuel, because there can be a variety of cars that would use it," said state Air Resources Board spokesman Bill Sessa. At issue are the proposed changes in the federal Clean Air Act contained in bills approved by the U.S. Senate in April and the House of Representatives last month. The bills differ in a number of ways, but both would mandate changes in the way gasoline is refined. A final version of the revisions must be hammered out in a conference committee.

Meanwhile, the state Air Resources Board staff is developing proposals for reformulated gasoline. The board is supposed to vote on them in September. Where federal and state regulations overlap, the state board's rules would take precedence. Unlike Congress, the state is also contemplating rules that would require the use of alternative fuels beyond gasoline.

The proposed rules would be among the most sweeping since state and federal governments began dictating what goes into gasoline. California led the way in the 1970s by setting limits on vapor pressure, or the tendency of gasoline to evaporate. It later required the phase-out of lead, a toxic substance linked to learning disorders in children.

Since then, other states that once regulated only octane have begun to set standards for gasoline components.

The federal government got into the act later. In 1975, it followed California in requiring the phase-out of lead nationwide, perhaps the biggest change in the refining of gasoline until now. Just last year, the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency set national standards for vapor pressure.

"We have gone through major sea changes in the required chemical composition of gasoline over our history," said industry analyst Bernard J. Picchi at Salomon Bros. in New York. "Perhaps the most dramatic switch was the change from leaded to unleaded gasoline, and I think that was actually a more wrenching change for the industry than what is being contemplated here -- and that transition took place without there being a major dislocation."

In contrast to the API, the EPA estimates the added cost to consumers of the new gas rules will be only 5 to 10 cents per gallon. Moreover, the API's $10-billion to $30-billion capital estimate is high, critics argue.

The Bush Administration puts the cost of the entire clean air law -- not just provisions dealing with reformulated gasoline -- at just $21.5 billion.

There is also disagreement over what effects the House and Senate versions of the Clean Air Act would have.

Both bills would require the addition beginning in 1992 of high levels of oxygenates to gasoline sold in the nine smoggiest U.S. cities, including Los Angeles. More oxygenates would promote more efficient burning and reduce certain emissions, proponents argue.

Both bills would also make refiners reduce aromatics and benzene, the most polluting components of gasoline. (Unlike the Senate bill, the House bill gives refiners flexibility on gasoline formulation, if they can meet or exceed emissions standards.)

"The legislation passed by the House sets extremely stringent standards for reformulated gasoline," said Charles J. DiBona, president of API.

He added in a statement: "Unless substantial changes are made in this legislation by a House-Senate conference committee, it may not be possible for the industry to continue to meet the fuel needs of consumers."

Among other things, API argued that there is a significant shortage of a key oxygenate, methyl tertiary butyl ether, or MTBE. Lower-emissions gasolines now on the market all contain MTBE, but generally at less than half the levels that would be required by 1994.

Critics of the oil industry argue that refiners are less worried about higher costs or fuel shortages than they are about losing control of what goes into the nation's gasoline pool -- and giving up some of the attendant profits.

By requiring higher levels of oxygenates, Congress will force refiners to go to outside sources for the chemicals if they can't make enough themselves.

If the oxygenate is MTBE, that would mean eventually relinquishing control of 15% of the gasoline pool. Some of that business would undoubtedly go to foreign manufacturers.

If refiners are forced to rely on ethanol, it would mean a sizable increase in business for Archer Daniels Midland, the agriculture conglomerate that produces about 45% of the nation's ethanol.

Critics also argue that API has exaggerated the disruption that would result from the new rules.

Even some refiners admit privately that they can accommodate the requirements of the proposed clean air bills, though they may not like having to shell out the capital investments.

"Nobody likes to be forced to make dramatic changes that aren't kind of market-driven and that you have time to work into," said David Kem, general manager of marketing in North America for Conoco Inc. in Houston. "But this industry has adapted to other things, and I think it will adapt to this."

"I do not think it is going to result in the closure of refineries; in fact, I would argue just the opposite," Picchi said. "As a result of the new higher level of capital spending required to retool refineries . . . , you'll probably see refiners at the same time taking advantage of the situation to spend more money to expand capacity."

That's because the clean air requirements could make it easier to get permits that might otherwise be hard-won, particularly in environmentally sensitive areas such as Los Angeles, he said.

Atlantic Richfield, for one, is planning to expand gasoline-making capacity at its refineries in Carson and Cherry Point, Wash., at the same time that it retools to make more reformulated gasoline.

In the next five years, Arco plans to spend $1.1 billion at the refineries to make reformulated gasoline. After that, it will spend $1 billion to $1.3 billion to meet future requirements for the fuel.

Shell, the nation's largest gasoline marketer, which introduced a reformulated gasoline in April in the nine smoggiest cities, is studying upgrades at six of its seven refineries -- a process that will take years and cost billions.

MTBE capacity nationally is about 100,000 barrels per day, well short of the 289,000 barrels expected to be needed for gasoline consumed in the nine smoggiest cities as required under the proposed revisions, said Roy Sugimoto, a researcher with the consulting firm DeWitt & Co. in Houston, which tracks MTBE projects.

But 110,000 barrels per day of new capacity is already planned, and construction on several plants could begin within the next year or two at an average cost of $160 million to $180 million per plant, he said.

Tenneco is planning one of the new MTBE plants -- a 12,500-barrel-per-day facility to be completed in the spring of 1992.

"We are aware of an additional five to six projects of similar size now being planned in the industry and expect that between eight and twelve projects could be built in the United States by 1994 to meet growing octane and clean-air demands," Tenneco executive Michael Falco wrote in a letter to Sen. Thomas A. Daschle, the South Dakota Democrat who co-wrote the Senate amendment.

Meanwhile, it's too early to say whether any of the existing reformulated gasolines is taking its market by storm. But early signs are that the fuels are winning some public acceptance.

Arco says EC-1, being sold in the Los Angeles area to former customers of its leaded regular gasoline, has been gaining market share slowly each month and now accounts for about a third of the leaded regular market in Southern California.

That performance is particularly good given that the regular leaded market is declining, Arco's Babikian said.

Have the new gasolines had a measurable effect on air quality? Again, it's too soon to tell. California air quality officials confirm Arco's claims that EC-1 would cut pollutants from older cars by about 20%. But clean-air advocates agree that the current reformulated gasolines are only a first step.

"We have no idea how clean you can make a refined petroleum product," said David Hawkins, senior attorney with the Natural Resources Defense Council, an environmental group. "And I'm not sure the industry has any idea either."
