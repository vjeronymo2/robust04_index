May 13, 1990, Sunday, Home Edition

POP FACES: CHADWICK'S 'HOUSE': IS THIS BLASPHEMY?

A lot of rock music has been termed blasphemous, though rarely by the people who created it.

But that's the term used by Guy Chadwick, singer-songwriter of the English quartet House of Love, to describe his use of religious imagery in several songs on the band's new album, "House of Love." The group will be at the Roxy on June 15 and 16.

"I don't mean to be blasphemous," the sunken-cheeked Chadwick, 30, said recently over a beer in the bar of a West Hollywood hotel. "But I'm sort of challenging my own beliefs. I don't know how I feel about religion or God. The power of religion has always interested me, and to an extent I'm flirting with it in a dangerous way. I do sometimes think, 'God, should I write that? Will He let me get away with that?' "

The truth is, Chadwick's references to Jesus and God are most often ambiguous and ambivalent, and at worst questioning. His attitude, he says, was shaped by his upbringing, in which, after spending his first two years in Germany where his father was stationed with the British Army, he was exposed to a colorful world of wonders first in Singapore and then Malaya. But at age 13 he found himself stuck in a relatively drab boarding school in the English Midlands.

"It was dreary, and in some ways affected me," he said. "I remember being disappointed in my adolescence -- a particularly bad hand dealt me, moving from somewhere like (Asia) to such a boring place."

Then in the late '80s, the flowering punk scene turned his head. "It literally made me pick up a guitar and learn to play," he said. "It was really that corny."

After several years of struggling to get a foothold in music, Chadwick signed an ill-fated solo deal with RCA in England in 1983. Then four years ago he formed House of Love, which released an independent album in 1988, drawing critical praise for its brisk, thoughtful guitar-pop. The new album, with a sound that harks back both to punk and '60s pop in a manner somewhat akin to that of the Church or the Stone Roses, has won even greater accolades in the British rock press.

Some rock fans may consider Chadwick's biggest blasphemy to be a song on the new album titled "Beatles and Stones," in which he sings, "The Beatles and the Stones / Made it good to be alone," countering the communal-spirit party line associated with those groups' music.

"If anything, that song's reverent," Chadwick maintained. "I vaguely remember seeing Jimi Hendrix on 'Top of the Pops,' " he said. "And I barely remember the Beatles."

And he readily acknowledges ambitions that are far from Beatlesque. Instead, he admires the accomplishments of the Only Ones, a late-'70s English band greatly loved by those who remember it, but remembered by few.

"At least they made some records, left something behind," Chadwick said. "It wasn't all in vain. They left something and got something as well." STEVE HOCHMAN
