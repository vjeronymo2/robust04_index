BFN [Text] Despite the progress made thus far this year compared to 1993, the amount of precursors confiscated during the first four months of 1994 accounts for only 3.5 percent of the total amount used by drug traffickers to process cocaine paste and cocaine. Drug enforcement personnel have already confiscated 37,646 kg of chemical precursors from several trafficking organizations in just four months this year. By contrast, Interior Ministry figures show that during 1993, only 74,200 kg of chemicals -- including sulphuric acid, acetone, hydrochloric acid, sodium carbonate, potassium permanganate, and ether -- were confiscated. Drug analysts and DEA experts noted that 13 clandestine airfields have been destroyed in the Upper Huallaga region, as well as a 2,000-meter strip in Quempiri (near Satipo) and another near Tarapoto. They have also destroyed 10 pits and 11 maceration labs that were equipped with microwave ovens and other sophisticated equipment. Intelligence agents have concluded that the geographical location of the Apurimac-Ene basin is the reason why it has become in recent months the main route for chemicals for the Peruvian-Colombian mafia organization laboratories. Drug enforcement forces have noted, however, that no major seizures have been made yet owing to the complicity of the local residents and coca growers, who in some cases have been forced by former Shining Path members -- who now work for the drug mafia -- to remain silent to protect the traffickers. The analysts believe the traffickers have conscripted residents and peasants in the jungle of Satipo, Oxapampa, Ciudad Constitucion, Puerto Bermudez, Icozaxin, Huanta, La Mar, San Francisco, Palmapampa, Santa Rosa, La Convencion, and Palestina in the illegal drug trade. A few weeks ago, police confiscated chemical supplies in the townships of San Pedro (Villa Rica-Oxapampa) and Palestina, where a CB-1420 aircraft crashed and its passengers managed to escape with the help of local residents. The chemicals are also being transported in small boats along the Pichis, Palcazu, Ene, Perene, and Apurimac rivers. The township of Palmapampa (La Mar-Ayacucho), which is on the Apurimac River, continues to be the transaction center for the "firms," which are guarded by mercenaries armed with modern weapons. The commander of drug enforcement operations is reinforcing his personnel with specialized personnel in the region, where until recently there were just 12 policemen and a small Air Force base, which tried to detect the small planes used by traffickers. Some of those "firms" have moved their operations toward the Tambopata basin where, protected by the intricate wilderness, they set up laboratories to produce the drugs that are sent to Brazil.
