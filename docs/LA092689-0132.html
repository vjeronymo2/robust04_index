September 26, 1989, Tuesday, Orange County Edition

BRIEFCASE: SCIENCE / TECHNOLOGY

The situation: A U.S. air base has just been battered by enemy rockets and its runways are now pockmarked and littered with wreckage. But the runways must be cleared before American jets can take off to launch retaliatory strikes.

The problem: How do you quickly repair the runway damage without putting servicemen's lives in danger from another enemy attack?

The solution: Send in the robots.

To test the feasibility of such a situation, the Air Force has granted a contract to Odetics, an Anaheim technology company, to study how robots could be used to do the job. The firm is developing a system, dubbed Rapid Runway Repair, in which remotely controlled robotic vehicles would be used to clear a runway within two hours after an attack, Odetics officials said.

The Air Force believes robots may offer several advantages over using humans for the same tasks. Those benefits include "a reduction in manpower for repair operations, savings of vehicles and facilities maintained for runway repair and the removal of personnel from the hazards of working in a post-attack environment."

Odetics said it expects to complete the study early in 1990. The company did not provide a specific dollar value for the Air Force contract. Compiled by David Olmos, Times staff writer
