April 26, 1990, Thursday, Home Edition

WORK FOR PRISONERS

In response to David Glidden's column "Put Inmates to Work, Make Prison Produce," Opinion, April 1:

Obviously, Glidden has no contact with prisons or their inmates. Prison slave labor is alive and well, it is called UNICOR, the Federal Prison Industries. Prisoners are paid from 22 cents per hour to $1.10 per hour to produce goods for the military (in direct competition with "major corporations"), the judicial branch of government and prisons themselves. It is a multimillion-dollar industry. UNICOR makes military uniforms, sheets, curtains, furniture, cables used on aircraft carriers, safety glasses/goggles, business forms, etc. Part of the profits are used to fund the education programs at all federal prisons.

I have a master's degree in education. I taught school for 10 years. When I was arrested, I was an executive assistant at a concert company. I have no prior arrests. I've never been in trouble. I am 42 years old, white, female. I teach the GED program to fellow inmates who are helping themselves to be more productive when they leave here. My crime -- I unwittingly carried 2 kilos of cocaine from Los Angeles to Seattle by air. My sentence -- 8 years, 1 month with no possibility of parole. We are not "caged animals." We don't live "passively like domestic pets." We work very hard. Before teaching GED, I worked in the prison kitchen cleaning dishes, floors and pots and pans. For 98 hours of work, I was paid $10.78. Candy bars cost 55 cents and copies cost 15 cents a piece.

I am not "plotting" my next crime. I did not plot the last one. I have discovered many women like myself, who were working, trying to make ends met, but ended up supplementing their income. We are not waiting for our next opportunity "to prey on society." We want to go home and take care of our children.

Perhaps, if inmates had been given decent-paying jobs, they would have had the "byproduct of self-respect," and never been incarcerated. It costs $25,000 per year to keep me in prison; that is more money than I ever made teaching school.

JACQUELINE HENDRICKS

Dublin, Calif.
