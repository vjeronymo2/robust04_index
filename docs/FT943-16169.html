940706 FT 06 JUL 94 / Plugs ruling is setback for two-pin campaign The campaign to abandon the UK's three-pin plug and socket system in favour of a proposed two-pin 'plug for Europe' received a blow last night after the Department of Trade and Industry announced new regulations for domestic appliances. The controversial regulations, first proposed in January 1992, will require most domestic appliances made in or imported into the UK to be supplied with a correctly fused 13-amp plug from February 1 next year. Supporters of harmonisation have been fighting the proposals because they believe that they will lock the UK into the three-pin, 13-amp ring-main system for years to come. Mr Simon Hossack, a leading campaigner for the harmonised European system, said: 'This is a triumph of politics over common sense.' The regulations were a barrier to trade and would impede progress of a vital single market goal, he said. Mr Hossack added: 'Whatever plug is fitted to appliances will effectively dictate the plug and socket system that the country uses. There would be no incentive for the consumer to fit the new harmonised system when rewiring if he had to cut off the plug supplied with the appliance.' The new regulations are strongly supported by the UK plug and socket industry and the Royal Society for the Prevention of Accidents on safety grounds. In a parliamentary written answer yesterday Lord Strathclyde, consumer affairs minister, said: 'Fatalities and injuries occur each year because of incorrectly fitted electric plugs. These regulations represent an important step forward in consumer safety for the British public.'
