BFN [`Ke'-signed report: "Support for the Development of Air and Space Travel Is Essential"] [Text] "State scientific support for the air and space industry is essential," said Economics Minister Guenter Rexrodt, opening the International Air and Space Show at Berlin's Schoenefeld Airport on Saturday [28 May]. "Although this industry is relatively small in terms of the number of employees and turnover, it is nevertheless of great strategic importance. The existence of an efficient air and space industry is of vital importance for Germany, if only because of its enormous technological development possibilities," said the minister. "Although on the one hand the aviation and space market offers enormous growth opportunities over the next decade, and on the other hand the state's financial capabilities are becoming increasingly constrained, the state must nevertheless offer help because the German aviation and space enterprises will probably be unable to face competition from stronger enterprises on their own," Rexrodt continued. In this connection the minister spoke out in favor of the government concentrating on key technologies in Germany and gradually abolishing subsidies for those industrial branches that are of diminishing importance. He recalled that the most successful joint European industrial program, the Airbus project, initiated by means of a German-French agreement exactly 25 years ago on 29 May 1969, or the development of the Ariana carrier rocket, which is used on 60 percent of commercial satellite launches these days, would not have been possible without state assistance. "In this way, we also succeeded in constructing a competitive European counterweight to the market- dominant American space industry," said Rexrodt. Against this background, Rexrodt sharply criticized German Aerospace's [Dasa] decision not to take part in this year's show. He said he was sorry that this enterprise did not consider it necessary to support the Berlin Air Show's efforts to regain its international importance. Earlier on, Wolfgang Piller, president of the Association of German Air and Space Industry, had said that the German aviation industry requires greater security, and warned that with the coming into effect of the GATT Treaty, the industry still lacks the opportunities that the U.S. aviation industry has. The Berlin show, which was visited by over 40,000 people on its first day, was first of all overshadowed by a disaster. On the morning of opening day, a small Russian Yak-58 aircraft crashed on approach. The show lasts until 5 June. Some 134,000 people visited it two years ago. There is no doubt that a subject of great interest this year will be the new Airbus A-321, as a result of which the show is developing into an important trade and information forum between East and West. Thus, the countries of Central and East Europe are particularly well represented at this year's International Air and Space Show, which is organized by the Berlin Fair Committee and the Association of German Air and Space Industry, with the support of the Brandenburg and Berlin Laender and the German Federation.
