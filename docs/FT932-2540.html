930618 FT 18 JUN 93 / Parliament and Politics: Property management tenders worth Pounds 1.25bn THE government yesterday announced plans to put Pounds 1.25bn of property management work out to tender from 1996, through the extension of compulsory competitive tendering to the management of 4m council houses and flats in England. The services to be put out to tender include rent collection, allocating properties and arranging lettings, and the enforcement of tenancy agreements. The successful bidders will also be responsible for managing vacant properties, caretaking, cleaning, repairs and maintenance. The government has decided to leave the administration of housing benefit, operation of the right-to-buy regulations and housing the homeless outside the tendering package. Councils will continue to be responsible for these functions. Local authorities will be expected to divide their housing stock into management units of up to 5,000 homes for tendering purposes. However, authorities with less than 7,500 homes can put them out to tender as a single unit. The 1996 start date will encourage the development of a market in providing housing management services, Sir George Young, the housing minister, said yesterday. He expected chartered surveyors, management agents, housing associations and specialist companies to be interested in bidding for the work. Councils will have to consult tenants in drawing up the tender specification, and will be encouraged to involve them in selecting the winning bid. The government expects that compulsory competitive tendering will save up to 6 per cent of the money spent on housing management. In the London Borough of Wandsworth, putting part of their housing management out to tender had saved Pounds 500,000 on annual running costs. Sir George said that he also expected that CCT would lead to more efficient management of local authority housing stock and greater responsiveness to tenants.
