March 31, 1990, Saturday, San Diego County Edition

FOLKS WHO PRESERVE FOLK

When lease problems prompted Bill Goldsmith and his wife, Pearl Wolfe, to close the Old Time Cafe in January, 1988, the San Diego folk music community suffered a big loss.

For more than eight years, the Leucadia coffeehouse had been the only county venue to regularly showcase touring folk acts from all over the world. It was Goldsmith's and Wolfe's desire to lessen the blow that led them, in the final days of the cafe, to establish San Diego Folk Heritage. The nonprofit, all-volunteer organization has since kept alive the Old Time Cafe's legacy by presenting a series of local folk concerts, including Saturday night's appearance by Odetta at the Del Mar Shores Auditorium.

"Bill and Pearl knew that, after the Old Time Cafe shut down, there wouldn't really be any other place in San Diego for touring folk musicians to play," said Bruce Reid, chairman of San Diego Folk Heritage's 10-member board of directors.

"So, before they closed the cafe, they invited a number of us Old Time Cafe regulars to a meeting to see if there was some way for the music to continue. Actually, most of us who went to that meeting didn't realize what we were getting into. The prevalent feeling of the original board members was that Bill and Pearl wanted to continue presenting concerts under a nonprofit structure, and they needed a rubber-stamp board of directors to make it fly.

"But what we found out was that their idea was to start a very active volunteer organization, and we were all going to do a lot of work. They served in an advisory capacity for six months; then they left town, and it was up to us to basically carry on."

San Diego Folk Heritage has, in fact, carried on -- with increasing success, Reid said.

"Initially, we did concerts all over town, and that was pretty difficult," he said. "It's gotten a lot easier since we moved to one stable venue."

That venue is the Del Mar Shores Auditorium on the Winston School campus, where San Diego Folk Heritage is now presenting two concerts a month by touring folk acts.

Past shows have featured topical singer-songwriters Tom Paxton and Peter Alsop, Irish harpist Patrick Ball, English concertina-player Alistair Anderson, and mandolinist-guitarist Peter Ostroushko, a former regular on public radio's "Prairie Home Companion."

Coming up are Celtic music group Black Thorn, with Paraguayan harpist Ed Lang, on May 12; Ronnie Gilbert, an original member of pioneering American folk revivalists the Weavers, on May 19, and the Iron Mountain String Band, which plays old-time hillbilly music, on June 2.

"We're booking only traditional folk musicians, even those who aren't that well known," Reid said. "Tracy Schwarz, who recently played for us, is a good example. He's one of the foremost traditional interpreters in America -- he used to be with the New Lost City Ramblers, one of the bands that started the whole traditional music revival back in the 1950s -- and there's just nobody else in town who would have presented somebody like him."

San Diego Folk Heritage also publishes a bimonthly newsletter filled with concert updates, artist profiles and record reviews that is sent out to the more than 4,000 local folk fans on the groups mailing list, which it inherited from the Old Time Cafe, Reid said.

In addition, the organization sponsors New England "contra" dances the first and third Friday of every month at the Trinity United Methodist Church in North Park.

"And just recently," Reid said, "we began doing monthly concerts by local musicians at the Del Mar Shores Auditorium, on top of our two other shows per month."

The next home-grown talent night is April 21, with folk singers Pam and Maggie sharing the bill with North County bluegrass band the Unstrung Heroes. THOMAS K. ARNOLD
