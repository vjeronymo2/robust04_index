August 10, 1990, Friday, Home Edition

MOVIE REVIEW;

'AIR AMERICA' STALLS IN FINDING ITS FOCUS

It used to be said that Vietnam was the great undeclared war both in real life and on the screen. Until the last decade or so, the war was virtually absent from our screens. Now, of course, it's rare that a month goes by without some Vietnam-themed feature or television movie. While the general run of such films is of a rather high quality, the themes and subject matter have become repetitive.

That's one reason why "Air America," (citywide) which stars Mel Gibson and Robert Downey and is about the CIA's secret airline operating in Laos in 1969, seems so invigorating at first. It's a great, untapped movie subject, and the director Roger Spottiswoode works up the material in high style. At its best it's a luxuriant black comedy. The buccaneer aviators who maneuver the experimental prop planes and huge, lumbering transports above the lush Laotian highlands are juiced by their own adrenalin. They're all a little high, and the movie itself has a slightly delirious, narcotized quality.

The black comedy arises naturally out of the material. The CIA kept a secret mercenary anti-communist army in Laos for more than a decade, and Air America was its air support. As a covert, CIA-run, civilian-operated airline in charge of transporting everything from pigs to opium, Air America did not even officially exist. The pilots, recruited both from civilian life and from the military, were generally not told about the nature of their missions, or even that they were working for the CIA. (That doesn't mean they didn't know.) But, if the dangers were extraordinary -- more Air America pilots died in Laos than in Cambodia or Vietnam -- so were the financial rewards. Pilots got paid by the flight, and often there were dozens per day.

The wacky disassociation of working for a non-existent airline in a non-existent war is what gives "Air America" its tone of funhouse grisliness. (The film opens with Nixon on TV telling the world there is no American presence in Laos.) These non-people don't even have the solace of having their heroism recognized. Their exploits, like their deaths, go officially unrecorded.

As long as the film is racking up its absurdist points, it's a free-wheeling, sporty entertainment. The shots of huge porkers being parachuted to earth in crates, of Buddhist priests blessing the planes before lift-off, or the sight of a miniature golf course carved out of the jungle, have a special kind of comic charge. They reflect what really happened in Laos. The absurdity isn't tacked on to the story; it is the story. And there are moments when the nuttiness has a majestic sweep, as when a storm-tossed plane emerges from dark clouds and into the huge, looming face of a Buddhist shrine.

As the film floats along, however, it remains essentially without focus. There's a conflict built into the script all right: Gene Ryack (Mel Gibson), a veteran of 15 years flying in Southeast Asia, tries to live down the guilt of transporting opium as a routine part of his job. Billy Covington (Robert Downey, Jr.), recruited from L.A. after botching his job as an eye-in-the-sky air traffic pilot, arrives in Laos with only the most naive assumptions about his job. When he discovers the extent of the corruption, he goes in for some free-style sabotage and, in the process, forces Gene into a moral reckoning.

But the conflict is a case of too little too late and, in any event, it seems de-fanged. Robert Downey, Jr. gives a wonderfully open and engaging performance but, the way the film has been set up, his arrant innocence seems like a plot convenience. It's true that the Air Americans were kept in the dark about a great many things, but, with a mentor like Gene, who is so supremely cynical that he can't say anything to anyone without smirking, Billy seems not so much callow as dumb.

"Air America" has a political consciousness: the battle sequences have gravity and the shots of the Laotian peasants are stunningly empathetic. The film exhibits a healthy "MASH"-like disrespect for the dangerous baloney of officialese. But the "48 HRS"-style buddy-buddy badinage and rough-housing between the aviators doesn't fill out the film's visual grandeur. It diminishes it. (So do the numerous what-the-hell anachronisms, like references to Mother Teresa and General Ky's liquor store in Orange County. In 1969!)

Mel Gibson gives a cocky, assured performance -- a star performance. But he doesn't provide the kind of shadowings that might make Gene's guilt-ridden con-artistry resonate. Or maybe the shadings were edited out. There's so much voluminous detail and incident in "Air America" that at times the film resembles a vast swatch of footage; you get the feeling 10 other movies could have been made from what was shot. Some of the characters, like Burt Kwouk's deliciously corrupt General Lu Soong, are given ample display; others, like Nancy Travis' refugee camp coordinator, are barely in the movie, even though their roles seemed conceived on a much fuller scale.

It's possible that the filmmakers got so high on the exhilarating possibilities in the material that they just let the cameras run, waiting for it to all come together in the editing room. "Air America" is far from a disgrace, but it's so rare to see a film with this much panoramic verve that you want it to deliver the real goods and not this cargo-load of tinkertoy war-is-heck ironies.
