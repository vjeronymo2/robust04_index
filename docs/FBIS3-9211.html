Language: English Article Type:CSO [Article by Brendan McNally: "Passive-Aggressive Tamara: Tesla's `Stealthy' Radar That Could Revolutionize War-Making"] [Text] In early 1992, members of the old federal parliament were amazed to hear senior military officer Oldrich Barak announce that the Czechoslovak army had a "stealth radar" called Tamara that had already demonstrated its ability to detect and identify the U.S. Air Force's multi-billion-dollar B-2 "Stealth" bomber. Tamara surfaced again last summer when the Czech government, bowing to Western pressure, quashed an attempt to sell six Tamara systems to Iran. Since then, Tamara has rapidly become one of the Czech Republic's best-known pieces of military technology. Despite being dubbed the "stealth radar," Tamara is in fact not even a radar. Radar systems "see" by actively bouncing radio waves off objects, while Tamara is a purely passive system that detects the radiation and electronic signals inevitably emitted by even the stealthiest aircraft. "If a B-2 bomber uses even its altimeter, we'll see them," Barak said. The ability to detect supposedly invisible aircraft like the B-2 sparks the popular imagination. But, for defense experts, Tamara's ability to "see without being seen" is a much more important quality. In the radar-dominated "electronic battlefield" that has existed since World War II, the ability to see has invariably meant being seen as well. As a non-radiating passive surveillance system, Tamara has effectively broken away from this structure. As a result, some believe it could radically alter the balance of modern conventional warfare. For this reason, some consider Tamara a potentially revolutionary "weapon." Tamara uses artificial intelligence to correlate and evaluate information. It can even "teach itself" to discern new patterns and identify new targets, according to Barak. When discussing Tamara, Barak likes to stress its non-military applications. Because the system does not radiate energy, Barak said Tamara could be an alternative to airport radars in residential areas, where radar saturation could pose health risks. Tamara could also be useful in monitoring coastal shipping routes such as the English Channel or the North Sea coast. Tesla Pardubice began developing passive Electronic Intelligence--or ELINT--systems during the late 1960s with an experimental system called Kopac. This was followed in the late 1970s by the second-generation system Ramona, at least one of which was sold to Syria. Tamara was developed during the late '80s. Only a few Tamaras have actually been sold abroad, Barak said. "There are 15 sets within Russia [and] some in the Czech and Slovak republics. The German Bundeswehr (federal army) has one Tamara and one Ramona." Barak said that attempts to sell Tamara to Iran began before he assumed his post. "We haven't had any discussions with Iran [since that time]," he said. "We cannot sell Tamara without the permission of the government." To sell Tamara to Western militaries, Barak said he knows he'll have to break through both the West's prejudice against Eastern-bloc hardware and a more powerful prejudice known as the "NIH syndrome," which applies to products "not invented here." Does Barak dream of the day when Tamara is part of the Pentagon's arsenal? Yes. But, he said, "There is one already in the U.S. somewhere. It was bought from another country. I don't know from where."
