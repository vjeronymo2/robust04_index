Language: Spanish Article Type:BFN [By Ramon Melenciano] [Text] Santo Domingo, 26 Mar (EFE) -- Although the Dominican Republic has officially voiced support for Costa Rica's candidate for the post of OAS secretary general, today, just one day before the elections, it is still unknown how the Dominican Government will vote. Dominican Republic President Joaquin Balaguer has expressed a need for the regional organization to be reorganized to enable it to fulfill its duty of being the guardian of Latin American democracy. This position corresponds with the one proposed by the group of nations supporting the candidacy of Colombian President Cesar Gaviria. Balaguer also stated that, together with Argentina President Carlos Menen, he is participating in Latin American efforts to restore the prestige of the OAS. Previously, Deputy Foreign Minister Fabio Herrera Cabral and Foreign Ministry officials had told EFE that the Dominican Republic would support Costa Rican Foreign Minister Bernd Niehaus. The support was based on the fact that Costa Rica is a small country, as well as on an agreed rotation for representatives from all OAS countries to occupy the post of secretary general. Ambassador Kemil Dipp Gomez, who heads the United Nations and OAS Department at the Dominican Republic's Foreign Ministry, said the Dominican Republic would maintain its support for Costa Rica even if there was a possibility that Niehaus could be replaced by Oscar Arias, former Costa Rican president and Nobel Peace Prize laureate. Niehaus is running against Colombian President Cesar Gaviria, who has the support of the United States, Mexico, and Brazil. In January and March 1994, Colombian Foreign Minister Noemi Sanin visited the Dominican Republic in an effort to obtain the government's support for Gaviria's candidacy. On both occasions, however, Dominican Republic diplomatic officials reiterated their country's preference for a representative from a small country, such as Costa Rica, to occupy the post of OAS secretary general. Therefore, Balaguer's statements have confused international diplomats as to which of the two candidates, Gaviria or Niehaus, will receive the Dominican Republic's support. According to a source within the Dominican Republic Foreign Ministry, there are two international policy issues that could force the government to withdraw its support for Costa Rica in favor of Colombia. The first is that the United States, the country that has the most political influence and economic relations with the Dominican Republic, supports Gaviria. At this time, the Dominicans are promoting bilateral agreements with the United States, which, according to the source, could sway the Dominican Republic's vote in favor of Gaviria. The other factor is that the Dominican Republic and Colombia are negotiating the possible release of four Dominicans jailed in Santa Fe de Bogota in connection with an alleged plot against the Colombian Government. Colombian Foreign Minister Noemi Sanin de Rubio proposed to President Balaguer in her two visits that Colombia might agree to allow the Dominicans to serve their sentences in their own country if they are convicted, but only if the Dominican Republic Government agreed to a prisoner-exchange program. Balaguer is being pressured by relatives and educational, cultural, and political organizations to expedite the release of the Dominicans. After voicing its support for the Costa Rican candidacy for more than four months, a change in the Dominican Republic's position would be interpreted as meaning that U.S. pressure or President Balaguer's interest in obtaining the release of his imprisoned citizens prompted the turnaround.
