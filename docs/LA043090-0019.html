April 30, 1990, Monday, Home Edition

SHIRER'S CBS RADIO SHOW

J.F. Beck's letter (March 19) concerning the chapters in my book, "A Native's Return," about Edward R. Murrow and his firing me from CBS makes some astonishing statements.

He speaks of my "claim to martyrdom." I never claimed it. The charge is false.

He asserts that when he was in the news department at the Los Angeles CBS radio station he had to listen to "complaints from the sales department about the declining quality of the ratings in Shirer's time period."

What complaints? The local Los Angeles CBS station's salesmen had nothing to do with selling my Sunday show, the only sponsored one I had. This was handled by the New York CBS office and a New York advertising agency which bought time for the sponsor for a coast-to-coast hookup. There were never any "complaints" from the sales people in New York. Why?

Because, I suppose, contrary to what Beck contends about declining ratings, and to his bland assurance that I "could no longer command a broadcasting audience large enough for the requirements of broadcast commerce," my Sunday broadcast, according to the reports I got weekly from MCA, my agent, had the highest rating of any daytime show on Sunday. If my ratings were slipping, as Beck asserts, surely the sponsor, not to mention Murrow and William Paley, would have complained. They never did.

Beck says that "my lack of broadcast skills, voice quality and delivery" had made me "dull beyond survival" on the air. However, at the very moment in 1947 that the controversy erupted, I was receiving the Peabody Award, then the most prestigious in radio, "for outstanding reporting and interpretation of the news." And that year, too, Billboard's poll of 100 newspaper radio editors cited me "as the most interesting news commentator on the air."

It is embarrassing to mention such things, but Beck's letter, full of misrepresentations, leaves his target with no choice but to respond with the truth.

WILLIAM L. SHIRER

Lenox, Mass.
