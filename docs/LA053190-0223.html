May 31, 1990, Thursday, Orange County Edition

JOSEPH N. BELL: NICE MIX OF LAW AND IDEALISM

Several weeks ago, 15 homeless people who had filed suit against the city of Santa Ana for confiscating their personal possessions were awarded about $3,300 each in a settlement with the city. Deep in the story, it was noted that their attorney, Christopher Mears, took no fee and charged only $373 as reimbursement for expenses. That seemed remarkable enough to look up Mears and see what he was about.

First off, he didn't find his waiving of a fee all that remarkable. "It happens a lot more often than you think," he said. "Personal injury lawyers" -- which he is -- "take a bad rap. It's a popular stereotype that has existed for years and is substantiated by a few bad apples, when nine-tenths are hard-working, sincere professionals. But no one is allowed to have good motives anymore. I'm really sick and tired that the only image of lawyers the public seems to have is this greedy stereotype."

We were talking in his bright, airy office in a converted grain silo in the Old Town of Irvine. His windows look out on a background of mountains and a foreground of agricultural fields, staked out for the summer's crop. "When I come in in the early morning," he said a little dreamily, "and the sun is coming up behind this building, the stakes cast fantastic shadow patterns."

This is the anomaly of Mears, a short, stocky man with longish, dark-brown hair forever drifting over his forehead and an almost cherubic face, rather like a clean-shaven Robin Williams. He is coldly surgical in court -- I watched him questioning a Westminster police officer who shot and killed the 18-year-old son of a Latino family he is representing -- and almost wistfully optimistic about the potential humanity of most people, as well as fiercely defensive of their rights. Especially those who are powerless in the Orange County social order.

Mears is a second-generation Orange County native who graduated from Santa Ana High School in 1969, won his law degree from Western State University ("I wanted to be a lawyer ever since I saw 'To Kill a Mockingbird' as a child"), and practiced in an Orange County firm until he went out on his own about five years ago. His biggest settlement was $15.5 million for an elderly woman whose medical insurance was canceled on the basis of a fraudulent report. (It was later reduced to $2 million, then settled out of court when that figure was appealed.) He takes all his cases, without apology, on a contingency basis -- usually, he says, one-third -- and mostly on behalf of people who couldn't otherwise afford an attorney.

"I was infected in the 1960s with the idealism of my times," he says. "And it was easy because my schoolteacher mother -- who raised me with my grandmother -- was the most non-judgmental person I still have ever met. She taught my brother and me that we weren't any better than anyone else."

Mears got involved in the homeless case when Orange County Legal Aid called and asked him to help. It took two years, and he probably could have won a sizable fee from the city had he taken the case to court, but he chose to settle it and get some money in the hands of the people he was representing.

"It could very well turn their lives around," he said. "I know one of them bought a car so he could get to job interviews. Another bought a ticket back home where he hopes to rebuild his life. Many of them used the money for housing so they would have a base from which to look for work.

"So many of these people could make the transition back into society with less help than most people realize. Most of them work as often as they can. They just can't get enough money together to break out of the vicious circle of looking unkempt because they live on the street and being unable to receive mail or offer a resume or complete a job interview because they don't have an address."

He told about two brothers -- skilled mechanics from a Montana town where work had dried up -- who came to Orange County looking for jobs. They slept in their car to save money, and one day the car was impounded for being illegally parked while the brothers were applying for work. They didn't have enough money to get the car out of hock, the cost of retrieving it is growing daily, and they are on the streets -- dealing with a system that has no flexibility to help them out of this dilemma.

He also told about one of his homeless clients who found a laboring job that required him to be at work at 5:30 a.m. Because the buses didn't run that early, this man walked from Santa Ana to Anaheim every morning to report for work that still didn't pay him enough to get him off the streets.

"In cities like New York and Chicago," he says, "you step over homeless people. You can't ignore them. But in Santa Ana, the homeless people were trying to stay out of sight, stashing their things under bushes. That makes the city of Santa Ana even more despicable for coming in with a slash-and-burn policy that not only ignored basic constitutional rights but, even more important, violated all the standards of human decency."

So what about the rights of the businessmen who put heat on the city because they don't want the homeless camping out on their doorsteps?

"They have a legitimate beef," Mears says, "but there are ways of dealing with this without doing violence to people's possessions. I can't solve the homeless problem in this conversation. But I do know one thing for sure: It has to start with the right level of concern -- with humanity and caring. This isn't just liberal naivete. Change has to start from within, and when it starts from that point, when we have the right attitude, answers will be forthcoming. It's only when we try to avoid or ignore the problem or function in spite of it that it doesn't get resolved."

Mears thinks it is easier to ignore these social problems in Orange County "because the veneer of normalcy is very thick here, very insidious. But there are also signs of hope. Every day I can see at least as many people helping others as hurting them. Each one of us is either part of the problem or part of the solution, and I guess I'm not cynical because I believe in the inherent humanity of man and in the power of the individual to bring about change."

To which 15 erstwhile homeless Santa Ana residents who are using a new-found stake to try to get their lives back on track would say a fervent "Amen."
