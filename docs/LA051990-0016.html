May 19, 1990, Saturday, Home Edition

GERRYMANDERING IN CALIFORNIA AND MERITS OF PROPS. 118, 119

Sherry Bebitch Jeffe ("Changing the Shape of Politics, in Homage to Gerry's Memory," Opinion, May 6) is wrong to suggest that reapportionment necessarily has to be a political process, or that today's battle over the two reapportionment initiatives on the June ballot, Propositions 118 and 119, boil down to a Republicans versus Democrats issue.

She is also wrong when she says that the debate over fairness in redistricting is simply "smoke and mirrors" to mask partisan motives. These are the usual refrains either of long-time political observers who have grown cynical, or of partisan Democrats who are the chief beneficiaries of the current unfairness, and who seek to discount efforts to change it by characterizing their opponents as similarly motivated.

The fact is that Californians have been without effective representative government for a decade. Millions of our state's voters have been disenfranchised by lines on a map. The Phil Burton-Jerry Brown gerrymander of 1982 cemented into place districts that so favored incumbents of both parties that you stand more chance of removing someone from the Soviet Politburo than of mounting a successful electoral challenge in California.

Not only does this make legislators less responsive to the voters, it rewards extremists in both parties because they only have to win their party primary in these lopsided districts, and not worry about appealing to the moderate center in the general election. The result is unresponsive, unrepresentative, bad government.

It is also one reason that the initiative process has gone wild, because popular initiatives are one of the few ways around an imperial legislature.

It does not have to be that way. Either Proposition 118 or Proposition 119 can help to promote more sensible districts that more accurately reflect voter sentiment, and that do not have as their chief goal the protection of incumbents. We will get better government in California if we promote competitive elections.

Nor is it true that all approaches are the same. Jeffe's article reports Rep. Henry Waxman (D-Los Angeles) as saying that the legislative process depends on who gets elected. But who gets elected, in turn, can be the result of fair and competitive elections, or of lines drawn on a map in a smoke-filled room. I would hate to see the day when "fairness" has no place in the political process. Propositions 118 and 119 are attempts to reintroduce it.

RICHARD P. SYBERT

Los Angeles
