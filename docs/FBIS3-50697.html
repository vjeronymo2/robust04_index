Article Type:BFN [Editorial Report] The following is a compilation of reports on Mexican economic developments monitored through 4 January. Source is cited in parentheses following each item. In 1993, Pemex placed on the international market a daily average of 1.347 million barrels of crude oil for total revenues of $5.578 billion. Pemex reports that an average 271,000 daily barrels of light Isthmus oil, worth $1.347 billion, were shipped to clients in the continent, Europe, and the Far East. In addition, a daily average of 870,000 barrels of heavy Maya oil, worth $3.124 billion, were shipped to these three main regions. Of these sales, 64.8 percent went to Mexico, 15 percent to Spain, and 6 percent to the Far East. (Mexico City NOTIMEX in Spanish 0051 GMT 29 Dec 93) The Mexican Secretariat for Commerce and Industrial Development [Secofi] reports that the country's economic stabilization effort and microeconomic reform makes for a promising outlook for 1994 regarding Mexico's NAFTA partners. The country's per capita GNP increased 1.1 percent in the past three years. Mexico's commerce grew 12.6 percent in 1989-1991. (Mexico City NOTIMEX in Spanish 0207 GMT 29 Dec 93) As of the end of 1993, private corporations owe $38 billion to foreign banks. This is 160 percent higher than the amount owed in 1989. In addition, it represents 10.3 percent of the country's GNP. (Mexico City Radio ACIR Network in Spanish 0200 GMT 29 Dec 93) As of October 1993, Pemex had produced 14.275 million tons of petrochemical products, including 710,000 tons of ammonia, 1.095 million tons of ethylene, 415,000 tons of high- and low-density polyethylene, and 9.36 million tons of acrylonitrile, dodecabenzene, styrene, methanol, and other substances. (Mexico City NOTIMEX in Spanish 2057 GMT 29 Dec 93) Pemex is the first Latin American firm to enter the European stock market, a Pemex source has reported. In 1993 the corporation placed bonds worth $1.5 billion in Europe. (Mexico City Canal 13 Television Azteca Network in Spanish 1300 GMT 29 Dec 93) Secofi has reported that despite the U.S. economic recession the assembly plant industry increased 9.4 percent annually, jobs created by 6.5 percent, and U.S. dollar revenue by 19 percent from 1989 to 1992. At the end of the third quarter in 1993, there existed in the country 2,182 assembly plants with 552,078 jobs, and a revenue surplus of $4.063 billion. (Mexico City NOTIMEX in Spanish 2322 GMT 2 Jan 94)
