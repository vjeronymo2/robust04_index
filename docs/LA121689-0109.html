December 16, 1989, Saturday, Home Edition

A NEW AGE IN KOSHER FOODS;

RETAILING: A DECADE AGO, THERE WERE 1,000 KOSHER PRODUCTS, CONSUMED MOSTLY BY ORTHODOX JEWS. TODAY, THERE ARE 18,000 PRODUCTS, WITH $30 BILLION IN GROSS SALES.

In Craig Winchell's business, security is of the essence.

His Sebastopol, Calif., complex is surrounded by an eight-foot fence that's topped with a twist of razor-sharp barbed wire. Employees are carefully screened. Specifications are exacting. A team of inspectors could drop in at any time.

Winchell is not an engineer with a defense contracting firm, but rather an Orthodox Jew who makes premium kosher wines -- Gewurtztraminers, Chardonnays, Cabernet Sauvignons. His Gan Eden winery is part of a growing crop of certified kosher specialty food makers reaching out to an clientele beyond the nation's devout.

The security measures, Winchell says, are to keep "unauthorized people out" of his winery. To maintain the strict kosher standards, all workers must be observant Jews.

"The kosher market is much smaller than the California wine market in general," Winchell says. "In the kosher market, we're dealing with the perception that European wines are better than California wines. . . . Luckily, we sell all we make to the general market."

Kosher foods were formerly only available in mainstream grocery stores during High Holidays, and, even then, consumers were lucky if they found much more than a box or two of matzo and some gefilte fish.

A decade ago, there were 1,000 kosher products, and their consumers were generally the same group that made them: Orthodox Jews. Today, about 18,000 kosher products, from Chardonnay to Cajun-style chicken, Pepperidge Farm baked goods to Slim-Fast diet aids, are available year-round. Industry watchers peg the kosher market at about $30 billion in gross sales for 1989.

Winchell was one of 80 kosher food manufacturers from around the country who gathered Friday at the Los Angeles Convention Center for the International Jewish Festival '89.

"The growth in kosher foods over the last 10 years has been absolutely phenomenal," said Rabbi Menachem Genack, rabbinic administrator for the New York-based Orthodox Union, the leading kosher certifier. "The number of companies that have come under the OU's supervision has nearly tripled in size from about 450 companies to about 1,250 or so."

Newcomers include Hiram Walker, Slim-Fast, the Sioux Honey Assn. and Pepperidge Farm; longtime kosher companies include Procter & Gamble, which makes everything from kosher cake mixes to kosher laundry and dish detergents.

Kashrut, the Jewish dietary code, is complex and largely based on Old Testament teachings. For example, meat and poultry must be raised on natural feed and be slaughtered according to Jewish ritual.

Only part of the renewed vigor of the age-old industry is due to Jews who are returning to religious orthodoxy, said Menachem Lubinsky, president of Lubicom, the New York-based marketing firm that organized the trade show.

Consumption of kosher foods is often associated with America's 4.3 million Jews, he said, but market studies show that there are 6 million Americans who specifically buy kosher products.

Some are Seventh Day Adventists and Muslims, who adhere to similarly strict dietary codes. Others simply "believe that kosher products are healthier and of better quality," Lubinsky said.

Many of the 80 food manufacturers who gathered Friday are the first to admit that they're using that perception as a strong marketing point for the general consumer. On the West Coast, Season Products Corp. sells most of its fish products -- like low-sodium sardines -- in natural food stores.

"Natural food people look at kosher certification for the wholesomeness," says Alan M. Kahan, food broker for Season Products. Then there's Bruce Y. Prince, executive vice president of Giamboi Kosher Food International, who was in Los Angeles Friday pushing his premium Italian food: pricey tomato sauces, pastas and extra virgin olive oil.

"We came up with a product that the public would buy that wouldn't be stuck in the aisle with the gefilte fish," said Price. "For the first time, you're getting a kosher gourmet pasta sauce in a microwaveable bottle. It sells itself."
