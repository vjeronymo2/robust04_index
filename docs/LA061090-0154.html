June 10, 1990, Sunday, Home Edition

POP VIEWS;

THE '70S REVISITED? YIKES!;

YES, AM HITS SUCH AS 'KUNG FU FIGHTING' AND 'SPIRIT IN THE SKY' ARE BEING SALUTED IN A NEW 15-ALBUM SERIES. PERHAPS THOSE SONGS WEREN'T THAT BAD AFTER ALL

When Rhino Records announced late last year that it would be releasing a 15-volume, 180-song series recollecting the disposable pop music of the early 1970s, naysayers couldn't be blamed for wondering if there was even the slightest market for it.

When it comes to '70s nostalgia, folks just don't bother getting out their usual rose-colored glasses. Americans as a culture hate the '70s -- especially the clothes, the trivial artifacts and the pop music. We tend to remember mood rings and wide collars and Grand Funk with only slightly more fondness and pride than, say, block-long gasoline lines.

I found that out a couple of years ago when, presaging the current rage for post-Vietnam/pre-Reagan kitsch, I threw a '70s theme party.

Guests were asked to come in proper period regalia: The wider the ties, the bigger the bells, the better. The house was a veritable time warp, from the Hot Chocolate music on the dance floor to the Tab in the refrigerator (nothing Lite allowed). It was meant to be both a celebration and an exorcism.

A few dozen of the demonized did show up in their leisure suits and halter tops, in search of closure and healing. Some friends, however, refused to come to the party on grounds of moral principle, declining to dignify the despised decade with their presence, as if they were Nora Dunn and the '70s were Andrew Dice Clay.

Others politely declined in the expectation that it would be "too depressing." Even some of the stalwarts who did come started moping around by themselves halfway through, as if hypnotically re-experiencing a particularly bad prom.

A lot of people are having the same reaction to Rhino's "Super Hits of the '70s -- Have a Nice Day," a real Rorschach of a cassette and compact disc series. (Sorry, but it's not available in 8-track.) Like: Can't we let this stuff stay buried?

A recent New York Times review took the standard critical view, with critic Jon Pareles declaring, "To me, the early 1970s was when rock bottomed out" and calling the Rhino compilation "a reminder of just how awful pop music was."

Obviously Gary Stewart, vice president of A&R for Rhino Records, doesn't agree. He sees the music in the 15-album series as in the same classic American Top 40 pop tradition as that of the '50s and '60s. Stewart, who compiled the series, thinks it's ludicrous to dismiss all the early-'70s hits just because they didn't meet the test of "significance."

He has a point. There's a potential arrogance and a form of ageism in assigning an entire generation's ear candy -- the hits by generally inconsequential artists -- to the scrap heap, while celebrating equally lightweight hits from one's own time.

The hits spotlighted in the Rhino series don't carry the artistic vision or musical energy of the great early-'60s works from Motown, Phil Spector, the Beatles or the Stones. The fact that everyone had bad haircuts doesn't help.

But that doesn't mean they didn't still spotlight craft and provide lingering pleasures. There is indeed an overwhelming banality to much of what Rhino has revived, but in retrospect it's an endearing banality; even that which was most insincere and calculated has a long-lost kind of benign innocence to its calculation.

No doubt about it, the '70s are back, mostly for camp's sake. First re-popularized amid England's acid-house scene, bell bottoms are now de rigueur for anyone who's truly hip today in L.A. Smiley faces can be spotted up and down Melrose. A Los Angeles dance club called 1970 specializes in period music ranging from Donna Summer to T. Rex.

Bands cover '70s songs now the way they used to remake obscure '60s delicacies. A short list from recent memory: Doctor & the Medics and Fuzzbox both recording "Spirit in the Sky" (see "Have a Nice Day Vol. 2"), Crowded House doing "Signs" (see Vol. 6) at the Pantages, John Hiatt singing "Kung Fu Fighting" (slated for a future volume) at McCabe's, Erasure resurrecting ABBA's "Gimme a Man After Midnight" at the Palace, and the Replacements slaughtering "Takin' Care of Business," "Rock and Roll All Night" and virtually every other hard-rock song of the period on their mid-'80s tours.

In that spirit, Polydor Records is preparing an album of '70s hits updated by current artists -- including Living Colour's Corey Glover tackling Rufus' "Tell Me Something Good," Voice of the Beehive putting the sting on the Partridge Family's "I Think I Love You" and Hothouse Flowers opening up Johnny Nash's "I Can See Clearly Now." The inevitable title: "Guilty Pleasures."

Camp is also the catchword for Rhino's "Super Hits of the '70s" series, which is not as inclusive as its name would indicate. Stewart decided to limit the series to acts that had only one or two hits in the Top 20 -- parameters that necessarily leave out album-oriented rock artists, along with acts that were actually strong enough to have, say, three or four hits. Thus you get Daddy Dewdrop but no Deep Purple; "Brandy" but not "Mandy."

"The idea was to collect all the odds and ends -- 90% of which is no longer available," Stewart said. "In doing that, you sort of produce some music that's even more of its time, as opposed to music made by artists who had career longevity.

"Most of the artists that had one hit, their vision or their musical style or their feel tapped right into that very moment and they probably weren't able to hit on that again. That makes the music more time-specific and dated, in a positive way."

Stewart sees the early '70s as "the last hurrah" for the Top 40.

"It was the last time people made records specifically for the singles market," Stewart said. " 'My Baby Loves Lovin',' 'Love Grows (Where My Rosemary Goes),' 'United We Stand,' 'Gimme Dat Ding' -- all share the same lead singer, and all were made by non-pros. They were writers and musicians who decided to get together and make some single hits."

Stewart is going to cut off the series, which is being released in chronological order, with Vol. 15 somewhere around 1975 -- "right up to when the first Ramones record came out," he said. "That's when

Top 40 radio pretty much became totally invalid or unnecessary."

Was Top 40 ever valid or necessary in the pre-punk, pre-disco period Stewart does cover? Even he admits the "Have a Nice Day" series is more for fun's sake than to argue a case for serious historical and artistic consideration. It's purposefully frothy -- and white.

"R&B records are kind of excluded from this series, basically because that music was genuinely good," Stewart explained. "Not that there aren't some good singles scattered in this series. But I think soul and R&B reached a sort of apex during those years, and they're going to get their own series. (Rhino also has '70s disco and glitter-rock collections set for release.) You can actually take those records seriously."

And you can't take "Have a Nice Day" seriously as representative of its period without its including any of what was uniquely great about the era -- Mott the Hoople, early Bowie and Alice Cooper, Al Green, Steely Dan, Roxy Music, the Philly soul sound, the Who, Marvin Gaye, et al.

But even some of the most shallow music of the era holds its rewards -- at least in retrospect. There was every good reason in 1973 to truly hate the DeFranco Family's "Heartbeat -- It's a Lovebeat," which awkwardly combined bad melodrama and sickly sweetness in a theretofore unreckoned dosage.

Yet you know that that's indeed little Tony DeFranco putting his 13-year-old heartbeat out on the line, a moment in time, without any sophisticated studio trickery to puff up his transparently thin vocal range. The song went to No. 3 on the charts. It's doubtful you'll ever find such nakedness in a bubble-gum record again -- at least not in an age when technology makes everything so very, very professional.

Some of these sides, it should be noted, are actually enduring or innovative quality work. Melanie used the Edwin Hawkins Singers on "Lay Down (Candles in the Rain)" nearly two decades before Madonna used the Andrae Crouch choir on "Like a Prayer," and to better effect.

Hotlegs' "Neanderthal Man," with a pre-10cc incarnation of Godley & Creme, probably has the most unusual production values -- tribal drums on top, vocals mixed way down -- of any single ever to hit the Top 40.

Stories' "Brother Louie" went to No. 1 with a controversial interracial love theme. The Buoys' "Timothy" introduced cannibalism to the charts 20 years before warning labels.

Less trend-setting but equally outstanding pop songs were Smith's "Baby It's You," Stealers Wheel's "Stuck in the Middle With You," Dobie Gray's "Drift Away" and Loudon Wainwright III's "Dead Skunk."

You can even admire, if not necessarily like, the solid Tin Pan Alley craftsmanship of "It Never Rains in Southern California" or the suicidal "Alone Again (Naturally)," the earnest likes of which we'll rarely see on the Billboard charts again.

Trade-offs have been made. Top 40 listeners and radio programmers have indeed come a long way in tolerance for dissonance and odd harmonic progressions ("Purple Rain" could never have been a hit in the '70s), but almost always only if it has a dance beat. Weirdness and classicism both get homogenized by the mutual insistence of modern pop's standardized drum sound; heartbeat -- it's a machine beat.

The hit parade of the Me Decade offered as much junk as the Madonna Decade, but it was an organic kind of junk, neither as wild-eyed as the quasi-hippie music that preceded it nor quite as greedily gumptious as the factory-made teen fodder it paved the way for. That may not mean that it might not be worthy of a good exorcism anyway, but a lingering -- or perhaps first-time -- fondness for the schlock monster is no cause for shame.

Having Hamilton, Joe Frank & Reynolds and Dr. Hook as part of your heritage is indeed a scourge, but it could be worse, thirty-something and late-twentysome-thing comrades -- you could have been born later and have "Hangin' Tough" for roots. In the words of the prophets: Hold your head up.
