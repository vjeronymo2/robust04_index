920226 FT 26 FEB 92 / Parliament and Politics: Major again rejects referendum - Scotland MR JOHN MAJOR, the prime minister, yesterday sought to calm the nerves of Scottish Conservatives by envisaging an increase in their representation at Westminster after the general election. His remarks, at prime minister's question time, came in the wake of Monday's debate on devolution at the Scottish Grand Committee sitting in Edinburgh. There was derisive laughter from opposition MPs pressing for constitutional change when he told the Commons that this was one of the factors he had in mind when promising to 'take stock' of the situation if the government was given a renewed mandate. Mrs Margaret Ewing, Scottish Nationalist MP for Moray, underlined her party's demand for independence by suggesting that the Scottish people should be given the right to determine their own future through a referendum. The prime minister answered: 'Your party seeks isolation for Scotland. I do not believe that prescription is in the interests of Scotland or in the interests of the rest of the UK.' He emphasised that ways of increasing the strength of the union would also form part of the taking stock exercise. In a letter to the prime minister, Mr Donald Dewar, the shadow Scottish secretary, pressed him to recognise, as the Conservatives 'headed for oblivion' in Scotland, that the status quo was no longer an option. Mr Dewar stated: 'The Scottish people must be given what they demand - a parliament in Scotland now.'
