BFN [Video report from Madrid by correspondent Sergey Dorenko on news conference given by Russian President Boris Yeltsin on 12 April, including recorded extracts of Yeltsin remarks -- from the "Vesti" program] [Text] [Dorenko] Today President Yeltsin and Felipe Gonzalez, chairman of the Spanish Government, summed up the results of the Russian president's official visit. Gonzalez thanked the visitor for interesting talks and reminded him that the new treaty on friendship and cooperation between Russia and Spain is a direct successor of the similar treaty of 1812. The Russian president said that our ties should be expanded on the basis of the treaty. [Yeltsin] On the whole, we used to have abnormal relations. Two big states with enormous possibilities have a general trade turnover not exceeding 2 percent. This figure is smaller than in any other European country. Why is it so? It is Spain, after all. [Dorenko] Staying in a NATO member-country, the president, naturally, could not fail to discuss the aggravation of our relations with the NATO bloc after the Bosnia bombings. [Addressing Yeltsin at news conference] Boris Nikolayevich, at the moment you are on NATO's south flank. If NATO continues to bomb the Serbs while neglecting Russia's proposals, can Russia put pressure not only on the Serbs? Can Russia make others heed its views? [Yeltsin] Yes, I think you have worded your question in a very correct way. I already voiced my displeasure to Mr. Clinton the day before yesterday and have told the Spanish leaders here that to sign the Partnership for Peace document while there is a kind of discrimination against Russia [changes thought], for instance, we are still unable to sign an economic agreement with the EEC. We have been eyeing this agreement for a whole year but we still cannot sign it. Have we got any any resources for changing the situation? I think we have. We were the first to take the correct view as regards influence on the Serbs. I am sure, nevertheless -- and we have just been discussing it; the chairman, Mr. Gonzalez, also agrees -- that the end there must come only through political negotiation and not through force. The task there cannot be accomplished by means of force. That will be an everlasting war. When all's said and done, everyone should understand that. If things worsen now, the blame will rest with those who took the decision on those three bombing strikes. The blame will rest with them. I consider that the statement is very tough and, it must be said, serious, including for the President of the United States and for NATO. I think that it will change the situation. The main thing now in the situation of Yugoslavia after these three bombing strikes is to hold the Serbs back from greater confrontation and greater military action. And we are now making such efforts. Our Deputy Foreign Minister Churkin is now constantly with Milosevic. If necessary, Foreign Minister Kozyrev will fly there the day after tomorrow to restrain the Serbs from greater confrontation. If it proves possible to restrain them and to switch once again to the negotiating track, to the political level, the level of negotiation, that means there will be no more bombing. As regards the linkage with this European, NATO document, Partnership for Peace, well, we are not so far making any direct linkage. But neither are we in any hurry to sign. [Dorenko] The president said that for him Spain was an experiment in civic accord. And here is what Boris Yeltsin had to say about the Memorandum on Civic Accord in Russia: [Yeltsin] We intend to sign it on 28 April, and so far we are not changing this date. Leaders of the State Duma, the Federation Council, and the government, as well as most political parties and public movements support the memorandum, although it is yet to be examined officially. Therefore I believe most of them will sign it on 28 April, and after that this document will be open for signing by anyone wishing to sign it. [Dorenko] And now the president's sensational response to questions about the state of his health: Yeltsin invites all journalists to attend his medical examination. [Yeltsin] I could wish everyone such health as I have at my age. A man who plays a rigorous game of tennis regularly for one and a half hours at a time, with professional tennis players for partners, a man who swims in the Black Sea in March, with the water temperature down just to four degrees -- can such a man be of poor health? A man who works 16-18 hours a day, practically without weekends -- you can only envy this. The communists, of course, are constantly spreading rumors. They would like to put me in a bad mood, make up stories -- but all this is not serious. Recently, when I read and heard about this I said to my doctors: Let us carry out the full one-day medical examination this year, in a clinic, witnessed by all the journalists wishing to attend. Let as send my blood tests to any laboratory, let them investigate how much of what I have in my urine, in my [words indistinct due to audience laughter]. So that, through all the stages of these clinical tests not just Russia but the whole world will know what do I have inside me. And so, I can assure you -- I am not going to Barcelona for treatment. This is a planned visit. I will simply meet my doctors somewhere in their surgery -- just to thank them once again for the splendid operation they performed on me when I had some trouble with my spine after a plane crash. It was a splendid operation -- hardly anyone in the world could perform such an operation as they did. Therefore I will just thank them because I am not suffering any after-effects whatsoever. [Dorenko] Tonight the president will meet with 200 Spanish entrepreneurs. Tomorrow he will visit Barcelona and return to Moscow.
