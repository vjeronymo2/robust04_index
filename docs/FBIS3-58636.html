Language: German Article Type:BFN [Interview with SPD Chairman Rudolf Scharping by Ulrich Rosenbaum; place and date not given: "Scharping: There Will Be No Cooperation With SED Successor Party"] [Text] [Rosenbaum] In Lower Saxony, you have just opened the election campaign for the super election year. Will it be a year of punches and jabs? [Scharping] No. The Social Democrats are interested in advocating their factual positions and in seeking a confrontation with the government when it arises from the factual positions. [Rosenbaum] What are the main topics? [Scharping] Combating unemployment, new economic growth that is compatible with the environment, modernizing the economy, relieving labor of the costs that have nothing to do with employees, reconstructing the east -- this is the business of the community as a whole -- the housing shortage, and eliminating other social injustices. [Rosenbaum] In view of the new threats from the East -- keyword Zhirinovskiy -- might the voters not finally prefer to leave the present Federal Government in office? [Scharping] Why? The Social Democratic Party of Germany [SPD] is currently the only party that can present a consistent foreign policy concept, which is completely in the tradition of the foreign policies of Willy Brandt and Helmut Schmidt. The key words are European integration, development of a new Ostpolitik that also has economic and employment policy prospects, and, now as before, close friendly relations with the United States of America. In the field of European integration, the chancellor's basic policies are supported by the SPD. But he is becoming increasingly lonely in his own party. By the way, however, I cannot imagine that the Bundestag elections will be very strongly influenced by foreign politics. U.S. President Bush, who was voted out of office shortly afterward, also indulged in this illusion. [Rosenbaum] The CDU/CSU [Christian Democratic Union/Christian Social Union] has now agreed on Roman Herzog as candidate for the president's office. Do you still proceed on the assumption that the majority of the Free Democratic Party of Germany [FDP] electors will vote for Rau? [Scharping] I proceed on the assumption that in the Federal Assembly there are, independent of party membership, enough autonomous women and men who will make their decision for Germany's best benefit. And the best for this office is clearly Johannes Rau. [Rosenbaum] Are you pleased that the FDP has postponed a statement on a coalition to September and will possibly not make one at all? [Scharping] At any rate, this shows that the coalition is no longer internally consistent and the FDP is thinking. However, my concern for the partners in the coalition is limited and the FDP has to make its decision itself. I will watch this with interest. [Rosenbaum] What about the secret agreement with Joschka Fischer that a Red-Green coalition is to be formed? [Scharping] Journalists never report things that are incorrect. At most, they sometimes make mistakes. As far as this matter is concerned, I am interested in gaining voters for the SPD from all other parties. [Rosenbaum] The CDU/CSU called on you to clearly dissociate yourself from the Party of Democratic Socialism [PDS]. What about it? [Scharping] There will be no cooperation between the SPD and the PDS. For the Social Democrats, the PDS is not just a normal political opponent, but the direct successor organization of the Socialist Unity Party of Germany [SED], from which we totally dissociated ourselves. By the way, the reproach from the ranks of the CDU/CSU is an absurd reversal of the situation. In eastern Germany there are several examples of the CDU hoisting PDS people into public office and, on the other hand, availing itself of the PDS support to deny public office to the Social Democratic winners of the Brandenburg election to the advantage of members of the old bloc parties and old friendships between the CDU and the PDS. If the exhortations addressed to the Social Democrats are to be even slightly credible, the CDU would have to expel people who make common cause with the PDS. [Rosenbaum] These days its own past is, once again, catching up with the SPD. I mean the accusations against Wehner [former chairman of the SPD Bundestag Group]. You could simply say: Why should this affect me? All that happened long before my time.... [Scharping] I specifically do not say this. Someone who bears responsibility for a party does not dissociate himself from the history of this party. I do not see any reason at all to rewrite history in any way. Herbert Wehner intensively advocated the growing together of the Germans, decent humanitarian conditions, and concrete help. This is of lasting value. The historians are to examine the rest. However, it is not a topical political question. [Rosenbaum] The parties have come to an agreement on the question of the move of the capital. Will there be peace now and will the decision in the major round be final? [Scharping] Yes. I am personally satisfied with the result, as far as the German Bundestag and compensation for the Bonn region are concerned. However, I add the following: From my point of view, the Federal Government can move earlier. It will take a relatively long time and it absolutely has to be connected both with the question of costs and the modernization of the government work itself. [Rosenbaum] If you were chancellor, would you raise these questions anew? [Scharping] Yes, then I would be able to do so because all these are questions whose organization is controlled by the Federal Government and the chancellor.
