BFN [Editorial: "What is North Korea Thinking Of?"] [Text] North Korea, which is suspected of developing nuclear weapons, announced that it will immediately withdraw from the International Atomic Energy Agency (IAEA) and issued a statement rejecting all nuclear inspections. These actions will deepen suspicions about North Korea's nuclear weapons development and heighten tensions on the Korean peninsula. The situation has worsened to the point where we have to really worry. North Korea has completed the removal of all the fuel rods from a five-megawatt experimental atomic reactor in defiance of the IAEA's demand for halting the action. In reaction, the IAEA has voted to adopt a resolution imposing "sanctions" against North Korea that include suspending technological cooperation. North Korea has reacted to the IAEA's sanctions by announcing its withdrawal from the IAEA. North Korea's reaction was beyond our anticipation. Meanwhile, North Korean President Kim Il-song recently told an American expert: "If issues, including normalizing relations between North Korea and the United States, are settled as a package deal and the United States provides us with assistance in our effort to shift to light-water atomic reactors, we will be willing to freeze our nuclear program." If North Korea quits the IAEA, it will obviously become impossible for both the United States and North Korea to hold dialogue and spur debates on a resolution in the UN Security Council on imposing sanctions against North Korea. We would like to ask North Korea: "What on Earth are you thinking of?." We would like to focus on two points of North Korea's recent statement. First, North Korea has announced that it will quit the IAEA, but it has not expressed its intention to withdraw from the Nuclear Non- Proliferation Treaty (NPT). North Korea joined the NPT in 1985 and signed a safeguards agreement with the IAEA in 1992 to accept nuclear inspections. In March 1993, however, North Korea rejected an IAEA demand for special nuclear inspections of two undeclared nuclear facilities in Yongbyon and then announced that it would quit the NPT. Later, following talks with the United States, North Korea expressed its intention to temporarily suspend its withdrawal from the NPT. Since then, North Korea has taken the stand that it accepts only bona-fide nuclear inspections while maintaining that it holds a "special status" while it delays withdrawal from the NPT. If North Korea quits the IAEA and rejects all nuclear inspections, this means it remains in the NPT without having nuclear inspections. This is a flagrant violation of the NPT. The "special status" theory, which North Korea has expounded, is no longer convincing. Second, North Korea's rejection of all nuclear inspections would make it impossible to determine whether or not it has developed nuclear weapons in the past or has diverted plutonium extracted from fuel rods to nuclear weapons development. North Korea's rejection of all nuclear inspections would also make it impossible to check that country's nuclear program in the "future." How should the international community deal with North Korea's dangerous actions? What is important is that the international community should deal coolly with the North Korean nuclear issue and take a resolute attitude without overreacting to any of North Korea's actions. To that end, the international community needs to find out what North Korea's real intentions are, and sensibly monitor North Korean signals. Former U.S. President Carter will be visiting North Korea. Liberal Democratic Party members will also be visiting Pyongyang following a visit by Social Democratic Party of Japan members. They will have a good opportunity to feel out their real intentions. Since North Korea wants to resolve the nuclear issue through dialogue with the United States, the United States should not close the channel for dialogue. It is important for the United States to cooperate with the UN Security Council and other nations. The role to be played by China, North Korea's most friendly ally, is increasingly growing important. During his meeting with Choe Kwang, visiting General Staff chief of the Korean People's Army, Chinese President Jiang Zemin stressed the inseparability of China and North Korea, describing Sino-North Korean relations as "teeth and lips." North Korea cannot maintain itself for a long period without China's supplies of oil and spare parts for weapons. China has frequently advocated "peace and stability" on the Korean peninsula and the "denuclearization" of the Korean peninsula. North Korea's withdrawal from the IAEA may jeopardize this. We hope that China will seriously persuade North Korea.
