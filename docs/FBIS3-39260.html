Language: German Article Type:BFN ["df"-attributed report: "Parties to the Wage Negotiations Have Given Up `Taboo' Positions"] [Text] Duesseldorf, 12-13 February -- In talks with HANDELSBLATT Klaus Zwickel, chairman of the IG-Metall trade union, and Dr. Dieter Kirchner, general manager of the Gesamtmetall employers' association, explained why the top-level talks about the conclusion of anew collective agreement in Darmstadt failed. Both parties to the wage negotiations gave up their previous taboo positions in Darmstadt. Giving up the vacation bonus or reducing wage costs by 15 percent hardly played a role, just as IG-Metall showed only little interest in wage raises. Gesamtmetall was willing to agree to a drastic shortening of work time on the condition that the working people renounce wage compensation; IG-Metall wanted to agree to that only if entire work forces were to work less than 35 hours per week, but not in case of a differentiation of work time between groups of working people within a company. Agreement could have been reached about advancing the introduction of the 35-hour workweek, which has been agreed on by collective agreement as of 1 October 1995, since IG-Metall was willing to compensate the employers for the resulting costs. The collective agreement on opening clauses, which permit the companies to deviate from the collective agreement regarding work time regulations, was no problem anymore. IG-Metall was willing to offer an annual work time on the basis of a 35-hour workweek, which would have given the employers options with a range of 30 to 40 hours per week, if the regular work time had been adhered to on the annual average. Despite these concurrences of the mutual negotiating positions, the top-level talks in Darmstadt failed because IG-Metall did not want to give up graduated wage compensation in a case of shortened work time for partial work forces. The IG-Metall Executive Committee now wants to explain the failure of the negotiations on 21 February and to hold a strike ballot on the possibility of a strike. There will probably be no further top-level talks before this date. After the beginning of the strike, the instrument of "Special Arbitration" would then be applied, within the framework of which Gesamtmetall and IG-Metall would try to settle the wage conflict under the chairmanship of an arbiter, who would be decided on either by drawing lots or by agreement. It is assumed that such a development is necessary also so that the two parties to the wage negotiations can avoid internal pressure from their members, to which they are still subject to at the moment.
