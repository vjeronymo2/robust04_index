Language: English Article Type:BFN [Text] Prague, Jan 18 (CTK) -- The Czech Foreign Ministry has neither been informed about Skoda heavy engineering company General Director Lubomir Soudek's negotiations in Iran last November nor has it been approached by anyone from the Iranian side, ministry spokeswoman Lucie Pilipova told CTK today. The latest issue of the Czech political weekly RESPECT reported that during the visit to Iran, the delegation from the Plzen, west Bohemia-based Skoda company led by Soudek negotiated with the Iranian Defense Ministry and Army representatives on the possibility of supplying Iran with weapons and military systems. The weekly said that representatives from the Czech Foreign Ministry had also been present at the talks. Pilipova declined to comment on the published article or on other reports on the topic that have appeared in the Czech media recently. Neither has a spokesman for the Iranian Embassy in Prague commented on the article, citing a statement issued by his embassy in December. At that time Iranian Ambassador in Prague Rasul Movahedijan distanced himself from the information on his country's alleged efforts to obtain nuclear technology from the Czech Skoda company. At the same time he spoke in favor of closer contacts with the company, emphasizing that this cooperation was directed solely towards peaceful projects. Skoda representatives admitted in December that Soudek had negotiated on the possible nuclear project -- the construction of a nuclear power plant -- during his November visit to Teheran, but added that no agreement was concluded. Israeli Ambassador to the Czech Republic Moshe Yegar, in an interview with CTK in December, voiced the view that any supplies for nuclear energy projects to Iran were extraordinarily dangerous and voiced doubts about Iran's sincerity in connection with the presentation of its nuclear program as solely peaceful one. RESPECT wrote that during the November talks with Skoda representatives, not only were supplies for nuclear energy projects discussed, but Iran also showed interest in Czech supplies of "special technology" -- ground-to-air missiles, modernized T-72 tanks, and the Tamara detection system, which can track aircraft equipped with special anti-radar devices.
