930305 FT 05 MAR 93 / Commodities and Agriculture: UK threatens France over fish deliveries disruption THE UK threatened last night to take France to the European Court in protest over violent disruption of deliveries of British fish mounted by French fishermen. Mr Allan Stewart, a junior Scottish Office minister, said Britain might 'need to have recourse to the European institutions' if 'quick redress' for the incidents was not forthcoming. He said he was encouraged by France's response to requests to provide protection for British cargoes when notice was given. British officials had made it clear to their French counterparts that they were monitoring fresh incidents perpetrated by French fishermen some of whom were 'totally out of control,' he said. He urged the French authorities to 'act to restore order.' Clashes between police and fishermen protesting against the increase in imports of cheap fish into France reached a climax some ten days ago when 800 fishermen stormed the vast wholesale fish market at Rungis on the outskirts of Paris, leaving hundreds of tonnes of damaged fish strewn over the floor. Only a few hours later Mr John Major, the prime minister, promised at Westminster that he would leave France 'in no doubt' that those who had earlier attacked a British consignment of fish in Brittany should be brought to book. France would be told it was expected to act where necessary to 'safeguard free trade, to pay compensation when losses have occurred and to bring the perpetrators to book.' Mr Major's response followed demands by Tory MPs yesterday that the prime minister raise the issue with the French government and ensure compensation was paid to 'all in Britain who have lost cash.' That spate of violence was calmed by French government assurances that the EC would agree to impose minimum prices on imported fish. Yesterday's threat came in a Commons debate called by Mr Phil Gallie, the Tory MP for Ayr, to discuss fish exports through France, and particularly the question of damage and compensation to a local Scottish company. Claiming that Pounds 10,000 worth of 'Clyde-caught fish' was destroyed in a French market by having diesel poured over it, Mr Gallie said that the recent violence had caused 'a serious disruption totrade.' A total of Pounds 2.5m worth of damage had been done and five French fishermen had been arrested, he added. Mr David Harris, Conservative MP for St Ives, said British exporters were now afraid to use British transport for fear their produce would be immediately set on by 'thugs.'
