BFN [Text] San Jose, 6 Apr (ACAN-EFE) -- While Costa Rican businessmen and government considered the signing of the Free Trade Agreement (TLC) with Mexico a landmark in Costa Rican history, union leaders expressed great reservations about this economic opening and the consequences it will have for the country. The signing of the TLC by Presidents Rafael Angel Calderon (Costa Rica) and Carlos Salinas de Gortari (Mexico) in Mexico City yesterday, Tuesday, was given wide coverage by the Costa Rican media. A television station even broadcast the event live. Costa Rican businessmen and government officials estimate that with the TLC, which has to be ratified by the parliaments in both countries, exports to Mexico should go from $60 million to $250 million in the next five years. This would make Mexico Costa Rica's fourth commercial partner, after the United States, the European Union, and Central America. In its editorial today, LA NACION newspaper pointed out that the signing of the TLC, considered to be one of the most advanced of the ones signed in Latin America, "gives a greater momentum to a new era of commercial opening," whose commercial benefits "are as great as the challenges it poses." After highlighting the benefits the TLC can bring to the country, offering a market of more than 85 million people to the Costa Rican producers, the newspaper warned, however, that "one cannot rest on one's laurels because the challenges imposed by this new reality are many." "More than ever before, it is necessary to expand the economic modernization program," the newspaper appraised. LA NACION published a big photograph of Calderon and Salinas signing the agreement in its front page, two pages with information related to the TLC, and one with the Costa Rican president's speech. The LA REPUBLICA newspaper, for its part, gave less coverage to this story and published a small photo of Calderon and Salinas de Gortari in its first page and highlighted that the signing of the TLC opens a ""new era in the relations between Costa Rica and Mexico." Costa Rican business sectors are focusing on the possibility of developing joint investments, starting next January, when the TLC, which will regulate the trade in approximately 12,000 products, will go into effect. Carlos Alvarado, the Costa Rican Chamber of Commerce president, announced that this same week, Costa Rican businessmen will start to make contacts in order to attract financial investments. Chamber of Industries President Samuel Yankelewitz said the TLC "includes all the elements for things to go well" and considered it was "just and balanced" for both nations. Doris Osterlof, the executive director of the Export Chamber, asserted: "Our industry is prepared" to compete with the Mexicans. However, Albino Vargas, secretary general of the National Association of Public Employees (ANEP,) questioned the Treaty because the labor sector had not participated in the three years of negotiations and also because the TLC does not state clearly the workers' benefits. Another union leader, Guillermo Garcia, told ACAN-EFE the TLC will benefit the country's development but said unions had reservations about the new economic model (the commercial opening) and the consequences it can have. Garcia added that the Costa Rican Government will have to abrogate protection measures for workers and labor organizations because of the pressures the economic opening will have on Costa Rican society. The experience of the decades between the 1960's and the 1980's, Garcia underscored, left a negative balance because "we were asked for sacrifice in exchange for future economic bonanza and the economic models that were implemented prompted a larger social gap and an unfair distribution of the benefits resulting from production."
