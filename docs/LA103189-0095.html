October 31, 1989, Tuesday, Home Edition

WARNER BLASTS SONY OVER GUBER-PETERS CONTRACT;

ENTERTAINMENT: THE MOVIE COMPANY SAYS THE PRODUCERS' CONTRACT WITH COLUMBIA PICTURES REPRESENTS A CONFLICT OF INTEREST.

In an ongoing legal battle for Hollywood's hottest producers, Warner Bros. on Monday lambasted Sony Corp.'s contention that Peter Guber and Jon Peters can be hired as chief executives of Columbia Pictures Entertainment without violating their exclusive movie production deal at Warner.

"That suggestion is not possible from a practical or ethical viewpoint. Guber and Peters would have intolerable conflicts of interest in decision-making," said Warner Bros. Chairman Robert A. Daly in a new affidavit.

In a similarly frosty tone, Warner Bros. President Terry Semel denied that he ever orally agreed to let Guber and Peters terminate their written agreement if they got the chance to own or operate another studio.

At issue is Sony's hiring of the producers of "Batman" and a string of other hits to run Columbia Pictures, which Sony is acquiring in a $3.4-billion tender offer set to expire today. Guber and Peters accepted their new Columbia posts on Sept. 27 without obtaining a release from Warner.

Sony and Warner are due to square off Thursday in Los Angeles Superior Court, where Warner is seeking a preliminary injunction to bar Guber and Peters from "reneging" on their five-year contract signed seven months ago. Warner contends that it has already invested or committed $13 million in 50 new projects being developed by Guber and Peters.

Sony, for its part, argued in its response last week that "California courts look down upon injunctions limiting a person's employment opportunities."

In an 11-page statement attached to Sony's filing, CBS Records President and Chief Executive Walter R. Yetnikoff detailed his behind-the-scenes role for the first time. Sony acquired CBS Records last year, and CBS Records and Warner's three record companies have continued a fierce rivalry.

In his sworn statement, Yetnikoff accused Time Warner Co-Chairman Steven J. Ross of bias against the Japanese or Sony dating back to 1987, when Sony decided to bid for the record company. According to Yetnikoff, Ross "even offered to act as my personal adviser to help construct a deal that would avoid a sale to Sony, and in fact attempted to do so."

Yetnikoff also insisted that Sony never set out to "raid" Warner, although the CBS Records executive said he, Guber and Peters had "long shared a hope of working together some day."

According to Yetnikoff, Sony approached three other candidates to head Columbia while Yetnikoff was "in resident treatment at a health facility in Minnesota" in August and early September. After leaving the Minnesota facility Sept. 10, Yetnikoff said, he decided to call Guber and did so four days later. In describing subsequent meetings, Yetnikoff said, "Guber and Peters assured us . . . that Warner presented no problem whatsoever. . . . They also assured us that they would have no trouble in obtaining a written release from Warner."

The CBS Records president said he never saw the Warner contract during the September negotiations. By Oct. 3, however -- one week after Sony's bid for Columbia was announced -- "it became apparent that Guber and Peters were having difficulty with Warner" and Sony asked "for the first time" to hear the contract's relevant language, the statement said.

Yetnikoff -- who is a lawyer -- said he concluded that a formal release from Warner was not necessary since Guber and Peters were being hired as chief executives and not as movie producers. (Sony contends that the Warner contract is limited to movie production.)

In Warner's supplemental filings Monday, longtime studio chief Daly took strong exception to the notion that Guber and Peters could work at two studios simultaneously.

"As chief executives of a studio, they would be heavily involved in creative decisions. For example, if Guber and Peters had a new concept for a film or were able to attract a promising new literary work or a star actor or director, to which studio would they allocate the asset?" Daly asked.

Daly also took issue with Guber's claim that Guber kept his negotiations with Sony secret for fear that there might be premature disclosure "and presumably trading on inside information, since Columbia and Guber-Peters Entertainment Co. are both public companies." The Warner Bros. chairman pointed out that he had respected Guber's confidence in the past when Guber confided that he intended to make large investments in two other publicly traded entertainment companies.

Semel, the No. 2 executive at the Warner studio in Burbank, refuted Guber and Peters' contentions that he was willing to let them out of their Warner contract.

"I do not make oral agreements to terminate written agreements, especially when they involve key talent and millions of dollars," Semel said in his latest filing. According to Semel, Guber was told that Warner would not release him from the five-year contract in a phone call on Sept. 25 -- "two days before Guber and Peters signed their agreement with Sony."

In that same phone conversation, Guber told Semel that he had "negotiated secretly because he feared Mr. Ross would have called (Chairman Akio) Morita of Sony and that would have put an end to his negotiations," the Semel affidavit said.

And Ross, in a new declaration filed Monday, vehemently denied any anti-Japanese bias, noting that he had entertained Morita in his home. Ross said he never counseled against the sale of CBS Records to a Japanese company, although he did suggest a tax-free spinoff to CBS shareholders as most beneficial.
