BFN [By staff reporter Kim Sung-pok] [Text] As former U.S. president Jimmy Carter was in Pyongyang to defuse tensions sparked by the suspected North Korean nuclear weapons program yesterday, a crack appeared in the South Korea-U.S. tuning of nuclear strategy against the North. An unusually heavy mood was felt at Chongwadae [presidential offices]. Even there were signs of embarrassment as Carter's first round of talks with North Korean leader Kim Il-song and subsequent press conference received a quick response from Washington indicating the resumption of dialogue. Chong Chong-uk, senior presidential aide handling the North Korean nuclear issue, avoided reporters. He meets the press daily to give them background briefings at his office, a daily practice since tension over the nuclear standoff increased. Instead, he huddled with his aides in charge of national security affairs, defense and diplomacy. He had an unscheduled meeting with U.S. Ambassador to Korea James Laney. Chong was to receive a visit from Russian Ambassador to Korea Georgiy Kunadze who observers said was to explain to him his government's position on the U.S.-led draft sanctions resolution to be put to the United Nations Security Council. But he canceled the meeting with Kunadze to meet Laney. Chongwadae appeared embarrassed to find out that Carter's visit to Pyongyang, which was billed as private and self-initiated, can in no way be viewed in that context. In his June 10 telephone conversation with President Kim Yong-sam who was leery of possible adverse affects of Carter's planned visit to the North, Clinton assured him that the trip to Pyongyang was self-initiated and had nothing to do with his administration. Clinton also assured President Kim that he will not change his mind in pursuing sanctions against the renegade communist country whatever conciliatory message Carter may bring from Pyongyang. President Kim has stressed from time to time that no dialogue will be sought with the North until the first-phase of U.N. Security Council sanctions are imposed. He has also added that this position was agreed to by the United States. Since Pyongyang's unilateral refueling of the 5-megawatt nuclear reactor without monitoring by the International Atomic Energy Agency (IAEA), President Kim has held the belief that a lack of tough measures by Seoul and the international community has allowed the North to take the initiatives at every turn of developments. Now it is necessary to turn the tables around, stressing the need to impose sanctions. However, yesterday's dispatches from Pyongyang quoting Carter and the quick response from the White House have Seoul and Washington turning toward dialogue rather than determined action by the international community. During his two and a half hour dinner meeting with Carter on the eve of his departure for Pyongyang, President Kim reportedly said he did not want to add to the "burden" on Carter's shoulder, a position indicating that he did not ask Carter to convey any important message of his own to Kim Il-song. He was said to have only explained in detail his government's position against the North's nuclear ambitions. Apart from the discrepancies between Seoul and Washington, there are some mistakes in judgment on the part of Chongwadae staffers and officials of the National Unification Board. They failed to give due weight to Carter's visit. It was too late when a Chongwadae staffer learned that Carter would be greeted by Vice Foreign Minister Song Ho-kyong of North Korea at the truce village of Panmunjom. Pointing out the fact, the official stressed the need to pay attention to Pyongyang's intention which he said is to regard Carter as a "political negotiator" not just a visitor on a private mission. But this was not taken seriously by senior officials, it was said.
