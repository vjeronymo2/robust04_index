September 19, 1989, Tuesday, Home Edition

OPERA REVIEW;

BOITO'S 'MEFISTOFELE' GOES TRENDY

What's a poor opera company to do? What's a poor critic to do?

On Friday, the San Francisco Opera presented Alban Berg's "Lulu" in a lavish, thoughtful new production by Lotfi Mansouri. For the most part, it respected the composer's wishes. Your faithful scribe admired the tasteful result but complained, sotto voce, that it seemed just a bit too timid.

On Saturday, the same company introduced a new production of Boito's "Mefistofele." No one could accuse it of timidity. Robert Carsen, the latest Wunderkind director on the trendy international circuit, had second-guessed the composer in just about every area except the harmonic progressions. Your faithful scribe found the tasteless result a bit too daring.

There must be a useful middle ground between rigid respect for convention and reckless insensitivity. San Francisco hasn't found it this season.

Carsen and his designing accomplice, Michael Levine, haven't just staged "Mefistofele." They have raped the old romantic opera.

They have done so with wild flights of imagination, with skill and guts. They have concocted a terrific show. It offers non-stop titillation, smothered with glitz. It takes pride in jolting perspectives and shocking sensibilities. Still, rape is rape.

Boito, you may recall, wrote his lush, somewhat sentimental, ultimately poignant version of the Faust legend in 1868. It is Italianate in its primitive passions, sweeping in its grandeur, and far closer to Goethe's epic than the perfumed version of Charles Gounod.

Boito thought big. He did not shrink from the philosophical confrontation between the elemental forces of good and evil. He created a serious portrait of a heroic devil who dares shake a cynical fist at a lofty deity.

Carsen trivializes all that, makes fun of it, and introduces stylized comic distractions. He plays the entire drama on a ersatz-baroque stage within the stage. All the world's a theater. Life is make-believe. Get it?

We got that message a long time ago. What else is new?

Mefistofele, in this context, becomes a two-bit hocus-pocus villain. He climbs out of the orchestra pit on a ladder. He toys with a lot of magic props, changes costumes constantly, favors anything red, conjures up puffs of smoke, smiles and cackles. He hitches the unfortunate Faust to a flying contraption that would have been rejected by a bus-and-truck company Peter Pan. He looks like some suave imitation bad guy who wandered in from an Offenbach operetta.

That's just the beginning. The phony boxes in the phony opera house behind the proscenium are haunted by mysterious masked figures in white shrouds and gold crowns. We think they represent a Greek chorus masquerading as a saintly presence.

Then there is Faust himself, a quizzical fellow in symbolic white tie and white tails. And his Margherita, a pleasant peasant bride who inhabits a not-so-merry merry-go-round that is decked with apple trees and cranked into rotation by a convenient crone. Helen of Troy becomes a statuesque diva and her court is turned into an audience of tuxedoed sugar daddies bearing floral tributes.

Oops. Almost forgot the mock raunch.

The motley crowd -- a very, very motley crowd -- at the kermesse includes a nearly naked Adam and Eve, plus a non-biblical pair of conspicuously copulating revelers. Many of the costumes reveal flesh in surprising places. The vision of Margherita is totally, literally, and not all that idealistically, denuded. The Walpurgisnacht choristers sport modern underwear, and less. The anachronistic outfits come equipped with prop genitalia.

And so it goes, quickly and lustily. Sight overpowers sound. Cutesy gags trample lyricism. Clever gimmicks distort honest emotion. It is fine, if one doesn't happen to care about the music.

Ironically, the San Francisco forces do care. Maurizio Arena conducts with splendid breadth and, where possible, with canny point. The overburdened chorus, trained by Ian Robertson, pays equal attention to Arena's stick and Carsen's shtick. The cast is strong, for the most part, and it plays gamely with the directorial conceits.

The center of attention must be Samuel Ramey. He sings the title role very well, in his somewhat monotonous way, and he makes a mighty noise -- mightier at the top, actually, than at the bottom. He seems to enjoy the hammy charades. He exudes obvious matinee-idol allure. He takes his shirt off -- a personal trademark, whatever the role -- with proud aplomb. It isn't his fault that he cannot make some of us forget Norman Treigle.

As Faust, Dennis O'Neill sings with as much ardor and flexibility as his rather small and dry tenor allows, and he is good sport. It is worth pondering how Luciano Pavarotti might have reacted -- and looked -- if a director wanted to twirl him above the set on a wire.

Gabriela Benakova, lirico spinto par excellence, mustered an eloquent Margherita and a sultry Elena, the hint of vocal indisposition notwithstanding.

The excellent supporting ensemble included Judith Christin as a giddy Marta, Emily Manhart as a mellifluous Pantalis, Daniel Harper as a befuddled Wagner and Douglas Wusnch as a staunch Nereo. The ballet provided a graceful Grecian divertissement under the direction of Alphonse Poulin, though the dancers didn't seem to appreciate the pervasive parody.

The production, incidentally, came from Geneva and will end up in Chicago. The opening-night audience hailed it with generally hearty applause.

Some boos greeted the director and designer, however, at their curtain call. Most of the disapproval seemed to emanate from the standing room. That area has always accommodated the most discerning patrons.
