931222 FT 22 DEC 93 / Ailing shipyard wins EU lifeline Swan Hunter, the Tyneside shipbuilder in receivership, yesterday won eligibility for Shipbuilding Intervention Funding subsidy which will be paid by the UK government. The decision, taken by European commissioners in Brussels, improves the company's prospects of finding a buyer by enhancing its competitiveness in bidding for merchant ship orders. But restrictions placed on fund eligibility mean that the concession, although a political triumph, only offers a breathing space while the company tries to secure its future. The company's eligibility for the 9 per cent subsidy is limited to a maximum of Pounds 7m for a two-year period, and will be applied to no more than two to three merchant orders. The European Commission described the conversion back to a merchant yard as 'partial and limited'. Factors behind the Commission's decision included the UK government's commitment that this was a 'one-off' request which did not set a precedent for other military yards, the limited effect on the EU's production capacity and the effect eligibility would have in increasing the receivers' chances of finding a purchaser for the yard. The decision was warmly welcomed by unions, local MPs and MEPs, local authorities and industry minister Mr Tim Sainsbury. Swan Hunter, he said, played a vital role in the UK's ability to maintain its naval defence capability. Mr Ed James of Price Waterhouse, the receivers which are running the company and are seeking to sell it as a going concern, said the news must increase its attractiveness to prospective purchasers. 'We hope that it will encourage them to develop an interest in what is a lean, fit and increasingly competitive yard.' Mr Steve Byers, Wallsend Labour MP, said yesterday: 'A prospective buyer can now see the whole picture. We are now waiting in the next six weeks for a buyer to come forward. If that doesn't happen, I think we have seen the end of shipbuilding on the Tyne.'
