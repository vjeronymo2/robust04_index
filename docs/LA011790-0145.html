January 17, 1990, Wednesday, P.M. Final

PEOPLE'S PHARMACY: PHARMACEUTICAL FIRMS PEDDLE INFLUENCE AS WELL AS PILLS

Is your doctor on the take? Is his office filled with drug company calendars, note pads, pens and other mementos labeled with brand names?

We're not talking about big-time bribery here, but pharmaceutical promotion may have as much effect on people's health as outright influence peddling.

In the highly competitive world of pharmaceuticals, the difference between one high blood pressure medicine and another may be slight. Arthritis remedies and antibiotics have proliferated, but true advances are few and far between.

To get doctors to prescribe one brand over another, companies spend over $5,000 per doctor on drug promotion -- over $2 billion a year. This drives up the price of prescription medicines.

Starting in medical school, doctors in training receive stethoscopes, books and little black bags from company representatives. Meetings and parties for medical students and residents may be underwritten by pharmaceutical manufacturers.

Once in practice, doctors are invited to dinners or sporting events. Some are given consulting fees merely for attending meetings at which the merits of a particular drug are extolled. Occasionally, doctors are invited to take expense-paid vacations in exotic resorts for "continuing medical education."

Some companies have actually paid doctors outright for recruiting patients into pseudo-scientific studies -- run not by the research wing of the company, but supervised by the sales and marketing department.

As federal spending on basic research has dwindled, the pharmaceutical industry has increasing power over research funding. And this power of the purse strings gives them other leverage.

It is time American physicians adopted an ethical code similar to that in England. British doctors see pharmaceutical reps only on the condition that no gift or financial inducement is offered.

At a time when congressmen and government officials have come under increasing scrutiny for bribery, doctors need to examine their own ethics.
