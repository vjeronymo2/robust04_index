July 12, 1990, Thursday, Home Edition

ERMA BOMBECK: IF OUR KIDS WERE IN CHARGE, WE'D BE UP A TREE

For years, it's been a standard greeting: "Hi, Honey, I'm home." Only nowadays, a kid answers.

According to national real-estate marketing research, children of the two-income, two-career family have inherited the role of prime occupants of the house.

They didn't pick out the house or design it. They don't even pay for it, but most of them spend more time in it than their parents. The obvious question is: Why don't children have more input into where they live than they do? The obvious answer is: Have you seen the way kids live when there are no adults around?

First, their choice of a house would be one humongous room built in a tree with a rope ladder. Anyone wanting to enter would have to have the secret code word. The entire house would have only two appliances: a television covering one wall, and a microwave covering the other wall (replacing Phyllis Diller's fantasy: an oven that flushes). Basketball hoops would be positioned over wastebaskets, clothes hampers, the kitchen sink, towel racks and clothes hooks. There would be no beds -- only pillows on the floor in front of the Nintendo library.

The bathroom would be self-cleaning, with a water heater the size of an active crater. Every child would have a plug implant so that wherever he is in the house, he could plug in a stereo, a radio or a hair dryer.

They would live in a disposable world. Disposable underwear, disposable towels, disposable clothes, disposable dishes and containers. The center of the floor would have a campfire so their friends could sit around and play with matches and toast marshmallows.

Off on one small tree branch would be a separate room the size of a broom closet. This would be for Mom and Dad. When they didn't pay attention or messed up, they would be made to go there without television, dinner or human sounds. The tree house would have a small patch of grass at the bottom of the tree, which the parents would be required to cut with scissors every two days.

Parents wouldn't spend a lot of time in their room, as they would have chores and be sent on errands to pick up videocassettes every night. Yet, they would have to clean it every 15 minutes.

Pure fantasy, you say with a laugh. Don't be too sure. I have just described my son's apartment.
