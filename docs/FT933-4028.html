930910 FT 10 SEP 93 / Press complaints body plans to resign if ombudsman appointed LORD McGregor and the other members of the Press Complaints Commission plan to resign en masse if the government imposes an ombudsman to deal with complaints against newspapers. The government is pushing ahead with proposals for an ombudsman with the power to award compensation to victims of press intrusion. Proposals are also well advanced to create new criminal offences covering electronic eavesdropping and taking pictures on private property, with tightly constrained public-interest defences. The Lord Chancellor's Office has also proposed a new law giving citizens the right to sue for up to Pounds 10,000 in damages for invasion of privacy. The stage now looks set for a bitter confrontation between press and government. Lord McGregor, chairman of the Press Council's replacement, has said he will resign on the day an ombudsman is appointed, as self-regulation of the press would be fatally weakened. His fellow commission members take a similar view. There has been considerable confusion and disagreement in the government on press regulation in the wake of complaints about intrusion into the lives of politicians and the royal family. A white paper due from the Department of National Heritage before the summer parliamentary recess failed to appear. One of those involved said: 'We are waiting for a majority view to emerge.' A consensus seems to be forming around the idea of the 'voluntary' ombudsman who would hear appeals from those dissatisfied with commission verdicts. The commission is paid for by the newspaper industry but now has a majority of independent members. The newspaper industry has said it will neither accept nor pay for such an ombudsman. Mr Dugal Nisbet-Smith, director of the Newspaper Society, the body representing local and regional newspapers, said: 'It is an utterly, utterly absurd idea. It will be opposed by every newspaper in the country.'
