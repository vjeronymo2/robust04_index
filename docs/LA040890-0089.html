April 8, 1990, Sunday, Home Edition

NO ONE HAS FAINTEST IDEA WHAT TO DO;

SOCIAL PROBLEMS: THE REPUBLICANS LACK THE WILL TO SOLVE THEM, BUT DEMOCRATS LACK THE WAY.

We are living in a time of unprecedented national consensus. The great ideological arguments of the past -- over Russia, Vietnam, Nicaragua, the welfare state, nuclear deterrence, etc.

-- are over. There are only two real issues on the American agenda. Debt: the budget deficit and associated debt problems (e.g., the S&L debacle). And race: namely, the social problems (drugs, violence, poor schooling, family break-up) of the urban underclass.

On debt, everybody knows what has to be done -- raise taxes and/or cut entitlements -- but no one of either party has the courage to do it. On race, the opposite is true: Everyone wants to do something but no one has the faintest idea what to do. When the two great issues of the day are in a state of paralysis, one for lack of will and one for lack of way, it is not surprising that both government and opposition appear marginal.

In an era of ideological consensus, the parties naturally find themselves engaged in narrow arguments over means. Everybody is for education, child care and clean air. In the clean-air bill now being hammered out in Congress, the fight is about a little less tailpipe emissions here, a little more high-sulfur coal there. Important issues, but hardly galvanizing.

Which explains the attraction of would-be Democratic issues such as protectionism, Social Security and demobilization. They all have the potential to galvanize. It is therefore a critique of sorts to say that the Democrats as a party are not hungry, not ruthless, not irresponsible enough to seize these issues as a route to power.

But let's not confuse partisanship with statesmanship. Populism is fun and it may be profitable. But it isn't vision.
