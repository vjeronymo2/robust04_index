September 10, 1990, Monday, Home Edition

IS THIS POLITICAL ACT TOO TOUGH TO FOLLOW?

For the election of 1990, Democrats can take heart in the fact that the public is considerably smarter than the politicians and strategists who try to game-plan each election campaign. Voters have put their faith in our Constitution's separation of powers and take pride in electing Democratic senators and representatives who will check and balance their chosen Republican President.

But Democrats may find much less to cheer about after that. For the presidential election of 1992 may be when the true political impact of the current Mideast crisis is seen.

The super summit events that dominated our screens this weekend are certainly causing some second thoughts within the most private councils of the Mario Cuomos and Sam Nunns and Albert Gores and Richard Gephardts and Charles Robbs and all other Democrats who may have entertained the notion of running for President.

These are Democrats who can all say with confidence that they could lead where President Bush has faltered in shaping new policies on issues from the federal budget and economy to global warming. But running for President is a very personal thing. And so now all prospective Democratic candidates must be looking at their TV screens and asking themselves this personal, soul-searching question:

Am I confident that I could have responded as swiftly and skillfully -- militarily and diplomatically -- as did Bush if the Iraqi crisis happened on my presidential watch?

Unless their private answer is a resounding yes, it will be hard for any of them to mount a campaign against Bush in 1992, let alone beat him.
