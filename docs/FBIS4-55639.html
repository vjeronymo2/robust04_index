CSO [Article by Kevin Drew: "Slovakia Pushes Reforms"] [Text] Bratislava--Slovak leaders are stepping up their economic reform program, pledging to put 100 billion Sk ($3.3 billion) worth of companies on the market when the second wave of privatization is launched in September. Around 300 companies, representing 33 percent of all state-owned property, are slated to go on the auction block this fall. In comparison, only 3 percent privatized between June 1992, when Vladimir Meciar was elected prime minister, and last month, when parliament forced him out of office. The drive towards privatization, timed to hit on the eve of the national elections scheduled for Sept. 30 and Oct. 1, is being urged along by government officials and economists who want to reinstill in the international community the confidence that Slovakia is committed to reform. "The aim is to push privatization so far that no future government could reverse it, not even Meciar," said one Western advisor who wished to remain anonymous. Inheritors of the poorer half of the former Czechoslovak nation, Slovak leaders have managed to make some progress towards a market economy. The private sector now accounts for nearly 40 percent of economic activity and government debt is low compared to many other post-communist countries. "It's not that Slovakia has backpedaled or done poorly," said Andreas Georgiou, resident representative of the International Monetary Fund. "Slovakia hasn't progressed as rapidly as it could have, but it has progressed." Between 1989 and 1992, 30 percent of the Slovak economy was privatized. But soon after Meciar was elected prime minister in June 1992, the Slovak government announced it was slowing the pace of privatization, arguing that rapid reforms could backfire by creating skyrocketing inflation, unemployment and public discontent. A number of the privatization projects carried out under Meciar's government have since been attacked as illegal. Early this month, for instance, the parliamentary committee for privatization found that 13 projects approved by the Meciar administration in February violated the government's own privatization rules by giving advantages to a select group of buyers. The former government approved the direct sale of state companies to pre-selected buyers, even though the buyers of some firms such as Tesla Stropkov, Skloobal Nemsova, ZOS Vrutkz and ZOS Trnava could not demonstrate that they had enough financial resources to develop the companies. The slowdown in privatization never achieved its goals, however. Under Meciar, unemployment and inflation shot up because the government never implemented a clear reform path. Budget deficits began to mount. Unemployment in Slovakia now stands at 15 percent, compared to 3 percent in the Czech Republic; inflation is 15 percent, compared to the annualized Czech rate of 10 percent; and the average monthly income is $160, compared to over $200 in the Czech Republic. The slowdown of privatization also sent warning signals to international investors initially eager to capitalize on Slovakia's well-trained and cheap labor poor. By mid-1992, only 122 Slovak companies had U.S. investment, totaling just $20 million. "Investors don't like uncertainty," said Douglas Swaim, senior investment officer of the Slovak-American Enterprise Fund. "From the inside, we're convinced that Slovakia will stabilize. But from the outside, you have to wonder." In the six months left before the fall elections, the new government hopes to assuage such fears. "We must finish our attempts at macroeconomic stability as well as microeconomic stability," Finance Minister Rudolf Filkus told reporters recently. Government leaders intend to reduce this year's projected budget deficit from 23 billion Sk to 6 billion Sk, lower inflation to 13 percent and keep unemployment under 17 percent. Courting foreign aid and smoothing trade relations with the Czech Republic are also priorities. "To some degree, Slovakia is a diamond in the rough," said Swaim. "If the country can get its act together with privatization and articulating a clear economic policy, it will have no trouble attracting investors."
