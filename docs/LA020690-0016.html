February 6, 1990, Tuesday, Home Edition

KASHMIR

India has ordered all foreign correspondents out of Kashmir (Part A, Jan. 28). The world's largest democracy is determined to continue its undemocratic hold on Kashmir and does not want the world to know about it.

India's international commitment to a United Nations supervised plebiscite in Kashmir remains unfulfilled.

The leadership in India presents this struggle as a resurgence of Islamic fundamentalism. The focus is thus shifted from the right of self-determination for Kashmiris, to an uprising of religious fanatics against secular India, with the implication that the movement is against democratic interests.

Reality is that, in the four decades of Indian independence, free elections have been an exception rather than the rule in Kashmir. A protester in Kashmir is routinely branded as a fundamentalist and as pro-Pakistani. Even kids below 10 years of age have been arrested under the anti-terrorism act.

In Kashmir, the economic base has not expanded; what has expanded is the depth and breadth of corruption. Kashmir, once a bread basket for the neighboring areas, is now a net importer of agricultural and consumer goods.

True friends of democratic India need to impress upon the Indian leadership that for a democracy to survive, human rights cannot be the exclusive domain of some but must be universal. Kashmiris must have their basic human rights: the right of self-determination by free and impartial elections.

RAFIQUE A. KHAN

Los Angeles
