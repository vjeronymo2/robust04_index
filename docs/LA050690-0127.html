May 6, 1990, Sunday, Home Edition

NEWS ANALYSIS;

STRATEGISTS DEBATE NATO'S MILITARY, POLITICAL FUTURE;

ALLIANCES: DESPITE U.S. SUPPORT, THE GROUP MUST PROVE IT CAN SERVE NEW EUROPEAN SECURITY INTERESTS TO SURVIVE.

Military officers and diplomats at NATO headquarters here are clearly buoyed by the firm support they have received from Secretary of State James A. Baker III for a future role for the organization.

At a time of uncertainty, they heartily welcomed Baker's reassurance last week that President Bush wants to enhance the political role of the North Atlantic Treaty Organization.

Many senior officials here worry that despite NATO's record as the most successful defense alliance in modern European history, the Cold War's end may force NATO into premature retirement -- a kind of yesterday's alliance.

Various political voices in Western Europe, particularly West German Social Democrats and their foreign allies, suggest increasingly that NATO has outlived its usefulness and ought to be scrapped.

These critics do not spell out exactly what should take the place of NATO, but there is talk of a European Community with a security dimension, or, better yet, a strengthening of the 35-nation Conference on Security and Cooperation in Europe (CSCE), which includes East European countries.

This disturbs NATO officials, who believe that the alliance should not be phased out but be praised and given new tasks.

"When you've just won the ball game, you don't retire the winning team," one senior American military man said. "Yet that's just what some Europeans are suggesting we do."

The military officers, diplomats, and strategic specialists who support a continued NATO are not simply cold warriors who do not want to lose their jobs or who stubbornly talk about potential threats from the Soviet Union.

They agree that military forces in Western Europe will be sharply reduced in the years ahead.

"We simply think that it is way too premature to disband NATO," said Simon Lunn, a senior strategist with the North Atlantic Assembly, a group of lawmakers in the member countries.

Another official said: "While nobody expects Soviet armored divisions to roll across the north German plain, they are still a force to be considered -- with 16 divisions in East Germany alone. So there is a need for a Western military force in being. Who knows what the Kremlin's policies will be a year from now."

As these people see it, there are still many military tasks for NATO, as well as new and broader reasons for its continuing existence. NATO's role, they say, should emphasize the political side of the 16-member alliance, which includes the key countries of Western Europe plus the United States and Canada.

"We have always had a strong political component," Secretary General Manfred Woerner said. "We have never been solely a military alliance."

Vernon A. Walters, the U.S. ambassador to West Germany, adds: "NATO has never been simply about soldiers and tanks and planes. It's also the seat of European political connections, the center of European decision-making since World War II."

Of the non-military potential of NATO, a senior U.S. diplomat here said: "With Europe in the throes of change, NATO is more than ever needed. NATO is a predictable factor. It is a structure that ties in the United States with Europe. It gives a united Germany a home. It gives Eastern European countries like Poland, Czechoslovakia and Hungary a sense of not being left in a void. And it is in a position to dampen and quell local conflicts, particularly those that might break out among ethnic regions in Central Europe and the Balkans.

"In short, it is an all-purpose organization that represents calm, certainty and stability."

Few Western politicians want to scrap NATO overnight, but some have argued that the CSCE, established in Helsinki in 1975, would be better suited to serve new European political and security interests since it brings together all European nations with the exception of Albania, plus the United States and Canada.

Supporters of this plan would strengthen the loosely organized CSCE, which is an umbrella group for arms control and human rights, and give it a secretariat and a wide range of responsibilities.

However, many NATO supporters argue that CSCE's very breadth is a drawback, perhaps a fatal weakness in any effort to fulfill a realistic security role.

"CSCE operates on a consensus basis," a top NATO planner said, "and it doesn't have any security apparatus. Can you imagine trying to get a unanimous decision on an urgent military matter with countries as diverse as the United States and the Soviet Union, San Marino and Malta? It would be like a mini-United Nations, and it just wouldn't work."

But what could be NATO's most important new role, according to some Western strategists who are hesitant about discussing the matter publicly, is the containment of a unified Germany.

One leading NATO diplomat said: "Let's face it, whether the Germans like it or not, they are feared by some nations and worry others. NATO can serve two important purposes: reassuring the Germans that they are members of a strong Western Alliance and reassuring Germany's neighbors that the alliance can embrace Germany.

"Even the Russians are realizing, though they won't admit it publicly yet, the advantage of NATO as a containing force for a united Germany."

Secretary General Woerner, a former West German defense minister, has even warned that a powerful and neutralized Germany would be a "loose cannon."

In looking toward Europe's future, one NATO ambassador observed: "The veneer of European integration is still very thin. The key to future harmony and stability in Europe is a united Germany that is balanced and linked to friendly powers, most particularly the United States. The only proven and existing organization that can play that role is NATO."
