July 14, 1990, Saturday, Orange County Edition

NONTOXIC PEST CONTROL BECOMES BIG BUSINESS;

EXTERMINATORS: TALLON USES NOVEL APPROACHES AND AVOIDS SPRAYING POISONS.

The best way to zap termites, said exterminator Jay Tallon, is to "freeze their little buns off."

That's just one of several novel ways Tallon and his brother, Joe, have devised to kill household pests without using poisons that may endanger pets, plants or homeowners. And it's become big business.

"We have what the consumer wants today. They don't want you to go out there and spray toxic chemicals on everything in sight," Jay Tallon said.

Tallon Termite & Pest Control, based in Redondo Beach, has developed an arsenal that eschews toxic pesticides in favor of high-tech devices and old-fashioned common sense.

Included are a "bug vacuum" for swooshing up spiders and other creepy crawlies, a steam-based "flea machine" for infested carpets, glue boards for catching mice and a tiny electric fence that keeps home foundations off limits to multiple-footed outsiders.

The Tallons have also perfected a way to literally look inside the walls of homes and pinpoint where termites may be munching away. Using fiber-optic instruments, pest-control experts peek inside those hard-to-reach places to find the unwanted denizens.

The voracious termites tend to cluster, Tallon said. Normally, they will be found in just a couple of walls, congregated in three or four of the timber support studs.

The first step in getting rid of them is to probe with fiber-optic instruments to pinpoint their location. Then a dime-sized hole is drilled at the bottom of the wall and another at the top. Liquid nitrogen is pumped through the holes into the inaccessible areas, freezing the termites.

"We freeze their little buns off," Tallon said with a grin, noting that termites are killed by the cold, which reaches minus-20 degrees, and not by the liquid nitrogen itself.

"Termites will tell you they're 98% water," a Tallon radio ad says. "When we're done with 'em, they are 200% frozen. The idea is dead simple. We freeze termites instead of fumigate them."

Eighty percent of the air humans breathe consists of nitrogen, and the freezing process is environmentally safe, Tallon said. When the temperature returns to normal, the liquid nitrogen evaporates.

Called the Blizzard System, the eradication process is registered with the Environmental Protection Agency, and Tallon said the company is the only one in the nation using the super-cold approach for excising termites.

But some species of termites succumb more easily to excess heat than cold, and the Tallons last year began marketing a companion system called the HeatWave.

Using a convection heating system, heat strips are applied to the wood requiring treatment and then the electricity is turned on. About 30 minutes are required for the heat to penetrate to the center of the wood and reach 135 degrees. The termites die within a minute or two.

Tallon is applying its non-traditional, environmentally safe methods toward the destruction of all types of household pests, including ants, roaches, spiders, fleas, mice and rats.

The company, which saw sales jump from $85,000 a month in 1987 to $700,000 per month this year, is expanding into the San Francisco Bay Area this summer and is opening offices in Florida and Hawaii.

"We hope to be throughout the United States over the next five years," Tallon said.

The traditional method of tenting -- enclosing a home and moving everyone out before spraying for bugs with pesticides -- is becoming increasingly distasteful in this era of growing environmental awareness, he said.

"We don't want to become spray jockeys," he added. "We've started a pilot program for comprehensive, non-conventional pest control."

Tallon's war on fleas features a "flea machine" that steams the carpet and kills the adult wingless hoppers hiding in the fibers. It also includes a nontoxic "birth-inhibitor" spray that prevents young insects from matriculating through a complete life cycle.

Rodents get no respite from the Tallons either. The company's crews use electrical barriers, consisting of a single strand of wire and low-voltage batteries, to prevent mice, rats and roaches from coming inside the house.

"We first exclude them from the structure," Tallon said. "We trap the ones in the house with mice glue boards. They have a gummy substance on them that attracts the mice to it, and (they) go and get stuck and they suffocate."

Then there's the "Bugvac" for spiders. "We vacuum all the spiders up," Tallon said. "We go outside as well as in the home and vacuum the corners and areas where spiders are."

The cost of treating a 2,000-square-foot house via the Tallon method ranges between $1,000 and $1,500, he said.
