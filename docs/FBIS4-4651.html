BFN [Text] Seoul, April 4 (YONHAP) -- President Kim Yong-sam's office may strengthen the government's overall policy coordination as senior administration officials continue to contradict one another on the North Korean nuclear dispute and other foreign and national security issues. As part of the plan reported Monday, the presidential office would either form a consultative body comprising Chongwadae, the National Unification Board, the foreign and defense ministries and the Agency for National Security Planning, or beef up Chongwadae's team on foreign and national security affairs. One Chongwadae official acknowledged Monday that confusion has emerged in the government's foreign and security policy "because of Foreign Minister Han Sung-chu's attitude, Ambassador to China Hwang Pyong-tae's recent remarks and Vice Foreign Minister Hong Sun-yong's publicly expressed opinion" over the weekend. "Chongwadae is undertaking a study from various angles in order to strengthen the coordinating function of its foreign and security policy," the official said, requesting anonymity. The consultative body is being considered because of the limitations of the current system including conferences of security-related ministers, chaired by the president, and the prime minister's strategic sessions with high government officials. But the new body would not be like the United States' National Security Council (NSC) since the present security-related ministers' meetings perform a similar function, he said. Alternatively, Chongwadae may beef up the office of the senior presidential secretary for foreign and national security affairs since it is the president who makes the final decision on all foreign and security policy, he said. Another Chongwadae official said, "following Ambassador Hwang's inappropriate remarks, our foreign policy was once again plunged into confusion when Vice Foreign Minister Hong suggested on Sunday that the government should consider dropping its demand for a special envoy exchange with North Korea as a precondition for a third round of U.S.-North Korean high-level talks." "It has become an urgent government task to strengthen policy coordination if we are to establish an effective North Korea strategy," the official said. "The weakest point in our foreign policy in the past started with showing all our cards," the official pointed out. Henceforth, the government will keep secret its negotiating strategy while beefing up the publicity function, he said.
