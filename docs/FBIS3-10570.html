Language: Portuguese Article Type:BFN [Article by Maria Helena Tachinardi] [Text] Brazil and Venezuela have reactivated their cooperation activities, thus overcoming a period of tensions that characterized the coexistence of these two countries for almost 150 years. At the conclusion of the visit paid by Brazilian President Itamar Franco to his Venezuelan counterpart Rafael Caldera, the importance of the meeting was underlined by the level of the Brazilian ministers who also came to La Guaira, that is, the heads of five important ministries in the foreign affairs field: the industry and commerce, mines and energy, foreign affairs, environment, and justice ministers. The two presidents issued a joint communique creating a high-level binational commission that will be in charge of stepping up cooperation in the border areas. The commission will be headed by the foreign ministers, and will include all the sectors of the Brazilian and Venezuelan economy. To mark the meeting the presidents signed a protocol which is to be added to the 1977 friendship and cooperation agreement between the two countries. Caldera stated emphatically: "What happened in the Amazon jungle a few months ago (the murder of Ianomami Indians) helped us open our eyes, and made us aware of the need to harmonize our policies and join efforts to attend to this vast Amazon region, which has great natural resources." The visit was also commented on by Alberto Poletto, president of the Venezuelan Foreign Trade Institute. In his opinion, the meeting contributed to increased bilateral commercial relations. Trade between Venezuela and Brazil amounts to approximately $800 million, with a $200 million balance in Brazil's favor. A partial agreement negotiated in 1991, by which Brazil granted Venezuela preferential taxes for 600 new items, is currently in force. Venezuela has granted Brazil preferential taxes for 300 new products. Venezuela purchases 37 percent of what it purchases from ALADI [Latin American Integration Association] member countries in Brazil. During the meeting the presidents raised several international issues, such as: -- President Itamar Franco said that Mercosur [Common Market of the South] can coexist with NAFTA or with the Andean Pact. -- Regarding the candidates for OAS secretary general, Franco commented that "Brazil received a request, which we are assessing, that this post be occupied by the president of a certain Republic. We have discussed the issue with the Colombian Foreign Ministry." Brazil has already announced its support for Colombian President Cesar Gaviria. The Venezuelan president said that Venezuela supports its Foreign Minister Burelli Rivas. -- Regarding Argentina's purchase of fighter bombers from the United States, Franco asserted that Brazil does not interfere in other countries' policies, and that its policy is one that seeks peace. Caldera noted that Venezuela's stance is similar to Brazil's. He does not believe there is an ongoing arms race in Latin America, nor that the United States wants to promote one.
