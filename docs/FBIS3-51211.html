Language: Spanish Article Type:BFN [Text] President Rafael Angel Calderon has received the best evaluation in his entire term as president. The results of the latest Multispecialty Development Counselors (CID/Gallup) poll, which was conducted from 3 to 6 January 1994, indicate that favorable opinions on his administration surpassed the negative ones by 42 points. Fifty-one percent of the 1,010 Costa Ricans polled by CID evaluated Calderon's work favorably. Out of those 616, 35 percent said his work had been very good, and 65 percent said it had been good. In addition, 37 percent maintained that his government's work has been neither good nor bad; 9 percent had an unfavorable opinion; and 3 percent did not reply. Calderon started his government in 1990. The opinions against him were two points higher than the favorable ones in November of that year. He continued to fall in the ratings until he got to minus 25 points in July 1991, which was his lowest point. From then on the favorable opinions started to increase, however, mainly because the period of economic reforms "with a high social cost" ended and current Presidency Minister Rolando Lacle--who is responsible for the official government campaign called "Costa Rica excels" [Costa Rica se supera]--was appointed. This perception of improvement is more frequent among people with a higher education and those who are Social Christian Unity Party followers and who intend to vote for Miguel Angel Rodriguez. It is evident that when the Calderon Fournier administration ends the president will have the satisfaction of having obtained the backing of most citizens, making him the president who has received the second highest rating from Costa Ricans. In increasing order, at the end of their presidential terms, Rodrigo Carazo had a minus 65 points, Luis Alberto Monge had plus 25, Rafael Angel Calderon plus 42 points, and Oscar Arias plus 60 points, which is the highest score received by a president. As in Arias' case, a president's popularity or high scores for a good administration are not decisive factors to win a campaign, as, after Arias' term, his party's candidate, Carlos Manuel Castillo, lost the elections. There is a relation between the political party, the president's opinion, and the way people perceive the country's future, however. The polls show that the Social Christians as well as citizens with a higher education are more optimistic when appraising the perspectives for the next two years. This indicates that the current government's work has given them confidence for the future. In total, 28 percent of Costa Ricans believe the future will be better, 29 percent that it will be worse, 28 percent that it will be the same, and 15 percent did not answer that question. If two lines where drawn--one to measure pessimism and the other optimism--we could see how, according to the CID/Gallup studies, expectations have been decreasing constantly. In 1990, negative expectations scored 65 percent, and this figure stands at 29 percent. In contrast, the feeling that the country's situation is going to improve has shown a sustained growth. Only 6 percent of Costa Ricans thought this way 14 years ago, and 28 percent do so now. On this occasion, the poll asked what special benefit did the community, where the pollee lived, obtain from President Calderon's government. In order of importance, the granting of bonds, especially for housing, and the building and repair of roads were the specific benefits the people mentioned more often. Less frequently mentioned were the community centers and homes developed by First Lady Gloria Bejarano. The latter was the opinion of 35 percent of the people polled. In addition, 47 percent of the citizens believe the government has done nothing for their community, and 18 percent had no opinion. The unfavorable opinions prevailed among those who live in the interior of the country and National Liberation Party followers.
