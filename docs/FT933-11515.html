930729 FT 29 JUL 93 / Councils face accounts shake-up NEW RULES requiring councils to keep registers of fixed assets will revolutionise local-authority accounting and are expected to improve financial management. A new statement of recommended practice, approved last week by the Accounting Standards Board, means that councils will have to show the value of property - such as schools and equipment - in their balance sheets at current cost and recognise the cost of using such assets. 'As local authorities become more like commercial organisations they have got to manage their assets properly,' said Mr Martin Evans, head of the technical division at the Chartered Institute of Public Finance and Accountancy, which describes the changes as the most radical in local-authority accounting for 100 years. 'Many authorities are asset-rich but do not know the value of the assets they hold,' he said. Most councils are obliged to implement the changes by the 1994-95 financial year. The move to capital accounting will also help comparisons between public and private-sector services in the move towards compulsory competitive tendering by local authorities. Indications are that few will be enthusiastic about the changes. Mr Martin Pilgrim, of the Labour-controlled Association of Metropolitan Authorities, said many councils were concerned about the cost of the exercise. 'Although it is inevitable that our present practices have to change, many authorities will not see this as positive or helpful.' Mr Evans said that in some respects the new statement goes further than current practice in the private sector. It requires that fixed assets be included in the balance sheet at current value and be revalued at regular intervals. Depreciation charges will then be charged in their accounts based on these amounts.
