July 6, 1990, Friday, Orange County Edition

HOSPITAL CITES NURSE ERROR IN PATIENT DEATH

A Garden Grove woman suffering from advanced breast cancer died last November after a nurse at FHP Hospital in Fountain Valley grabbed the wrong medication from a shelf and mistakenly administered a lethal dose of potassium chloride, FHP officials said Thursday.

"This was an egregious error on the part of the nurse and an inexcusable lapse in judgment," FHP said in a written statement, adding that the nurse was suspended immediately and fired a week later "as a result of her mistake."

On that same date, Nov. 21, 1989, FHP said that it notified the California Board of Registered Nurses of "the termination and the medication error."

The nurse, Maureen Daubert, could not be located Thursday for comment.

Francis J. Johnson, 74, died on Nov. 14, 1989, one day after paramedics brought her to FHP in what a hospital official described as a "semi-comatose" condition. Johnson, according to FHP, "was an end-stage victim of cancer who . . . was expected to die within a short period of time."

Dr. Gary E. Goldstein, a regional vice president for the health maintenance organization, said that Johnson had "perked up" the morning of Nov. 14. But by that afternoon, Goldstein said in an interview, Johnson's condition was listed as "extremely poor" and her doctor prescribed the diuretic Lasix to maintain her blood pressure.

Nurse Daubert, Goldstein said, walked to the shelf where medication is kept and, instead of removing Lasix, mistakenly grabbed "KCl" -- the chemical abbreviation for potassium chloride.

"The (doctor's) order was written for Lasix," Goldstein said. "The error is such that we viewed it as totally accidental -- an extreme medication error. A one-time event."

Goldstein said that as soon as Daubert realized her error, she and others tried immediately but unsuccessfully to revive Johnson.

In addition to firing Daubert, Goldstein said the hospital also has relocated the potassium chloride to prevent the mistake from happening again because of the alphabetical proximity. Goldstein said FHP will continue to investigate the incident.

The statements by FHP officials came on the same day that the Orange County coroner's office formally amended Johnson's death certificate to "acute potassium intoxication." The original certificate, signed by Dr. Sabry Fahmy Ghaly, attributed the death to Johnson's "metastatic breast cancer."

Investigators began looking into the woman's death last month after state officials were contacted by an FHP employee, Fountain Valley Police Investigator Steve Williams said. When deaths occur because of unnatural causes, California law requires hospitals to notify local coroners so that autopsies may be performed.

In its statement on Thursday, FHP said its ongoing "internal review" has found it "difficult to determine Mrs. Johnson's exact cause of death because any one of several acute problems could have been at fault, including septic shock, massive heart failure, and terminal cancer beyond treatment."

As to the issue of FHP not reporting Johnson's death to the coroner for possible autopsy, Goldstein said there was no effort to hide the mistaken dose of potassium chloride. Based on his and an FHP lawyer's questioning of four doctors and four nurses involved, Goldstein said that Johnson's grave condition because of cancer weighed heavily.

"The universal feeling was, the death seemed so obvious to us (because of Johnson's cancer and its complications) it never crossed their minds to make it a coroner's case. Half said, 'Gee, maybe we should have.' And half said, 'Gee, I don't know. . . .' It didn't cross their mind, unfortunately."

Bruce Lyle, a supervising deputy coroner, said that Johnson's death certificate was amended Thursday to reflect what his office believes to be the woman's true cause of death. Asked if FHP officials should have reported Johnson's death for a possible autopsy by the coroner's office last November, Lyle said:

"Absolutely. It would have been autopsied at that time. . . . We can't do anything until the case has been reported to us."

Dr. Ghaly, who signed the original death certificate attributing Johnson's death to her metastatic, or spreading, breast cancer, declined to be interviewed and referred a reporter to FHP's public information office. Goldstein said that contacting the coroner's office is usually the responsibility of FHP's nursing staff.

Johnson, who is survived by her husband and three children, died on Nov. 14 at 5:50 p.m. -- 40 minutes after nurse Daubert administered the potassium, according to Lyle, the deputy coroner.

Based on a review of hospital records and interviews conducted by state medical investigators, Lyle said, the coroner's office determined that Johnson's death was "accidental."

Garry Garcia, a son of Johnson, said Thursday that he was puzzled about how his mother died and the handling of the original death certificate. Garcia said his family did not learn of the mistaken potassium dosage until a coroner's official called June 4.

Asked about that, the FHP's Goldstein said, "I don't know for sure what was said or what wasn't said."

Fountain Valley Police Sgt. Williams said the circumstances surrounding Johnson's death, including the hospital's failure to notify the coroner of the potassium overdose, remain under investigation. Williams said he plans to present his findings to the district attorney's office later this month.

Moreover, Daubert remains under scrutiny from the California Board of Registered Nursing, according to Susan Brank, the board's assistant executive officer. Brank said that the board, which controls the licensing of nurses, has been examining Daubert since January.

But Brank said that, contrary to earlier, erroneous statements by police, Daubert's license has not been revoked and has been in good standing since she began working in California in September, 1981. Brank declined to say what the board has learned during its five months of investigation or what prompted the inquiry.

FHP said its notification to the board last November started the investigation and that FHP doctors and nurses "have cooperated fully."

According to officials with Fountain Valley police and the coroner's office, Daubert had been questioned as of Thursday by only the state nursing board. Investigators say they believe she has moved out of state.

Dr. Thomas A. Raffin, chief of respiratory medicine at the Stanford University School of Medicine, said that an infusion of potassium would typically follow a treatment with Lasix because the diruetic flushes the body of a number of nutrients and minerals.

"If the level of potassium in the blood falls, it can cause the heart to have a bad rhythm, which can result in death," said Raffin. On the other hand, Raffin said, a hyper-elevated level of potassium can stop the heart, also causing death.

In Johnson's case, the administering of potassium before, instead of after, the Lasix, proved deadly. Deputy Coroner Lyle said that Johnson's amended certificate of death lists the cause as "cardiac arrhythmia due to hyper kalemia." In lay terms, Lyle said, "it means too much potassium in the blood system."

Times staff writer Marla Cone contributed to this report.
