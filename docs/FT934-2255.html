931215 FT 15 DEC 93 / Labour attacks rises in cost of NHS bureaucracy Mr John Smith, the leader of the opposition, accused a leading NHS executive of 'grotesque mismanagement of public funds' as Labour yesterday stepped up its campaign against bureaucracy in the National Health Service. At prime minister's question time in the House of Commons, Mr Smith claimed recent reforms of the NHS had allowed health organisations to spend more money on bureaucracy and public relations. 'It is disgraceful that in the health service the sums of money being spent on increased bureaucracy, on huge salaries and perks for managers and on glossy public relations seem totally out of control,' he said. He referred to Sir Robin Buchanan, chairman of the NHS Supplies Authority and a former Tory councillor, whose organisation was recently criticised for spending decisions. Mr Smith said that the organisation had 'wasted hundreds of thousands of pounds changing a single word in their name'. In reply Mr John Major said that spending on NHS administration was 'very tightly examined and controlled' and that more patients were being treated than ever before. The prime minister added that NHS administration amounted to only 2 per cent of total costs. 'There are very few companies that could match that,' he said. However, Mr Smith's attack comes amid signs of disquiet within the cabinet over the cost of NHS reforms. Health service officials have said that Mrs Virginia Bottomley, the health secretary, is privately concerned about recent increases in NHS managers' salaries. Mr John Redwood, the Welsh Secretary, also issued a strong attack last month on growing NHS bureaucracy. Under the NHS reforms regional health authorities have been given greater autonomy for spending on hospital and ambulance services. Ministers say that greater competition in the health service should encourage institutions to keep costs low. But Mr Alan Milburn, a Labour Treasury spokesman, said yesterday: 'There is a very powerful message to get across about the extent of costs under the current administration and the increase in bureaucracy.'
