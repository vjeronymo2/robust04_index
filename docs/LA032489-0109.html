March 24, 1989, Friday, Home Edition

LONG-DISTANCE PHONE RATES TO DIP AGAIN;

BUT CONSUMERS WILL PAY 30 CENTS MORE PER MONTH ON LOCAL BILLS

Long-distance rates for calls between states will drop again April 1, but telephone customers will pay correspondingly more -- 30 cents a month -- on their local phone bills.

The lower interstate tolls and offsetting increase in local bills stem from a Federal Communications Commission program that, over several years now, has been shifting to customers a large part of the costs that long-distance carriers pay local phone companies for completing calls.

According to an FCC finding released in Washington, American Telephone & Telegraph will save even more than the company had estimated -- $705 million, compared to $533 million.

The FCC study also found that the local phone companies underestimated by even more last December when they proposed reducing their long-distance connection charges by $489 million to offset the 30 cents they will gain from local customers in the form of an increase to the "subscriber line charge."

Must Pass Along Savings

The subscriber line charge has been rising periodically in increments until, on April 1, it will peak at $3.50 month. Theoretically, that sum represents each phone user's contribution to the fixed costs of maintaining a nationwide telephone network, regardless of how much a customer may call long distance.

With residential and business users paying more, AT&T has to pay less under the FCC's shared-cost system. And AT&T also must pass along its savings in connection charges to its customers in lower prices.

AT&T will have to recalculate its interstate tolls now in light of the FCC finding, Los Angeles spokeswoman Daylanne Johnson said, but added: "This is more good news for our customers, since their rates will be even lower than we had anticipated."

Most likely, the new reduction will follow the pattern that AT&T proposed on Feb. 15, based on a $533-million reduction. That called for a rate cut averaging 1.6% overall. The April 1 reduction will drop prices nearly 40% lower than before the Bell breakup five years ago.

But the price changes -- under the February proposal, at least -- would vary according to a customer's calling habits. For example, rates on about a third of AT&T's interstate traffic -- those calls running less than 292 miles -- would have increased 4.7%, which AT&T described as "less than a penny a minute." On the other hand, rates during normal business hours (8 a.m. to 5 p.m., Monday through Friday) would have dropped 3.8%.

The business-day reduction normally benefits commercial accounts more than residential customers, who do most of their long-distance calling late at night and on weekends, when rates are discounted 35%. The proposal would have trimmed that discount to 33%.

A $705-million rate cut would likely exaggerate each of those changes.

Rivals Usually Follow Suit

While AT&T's competitors, principally MCI and US Sprint, are not regulated by the FCC -- and thus are not obligated to reduce their rates to reflect their lower costs -- in the past they have mimicked AT&T's reductions to maintain a pricing advantage in the nation's $50-billion long-distance telecommunications market.

The shared cost of the interlocking local and long-distance networks became necessary after the breakup of the Bell System owned by AT&T, which operated a seamless network through its 22 subsidiary companies.

In January, 1984, when AT&T was divested of all local operations to settle a longstanding government antitrust lawsuit, those local subsidiaries were regrouped under seven regional holding companies, including San Francisco-based Pacific Telesis, parent of Pacific Bell and Nevada Bell.

Nancy Lubamersky, Pacific Bell's director of access services, said the company is "very happy with the (FCC) order" because the reduced connection costs will make its services more attractive.
