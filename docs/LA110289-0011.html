November 2, 1989, Thursday, Home Edition

LIMITING LENGTHY CRIMINAL TRIALS

The column "When Justice Runs Out of Gas" (Op-Ed Page, Oct. 23) contains both dangerous thinking and naivete which is surprising from an attorney such as Royal Oakes.

Megatrials are anomalies which happen only rarely. However, in a system of justice as large as the one in Los Angeles, the overlapping of them will occur from time to time. Thus, the Richard Ramirez (Night Stalker) and McMartin Pre-school trials took place concurrently. These types of unusual occurrences should be viewed as just that and not as a reason to change a system of justice which has, generally speaking, worked wonderfully well for 200 years in this country and for much longer than that in Great Britain.

Oakes seems to attempt to put the blame for lengthy criminal cases on the defense, implying that lengthy voir dire is conducted only by defendants and directly stating that the defense uses unscrupulous tactics to lengthen trials. He also states that trial courts are so worried about having "perfect" trials that every whim of a defense lawyer to delay the trial is indulged. Courts of appeal have not and are not reversing convictions for minor errors committed by trial courts.

The real reason for megatrials is that prosecutors often file every possible crime rather than attempting to limit the trial and do a second trial if necessary. With the sentencing scheme in California, it is unnecessary to charge 50-100 counts of child molestation as a conviction on 15 counts would suffice for a prison sentence from which the defendant would never be released.

There is no need to reform a system which has worked wonderfully for 200 years merely because two or three unusual cases last a long time.

ANDREW E. RUBIN

Santa Monica
