November 23, 1989, Thursday, Home Edition

ERMA BOMBECK: UNDERCUTTING THANKSGIVING CARVING CHORE

Ever since Phyllis Diller balanced her turkey on a window ledge and stuffed it through the beak, this traditional bird has been an enigma to cooks everywhere.

We are intimidated by its size. (You could lose your luggage in there.) We are awed by its history and threatened by its independence. ("We'll be done when we say we're done!")

I cannot look at a pale, naked turkey with its thunder thighs, sunken chest and accordion neck without thinking, "I have seen my future and you are it!" It's unsettling to me. It is obviously the resemblance that has prompted my mother every year to call, "Erma, time for you to carve the turkey."

I have no idea how I got stuck with this dubious honor. After 40 years of marriage, I still haven't a clue as to how to cut up a whole chicken. We call them mystery parts. Anyone who can identify where they belong wins a big prize.

But back to the turkey. I approach the bronze bird like a sumo wrestler circling his opponent to find out where his weaknesses and strengths are. Then I begin. We're not talking Norman Rockwell here. It's more like a chain-saw massacre. If the legs don't fall off in my hands, my mother is lamenting, "It's not done yet, is it?" If they do fall off in my hands, my father says, "It's overcooked. It'll be dry."

Meanwhile, my husband hovers over me and predicts, "You are going to cut yourself." I have always wanted to answer him, "You have just ruined my surprise!"

I've tried studying all those little charts that are put out just before the holidays, the ones that tell you where to make the incisions that leave you with a wall of white meat that slides off in thin slices. It never works for me.

Usually I resort to a list of things to blame for the miserable mound of meat on the platter: The knife was dull; the light was burned out over the stove; the turkey was too hot when I carved it/too cold when I carved it; the Republicans did something; nuclear testing is at work; glasnost . . . take your pick.

Today will be no different than Thanksgivings past. I will once again arm-wrestle a 20-pound bird to the platter and under-whelm everyone. But as in the past, I will allow myself a few minutes to reflect on how simple life would have been if the Pilgrims had just tossed up a salad and thrown a few steaks on the grill.
