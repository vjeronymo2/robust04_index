February 27, 1990, Tuesday, P.M. Final

LIZ SMITH: ROONEY CONTROVERSY HAS CBS ON THE DEFENSIVE

CBS HAS been backing up to the hairbrush over the Andy Rooney controversy which came to be a much stickier nettle than anybody at that network wanted to grasp. It was like the "Jumping Cholla" cactus of Arizona; CBS was closer to the sting than they thought. This recent incident, wherein Andy insulted, first, gays and then, according to the gay magazine The Advocate, went on to insult blacks, had Rooney apologizing for the former, but denying doing the latter. CBS headman David Burke suspended Rooney for three months anyway. And now, feeling and thinking souls at CBS -- crawling under an avalanche of mail and protests that run 90% in Andy's favor -- want Burke to bring a quick end to it.

HERE ARE some facts and thoughts you might have missed. At first, Burke fired Rooney straight out, totally. Andy was "out the door," as they say. The issue wasn't so much whether Rooney had made biased statements but that he had behaved in an insubordinate manner. Burke has had to take the heat for suspending Andy and not taking Rooney's word for it that he hadn't done what The Advocate said he did. But, in reality, Burke was in no position to state publicly what his action was for without more embarrassment.

Those denizens of "60 Minutes," Don Hewitt, Mike Wallace and Ed Bradley, all went to bat for Andy, who they do not believe is a racist. They talked Burke into making the punishment something less than a firing. At first Burke changed it to a six-month suspension, then downgraded that to three.

THE PUBLIC majority wants Andy back even if he made mistakes or didn't use good judgment or expressed himself sloppily. And some want Andy back no matter WHAT he said or did. You can whomp up a good and maybe even important argument these days about Andy Rooney and "free speech." And whether a man hired to give opinions on the air is allowed to have opinions.

The interesting comment on all this is by writer Peter Davis of Newsday. He says that blandness flourishes on the Big Three networks and that the "dirty secret" of CBS is that an all-white, male-dominated hierarchy is extra scrupulous about not offending gays, blacks, women or other minorities. They don't intend to hire importantly from such groups if they can avoid it and so hope bending over backward takes off some of the heat.

As Davis puts their philosophy, it is "See how fair we white males are -- we even kick people out who say anything about the rest of you guys."

ANDY ROONEY has been asking, in effect, "How do we get out of this mess?" He takes the blame heaped on him -- and the praise -- to heart. He has been mea culpa-ing all over the place in private. He knows he didn't handle any of this well. Burke's problem, however, is not to be perceived as a weak manager, a wimp, an executive who doesn't mean what he says.

It may be said there are no winners in this mess. But actually there can be. If gay radicals could believe more in "educating" the public in their favor rather than in dire punishments for all who offend them, they can see Andy back on the air as a chastened man who saw the light. Public consciousness HAS been raised. On the issue of racism, I think we should accept the word of a 40-plus veteran journalist that he didn't say those things. And he'll be watching his step in the future on this issue too.
