December 2, 1989, Saturday, Home Edition

STATE'S DRINKING WATER SUPPLY FROM DELTA FACES NEW THREAT

In Southern California we cannot survive on water supplies available locally; natural supplies meet less than one-third of our current needs. We must import the rest from Northern California and from the Colorado River. Now, according to your editorial, another element, a suspected cancer-causing agent, trihalomethane, has been added to the controversy surrounding Southern California's need for water from the Sacramento-San Joaquin Delta.

Water demands in Southern California are expected to increase roughly 20% by the year 2000. Our region still faces a highly undependable water supply in the future even though we lead the state in efficient use of water, including water conservation, water reclamation and ground-water management. Without a reliable, high-quality water supply, Southern California cannot avoid the adverse impact on our economy, our life styles, our very livelihood.

The latest report of potential contamination of one of Southern California's important sources of water adds urgency to the cry that elected officials and policy-makers address the management and optimum utilization of this most precious resource. In providing us with the keys to the solutions, these decision-makers can place emphasis on understanding and appreciating the water needs of all regions and all sectors of the state, and build a bridge of cooperation among them all.

A solution is out there. Everyone needs to be involved.

JOAN WILSON ANDERSON

Executive Director

Southern California Water Committee Inc.

Irvine
