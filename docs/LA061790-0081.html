June 17, 1990, Sunday, Ventura County Edition

OXNARD FACES HARD CHOICES IN TRIMMING $2.8 MILLION

For the last three years, Oxnard has been able to use reserve funds to fend off a government administrator's most dreaded foe: a deficit.

But the fiend has returned with a price tag of $2.8 million, and officials for the city -- operating on an annual budget of $61.7 million -- have vowed not to touch its reserves, which have dwindled to a dangerously low level.

As the city begins to discuss ways to chop the deficit, many residents fear that, without the use of reserve funds, the hungry deficit will eat away at city services such as police and fire protection.

Earlier this month, voters overwhelmingly rejected a 5% utility-tax increase that city officials had hoped would solve the city's financial crisis by generating $5 million annually. The loss at the polls came despite a $40,000 publicity campaign for the measure, funded primarily by developers and city employee groups.

City officials have said service and personnel cuts are now inevitable. The City Council began meeting last week to discuss which cuts to make and how deep to make them. But council members decided to delay final decisions until Tuesday, when the results of a long-awaited management audit are to be released.

In an effort to balance the budget for the fiscal year beginning July 1, City Manager David Mora has compiled a list of 64 personnel cuts -- including 16 in the Police Department and eight in the Fire Department.

An early target for cuts, the Colonia Youth Boxing Gym was taken off Mora's cost-cutting list last month after several Latino parents urged the council to spare the gym, which costs the city about $44,000 a year and serves about 50 boys a day.

While some city officials have characterized the city's fiscal situation as a full-blown crisis, others are more optimistic. "This is not the end of the world, and we are acting like it is," Councilwoman Dorothy Maron said at the beginning of last week's budget hearings.

This year's deficit is primarily the result of several unexpected expenditures, including a $690,000 increase in the cost for workers compensation and $460,000 to remove asbestos from city buildings, Mora said.

"If we didn't have these new elements, we'd be balanced," Mora said.

Management and Budget Director Bill Mayer said Oxnard's long-term budget problems have developed because of a series of revenue losses beginning in 1986.

For example, that year the federal government ended a revenue-sharing program that generated $2.2 million a year for Oxnard. The same year, the city lost an additional $1.5 million because of reduced franchise taxes paid by Southern California Gas Co.

In 1987, the city lost $2.1 million when the City Council eliminated a 2% utility tax two years before it was scheduled to expire.

The following year, Oxnard began losing $600,000 annually when fines from convicted drunk drivers began to be diverted from the city to the county.

However, Mayer said the city is beginning to see increased sales-tax revenue generated from several new businesses, such as the Price Club, Home Depot and the Oxnard Auto Mall, all of which opened recently near the Pacific Coast and Ventura freeways.

He said the city is expecting increased sales and property taxes from several new developments -- including the 450,000-square-foot Plaza del Norte shopping center, to be completed in September, and the 4.4-million-square-foot Town Center business park and shopping mall, to be completed within 15 years.

The developments "have the potential to ease the problems that we have," Mayer said.

Still, revenue from such new developments are not expected to solve this year's budget crisis, and city officials say Oxnard residents are just going to have to tighten their belts.

The Police Department would suffer the biggest financial loss -- $795,000 a year -- under Mora's plan. Mora has suggested that the city eliminate 16 positions, including six motorcycle traffic officers, a youth-services officer and a victim-assistance specialist. Anticipating such cuts, the department has left vacant four positions on the city's drunk-driving task force. Mora has suggested that the unit be eliminated.

The 154-officer department already has eight vacancies. If the recommended cuts are made, those jobs would remain unfilled and eight more would be eliminated through attrition, city officials have said.

Police Chief Robert Owens told the council last week that the recommended cuts would hurt the department's ability to protect residents. "I don't want to give up anything," he said.

Owens told the council that the victim-assistance specialist works as a trained counselor for victims of rape and child abuse. The youth-services officer acts as a liaison to the city's elementary and junior high schools, he said. He also warned that, when the city eliminated the motorcycle traffic team in 1978, the number of traffic deaths in Oxnard jumped.

Since August, when the drunk-driving enforcement team was eliminated, the Police Department has arrested 495 fewer motorists than during the same period a year earlier, when 1,690 were taken in, police said.

Last month, dozens of police officers and firefighters tried to eliminate the need for personnel cuts by campaigning door-to-door in support of the 5% utility-tax increase.

In an effort to save money, Councilwoman Ann Johs suggested last week that the county be asked to take over the police and fire protection in Oxnard. The council is scheduled to discuss the proposal on Tuesday.

The Parks and Recreation Department would suffer the largest personnel losses under Mora's plan.

The city manager has recommended the elimination of 17 positions there, including two landscape workers, a park ranger and nine custodians. Mora suggested that the city contract with a private firm to take over the city's custodial services.

Mora has also suggested that the city close several buildings operated by the department on Sundays and eliminate the Junior High Grad Night Program. The city now spends $19,400 to supervise and transport almost 1,000 graduating junior high school students each year to a local amusement park.

The city can save $478,000 by implementing these cuts.

The loudest outcry from residents was prompted by Mora's recommendation to close the Carnegie Art Museum, which would save the city $140,000 a year.

Mona Broyles, chairwoman of the Oxnard Cultural and Fine Arts Commission, called the proposed closure "totally unacceptable." She has urged the council to "please seek another alternative cutback." Once the museum is closed, Broyles said, it will be almost impossible to persuade other museums to loan art exhibits to the Carnegie in the future.

Museum Director Andrew Voth said closure could permanently ruin the city's reputation in the art world. Just the rumor that the Carnegie will close has lost the museum $20,000 in grants, he said. "It's devastating."

The 10,000-square-foot museum, which houses a permanent collection of 250 paintings and sculptures, opened in 1981 and has a staff of one part-time and three full-time employees.

Mora has proposed that the city save $343,500 by eliminating two emergency rescue squads and eight firefighters.

The emergency vehicles, each staffed by four full-time firefighters, carry emergency tools -- such as the "Jaws of Life" -- to the scene of a fire or accident.

The Fire Department has 43 firefighters and seven vacancies.

Fire Chief Richard Smith told the council last week that there are 22 firefighters on duty at all times. He warned that elimination of the two squads would reduce fire service by 9%.

Smith said the department normally dispatches 12 firefighters to battle a routine single-family house fire, leaving 10 firefighters to monitor the rest of the city.

"It's a little bit frightening, frankly," he said of the proposed cuts.

However, he noted that the city has a mutual-assistance agreement with fire departments from nearby cities.

Mora has suggested that the city close its main library on Sundays to save $11,000 in overtime salaries, which would entail no personnel cuts.

The city manager has also recommended that the city eliminate its $398,000 contribution to the Oxnard Convention and Visitors Bureau, which employs six full-time staff members.

Robert D. Varley, executive chairman of the bureau, said elimination of the city contribution would force closure of the bureau. He said the elimination would have a devastating effect on tax revenue generated by hotels and by other tourism-related businesses.

Mora also suggested that the city save $200,000 by eliminating three employees from the city's street-sweeping unit. City officials said such a reduction would mean that the average residential street would be swept every three months, while streets in business districts would be swept twice a month.
