December 16, 1990, Sunday, Home Edition

Q & A

Q: Enclosed is a copy of a $1,000 note from the Bank of the United States in Philadelphia that was given to my father in 1925 as partial payment of a debt. It is dated 1840. Upon his death in 1987, it was discovered in a photo album. I am wondering if it might have any value. -- Upland

A: It is worthless as a note but might be worth something to a collector. But collectors would be interested only if it is authentic and not one of thousands of fakes sent out in the 1950s as promotional material. The fakes are printed on parchment paper; the real thing was printed on heavy white paper.

Because you say your father received the note in 1925, let's assume that it isn't one of the 1950s replicas.

Although the name Bank of the United States may have sounded very official and governmental, it was really just another bank. According to a pamphlet put out by the Pennsylvania Bankers Assn., there were two different banks that called themselves the Bank of the United States. One closed in 1811. The other -- after switching to a state charter -- collapsed in 1841.

Nicholas Biddle, the second bank's president from 1823 to 1839, was brought to trial for mismanagement but acquitted. It was charged that many of the bank's assets were of questionable value. (Sounds like today, doesn't it?) Biddle died "heartbroken" in 1844, according to the association.

The collapse of this bank eventually led to the setting up of the Federal Reserve System.

Your note obviously became worthless as a note when the bank collapsed in the 1800s, because these things weren't insured. The note might be worth $250 to $300 to a collector -- but only if Biddle signed it.

There are two problems with this. Biddle tended to sign in an acidic ink, so few of his signatures survive. And because Biddle quit the bank in 1839, and your note is dated 1840, he would have had to sneak back into his old office to sign it.

Q: I have 100 shares of Rose Marie Reid stock from my late mother's estate. Rose Marie Reid has apparently been acquired by some company, because I see the name on tags of bathing suits each spring in the department stores. Could you find out who bought Rose Marie Reid and what this stock is worth? -- Thousand Oaks

A: Rose Marie Reid went out of business in August, 1965. The next month all of its properties were repossessed and sold for non-payment of taxes. The stock is worthless, according to R. M. Smythe & Co., which tracks down long-lost companies.

Now, let's look into that bathing suit. Jonathan Logan, another apparel firm, had a licensing agreement with Rose Marie Reid in the 1960s. It got the rights to the Rose Marie Reid name, although nobody at Jonathan Logan is quite sure how this all came about. Jonathan Logan was later acquired by United Merchants & Manufacturers Inc.

Q: In cleaning out my late father's papers, I came across a 1923 German Treasurer Exchequer Bond with coupons maturing between March 1, 1924, and Sept. 1, 1927. I suspect that the events that occurred in Germany during this period negated collectibility of the bond. However, I am wondering if the United States after World War II might have entered into any agreement with Germany for honoring claims of bondholders during this period. -- Bethlehem, Pa.

A: Haley Garrison, head of Antique Stocks and Bonds in Williamsburg, Va., says the bonds you hold are worthless.

Garrison says 1923 was the year that Germany, devastated by skyrocketing inflation, printed these bonds aplenty. "Immigrants brought truckloads of this stuff in hopes that it might someday be redeemed," he adds.

But the bonds weren't redeemed then, and they aren't worth anything today. You can contact Garrison at (800) 451-4504 if you'd like more information. JOHN CRUDELE
