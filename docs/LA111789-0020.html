November 17, 1989, Friday, Home Edition

PUTTING OUR BACK YARD ON FAST-FORWARD TO PEACE;

CENTRAL AMERICA: TELL GORBACHEV TO DIG INTO HIS BAG OF TRICKS AND FORCE HIS ALLIES IN OUR HEMISPHERE TO YIELD TO THE NEW REALITIES OF THE '90S.

We are trapped in a miracle of the satellite TV age. The world is changing so rapidly that history seems to be on fast-forward one minute and rewind the next.

Fast-forward: Live and in color, we're watching communism evolve itself out of existence in Eastern Europe. The Soviet satellites are changing faster than we can change channels.

We're seeing hundreds of thousands of people who lived under communism for two generations fleeing to places where they can work and speak and save and spend in freedom. And we're seeing their iron-fisted rulers benignly step aside, admitting the failure of communism's catechism and cause.

Rewind: A newscaster insists it's San Salvador today, but the picture on our screen looks more like Tet in the streets of Saigon -- or is it Havana or Managua? No matter, it's Communist-backed rebels killing hundreds of innocents, one more time, as they try to force on yet another country the system we just saw their Marxist bankrollers admitting has failed and fallen two continents and one ocean away.

There's no logic to the order of things. It's as if the past can't wait for the future to catch up.

We're watching and our President is watching, and none of us seem to have much influence over those pictures on the screen. So rapidly is history shifting between fast-forward and rewind that we've begun to understand at least this: Remote control doesn't work.

Changes generated by Soviet President Mikhail S. Gorbachev have swept not just his nation but our world. In China, the people rose up and were gunned down. In Eastern Europe, the people rose up and gained new freedoms.

In the Americas, Gorbachev's disciples look to the future and see only the past. Nicaragua's nervous Marxist Daniel Ortega scrapped a truce and went for his guns, flinching out of fear that his countrymen would soon draw their ballots. El Salvador's leftist rebels, in their own desperation, figure it doesn't matter if they win people's hearts and minds, or merely pierce them with bullets.

The past that today's Central American communists see as their future is Fidel Castro, who came to power under Nikita Khrushchev, became a global crusader under Leonid Brezhnev and outlived Yuri Andropov and Konstantin Chernenko. Castro has given them their weapons -- he's even given Ortega a wardrobe of hand-me-downs.

But suddenly, Cuba's master of tirade seems to have acute laryngitis; his silence coincides with those satellite pictures from Europe and Moscow that put the lie to his decades of words.

There is something that George Bush can do. He can borrow a page from the diplomatic playbook of Henry Kissinger and take it to the summit-at-sea.

It's time for "linkage." It's time for Bush to stop looking so painfully repressed as he reacts to the good news from the East. It's time for him to let Gorbachev know in firm, unmistakable words that there is much the United States can do to help the Soviets repair their shattered economy -- and that we would like to continue the Bush policy of not seeking to embarrass the Soviets in their own back yard.

But the Soviets need to undo the mess they've made of our back yard as well. Say it and mean it: We can't cooperate to the fullest as long as military aid is being funneled from the Soviet Union and Cuba to Nicaragua and the rebels in El Salvador.

In Jackson Hole, Wyo., Secretary of State James A. Baker III discussed this at some length with Soviet Foreign Minister Eduard A. Shevardnadze. The Soviets clucked sympathetically, proclaimed their willingness to comply, but maintained that they were limited in what they could do to stop Castro from shipping military aid to Ortega et al.

That's not good enough. It's time to tell Gorbachev that he's got to dig down into his bag of geopolitricks and come up with the right stuff -- carrots or sticks -- that will force Fidel to yield to the new realities of the '90s.

The Soviets need Western technical assistance for their economy, and their need for defense cuts is even more urgent than ours. Eastern Europe needs a new Marshall Plan.

Our price must be a hemisphere at peace, here in the West. And while the rest of us can only sit and watch satellite pictures on our TV screens, it's time for George Bush to speak up.
