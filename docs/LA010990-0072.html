January 9, 1990, Tuesday, Home Edition

ERMA BOMBECK: A TASTE OF 'GOOD OLD DAYS'

Just imagine for a moment that each of you has a time capsule attached to your body. Contained within it is a memory of something that reflects the world in which you lived, something you want to remember. What do you think it would be?

According to a limited, non-scientific sampling of readers throughout the years, the answer is "food." Man's fondest memories have revolved around it. How it tasted. How it smelled. It has been the one thing that has changed the most dramatically.

The slightest mention of the "good old days" (30 years prior to wherever you are now) triggers stories of "juicy white corn on the cob with butter dripping off your elbow" . . . "sweet strawberries as big as your fist" . . . and those wonderful tender fryers that tasted like real chicken.

Roy Anderson of Tularosa, N.M., asks, "Why is it all a memory?" Specifically, he remembers watermelons (some weighing more than 50 pounds) being chilled in the ice house, awaiting the next day when the knife would reveal deep ravines of red, dotted with black seeds for spitting. "Watermelons are not bred for flavor anymore. They are bred for shipping. The test is to throw a ripe melon to the ground from the roof of the barn. If it bounces without breaking the skin, save the seeds!

"Opening a watermelon today is about like getting at a coconut that has just fallen from the tree," he complains. "One needs a heavy-duty knife. (A machete is adequate.) Lay the edge of the blade to the melon and rap soundly on the backside of the knife with a four-pound hammer. If a crack appears in the melon, insert a log-splitting wedge and whack again. The last two melons we bought at the grocery had the consistency of winter squash, the color of chicken lungs and tasted like cucumbers. We took them next door and gave them to the neighbor's chickens."

I have to agree. I don't remember my mother's chicken dressed in a caftan of fat that you could peel off in one piece. Or fresh peaches that taste a lot like the back of a postage stamp.

Food today is like a highway under construction: You travel at your own risk. Add some oats, take out the salt, lower the fat, compress the nutrients, spray for disease, inject for longevity, corral the calories, remove the seeds, alter the color, and slap a label on it to let people know what it is supposed to be. Dispensing food is a matter of keeping it alive long enough to execute it. It's downright inhumane.

There's a lot that was bad about the "good old days." No one denies that. But I'd trade the beeper, the electric salad shooter, the stress tapes, the electric gum massager and my bifocal mirror for one large beefsteak tomato that oozes flavor and runs down your chin when you bite into it.
