May 20, 1989, Saturday, Home Edition

TEACHERS LEAVE CLASSROOMS FOR PICKET LINES

As an observer of the Los Angeles public schools for the past 20 years in the capacity as a trainer of teachers at a leading university in Southern California, I can say without hesitation that the L.A. Board of Education deserves the teachers' strike. Those who don't support it deserve what they have had, and will continue to get, for the last two decades: teaching conditions in an urban system that are scandalous for a city this size; an insensitive and overly top-heavy administration that misdirects a budget that is larger than the budget for the entire city of Los Angeles; teacher salaries and duties which make a mockery of the training and skills needed to be a teacher; and a school system that can't find or pay good teachers and has to rely increasingly upon emergency credentialed people.

Conditions can't be worse. Why would anyone want to teach in the Los Angeles school system?

I find it amazing that the schools can't find the money for teacher salaries, but, somehow, magically found new revenue to pay above-average salaries for substitutes during the strike. In all honesty, this strike couldn't have happened to a better school district. I'm only sorry that it took over a decade for the teachers to get their act together this time.

ALFRED LIGHTFOOT

Professor of Education

Loyola Marymount University
