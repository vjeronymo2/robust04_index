December 15, 1989, Friday, Home Edition

U.S. INVESTIGATING POSSIBLE AIR FARE COLLUSION

Justice Department antitrust lawyers are investigating whether airline fare increases in September resulted from any collusion among the carriers, the department said Thursday.

Civil investigative demands -- the equivalent of criminal subpoenas -- seeking internal pricing information were sent to several airlines soon after the increase went into effect Sept. 29, a department spokeswoman said. Industry sources said most major airlines received the department's requests for data.

Although acknowledging that the investigation is under way, Amy Brown, the department spokeswoman, declined to state whether it signaled a renewed interest in assuring price competition in the airline industry or whether the nature of this particular increase seemed different from other industrywide price changes in recent years.

The increases, which appeared first in a Sept. 18 announcement by American Airlines, ranged from $10 to $80 a trip and were pegged to the number of days before the flight that tickets were purchased -- with the biggest price hikes imposed on tickets purchased closest to departure. The increases appear to have been adopted throughout the industry.

"We cooperated and returned the requested documents a couple of weeks ago," Pam Hanlon, a spokeswoman for Pan American World Airways, said. "We would be reluctant to disclose" the specific items sought by the department, she added.

However, a source familiar with the civil investigative demands said they included any correspondence or other contact regarding pricing among the airlines.

"We were contacted several weeks ago by the Justice Department and asked for information about the fare action late in the fall," said Mary O'Neill, an American Airlines spokeswoman. "We have cooperated fully."

Edwin I. Colodny, chairman and president of USAir, said Thursday: "It's my understanding that the Justice Department asked all the airlines for information. I'm not worried about it. There is no collusion." He spoke in a brief interview at a Los Angeles Chamber of Commerce luncheon.

Existence of the investigation was first disclosed Thursday by the Wall Street Journal.

The investigation is the Justice Department's first civil antitrust inquiry into airline pricing to surface publicly since 1985 when American Airlines, its chief executive, Robert Crandall, and the Justice Department entered into a consent decree barring conversations on fares between American and competitors.

Though he declined to discuss details of the current probe, Brown said it is not related to the investigation of predatory pricing that James F. Rill, assistant attorney general for antitrust, discussed in Senate testimony Sept. 20.

That investigation, which is looking into possible cutting of fares below cost to drive competitors out of the market, is continuing, Brown said.

Concern about rising air fares and decreased competition has been expressed in Congress recently. After airline deregulation in 1978, air fares dropped sharply under pressure from new, price-slashing competitors. But some of these competitors have dropped out of business, giving rise to fears about lack of effective competition.

Times staff writer Denise Gellene in Los Angeles contributed to this story.
