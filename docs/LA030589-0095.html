March 5, 1989, Sunday, Home Edition

CURRENT PAPERBACKS: A FRIEND FROM ENGLAND BY ANITA BROOKNER (HARPER & ROW: $6.95)

Thirty-two-year-old Rachel Kennedy, owner of a London bookshop, is self-sufficient, solitary. "I've learned how to be alone and to put a good face on it," she says.

She becomes a frequent dinner guest at the home of her father's former accountant and his wife, Oscar and Dorrie Livingstone. Somehow Rachel concludes "that Oscar and Dorrie thought me a suitable companion for Heather," their grown daughter, and Rachel condescends to become her mentor.

When Heather impulsively flies to Venice to marry the man she loves, Rachel follows her -- to reprimand her. Heather responds by saying that Rachel's own total control -- good manners masking disengagement -- amounts to a "mismanagement" of life.

In the end, Rachel admits her limitations: "To strike out and claim one's own life . . . even to embrace caprice, was, though monstrous, sometimes admirable."

"What we are reading is . . . an allegorical fable between a false life of repression and a true life of risks and engagements," Hermione Lee wrote in these pages, calling "A Friend From England" a "harsh, dark fable."
