Language: Dutch Article Type:BFN [Interview with Bosnian Muslim Premier Haris Silajdzic by Bob Kroon in Geneva; date not given: "Silajdzic Counts on American-Russian Cooperation"; first two paragraphs are GAZET VAN ANTWERPEN introduction] [Text] Although the talks in Geneva have stalled, the diplomatic activity is more hectic than ever just one week before possible actions by NATO. The United States is counting on "Russia playing a constructive role," and expects Moscow to exert pressure on Serbian President Milosevic. Meanwhile, together with Germany and Turkey, the United States will place Croatian President Tudjman and Bosnian Leader Izetbegovic under pressure. Bosnian Muslim Premier Haris Silajdzic says that he is very pleased with Clinton's radical change of tack. "The Serbs could always count on the support of the Russians, whereas Washington kept out of harm's way," he said, before returning to Sarajevo. "Now the balance has finally been restored. Bosnia is not an isolated problem. It concerns Europe and the whole world. The ultimatum should have been announced last year, then a great deal of suffering would have been spared." [Kroon] Bosnian Serb leader Karadzic has said that he will only withdraw his heavy weapons if the numerically stronger Muslim troops return to their barracks. Was that the agreement? [Silajdzic] Absolutely not. There are more than eight million Serbs, several times the number of Bosnian Muslims. However, we are much more motivated, because we are fighting for our homeland. Karadzic is bluffing. I do not think that he wants NATO on his back. [Kroon] Karadzic says that air strikes would mean the end of NATO, and he cannot guarantee the protection of international aid workers in the chaos that might result from them. [Silajdzic] That is laughable. And as for the aid convoys, the Serbs are protecting them so well that most of them never even reach their destination. [Kroon] You feel strengthened by U.S. support. Does that mean that you will no longer be satisfied with a tripartite division, according to which the Muslims are allocated 33 percent of Bosnia? [Silajdzic] We are still proceeding on the basis of a three-way split. However, the issue is not whether we get 33 or 33.3 percent. We want habitable areas in both eastern and western Bosnia, areas to which the refugees can return. [Kroon] Do you see any possibilities of forming a coalition with the Croats, now that Nationalist Leader Boban has been shunted onto the sidelines? [Silajdzic] That would be an extremely logical development. The new delegation leader Akmadzic seems willing to cooperate. A coalition between the Muslims and Croats would solve a number of problems, both for us and for them. However, Akmadzic is still demanding Mostar as the capital of Croatian territory in Bosnia, and we cannot accept this. The majority of the population of Mostar is Muslim, it is not a Croatian city. Furthermore, it was they who barbarously reduced Mostar -- one of the most beautiful cities in Europe -- to rubble. Mostar belongs to all sectors of the population. [Kroon] What is your view of the new developments? [Silajdzic] I hope that the Russians actually cooperate with the United States. If they do, then the problem can be resolved within several months. However, President Yeltsin is facing difficulties. Many Russian generals regard people like Karadzic and General Mladic as some kind of brothers-in-arms. Moreover, we should not underestimate the problem posed by someone like Zhirinovskiy, as happened way back in the case of derided artist Adolf Hitler. However, it is not Zhirinovskiy himself, but rather what he embodies that is the issue -- the frustrations and apparent hopelessness of the situation in Russia. That neofascist Milosevic in Belgrade remains one of the main guilty parties. If the Russians can bring him into line, then we will have come a whole step further.
