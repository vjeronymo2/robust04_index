May 20, 1989, Saturday, Home Edition

U.S. ATTEMPTS TO OUST GEN. NORIEGA

Congratulations for your editorial "Unnecessary Deployment" (May 12), which not only pinpoints the overreaction of the Bush Administration to the political crisis in Panama, but also reminds the readers of your newspaper that the intervention of U.S. forces into Latin American countries is a "sensitive issue" to their people.

There is more to this recent Panamanian crisis, however, than what The Times has reported so far. For one thing, it is necessary to keep Gen. Manuel A. Noriega's former ties to U.S. intelligence services in the consciousness of the American people. Apparently, Noriega agreed to the training of Contra forces in Panama in exchange for the freedom to engage in narcotics traffic to the U.S., which is a part of the still unsettled Iran-Contra affair.

For another thing, the "opposition" to Noriega appears to be composed of the same "fat cats" who the U.S. government generally supports in Third World countries. When you juxtapose a picture of the obviously well-fed Guillermo Endara, bloodied face notwithstanding (Part I, May 11), and mention his being from an affluent neighborhood of Panama City, to the trim Noriega with his pockmarked face (testimony to his humble origins), the impression that surfaces is that nothing much would change if the one replaces the other.

If the U.S. government doesn't use tough soldiers to see after its interests in the Third World, it's the rich and well-born, with their very narrow concerns, who are employed, and therein lies a good deal of the problem with U.S. policy toward these poor countries.

ANTHONY GARAVENTE

Long Beach
