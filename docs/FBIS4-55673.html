BFN [Report by Novak Vulic: "From Loser to Accomplice"] [Excerpts] The merchant navy is in agony! We reported this almost eight months ago, yet the spasm and fear of losing the fleet is increasingly great. The immense effort of the Jugooceanija and Prekookeanska Plovidba companies and the state to improve the position of the shipping trade with loans essentially boils down to buying time. Chances of the fleet surviving until the end of the year are realistic only if the government decision on a $30 million loan is implemented. In addition to the loan, saving the installations that are of strategic interest for Montenegro (and therefore, for the federal state) also call for the immediate end of the embargo. Since chances for a change of policy are slim, there remains only a glimmer of hope that perhaps one day at least a part of the vessels of the current fleet will sail under our flag. On the eve of the second anniversary of UN Security Council Resolution 820, the Montengrin fleet is poorer by four vessels, while the threat of a mortgage and auction is looming over the bow of the remaining 37. Ships age faster than people, they diminish in value, sailing ability, and the ability for equal market competition. Debts, on the other hand, like all misfortunes, progressively multiply and oppress. It was warned long before the first results of the international isolation were apparent that a moored boat was the most expensive for its owner. It was also warned that a loss of sailors would ensure. And that the moorings in the Bar harbor and the docks of the Bijela Shipyard were already too quiet, even without boats. In the course of last year, the amount of goods that passed through the "southern gates of the Balkans" corresponded to the amount that in normal circumstances is unloaded in a matter of 15 days. For the sake of not completely forgetting work after the overhaul of the Sumadija [name of vessel], in Bijela nowadays they keep themselves busy with occasional interventions on smaller vessels. The majority of employees are on compulsory leave. Since there is no way of charging fees for services rendered, the shipyard regularly applies for assistance with payment of the minimal salaries. All development programs have been postponed and are already covered with a thick layer of dust. [passage omitted]

The Effect of the Sanctions
Up until the sanctions, the maritime economy contributed to a fifth of the foreign currency revenue in the republic. The information on losses accrued under sanctions still treats results achieved after surveying the situation at the end of October as current. The amount involved is $217 million, but only for maritime companies. However, we need at least to add to this the $60,000 for daily maintenance of vessels and loans from domestic sources, which also need to be repaid. In line with a decision of the Montenegrin and Federal Republic of Yugoslavia's governments, by the end of October and on various bases, the maritime economy was granted almost $15 million. It is interesting, for example, that former Montenegrin companies Jugooceanija and Prekookeanska Plovidbva (in the meantime they changed their flags, reregistered, and changed their mooring port), in addition to calculating losses on freight which amounted to almost $100 million, also registered and calculated losses incurred by devaluation of property. The result can only surprise the uninformed. By the end of October 1993, it surpassed $35 million. This will rapidly increase, because after two years of being moored, vessels are more ruined than if they had been in use for five years.
