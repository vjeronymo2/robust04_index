October 7, 1990, Sunday, Home Edition

WHAT IF THE WORLD CALLED 'LEADER' AND NOBODY STOOD UP?

A geopolitical order emerges very different from the one we have known since World War II, not only in its actors but in its character.

Of the three dominant industrial powers today, two, Germany and Japan, make no effort to translate this power into any other form of influence than the financial and commercial.

The third power, the United States, is the only one of the three to think of itself in classic geopolitical terms and to assume a responsibility for what international society should become. It possesses great military power, which it cannot now really afford to employ, given the national choices it has made with respect to budget and taxes. It is in crucial respects dependent on its Japanese and European creditors, which have proved reluctant to finance the American deployment in the Persian Gulf.

A period thus has begun where one country professes a global vision and global responsibilities, yet depends financially upon others who do not.

The debate between Japan, Germany and the United States reflects contradictory assumptions. The United States says to them: You need an orderly world in order to prosper. You must either police the world yourselves or finance our policing it.

Their reply, as yet unspoken, but clear enough, says (to take the current example) that it makes little difference to them who owns the oil wells of the Arabian Peninsula, since whoever does will still need to sell as much oil as possible at as high a price as possible.

Americans ask: But what about the principle of nonaggression? What about making the world a just and orderly place? The answer implicit in Germany's and Japan's actions is that, while they will cooperate in whatever the world community may collectively choose to do, disorder does not greatly concern them so long as it is confined to the Middle East or Africa or Southeast Asia. Or, to put it less harshly, that they do not consider themselves capable of imposing international order nor entitled to do so. Let Arabs, Africans, Israelis and Asians be responsible for their own affairs.

There are many Americans for whom this logic is appealing. They ask if it is logical for the United States to conduct a global policy meant to keep order in the common interest when to do so engenders hostility toward the United States and produces little credit.

The conclusion logically drawn from what these Americans argue, and from the nature of Germany's and Japan's employment of power and their perception of international obligation, is that we may soon find ourselves with no nation really prepared to assume global responsibilities, or at least to do so unilaterally.

With Russia gone from the arena, will this produce a better, or worse, world than we have known since the 1940s? The conventional wisdom says worse. I am inclined to say better. Of course, that is not saying much.
