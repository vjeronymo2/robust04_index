May 31, 1990, Thursday, Orange County Edition

NEIGHBORHOOD PROFILE: MID CITY: FOCUS;

MID CITY: THRIVING AMID CHANGE

Buses run up and down the streets of Santa Ana's Mid City neighborhood with the frequency of new lovers -- another one will be along any minute. And it's a good thing, too. The buses give residents mobility and a feeling of self-sufficiency.

The predominantly Latino section has few of the conveniences associated with living in the suburbs. There aren't strip shopping centers, banks, dry cleaners or even gas stations on every block; there are no big chain supermarkets or major retail stores within easy walking distance. But the neighborhood still manages to take care of itself.

It is a place where small businesses thrive and Latino store owners and the community rely on one another. Most of the shop signs are in Spanish, making those businesses a comfort zone in a neighborhood with a healthy mix of first-generation American-born Latinos, longtime immigrants and Mexican nationals.

The Clinica Medica Latina is here, as well as other businesses dealing with everything from aseguranza (insurance) to immigration. The Top Value Super Mercado is the all-purpose general store. Signs in the windows announce "boletos de Lotteria aqui" (lottery tickets here), the availability of giros (money orders) for 15 cents and that the store accepts estampillas de comida (food stamps).

Inside, name-brand groceries line the shelves, but the store also makes room for specialty items and Mexican delicacies. Near the entrance, a woman rolls fresh hot churros in a sugar-and-cinnamon mixture, while 10 feet away a matronly woman sells tortillas hechos a manos (handmade tortillas) for $1.35 per dozen.

In the meat case, tripe, catfish, pig feet, tongue and beef lips kiss glass display windows. And in another section of the mercado, there are Spanish-language videos for rent, T-shirts for sale at three for $9 and a variety of other products imported from Mexico.

But Mid City is also a neighborhood in transition. As much as the area takes care of its own in commerce, in other aspects, things are beginning to change.

"I enjoy Santa Ana; there are a lot of nice people here. It's just been in the last few months. . . ," lamented Guadalupe Saenz, a ten-year resident of Mid City who also works as a clerk at the neighborhood's elementary school. "People used to keep up their yards and lawns -- we didn't have any problems, but now. . . ," her voice trails off.

But now there are signs of trouble with a capital "T" and that rhymes with "G" and that stands for graffiti, which is generally followed by the other "G" word: gangs.

"I don't understand this," Saenz said. "I've met gang members who are really nice kids. But it's like they have a split personality. At home, they're really good kids, but outside on the street when they're with their gangs. . . ."

Fortunately, the majority of youths in the neighborhood are not in gangs, but the small percentage who are cause major headaches for the rest. "Not every kid you see is in a gang," said Cpl. Rick Reese of the Santa Ana Police Department's gang detail.

"There are a lot of good kids out there who never receive any credit. Gang members are in the minority, but they're at the forefront because of all the violence," he explained. "It's getting out of hand. They may have gotten into gangs to protect the barrio, but they only end up ruining it. Where there are no gangs, there are no problems."

As disturbing as the appearance of graffiti is -- along with its implications -- Mid City has a way to go before it has the gang problems of other neighborhoods. The fact that it doesn't yet is a tribute to the tenacity and determination of parents who are working with the area school to give their children alternatives to the downward, unproductive lifestyle of gangs. A big part of that success is spelled P-T-A.

The PTA at Martin Elementary School has managed to turn outrage into outreach. "Our parents are an active part of our school," said Helen M. Matthews, the school's principal. "We have meetings on the third Wednesday of every month, and at every meeting we have between 200 and 250 parents. They are always willing to help."

To help prevent students from becoming targets for drugs and gangs, school programs such as the city-sponsored, after-school PRIDE (Parks and Recreation Inspire Dignity and Esteem) Club and the police-sponsored DARE (Drug Abuse Resistance Education) program are heartily endorsed by parents and heavily attended by children.

And it doesn't stop there. The parents aren't satisfied with shifting all the responsibility to the schools. They use tips garnered from PTA meetings on how to help their children with homework; and when the school needed to stock the new computer lab, the PTA mobilized, raised $3,000 and supplied the lab with one-third of the requisite equipment.

The parents get involved in other ways, too. Within the 250-strong PTA is a small core group of volunteers who regularly help at the school by painting over graffiti, which appears sporadically, working as teachers' aides in the classroom and duplicating flyers, bulletins or whatever else the school needs.

The backbone of that core group is Cristina Garcia, president of the School Site Council. "Mrs. Garcia is one of our main contributors. She is always there when we need her," Matthews said.

Other supporters include Raquel Martinez, a grandmother who volunteers a lot of her time at the school; Socorro Gonzales, the PTA president; Maria Pino, a parent-volunteer who helps with fund-raising activities; and Rosa Perez, who was praised by President Bush at Santa Ana Stadium in March for her involvement in fighting gangs and drugs.

As Guadalupe Saenz points out, there are a lot of good people in the neighborhood. And while some change may be inevitable in Mid City, it will be tempered by a history of cooperation and fine-tuned by a generation of children who are growing up experiencing the positive aspects of having a whole neighborhood watching out for them.
