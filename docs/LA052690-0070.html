May 26, 1990, Saturday, San Diego County Edition

GOING THE EXTRA STEPS;

SAN DIEGO, MEXICAN DANCERS TO COLLABORATE SOUTH OF THE BORDER

Despite San Diego's proximity to Tijuana, cross-cultural exchanges with Mexican artists have been few and far between. But this weekend, some of those invisible barriers will begin to crumble when Three's Company -- San Diego's leading modern dance troupe -- joins forces with 15 dancers from the other side of the border. The one-night stand is scheduled for 7 p.m. Sunday at the Tijuana Cultural Center.

"We've done five or six performances in Mexico," said Jean Isaacs, the troupe's bilingual artistic director. "But this is the first time we're actually working with Mexican dancers."

The impetus for the cross-cultural collaboration was a $4,000 grant from the city of San Diego.

"I never expected to get it," Isaacs said, "because I'm not an ethnic artist. But the grant is really doing what it's supposed to do -- encourage multicultural activity. We have quite a few men and women coming up to our studio to take class with me as a result of our work there, and Ricardo Peralta (one of the Mexican dancers involved in the project) has asked permission to use my work for his company. I never give my pieces away, but, since they don't have any money, I donated it."

Isaacs credits Miriam Soto, a Mexican part-time student of Three's Company's, for encouraging her to take the troupe into these uncharted waters.

"Miriam made it happen," recalled Isaacs, who also has an undergraduate degree in Spanish literature. "She said, 'You speak Spanish, you should teach modern dance technique in Mexico.' I applied for the grant, and now, Miriam is the paid coordinator of this project."

With support from the Mexican government in the form of free facilities, Isaacs began training the dancers a month ago for the performance.

"The Mexican dancers haven't had much modern dance, but they have good classical training," Isaacs noted. "And they are very grounded dancers. They understand the sense of weight this kind of dance requires. We sit and talk, and some of them tell me, 'Hey, this has really changed my life.' I really wish we could keep this going. There's so much to do.

"They're getting support and attention from the media in their own community as a result of this concert. We can't let it end with this performance."

When Isaacs began training dancers in Tijuana, she had low expectations. However, the teacher was pleasantly surprised by what she found out about her well-motivated students.

"I found the technical level quite high, and they all look good on stage because they have an innate dramatic sense that compensates for any (kinetic) weaknesses," Isaacs said. "Dance is a strong part of their culture, and they just

understand it."

As a result, instead of a choreographing a watered-down dance for the fledgling modern dancers, Isaacs taught them the most challenging piece in her repertory -- adjusting the designs to accommodate the contingency of men among them.

To help ensure the future of Mexican-American cultural relations -- at least in the field of modern dance -- Isaacs and her dancers have extended their involvement well beyond the terms of the grant.

"Everyone in our company is working for minimal salaries," she explained, "and we've given the Mexican students scholarships to study with us in San Diego. We had to, because they couldn't afford the lessons otherwise."

Isaacs is eager to take Three's Company back to Mexico again, but the dance maker is even more excited about the new talent she discovered south of the border.

"We have to find some way for Ricardo (Peralta) to show his work here," she vowed. "I'd like to do it, if we can fund it. It's too late for this year's low-tech (Three's Company's summer dance series in the studio), but maybe next year.

"There's a real need for this," she said, referring to the cross-cultural training. "We're only 15 minutes away. There shouldn't be a border. Geographically, and culturally, it's all California.
