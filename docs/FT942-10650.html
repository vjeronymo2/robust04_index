940510 FT 10 MAY 94 / MPs win changes to bill on red tape De-regulation measures will be subject to greater parliamentary scrutiny after the government said yesterday it would accept significant changes to its bill. The de-regulation bill would enable government departments to repeal hundreds of regulatory measures without primary legislation. Ministers have privately conceded that the bill, which has reached its report stage in the Commons, is likely to face fierce opposition in the Lords. Critics of the bill have argued that parliamentary orders - which cannot be amended and on which time for debate is limited - are not a suitable vehicle for potentially complex and far-reaching changes in the law. The cross-party Commons procedure committee had recommended tough new powers for the select committee set up to scrutinise all de-regulation proposals. In an attempt to placate critics, Mr Tim Sainsbury, trade and industry minister, said the government would accept the committee's recommendation for the new body, which will consider the final version of draft orders implementing de-regulatory changes before they are debated by the Commons. The bill requires ministers to wait 40 days between laying a draft order before parliament and submitting a final draft for debate. An amendment proposed by ministers would extend that period to 60 days if the de-regulation committee felt that 40 days was not sufficient for scrutiny of a proposal. But some backbenchers remain concerned that the government could still push through significant de-regulation measures without adequate scrutiny. Mr Gary Waller, Tory MP for Keighley, said: 'While I support the objectives behind the bill I am still not happy with the mechanism.'
