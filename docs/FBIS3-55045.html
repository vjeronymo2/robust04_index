Language: English Article Type:BFN [By Diplomatic Correspondents Dmitry Voskoboinikov, Igor Porshnev, Vitaliy Trubetskoi and others; from the "Diplomatic Panorama" feature] [Text] The forecast about personnel changes in Russia's Foreign Ministry, published by INTERFAX on New Year's eve, has been completely realized. On December 30, Prime Minister Viktor Chernomyrdin endorsed the appointment of three deputy foreign ministers. Russia's former ambassador to Spain, Igor Ivanov, has been appointed to the post of first deputy foreign minister. The other deputy foreign ministers are Albert Chernyshov, ex-ambassador to Turkey, and Aleksandr Panov, ex-ambassador to the Republic of Korea. Panov has been replaced by former deputy foreign minister Georgiy Kunadze. According to INTERFAX sources, Chernyshov and Panov will be in charge of Asian affairs (Panov - Japan and Asian and Pacific Region, and Chernyshov - the Middle East). The letter of resignation handed to President Yeltsin by former first deputy foreign minister Anatoliy Adamishin following his election to the State Duma as a representative of the Yavlinskiy- Boldyrev-Lukin bloc makes it unclear what Ivanov will do, said a high-ranking Russian diplomat. He said that according to initial plans, Kozyrev was to have two first deputies - Adamishin and Ivanov, the latter to be in charge of Europe. Now that Adamishin has decided to quit the Foreign Ministry, Ivanov's responsibilities are likely to be enlarged, he said. In his opinion, the vacancy will be filled soon. "Practice shows that the minister must have two first deputies," he added. He found it difficult to say who exactly will become one of Kozyrev's first deputies. "The situation will become clear next week, when Kozyrev will resume his work following a brief leave," he said. He also noted that the personnel changes should not be perceived as an alarm signal. "It is normal practice, allowing diplomats to develop," said the Russian diplomat.
