November 29, 1989, Wednesday, Home Edition

DR. JOYCE BROTHERS: TEST WHAT YOU KNOW ABOUT FEAR

Should children be afraid? How much do you know about normal and abnormal fears in children? Here's true-or-false test on this subject.

1. -- A healthy child has no fears.

2. -- The child who fears that every cold may turn into pneumonia is intelligent, not unhealthy.

3. -- The young child's fears are basically the same today as they were in the past.

4. -- If a 7-, 8- or 9-year-old expresses a fear of strangers or robbers, parents should be concerned, because chances are this is an indication of neuroses.

5. -- Parents can help reduce the level of a child's anxieties about almost any problem.

6. -- If a child has fears, it may be an indication that he's less intelligent than the child without fears.

7. -- Parents should protect their children but not worry them about so-called "bad" people who might take advantage of them.

8. -- The greatest fears and worries of 15-year-olds center on whether or not their world will be destroyed by nuclear war.

ANSWERS:

1. -- FALSE. Everyone needs to have some fears. Without them no one would survive, for we would take too many foolish risks that could destroy us.

2. -- FALSE. This is an unrealistic fear that may be an early indication of hypochondria. This shows an excessive preoccupation with sickness and may be reflecting a parent's anxiety.

3. -- TRUE. Children between the ages of 4 and 7 fear unknown things and bad things.

4. -- FALSE. These are normal anxieties in children of that age, because they're more aware of things happening in the neighborhood and community.

5. -- TRUE. They can do this by being calm and rational themselves and by talking about specific problems that concern the youngster. Children's fears often reflect their parents' anxiety levels.

6. -- FALSE. Actually, a child who is fearful is apt to be more intelligent because he or she is more aware of the dangers in the world and the environment.

7. -- FALSE. Of course parents should protect their children, but part of the protection is to warn them that all strangers may not be nice. They should know how to protect themselves from sexual abuse just as they should know about the importance of obeying traffic rules.

8. -- FALSE. Probably the biggest fear and anxiety of 15-year-olds is related to sex. By this age, many children have been pressured by peers either to have or not to have sex. Those who do may feel guilty and fearful of the consequences, and those who don't may worry about being liked by peers and being considered "normal."

If you answered six questions correctly, you're better informed than most on this subject.
