September 13, 1990, Thursday, Home Edition

UPDATING A SILENT CLASSIC;

FILM: A TRULY ECLECTIC MIX OF LIVE MUSIC ACCOMPANIES TONIGHT'S HOLLYWOOD BOWL SCREENING OF THE GERMAN SILENT MOVIE 'THE CABINET OF DR. CALIGARI.'

The days of outdoor movies at the Hollywood Bowl continue. On the heels of last year's "Alexander Nevsky" and last month's screening of "Singin' in the Rain" comes a screening tonight of the 1921 German silent film "The Cabinet of Dr. Caligari."

But this latest cinematic-music event -- part of the Los Angeles Festival -- promises to be considerably more inventive if not controversial. The film will be accompanied by a live soundtrack provided by the Los Angeles Philharmonic performing John Adams' tone poem "Harmonielehre" under the baton of David Alan Miller.

In addition to this odd mixture of Expressionism and contemporary music will be two especially different local ensembles: the five-member World Kulintang Ensemble (an ancient Philippine gong and drum ensemble) from Reseda and the F.A.M.E. Gospel Choir from central Los Angeles.

"It's a West Coast event, with strong ties to Los Angeles and San Francisco, but with other elements scattered here and there as well," Los Angeles Festival director Peter Sellars explained by telephone while rehearsing at Cahuenga Pass. "In Los Angeles, one type of culture doesn't swallow up another, they all exist in diversity, and this concert celebrates this idea."

"Months ago, Peter told me he had a dream and vision of our ancient gong music being performed on the same stage as the L.A. Philharmonic," recalled Eleanor Academia-Magda, director of the Kulintang ensemble. "It came as no surprise a few weeks ago when he asked us to join this event."

Originally, the performance was to include the world premiere of a silent film by Sellars entitled "The Cabinet of Dr. Ramirez," a non-satirical but occasionally humorous examination of the world of Wall Street. Because of his duties at the Los Angeles Festival, he was unable to shoot the film last July as scheduled.

Sellars wrote the movie with Adams' "Harmonielehre" in mind as a soundtrack. He defends this process of filmmaking, putting the images to pre-existing music, as a product of today's culture as well as a return to movies like Walt Disney's "Fantasia."

"We're in an era of MTV and the idea of matching images and music in this way is very logical," he explained. "More and more, our culture is growing non-verbal and that's not good in many ways, but in some ways it's a relief.

"I remember first hearing 'Harmonielehre' at about 2 a.m. as I was walking home from the Kennedy Center in Washington. (Adams) had sent me a rehearsal tape and I was listening to it on my Walkman.

"I pictured the music as the soundtrack of a lost Alfred Hitchcock movie. I could imagine a fevered Jimmy Stewart riding in a car while the music plays."

Because the initial inspiration for the film was "Harmonielehre," Sellars wanted to keep an event dedicated to that work. He hopes that using "Dr. Caligari" instead of "Dr. Ramirez" will enhance both the film and add a dimension to Adams' work that is not immediately perceptible.

"The silent era of film is my favorite time for movies," Sellars said. "And the artists who worked on 'Caligari' weren't very prominent after the film opened. This was their one claim to fame. It's a great movie, but not that good of a movie.

"And most people find John's music very transparent, and can't immediately hear the connection he has made with Schoenberg." (The title for Adams work comes from a treatise on harmony by Arnold Schoenberg.)

"By putting this film -- with all its German Expressionist roots -- alongside the music, elements of the music that usually go unnoticed are drawn out," Sellars said.

" 'Harmonielehre' is basically a musical gloss on the styles and moods of Expressionism, though very whimsical," Adams said during a recent trip to Los Angeles, where he is working on a documentary film for children with Sellars. "I'm sure that this will change the original intent of the film, since music creates the context of a cinematic situation."

Adams admits that these recent projects have made him eager to work in the area of film as he imagines the numerous possibilities. But he remains skeptical about playing the role of a traditional Hollywood film composer.

"Every now and then I get a package from someone in Hollywood who wants me to score a movie, but then I look at the scripts, which usually aren't that good," Adams said. "I think of all the work it would take and usually conclude that it's just not worth it.

"Ideally a filmmaker should consult with the composer from the very beginning of a project but because film is so expensive, collaborating like that is almost impossible."
