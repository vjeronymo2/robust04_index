March 23, 1989, Thursday, Home Edition

LIFE IN THE TV PRESSURE COOKER

Kim Le Masters calls it "the civilian equivalent of war."

Fred Silverman calls it "a constant merry-go-round."

Tony Thomopoulos calls it "a terrific opportunity for anyone to have."

Brandon Stoddard just called it quits.

The unexpected resignation of the ABC Entertainment president Tuesday served to put the spotlight on one of the most powerful yet pressure-packed jobs in the entertainment industry -- the television networks' chief programmers, whose responsibility it is to try to satisfy not only their medium's insatiable appetite for thousands of hours of television shows a year but also their bosses' constant hunger for bigger ratings and even bigger profits.

But is the job itself satisfying? Stoddard, ultimately, decided no.

While acknowledging that "it's been a joy and a privilege in the last 19 years as a broadcaster to put television shows on the air that hundreds of millions of people have enjoyed and learned from," the 51-year-old executive concluded that along with the power came too much frustration and aggravation. In short, he said, "it's just no fun anymore."

Stoddard, who has been at ABC since 1970 and will remain there as president of its in-house production division, said that the job is more difficult now than ever before because of the "new realities" of network television, in which audiences and profits are harder to come by as the competition for cable TV, independent stations and videocassette recorders increases.

Past and present programming chiefs agree.

"I know exactly what he's talking about," said Kim LeMasters, president of CBS Entertainment for the past year. "There are days when it looks so dark and gloomy that it's not fun to even get out of bed."

And Brandon Tartikoff, president of NBC Entertainment since 1981, admitted Wednesday that, "If you catch me on the wrong Tuesday in February, I'd complain far worse than Brandon Stoddard did in the press about how hard these jobs are. It does get to you. But the thing that gets you into your car early in the morning is some of the shows you work on and some of the people you get to work with."

With all this complaining, it's hard to believe that Martin Starger, who was head of programming at ABC from 1970-1975, recalls that he didn't feel that way at all. "I never thought of it as horrendous. Not at all. It was very exciting for me. I have to say I had fun," he said Wednesday.

Starger was quick to admit, however, that his tenure "was a little different in those days than it is now. I don't know if it was worse. But it was a different world in the sense that in the '70s there were three networks in competition with one another and you programmed only against your two competitors. Cable was still in its infancy, and videocassette was a phrase that no one used at that time. Now, the competition is a lot more than two networks."

Another difference, Starger noted, is the fact that as an executive he probably had more autonomy than his counterparts today (when it is reported that even CBS Inc. president Laurence Tisch sometimes overrules his executives on programming decisions).

"The people I worked for rarely interfered with the programming department's recommendations," recalled Starger, now president of Marstar Productions. "Sure, there were the usual problems with budgets and money. But never autonomy."

In addition, the programming departments themselves were smaller and less bureaucratic. "They were very vital, very exciting places to work, with one-third and one-fourth the executives that networks have now," said Starger, whose staff included Barry Diller (now chairman of Fox Inc.) and Michael Eisner (now chairman of Disney). "I think that's part of the frustration."

Another change has been the increasing attention that the news media has paid to television and its top entertainment executives.

Fame has its price, LeMasters noted. Now, instead of journalists simply reporting that a CBS or ABC program failed, "we hear that it was Kim LeMasters' dumb idea, or Brandon Stoddard's dumb idea -- and that does wear and tear on you."

"The job is more identified with the person, or the personality of the guy who runs the entertainment division," he said. "The irony of ironies is that, although we who run the division may want to think we are the Il Duce, we all work off committees. But people can be quite vicious, and if you have any sensitivity at all, it plays on you."

Tony Thomopoulos, president of ABC Entertainment from 1978 to 1982, pinpointed the start of the fame game as 1975, when Fred Silverman jumped from CBS to ABC and landed on the cover of Time magazine.

"When he went on the cover of Time, the whole function of the job changed," he recalled Wednesday. "Before, we'd gotten exposure within the entertainment community but never on a national basis. All of a sudden we were media figures rather than just guys or gals doing a job."

And with the new interest in the personalities of the executives came a new interest in the work they did. "All of a sudden, the ratings games became a way of life not only for industry people but for the general audience. Just pick up a newspaper and you can find Nielsen overnights. For many people, it's like reading the box scores in sports."

Harvey Shephard, who was senior vice president of programs at CBS from 1980 to 1986, called this phenomenon "one of the great spectator sports in our country. Even my father in New York one day said to me, 'Boy, did you bomb on Tuesday nights!' "$

NBC's Tartikoff said he only recently has found a way to deal with being written about -- he's now reading a book byt Richard Nixon instead of reviews by The Times' Howard Rosenberg. "I've adapted a little bit," he shrugged. "I don't read everything I get my hands on anymore."

One thing that hasn't changed about being programming chief over the years is the hours it requires. All 24 of them.

"The worst thing about the job is that it never ends. You solve one problem and three more take its place," lamented Fred Silverman, who was head of programming for CBS from 1970 to 1975 and then president of ABC Entertainment from 1975 to 1978. "You're living to work instead of working to live."

Silverman recalled with horror those days of the constantly ringing phone and the constantly filled appointment calendar. "Once," he said, "I was flying on a plane to somewhere, and a (program) supplier bought a seat on the flight so he could sit next to me and sell a show."

Thomopoulos agreed: "It's the kind of job that, to do it properly, you must live, breathe and sleep with it. The sheer magnitude of it becomes all time-consuming."

Another is the constant pressure created by having that "report card" of ratings follow you day in and day out.

Shephard, now president of Warner Bros. Television, recalled said that even for the five years when CBS was No. 1, "People would say you must enjoy it, but in reality, you couldn't. It was expected that the following year, you would have to repeat that success, and you couldn't even look to weekends to unwind and relax, because there was no hiding from the ratings."

Thomopoulos, former president of United Artists, believes the stress is greater in television than in motion pictures because of the ever-present Nielsens. "In the final analysis, you're also judged by the quality of the product and the box office results. But it comes in better intervals so you have time to regroup."

Given all this, the burn-out factor associated with the job is considered high by most standards. LeMasters, for instance, said that while he wants to "savor this for awhile," he knows that in three years' time he'll be looking in the mirror "to see if the psychological damage is too great, or can I stand some more and go on from there."

Of course, he noted, "people would argue that in my case it was six months."

Tartikoff, for his part, admitted that being programming chief is "like relationships -- you fall in and out of love."

So what keeps him going? "I think that what you've got to have motivating you at all times are two things. One, you've got to have a love for the medium of TV. And, two, you've got to have some sort of vision that you're hoping to accomplish, and if you accomplish that vision, you're leaving it a better place."

Stoddard did all that, but still the job began to close in on him. "Frankly," he said, "it's like swimming faster and faster in a pond that's getting smaller and smaller."

What happened to Stoddard might have been similar to what Silverman felt in the final analysis. "You turn around one day and say, 'A piece of my life is gone and there isn't a hell of a lot to show for it.' "
