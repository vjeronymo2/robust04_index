August 12, 1989, Saturday, Home Edition

STUDY URGES USE OF SHORT-TERM THERAPIES FOR WOMEN'S DEPRESSION

New forms of psychotherapy lasting as little as 12 to 16 weeks are proving increasingly effective in treating depressed women, according to a massive study of women and depression that suggests that long-term therapy and years of medication may not be necessary for many of the millions of American women who suffer from the disorder.

The two-year study, preliminary results of which were presented Friday at the annual meeting of the American Psychological Assn., confirmed previous findings that women are twice as likely as men to experience diagnosable depression and are three to five times as likely as men to attempt suicide.

"Women are especially vulnerable to depression, but new treatment technologies report a success rate of 80% to 90% in providing at least initial, and sometimes long-term, relief," said Ellen McGrath, chairman of the association's task force on women and depression, which intends to release its findings in full this fall.

According to McGrath, the task force found in reviewing recent research that several forms of short-term therapy are well suited for treating women's depression. On their own or in combination with each other or with anti-depressant medication, the therapies offer at least temporary relief within three to four months, the group contends.

One of those therapies, so-called interpersonal therapy, focuses narrowly on current "interpersonal problems" such as grief or a dispute over a woman's role in marriage. The emphasis on relationships makes the therapy especially appropriate for women, who derive much of their identity and sense of well-being from relationships, members of the task force said.

The group also called for wider use of cognitive therapy, which aims to correct distorted thinking such as negative and pessimistic views of oneself and the world; and behavioral treatments for depression, which aim to teach social skills, problem-solving, assertiveness and other skills.

Robin Post, a clinical psychologist at the University of Colorado Health Sciences Center and a member of the task force, said studies indicate that those therapies have proven effective in as little as 8 to 16 weeks. However, she said depression is often cyclical; about 20% to 45% of women who are helped will experience a recurrence.

Post and others on the task force urged therapists to set up peer support groups and "refresher courses" for graduates of short-term therapy. They said it is important for women to understand that they can get relief from depression without embarking upon a decade of psychotherapy and years on anti-depressant drugs.

The task force, made up of nearly two dozen therapists in research and private practice, began the study in response to accumulating evidence that depression is increasing, particularly among women. Reviewing published and unpublished data from studies on depression that included thousands of women, they concluded that one in four women will suffer so-called major depression -- which goes beyond simple reaction to a specific life stress -- in her lifetime.

The group attributed the disproportionately high rate of depression among women to a mix of social, biological and emotional factors. Those range from an unequal burden of poverty that falls on women, to hormonal changes, to what McGrath described as women's tendency to focus on depressed feelings "at the expense of action and mastery strategies."

The group also made the following findings:

* The rate of sexual and physical abuse of girls and women appears to be higher than previously thought. One study found that 37% of adult women had experienced some form of physical or sexual abuse by the time they reached age 21, McGrath said.

* Married women are more likely to be depressed than single or divorced women or widows, the group found. A woman in an unhappy marriage is three times more likely than her husband to feel seriously depressed.

* Depression in women is misdiagnosed 30% to 50% of the time, sometimes as a physical problem such as untreatable dementia. Nevertheless, women are frequently prescribed powerful medication: 70% of all prescriptions for anti-depressants go to women.

The task force called for therapists and counselors to learn, through continuing or graduate education, more about the evolving short-term therapies. The group said a mix of interpersonal therapy and cognitive and behavioral approaches appears to be "one of the optimal treatments . . . for mild to moderately depressed women."

Anti-depressants "are effective and even necessary in treating certain kinds of depressions for women," the group reported. But they cautioned that the drugs work differently for men and women and in some cases have not been tested adequately in women before marketing.
