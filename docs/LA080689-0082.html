August 6, 1989, Sunday, Home Edition

WALL STREET / JOHN CRUDELE: INVESTOR SIMMONS MAY BE MAKING MOVE ON LOCKHEED

What does Harold Simmons, the billionaire Texas investor, really have in mind for Lockheed Corp.?

Simmons currently owns around $280 million worth of the Calabasas-based aerospace company's stock. But he has confused Wall Street over the past few months by selling some of his Lockheed holdings.

Michael Lamb, president of Wealth Monitors Inc., a Kansas City, Mo., investment advisory firm, has been studying Simmons' moves and thinks that he has the guy figured out.

"I think Simmons wants to own and control Lockheed eventually," Lamb concludes.

Then why has Simmons sold some of his Lockheed stock?

Here, chronologically, is what Lamb thinks happened

April 3: Simmons holds 5.3% of Lockheed's stock. At this point, Wall Street figures that the company -- although it has a market value of about $3 billion -- is vulnerable to a takeover.

April 4: Lockheed announces a major restructuring that includes the establishment of an employee stock ownership plan and the sale of some assets.

April 4: Simmons says he might sell some stock, and that same day unloads 200,000 shares. That brings his total holdings down to 4.9% of Lockheed's shares (4.2% after his holdings are diluted by the establishment of the ESOP by Lockheed). Wall Street figures, at this point, that Simmons is being scared away.

Buys More Shares

Lamb thinks that Simmons purposely wanted to get his holdings under 5% so that he wouldn't be required to publicly disclose to the Securities and Exchange Commission certain discussions about Lockheed. "Simmons at this point went into the shadows," Lamb says.

May 15: Simmons meets with Lockheed's management. Nothing is known about the meeting because Simmons -- who at this point still owns less than 5% of Lockheed's shares -- isn't required to disclose what was discussed. "That same day, Simmons started buying for a solid month, except for one day, May 16," says Lamb.

Between May 15 and June 8, Lamb says Simmons bought $69 million worth of Lockheed stock. And from June 9 to July 17, he bought another $34 million worth of stock.

Aug. 1: Simmons reports that he purchased another 909,100 shares of Lockheed, bringing his total to 6,007,200, or 9.46%.

"He now has $280 million in Lockheed, his biggest investment ever," says Lamb. "He wants to own this company. He's been sitting with $4 billion in borrowing power for two years."

Lamb's view is unconventional. Most people on Wall Street still think that Lockheed is too big for Simmons to take over. And the government would be very reluctant to allow a corporate raider to bother an important defense contractor like Lockheed.

Because of that, Lockheed's stock has not reacted much to Simmons' buying pressure.

Lockheed does have one thing that could make it vulnerable. The company has a very over-funded pension fund that could attract a corporate raider.

If Lamb is right about Simmons action, he will have solved one of Wall Street biggest mysteries. Simmons, in the eyes of Wall Street, wouldn't be fickle. He'd be smart.

The way that Wall Street sees it, Federal Reserve Board Chairman Alan Greenspan is a little like the pilot of the jet that crashed in Iowa. His steering mechanism isn't functioning, so he has to speed up or slow down his engines to go in a straight line.

Greenspan, of course, is controlling the economy. He gunned the interest rate engine early this year when it looked like inflation was getting out of hand. And now he's revving up the economy by lowering interest rates because he's worried about a recession.

The big question is, can Greenspan keep this baby flying? The financial markets apparently think that he can, or else bonds wouldn't have risen as much as they have this year and the stock market wouldn't be up 8% in July alone.

But some experts think that the financial markets are playing with fire. Even if Greenspan can successfully keep the economy humming for a while at a most desirable moderate-growth, moderate-inflation pace, most experts agree that Greenspan has not found a cure to the common recession. And when that recession does come -- whether it is later this year, next year or the year after that -- the stock market will not like it.

Recession Scare

(In testimony last week, even Greenspan admitted that he won't know if he's avoided a recession until well into next year.)

"I find it very curious. You are dealing with a study in mass psychology," says Jon Groveman, president of Ladenburg, Thalmann & Co., referring to the stock market's recent ascent. "People are buying stocks because they are going up, rather than buying them in the expectation that earnings are going to improve. And that could be dangerous."

In fact, Groveman and others worry that the more stocks go up, the more treacherous it will be when the markets start reacting to fears of a recession. Just how dangerous the recession scare can be was witnessed during the last week of June, when the Dow Jones industrial index fell a quick 92 points on worries that the economy was slowing too much.

The markets were acting skittish again this past week, especially on Tuesday, when signs that the economy is continuing to slow began to reappear.

Richard Hoey, chief economist at Drexel Burnham Lambert, thinks that the markets have been reacting correctly -- if they think that Greenspan has the magic touch and can execute the so-called "soft landing" for the economy. Since the Federal Reserve has been easing interest rates lately, Hoey thinks Greenspan may actually be able to avoid a recession in 1989.

But Hoey thinks that a recession will start soon -- probably in the middle of 1990. The Fed, he believes, is laying the groundwork right now by loosening interest rates. That move will cause an aggravation of inflation and the need for higher interest rates before next year's recession.

Donald Straszheim, head economist at Merrill Lynch, sees things a little differently. He thinks that the recession is already starting and that the nation's gross national product will fall this quarter and next.

But Straszheim also believes that the financial markets will ignore the recession. "I think the market will look right through the period of weakness," says Straszheim, focusing instead on the economic recovery he that predicts in early 1990.

So place your bets. Will the economy go into that recession? And, can the financial markets really ignore the problems that businesses will have and continue to move higher?

Three major drug companies have already agreed to be acquired in the past few months, and experts say more consolidations are likely in that industry.

For the investor looking to make a killing from one of these upcoming takeovers, the only trick is to own stock in the company being acquired (which will go up) and not the one doing the acquiring (which, likely, will go down).

Industry analysts think Pfizer Inc., Rorer Group, Upjohn Co., Warner-Lambert Co. and Syntex Corp. are the most likely drug companies to be acquired. American Home Products, a drug company that is also involved in other business, could also be vulnerable.

You would probably be wasting your time and money on Glaxo Holdings, Merck & Co., Eli Lilly Co., Johnson & Johnson or Hoffmann-LaRoche, the analysts say. These five will more likely be the ones doing the acquiring rather than be the companies bought.

But analyst aren't even willing to concede that these five industry giants are safe. As David Saks of Wedbush Morgan Securities says, all the rules have already been broken regarding mergers in the drug industry, so "now any idea makes sense."

Further down the road, analysts say, biotechnology companies could come under attack. But the attractiveness of these biotech firms to drug or chemical companies will depend greatly on the biotech firm's ability to translate scientific discoveries into useable products.

Smith Barney restricted its salespeople from buying Midway Airlines stock last week. A spokesman, however, wouldn't say whether it was because the brokerage firm was representing a corporate raider who might go after Midway, a Chicago-based airline. Midway's stock has been trading very heavily for more than a week. . . .

All those speculators waiting for Paramount Communications' next hostile takeover attempt had better make themselves comfortable. A Wall Street source familiar with Paramount says the company never had a contingency takeover target in the event -- as happened -- that it didn't get to acquire Time Inc. Paramount's chief executive, Martin Davis, moreover, would be reluctant to attempt another unfriendly takeover so soon, the source says. The source thinks that the next deal for Paramount will be a friendly one. . . .

Is someone after Conrail? Last week, Conrail -- also known as Consolidated Rail Corp. -- established an employee stock ownership program. The week before that, the company raised its common stock dividend. Both are viewed by Wall Street as defensive measures against a takeover, confirming an earlier report in this column about the company's nervousness. But is someone really after the company, which is protected legislatively from takeovers until early next year, or is Conrail just paranoid? A source on Wall Street says Conrail spotted some suspicious buying of its stock a few months ago coming out of Chicago and thinks that someone may be accumulating its shares. . . .
