December 19, 1989, Tuesday, Home Edition

DR. JOYCE BROTHERS: FANTASY CURBS, DISPLACES RAGE

DEAR DR. BROTHERS: I often have problems in relationships because of my short fuse. I get angry, have an outburst of rage and say things I regret. As a result, the person walks out, never to return, and most of the time, I suffer from loneliness. My sister tells me that she has the same kind of temper but when she's angry she works through it in her imagination. Is this crazy? -- G.U.

DEAR G.U.: I don't think so. It's not a bad idea to fantasize about what you would do if you could, and then let it go. Some people have told me that when someone cuts them off in traffic or is otherwise rude and unfriendly, they fantasize whipping out their make-believe revolver and shooting out the person's tires. This harmless dream relieves their rage.

Dr. Ned Marcus, a psychiatrist, points out that dreams are a way of releasing suppressed anger. Freud believed that dreams are a safety valve through which our mean, dark, horrid impulses could escape harmlessly.

Fantasies never hurt anybody. You are allowed to dream about or think of almost anything you wish, and unless you act upon the dream, there's no reason to feel guilty.

Once you learn to handle your anger, you'll probably find that you are less lonely and that your relationships are vastly improved.

DEAR DR. BROTHERS: Should a 6-year-old be severely punished for lying? My husband believes they should be, and recently, when our son reported that his teachers complimented him and told him how good he was at a given task, we discovered that he'd lied. Indeed, he had not done the task properly and far from being complimented, he had been kept after class because of his disruptive behavior. -- M.P.

DEAR M.P.: I don't believe in "severe" punishment for a 6-year-old and certainly not for lying. What is important for parents and teachers to explore is why the youngster felt it necessary to lie. I suspect he may be developing a habit of lying because of his fear of punishment.

When a 6-year-old lies, intelligent understanding is needed. It's fairly obvious that your son wants his teachers to compliment him, to tell him what a good boy he is and to reassure him that he's worthwhile and capable of doing the tasks required. Most children want desperately to please parents and teachers. They like and need praise.

If the only time a child gets the attention of his parents is when he misbehaves, then he may deliberately do naughty things in order to draw attention to himself.

Why not try to arrange a conference with your son's teacher to try to find out what she feels your son needs? When a child consistently lies about his performance at school, he may need some professional counseling. This may be a cry for help.
