November 16, 1989, Thursday, P.M. Final

LIZ SMITH: EATING OUT OF CARTONS -- IT'S AN ALL-TIME NO-NO

"NEVER, NEVER eat anything out of a carton, even if you are at home alone with the shades drawn. Doing so is wicked and constitutes Miss Manners' one exception to the generally genial rule about violations of etiquette not counting if you don't get caught," writes Judith Martin.

So now we know why the Corleone family of "The Godfather" films has had so much trouble in its dynasty. It all probably stems from "eating Chinese takeout" from cartons in that famous scene from the first movie.

And that's nothing. Frank Sinatra's late mother used to rebuke him for drinking from the milk carton while standing in the cool shadow of the refrigerator. Dolly Sinatra knew her "Miss Manners" stuff, you see.

THE MOST coveted role in Hollywood for any young actor is the lead in Tim Burton's next movie, "Edward Scissorhand," for 20th Century Fox. (It will be Burton's first movie since the sensational "Batman.")

The star is to be Johnny Depp of TV's "21 Jump Street," and he'll do his acting during a series hiatus.

WHEN DENNIS MET MEG -- The delightful Meg Ryan and the divine Dennis Quaid, those co-stars of "D.O.A." and "Innerspace," are engaged -- formally. Their long-playing off-screen romance seems to have reached a more serious phase.

NOW ABOUT "Steel Magnolias." My pal Pia Lindstrom insists it's a movie that people will enjoy, provided they never saw the brilliant off-Broadway play by Robert Harling. I'm not sure. I think it's the most overwrought cinema in recent memory.

The movie is half way over before it settles down enough that you can understand what the actors are saying. By then you have a terrible headache. The actors don't interact; they seem each to be doing some individual thing. I know Ray Stark, Herb Ross and writer Harling felt they had to "open up" the play, but they've sunk it under big-screen, epic entertainment values.

The cast, however, is for the most part, fascinating. Dolly Parton rises above everything; she is subdued, simple, believable and simply great as the beauty shop owner. Shirley MacLaine is excellent once they let her quit screaming and dragging a big dog around as in her chaotic entrance. Olympia Dukakis is wonderful but seems to be in a Noel Coward drawing room comedy. Daryl Hannah plays well against her sexy image as the "born again" beauty operator.

I was disappointed by the all-important relationship between the mother (Sally Field) and the saintly daughter (Julia Roberts.) This is crucial to the plot, but I felt the most they convey is the usual mother-daughter squabbling. And Julia is more like a spoiled brat than a girl whose sweetness touches all lives.

So, I'd say go see the play, one of the best ever of its kind. Then see the movie, just so you'll be able to shake your head at how Tri-Star missed the boat by a Louisiana country mile.
