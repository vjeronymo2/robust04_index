January 8, 1989, Sunday, Home Edition

REMEMBERING FAWN BRODIE

Regarding David Johnston's double review of "A Gathering of Saints" by Robert Lindsay and "The Mormon Murders" by Steven Naifeh and Gregory White Smith (Book Review, Dec. 18): Johnston states in his review that Robert Lindsay's book on the recent Mormon forgeries and murders "is easily the best single volume ever written to explain the church as it is, not as it wants to be seen." I have not read Lindsay's book, but I don't know whether it can "easily" claim this distinction over the book the reviewer omits mentioning, the remarkable biography of Joseph Smith, "No Man Knows My History," by the eminent historian Fawn M. Brodie, who taught for many years at UCLA and is now deceased.

Originally published in 1945, Brodie's book is wonderfully written, a thoroughly compelling yet scholarly look at the man who founded the church, and its history. It's a marvelously understated, witty, yet compassionate study, which so upset church leaders that one of their members, Hugh Nibley, published a rebuttal entitled "No Ma'am That's Not History." It was virtually impossible to obtain a copy of Brodie's book in Utah when I was growing up.

It's worth reading on a number of levels: as a record of the Utopian era in American development; as a history of Western conquest; as a study of the development of a uniquely American religion. Also, I agree with Brodie, who expressed surprise that no one has ever opted to regard The Book of Mormon as an example of early American frontier literature.

JUDITH FREEMAN

LOS ANGELES
