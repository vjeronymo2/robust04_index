May 18, 1989, Thursday, Home Edition

REVOLT IN GUATEMALA, WHILE EASILY QUELLED, POINTS TO WIDER GRIEVANCES

A failed coup attempt by senior military officers last week appears to have been triggered by a new outbreak of guerrilla warfare and the emergence of civilian President Vinicio Cerezo Arevalo's controversial protege as a leading candidate to succeed him.

But while the revolt was thwarted without a shot by loyalist commanders and denounced by nearly every civilian group, the grievances voiced by the plotters are shared beyond their small circle and spell a lingering threat to Guatemala's incipient democracy, according to political sources and foreign diplomats.

Since a similar coup attempt a year ago, these people say, Cerezo has yielded much of his power to the military hierarchy that saved him. The latest revolt, in their view, has strengthened those commanders committed to civilian rule while increasing the president's dependence on them.

The army high command moved swiftly this week to stifle internal dissent, indicting 24 active and retired officers in a military court on rebellion and sedition charges for the May 9 uprising. But the crackdown may carry a price for Cerezo, Guatemalans close to the president believe.

"These coup attempts are like heart attacks," said Juan Jose Rodil, a reformer dismissed from Cerezo's Cabinet after last year's coup attempt. "They kill part of the system and affect the health of the whole body. To survive, the patient has to accept certain restrictions."

Guatemala, rent by Central America's most enduring conflict between the extremes of left and right, has never sustained a modern democratic government. Human rights groups estimate that up to 100,000 people died in political violence under a string of military dictators in the 1970s and early 1980s.

After decimating a force of several thousand leftist guerrillas, the armed forces switched to a strategy of isolating the insurgency through rural development and civilian rule. Cerezo, a Christian Democrat, began a five-year term in January, 1986, after winning 68% of the popular vote.

High Expectations

But Guatemalans' high expectations of him have not been fulfilled. Despite modest economic growth and greater margins of civic freedom, the 46-year-old president is viewed today as unable to close a widening gap between rich and poor or to stop official corruption and lingering human rights abuse.

Against this backdrop of popular disillusion, some military officers contend that the new counterinsurgency strategy is failing and would be further undermined by the election of Alfonso Cabrera, Cerezo's hand-picked presidential candidate, whom they view as a symbol of corruption.

A manifesto drafted for the May 9 revolt charged that public funds were being diverted into a campaign "to install a party dictatorship for the benefit of a political clique of unscrupulous Christian Democrats" and into front organizations of a resurgent guerrilla army.

It accused Gen. Hector A. Gramajo, the defense minister and most powerful military figure, of "support and complicity" in this process.

Troops loyal to Gramajo and the president used tanks to corner soldiers involved in the four-hour rebellion before the proclamation could be broadcast or published.

The uprising was undertaken before dawn with 300 soldiers from the air base at Guatemala City's airport and one of three army barracks in the capital. Wearing sky-blue kerchiefs to identify themselves, they seized the telephone company, police headquarters and the national radio network as well as Gramajo's wife and three children.

But the rebels miscalculated. Apparently unaware of technology that averted a total shutdown of the telephone system, they failed to prevent the high command from enlisting other units against them. By 9 a.m. it was over.

Cerezo identified the chief plotter as Col. Cesar Ramon Quinteros, an Israeli-trained officer who lost to Gramajo in maneuvering for the army's top post and was shifted from a troop command to head the largely symbolic Central American Defense Council.

Among the others indicted were three other colonels on the council, commanders of the two rebel units, a former police chief and four officers who were cashiered for the May 11, 1988, coup attempt but later freed under an amnesty. Three of those charged have eluded arrest.

Relaxed, Confident

In an interview, Cerezo said the military leadership was "much more annoyed" by this year's revolt, which he described as better organized, and ready to prosecute to the limit. He appeared relaxed and confident of finishing his term.

"The reaction of the people and the political parties and, in general, the attitude of the army allow me to think that we have taken a step toward the consolidation of democracy," he said.

Independent analysts are not so certain. Cerezo was able to dispel much of the tension lingering after last year's revolt, they say, with such policy changes as breaking off peace talks with the guerrillas, re-equipping the army, making economic concessions to business executives and canceling moves to upgrade ties with Communist nations. The new spasm of discontent seems directed more at the army leadership.

"It's more dangerous for Cerezo now," a Western diplomat said. "He doesn't have as much to play with except (getting rid of) Gramajo, the man he needs most."

One catalyst for the revolt, according to lawyers for the plotters, was an April 25 guerrilla ambush that killed the second in command of the Huehuetenango army base in western Guatemala. The victim's brother-in-law, an air force colonel, took part in the coup attempt.

The ambush followed stepped-up guerrilla attacks on army road crews in the mountainous interior, the loss of an air force plane that was apparently shot down and the bombing of a police station in the capital.

Days before last week's coup attempt, one of the cashiered plotters of the 1988 revolt was quoted in Guatemala City newspapers as saying that "tension in the army is even higher now" because the commanders will not fight back aggressively.

Col. Luis Arturo Isaacs, the army spokesman, denied that the guerrillas, whose armed strength he estimated at 800, are a renewed threat. But he acknowledged that the army's strategy of focusing on their civilian support base instead of direct pursuit "is hard for many officers to comprehend."

"Gen. Gramajo is 20 years ahead of the institution on how to make the army work in a democratic framework," a military specialist said.

Coup rumors began sweeping through a tense capital last month as Cerezo was buffeted by unusually strong criticism from Guatemala's Roman Catholic bishops and lashed out bitterly against rightist-controlled newspapers.

The final trigger for the coup attempt appears to have been the dismissal two weeks ago of Navy Capt. Romeo Guevara Reyes as national police chief and his replacement by a civilian Christian Democrat.

Guevara took part in the uprising. His spokesman, Carlos Chen, said Guevara had been pushed aside to give the ruling party a freer hand to put off-duty police officers to work in Cabrera's campaign for the party presidential primary on June 18.

Some analysts see the coup attempt as less a threat to Cerezo, who is now viewed as a lame duck, than to his longtime friend and protege. As secretary general of the party, Cabrera helped Cerezo win the nomination in 1985, then served as foreign minister.

The president supports Cabrera's candidacy, overlooking his friend's bouts of public drunkenness and mysterious wealth. Newspapers have suggested that Cabrera is involved in narcotics trafficking -- talk that Cerezo calls unfounded and ideologically motivated.
