CSO [Editorial: "Pakistan-U.S. Relations--Vain Delusion"] [Text] No two countries in the world maintain similar relations all the time; these change as the result of different situations and events. Pakistan-U.S. relations are no exception to this rule. The relations between the United States and Pakistan have seen many ups and downs during the last 45 years. There have been periods of close and warm relations as well as of coolness. Various conditions are affecting the Pakistan-United States relations at present and we see changes. We have commented on President Clinton's report sent to the U.S. Congress the other day. He had made two surprising statements in that report: (1) Pakistan is blackmailing India over the Kashmir issue and is saying that if the Kashmir issue is not resolved a nuclear war will break out between the two countries. In Mr. Clinton's opinion this is equivalent to Pakistan trying to blackmail India. (2) The U.S. President also has said that the situation will remain dangerous and peace cannot be established in South Asia until the Kashmir issue is resolved. President Clinton's sentiments have not only surprised but also worried the people of Pakistan. They have been forced to wonder why did this change in the U.S. stand occur so suddenly. Only yesterday, a ranked U.S. official, Robin Raphel, had said emphatically that the United States considered Jammu-Kashmir a disputed region and a decision needed to be made about its future. In addition to Robin Raphel, other U.S. spokespersons had expressed the same opinion. It appears that when India reacted strongly to this statement and used various tactics to pressure Ms. Robin [Raphel] and the United States, the United States was forced to change its stand. Recently, India and the United States held secret talks in London to discuss India's nuclear program. These talks were unsuccessful and India refused to make any agreement with the United States about its nuclear program. Its important officials have announced that India will continue with its missiles program as scheduled and will complete all tests by this coming July. These events indicate that India is not willing to listen to the United States. The Indian prime minister is visiting the United States these days. The Indian government and news media have used various tactics to pressure the U.S. government. They are trying to achieve all Indian goals during Mr. Narasimha Rao's visit to the United States and not to accept U.S. advice or pressure on any issue. Against this background, it is not easy to believe the statements made by two important Pakistani leaders today. One is forced to ask if these expressions are the result of some misconception or were made to make Pakistani people optimistic without any basis. The Pakistani ambassador to the United States said, "The relations between Pakistan and the United States have improved amazingly. The United States that was ready to declare Pakistan a terrorist nation until yesterday has adopted a very amiable attitude toward us. The United States says that if Pakistan assures it of freezing its nuclear program, it is ready to nullify the Pressler amendment." Similarly, Foreign Minister Sardar Asif Ahmed Ali said that Pakistan and the United States have agreed to make South Asia a nuclear-free zone. The United States will treat India and Pakistan equally on this issue. Mr. Asif Ali also claimed that the United States has admitted to the Pressler amendment being "a bad law and a very bad policy." We are entering a new era in which both Pakistan and the United states have agreed to improve and strengthen their mutual relations. He also said that the Pakistani stand over Kashmir and the nuclear program is better understood in the United States now after the recent diplomatic efforts by the government of Pakistan. Mr. Asif Ali also claimed that the United States has begun to feel that the Kashmir issue is a permanent danger to peace in South Asia. These opinions expressed by Dr. Maliha Lodhi and Sardar Asif Ali are very encouraging provided they represent the situation accurately. This, however, is not easy to understand in the light of President Clinton's report mentioned earlier. The foreign minister said that the United States has declared the Pressler amendment a bad law and a very bad policy. It could be really true, but we have not seen any proof of it. If the U.S. viewpoint has really changed, why did not it reinstate its aid to us? Why did not it decide to supply the 38 F-16s we have already paid for? Similarly, if the United States has decided not to require us to roll back our nuclear program unilaterally and also understands our positions, then what reason does it have to keep the Pressler amendment? The United States knows that India has refused to compromise its nuclear program. Therefore, if it wants to treat both countries equally, it should refrain from punishing Pakistan for keeping its nuclear program. However, we notice that this did not happen. The United States, in order to temporarily suspend the Pressler amendment, is asking us to open up our nuclear installations for inspection as a price for it. Does it show that it is treating India and Pakistan equally? There is no doubt that Ms. Bhutto's government is trying to make the United states understand its position and has succeeded in it to an extent. However, it is not appropriate to make the situation look so positive that it does not represent the facts. We hope and pray that all blocks in the path of Pakistan-United States relations are removed. At the same time, we should not express extraordinary optimism and should wait for the results of Narasimha Rao and President Leghari's visits to the United States. Only then will a clear picture will emerge.
