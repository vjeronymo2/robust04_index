BFN [By Jose Luis Castillejos] [Excerpts] Guatemala City, 12 Jun (NOTIMEX) -- Guatemalan President Ramiro de Leon Carpio today said Central America will present joint proposals at the Fourth Ibero-American summit. The proposals will deal with sustained development, ecological defense, and the review of the Cuban and Haitian cases. The Central American countries "are taking a joint proposal on the urgent need to form an alliance for sustained development in the region and ecological defense, which we hope the rest of the Ibero-American nations will join," De Leon Carpio said. The president traveled to San Jose, Costa Rica, at 1430 [2030 GMT] to meet with his Central American counterparts to adopt a united position on these topics. De Leon expressed optimism about the positive results of the Ibero-American Summit to be held in Cartagena de Indias, Colombia, on 14-15 June. He added that the meeting of government leaders from Belize, Costa Rica, El Salvador, Guatemala, Honduras, Nicaragua, and Panama, which begins today and ends tomorrow in San Jose, has the primary objective of establishing a "common front" for the defense of the region's interests. Guatemalan Foreign Ministry sources told NOTIMEX that during the Cartagena summit the presidents will sign a document on the importance that greater trade integration has for the hemisphere's economies. A diplomat indicated that during the Ibero-American Summit the Central American presidents want to meet with Colombian President Cesar Gaviria and Venezuelan President Rafael Caldera to discuss a free trade agreement between those two countries and Central America. [passage omitted] De Leon also said the Central American presidents will propose the review of the current crises in Haiti and Cuba. The Guatemalan president said that in Cuba's case, the island's return to the OAS must be conditioned on respect for human rights. The president expressed concerned over the Haitian situation, indicating that the current economic blockade has been insufficient to restore a democratic regime and that, in addition, the threat of a military invasion is looming over Haiti. The violence in the Haitian capital due to the international sanctions against General Raoul Cedras' regime "greatly worries us, and we must seek effective solutions," De Leon added.
