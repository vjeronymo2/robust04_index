September 15, 1990, Saturday, Orange County Edition

HANDLING OF POSTER SITUATION WAS TRAGEDY

We at The Works Gallery are appalled by the total lack of vision and courage shown by the Orange County Airport Arts Commission and the Airport Administration regarding the commissioned poster by artist Jim Morphesis ("Airport Poster Plan Melts Away, but Not the Art Uproar," Sept. 2.) I am horrified by the lack of integrity exemplified by our civic "leaders" in the process of effectively censoring a truly beautiful work of art.

Beyond the obvious stupidity displayed by the Airport administration in censoring a work of art no more "obscene" than any classical Greek or Roman work of art, the handling of this situation is a real tragedy. When one individual -- (airport commissioner) Robert Cashman -- has the power to override an entire committee which voted 9-0 to support the work of art and the selected poster, and to make all decisions pertaining to the art collection of the airport -- that is a tragedy.

When a single Orange County resident can elect himself "tastemaker" for an entire community -- after he is quoted in the press as saying, "If they gave us Michelangelo's 'David' today, I'd put it in a warehouse . . ." -- that is a tragedy.

It is one thing for a printer to pull his offer to print a poster he doesn't like; this is a free country. But when that printer also is an official on the committee deciding the poster's fate -- that is where it gets interesting. This conflict of interests is the direct result of poorly handled business planning, typical of the entire airport project. How Cashman could turn a unanimous vote in support of the poster on Wednesday into a vote of "Gee, I guess it's too late to print it now, that's just too bad" 72 hours later, is another example of back-room politics and nepotism.

The Morphesis incident is just one more example of the growing atmosphere of bureaucratic restraint on the arts being manifested again and again by individuals who feel they are qualified to decide for the rest of us what is and what isn't good. I feel it is ironic that the image that Cashman found offensive was that of Icarus, a man who fancied himself a god and in the effort to emulate one caused his own demise. Maybe Robert Cashman has just flown too close to the sun.

MARK MOORE

Director, The Works Gallery

Costa Mesa
