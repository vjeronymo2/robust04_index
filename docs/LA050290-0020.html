May 2, 1990, Wednesday, Home Edition

CALL IT APPEASEMENT

Does the United States still believe in anything? Does America still stand for independence and freedom and human dignity? Evidently not. A half century of brave words about "captive nations" has been shown as so much cheap talk as the Bush Administration puts its stamp of approval on another "expedient solution," the 1939 Nazi-Soviet pact that condemned the Baltic states to communist enslavement.

Reports that President Bush and Secretary of State James A. Baker III have entered into a cabal with French President Francois Mitterrand, West German Chancellor Helmut Kohl and Soviet Foreign Minister Eduard Shevardnadze to strangle Lithuanian independence in its cradle give new meaning to Lithuanian President Vytautas Landsbergis' charges that a new Munich is at hand.

Tragically, just when America's ideals have caused a worldwide resurgence in democracy, the United States itself is undercutting its own legacy. The Bush Administration has somehow gotten into its head the notion that appeasement is now the best policy. Those who have tried to bring them to their senses, like arms control negotiator Edward J. Rowny, have been eased out of power. Agreement at any price is once again the order of the day.

Don't we ever learn?

We won the Cold War because of our principles. We may end up losing the peace because of our lack of them.
