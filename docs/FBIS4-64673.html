BFN [Report on interview with UN Secretary General Butrus Butrus-Ghali by Kjell Dragnes in Oslo; no date given] [Text] The UN role in Bosnia is not a fiasco, UN Secretary General Butrus Butrus-Ghali said. The problem has not been solved, but people are working actively so that it will be. Fourteen years as Egyptian foreign minister, the peace settlement between Egypt and Israel in 1977, the many subsequent crises in the Middle East -- all of this was a tough, but good preparation for Butrus-Ghali who is now 71 years old and has been head of the United Nations since January 1991. "I believe that I am well-trained to tackle the many crises the United Nations has to tackle," the secretary general said in an interview with AFTENPOSTEN. It is not a tired leader -- despite all the crises and the many rounds of talks here in Norway -- who today ends his two-day visit to Norway. The only thing he regrets is that he has no opportunity to travel home to Cairo to see one crisis move toward its solution: the signing of an agreement between the PLO and Israel tomorrow. "But I have asked your Foreign Minister (Bjorn Tore) Godal to represent me," he said. How do you view the development within the United Nations from peacekeeping to peacemaking operations? "I think that we should clearly limit our role to peacekeeping, and this is in line with the spirit of the United Nations. But if we need peacemaking in order to be in a position to distribute food, to give humanitarian assistance, to stop those people who do not respect the resolutions adopted by the United Nations, who are committing genocide and atrocities, the international community should not hesitate to make use of peacemaking operations," the UN secretary general said. He said that 15 of the UN's 17 operations are peacekeeping operations. Article 7 of the UN Charter gives scope for peacemaking operations, but it was only once the Cold War was over that these became possible. It is in this context that Butrus-Ghali sees the former Yugoslavia. The general picture is that the United Nations has not had particular success, but the UN head does not agree. The United Nations has succeeded in preserving peace in Macedonia, has given aid to 2 million refugees and exiles, and on the whole has maintained the safe areas in Bosnia. Thorvald Stoltenberg is given much praise for his role as mediator, and Butrus Butrus-Ghali was not sparing in his praise of Norway's efforts -- in the form of soldiers and money. With a scarcely audible sigh he said that if only others among the UN's 184 members had been as well-disposed.... Some 160 have not paid their membership fees or made contributions to the UN operations. The United Nations finds itself in the midst of a major economic crisis, with a deficit of $1 billion, and this does not make it any easier to tackle the world's problems. "But if the members contribute money and troops I am sure that the United Nations can tackle the various crises," the secretary general said emphatically. The United Nations celebrates its 50th birthday next year. Many people take the view that the organization should be reformed. What is your view? "We will have many ceremonies next year, and we hope that through these we will be able to mobilize world public opinion in favor of the United Nations, give a better picture of it, and win greater support for the organization," the secretary general said. "In addition there will be a very important conference in Copenhagen which will create a greater understanding of the social element -- something that is important these days." The UN leadership has also distributed to member countries a questionnaire on reforms, especially of the Security Council. Most people are agreed that reforms are necessary, that new members should be admitted to the Council. But they disagree about which. "I believe that it will be a long debate," the general secretary said, adding, "I hope they will be able to accept certain fundamental changes to strengthen the Security Council and strengthen the UN system." The message from the secretary general is that the reforms will be a long process, but once the member nations make up their minds to invest in the United Nations, demonstrate the political will, and realize that the United Nations is the only organization which can tackle global problems, they will then be on the right road. Yesterday was filled with meetings, first with Prime Minister Gro Harlem Brundtland, then with Foreign Minister Bjorn Tore Godal, and then an audience at the Palace. Yesterday evening the prime minister hosted a dinner for the secretary general and his wife, Maria Leia Butrus-Ghali, at Akershus Castle. Today Butrus-Ghali will honor the memory of the very first UN secretary general, Trygve Lie, by laying a wreath on Lie's grave in Grorud churchyard. Talks at the Storting are also on the agenda before he travels on to Geneva this afternoon.
