May 21, 1989, Sunday, Home Edition

WALL STREET / JOHN CRUDELE: TAKEOVER CANDIDATES ARE TODAY'S BLUE-PLATE SPECIALS

Once a month a group of about 15 stockbrokers from different investment firms in Chicago get together for lunch at the Brokers Inn on South LaSalle St., across from the Chicago Board of Trade, to exchange ideas.

But these days the brokers aren't discussing which companies will have blockbuster new products, or which industries are about to have an earnings revival, or even where they think the stock market is going. Instead, they sit around for about 90 minutes trying to pick the next corporate takeover candidate.

"We bounce stories off each other," says Mike Rzepka, a broker at Prescott, Ball & Turben Inc. "Lately it's mostly hot stories."

"Hot" in Wall Street's lingo means stories about corporate takeovers, restructurings and the like. "It's one of the only ways to make money in this market," adds Rzepka.

Stock prices overall have been up this year; in fact, up sharply. Even so, most of the professionals who work with the stock market seem unusually insecure about their good fortune. Now even brokers, usually among the more conservative of the Wall Street bunch and the closest link to the public, seem to have become far more aggressive, handling stocks as speculators and traders do. And they've been bitten by the takeover bug, hoping to make the money and run.

Alfred Goldman, head of technical market analysis for A. G. Edwards in St. Louis, says interest in takeover stocks is "definitely up" over the past few months, especially among those clients he calls "stockaholics."

"There has been a definite increase among individual investors in playing each day's stock du jour," says Goldman, whose job it is to spot unusual trading patterns in a stock that could be a forerunner to a takeover and relay the information to his brokers.

It's difficult to say whether the Chicago lunchers are an anomaly or the beginning of the next trend. Experts say the get-together such as the one at the Brokers Inn takes place in all major cities, and more frequently the latest in corporate takeovers and rumors is the conversation.

There has, of course, always been a hard-core group of brokers (and investors, for that matter) who've been willing to put their money into risky takeover situations. But only recently does it seem that the allure of quick and big profits from the takeovers of companies such as NWA Corp and RJR Nabisco has started to grab the attention of a wider group of brokers. And these brokers are hooking clients on their ideas.

The Chicago luncheon meetings were begun six months ago and are the clearest indication of how far some brokers have come since the crash. This type of get-together is not only unusual because of the topic discussed, but because brokers have no qualms about sharing ideas freely with their peers (and competitors) at other firms.

In a sense, the same pressure that brought brokers to deal in speculative stocks seems to have created a camaraderie among brokers that transcends company lines.

There were about 100,000 stockbrokers before the 1987 crash. Of those, the Securities Industries Assn. estimates that 8% to 10% have lost their jobs. Those who have hung on have seen their compensation decline sharply over the past two years. One estimate is that brokers, on average, now earn only about 80% of what they did before the crash.

Brokers have resorted to hawking fixed-rate investments such as certificates of deposit and exotic investments with high commissions to clients who still want no part of stocks.

Newer brokers without an established client list have been doing even worse, with some probably wishing they were a Maytag repairman so they'd get a little more action.

"If it weren't for (trading in takeovers)," said another broker in the Chicago group, "my business would be down 60% instead of 40%."

"My clients like that kind of action," he added. "It's where the money is being made."

James Pascoe, a broker in the Darien, Conn., office of Janney Montgomery Scott, doesn't play speculative stocks but he does get involved in takeover deals that have been announced but not completed. That is arbitrage in its truest form. And Pascoe says a lot of brokers he knows are doing the same.

"If you can make 7% or 8% in a couple of months, it's a lot better than having money in the bank or in the market," says Pascoe, who says he's still nervous about the general market.

Small investors are also apparently still nervous from the crash, but there may be signs of hope for brokers. Stock prices have blossomed in May, thanks mainly to encouraging news on inflation. And even though the return of the small investor to the stock market has been incorrectly predicted numerous times before, this time, experts say, it really could occur.

Jeffrey Schaefer, senior vice president of the securities association, says that as late as last March 30 there was evidence of only a "very slight" growth in interest in stocks by small investors. "Only now are they getting more aggressive," says Schaefer.

William Coppel, vice president of marketing at the discount brokerage firm of Quick & Reilly, also thinks that the May rally has caught the attention of small investors. But, he says, "it's not dramatic."

If the stock market does continue to rise and if small investors are lured back into the market, then maybe those folks at the Brokers Inn can worry about the soup du jour, rather than the rumor du jour.

How did the financial market handle last week's attack of the killer economic figures? At first glance, not too badly. Inflation on the producer level rose just 0.4% in April, which wasn't nearly as bad as expected. And inflation on the consumer level, which rose 0.7%, wasn't any worse than Wall Street expected. And for good measure, the nation's trade deficit shrank $1 billion in March to $8.8 billion.

But the numbers weren't really anything to cheer about, either. The trade deficit fell because America's exports rose sharply, which means that the nation's economy may not be slowing as much as experts had hoped. This means that the pros could soon start worrying about inflation and higher interest rates all over again.

And inflation on the consumer level is up 5.1% from April, 1988, to April, 1989. "Year-over-year inflation numbers look pretty bad," says Edward Yardeni, chief economist at Prudential-Bache Securities.

What will happen to interest rates in the months ahead? Experts say the key is the dollar. The only way to get the greenback down -- as the United States seems to want -- may be to lower interest rates in America and raise them overseas.

Can a new line of copiers and some new printers save Xerox Corp.'s independence?

B. Alex Henderson, an analyst with Prudential-Bache Securities, believes that these new products, together with a cost-cutting effort begun by the company last year, may be all that stands between the company and an unwanted takeover attempt. If Xerox mishandles these products and doesn't improve its earnings, there could be trouble, says Henderson.

"If they manage to bungle this, the probability that someone goes after them is high," says Henderson.

Wall Street speculators, of course, always jump the gun. So for the past couple of weeks there have been rumors of an imminent bid for Xerox. The Stamford, Conn.-based technology company's stock has moved predictably higher, although its rise moderated when Hanson Trust -- one of the rumored suitors -- said in no uncertain terms that it wasn't planning any big acquisition at this time.

Still, the speculators aren't disheartened. They think that Xerox, with its numerous product lines, its servicing capabilities and its financial services subsidiary, can be easily dismembered by some enterprising raider.

Prudential-Bache's Henderson, meanwhile, thinks that Xerox's stock -- which was trading in the mid-$60s last week -- could reach $73 in six months and $82 in 12 to 18 months. And that's without any of the takeover premonitions coming true. In a takeover, Xerox could be worth $100 to $125 a share, Henderson estimates.

Wall Street may be getting more and more optimistic about a so-called "soft landing" for the United States economy, but economists are even more convinced that the landing will be hard -- with a recession in 1989.

Blue Chip Economic Indicators, which surveys the nation's top economists, says 21% of the experts it surveyed in May think that a recession will occur this year, and another 47% think that it will begin in 1990. That's a total of 68% who think that there will be a recession either this year or next.

In Blue Chip's January poll, 65% of the economists surveyed felt that there would be a recession this year or next, although the distribution was a bit different. Then, 35% of the economists thought that the recession would come in 1989 and 30% thought that it would start in 1990.

Occidental Petroleum's stock rose last Tuesday on a rumor that the company would announce a restructuring at this week's shareholder meeting. There was also a recurring rumor that Occidental's 87-year-old chairman Armand Hammer was ill. An Occidental Petroleum spokesman said there would be no major announcement this coming week, and he said Hammer was fine but volunteered to "stay in bed for a couple of days" if it'll help the stock price . . .

An analyst at Wertheim Schroder & Co. got people who are speculating in Time Inc. stock very excited last week when he told a little-known news service subscribed to by most Wall Street traders that a recent lawsuit against Time by Viacom could endanger "a leveraged buyout of Time (that was) recently proposed by a major investment banking house." But a Wall Street source says there is no such offer and that the analyst now has "egg on his face" . . .

CNW, of course, is a railroad, and Japonica Partners, a private company, is trying to acquire it. A review of the transaction by the ICC could slow things down quite a bit -- maybe for years . . .
