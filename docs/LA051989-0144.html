May 19, 1989, Friday, Orange County Edition

BREEDING BIRDS: RED-TAILED HAWK

RED-TAILED HAWK

(Buteo jamaicensis)

Description: About 22 inches long, with a 50-inch wingspan. Wings are broad and fairly rounded, plumage is variable. Reddish upper-tail, paler red under-tail; pale mottling on shoulders,

contrasting with dark mantle and often forming a "V" in perched birds.

Habitat: Woodland and open country with scattered trees; often seen perching on light poles.

Diet: More than 85% rodents; also amphibians, crayfish, fish and refuse.

Displays: Aerial display: pair spiral, recross, male usually circling behind and above female.

Nest: Bulky nest of sticks and twigs built in crotch of large tree with commanding view. Alternately uses several perennial nests.

Eggs: White to bluish white, spotted with brown or unmarked; about 2 1/2 inches long.

Natural history notes: The most common and widespread of the buteos (high-soaring hawks that are among the easiest daytime birds of prey to spot). While it shuns the deepest woodlands and most heavily-urbanized areas, it is a common suburban bird in the county. In addition to the local breeders, many migrating red-tails visit the county in spring and fall.
