BFN [Text] Washington, May 20 (XINHUA) -- U.S. President Bill Clinton and Indian Prime Minister P.V. Narasimha Rao today called for the creation of "a new partnership" between India and the United States. This came in a joint statement, released here today by the Indian Embassy, was issued at the end of the Indian Prime Minister Rao's visit in the United States. The two leaders promised to cooperate in the search for solutions to global challenges posed by weapons of mass destruction, AIDS, environmental degradation, population growth, poverty, international terrorism and narcotics trafficking, the statement said. Rao, who arrived in the United States on May 14, has met with President Clinton, Vice President Al Gore and acting Secretary of State Strobe Talbott in Washington, and wound up his visit yesterday. It was the first visit by an Indian Prime Minister to the United States since 1987. Agreement on the wording of the joint statement was reached Thursday [19 May] evening, hours after Clinton and Rao held a joint news conference at the White House. "The President and the prime minister agreed to expand the pace and scope of high-level exchange on the full range of political, economic, commercial, scientific, technological and social issues," the statement said. Clinton praised Prime Minister Rao's economic reforms and noted that the enthusiastic response of the business communities in both countries has produced record levels of bilateral trade and investment. Recognizing the potential to build a strong and positive economic relationship, both leaders agreed to work to remove impediments that exist to bilateral commerce. Seeking to demonstrate agreement on goals, the statement said that the two leaders offered their strong support for efforts towards the non-proliferation of nuclear weapons and their means of delivery. At the joint news conference yesterday, Clinton acknowledged the two nations have some difference on human rights and nuclear non-proliferation. The statement also said that Clinton and Rao agreed on the need for bilateral negotiations between India and Pakistan to resolve outstanding issues including Kashmir.
