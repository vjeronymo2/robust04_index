December 6, 1989, Wednesday, Home Edition

DR. JOYCE BROTHERS: SENSIBLE STEPS IN DISCIPLINE

How to discipline is a source of many family arguments. How much do you know about this important subject and how different styles affect children?

Here's a chance to test your views with those of some experts.

QUESTIONS:

1 -- It's not important for parents to agree on styles of discipline.

2 -- Children should never know that a parent is angry with them because it erodes their self-esteem and their sense of belonging.

3 -- There's no need for a parent to explain the reason for demanding certain behavior. A command in itself should be sufficient.

4 -- Children raised by traditional-style authoritarian parents are more likely to abuse drugs and alcohol as adults than children raised in other homes.

5 -- Authoritarian and authoritative parents discipline in the same way.

6 -- Basically, permissive parents are probably the best parents in terms of how their children develop.

ANSWERS:

1 -- FALSE. Consistency in discipline is important, and if a child discovers he can get away with something with one parent, but not with the other, or if their styles of discipline are totally different, the youngster will be confused.

2 -- FALSE. It's all right for children to know that a parent is angry. Anger is an acceptable part of love, but children need to learn constructive outlets for anger. Parents and children need to learn to talk about their feelings with each other.

3 -- FALSE. Parents who use the do-it-because-I-say-so discipline are authoritarian and their children don't learn how to think, analyze or make decisions on their own. They become followers rather than leaders.

4 -- FALSE. Studies conducted by Berkeley psychologist Diana Baumrind revealed that while children raised by authoritarian parents were immature, showed poor self-esteem and weren't motivated to do well in school, they had a relatively low level of alcohol and drug abuse.

5 -- FALSE. Authoritative parents take the time to explain the reasons for following certain commands, unlike authoritarian parents. They set limits of behavior, but they consider the child's point of view when setting limits. Children of authoritative parents, unlike children of permissive or authoritarian parents, are outstandingly well-adjusted, mature, happy, motivated, independent and socially adept.

6 -- FALSE. The 12-year longitudinal Berkeley study revealed that the children who were worse off were those raised by permissive parents. Like those reared by rigid authoritarian parents, they had low self-esteem.
