May 20, 1990, Sunday, Home Edition

COLUMN LEFT;

THE WAR OVER WHO KEEPS THE LOOT;

THIS NATION ISN'T BROKE; BUSH JUST WANTS THE MONEY TO STAY IN PRIVATE HANDS.

Saying that the United States is a rich country is like saying that Iceland is a cold one. But that statement made news down here in south Florida's gleaming montage of speedboats, speedy cars, glass towers, glossy people, and half the treasure, legal and illegal, of countries from Guatemala to Argentina.

The headlines were made by a Brookings Institution report restating the obvious: The U.S. economy is almost three times as big as any other in the world, and it is still growing and growing.

The report, calling for the universal solution of "more leadership," is really a book-length attack on what appears to be some of George Bush's zanier points-of-light thinking. The President likes to deflect calls for action or leadership by pleading national poverty. The United States, says our only President, has "more will than wallet."

Ridiculous, says Brookings, with righteous and accurate indignation. The report, called "Setting National Priorities," concludes: "The question is not whether America has enough wallet, but whether it has the will to use its abundant wealth to solve the problems it faces."

But Bush the bad-mouther is not zany at all. He's protecting the traditional power of rich people who control American culture and charity. What Bush is trying to create, with some success, is perceptions of public poverty. That, he and many other conservatives reckon, creates or adds to private wealth -- particularly for those whose wallets bulge with revenues from capital gains and trust funds. The "investing class" is Bush's core constituency.

Squinting through the day's news in Florida sunshine, you can see the skirmishes in the internal war over the distribution of growing wealth. In the Sun-Sentinel in Fort Lauderdale, there is a photograph of some of the 2,000 teachers and school officials demonstrating at the state capitol to protest a $300-million shortfall (their estimate) in funding to educate the 100,000 new students expected to enter Florida schools next year.

The minority leader of the state's House of Representatives, Dale Patchett, a Vero Beach Republican, was quoted in the Miami Herald, in response to calls for spending, as saying that there is no money. The only thing left to be taxed in Florida, he said, is "the time of day."

Good line. But among the things the state of Florida does not tax is income.

Bush's fundamental argument -- and Patchett's, too -- is that the power of America's fantastic wealth is better kept in the hands of the people than of the government. That's a pretty good argument, but it would be better if he was talking about all the people. In practice, most of that wealth and power -- like the revenue that would be raised by progressive income taxes -- is being transferred from the middle class to the trust-fund rich.

Another item in the news last week shows how it works. The Bass Foundation and the Philecology Trust contributed $20 million to establish an Institute of Biospheric Studies at Yale. Edward Bass, whose uncle was a fabulously successful oil man, is a 44-year-old Yale graduate who worries about the ecosphere and has used the power of his money to determine what research and study should be done at one of the country's great institutions.

Trusts and foundations -- basically ways to avoid income and inheritance taxes under American law -- give the rich control over the great range of American institutions between government and commerce. A few rich people exercise enormous power over universities and hospitals, theaters and symphony halls and museums -- whatever interests them. Bass happens to be interested in biospherics -- so Yale is, too. If he were interested in philately, Yale would be collecting stamps.

On balance over the years, such philanthropists have probably done a great deal more good than harm. But they did it their way. Other Americans need not apply when it comes to institutionalizing much of art and science in this great and wealthy and productive democracy.

George Bush, as President, is a diligent protector and proponent of his own kind. He is pretending that America is a poor country to protect the privileges and power of the better people -- at least the ones with the better wallets.
