BFN [Report by Marina Lebedeva and Vladimir Ardayev: "Human Rights in Conference Hall and Outside It"] [Text] Alma-Ata [Almaty] -- The International Conference on Human Rights, "Public Organizations and Power: Interaction in a Democratic Society," has ended in Alma-Ata. The discussion corresponded fully with the main theme of the conference. But the "thorniest" aspects inevitably concerned events unfolding nearby, outside the conference hall of the Alma-Ata sociopolitical center. Formally, following the adoption of the new Constitution and the reconstruction of the main legal base, practically no laws remain in Kazakhstan which directly violate human rights -- human rights must not be violated by law here. But this year's elections have shown the weakness and lack of coordination of the social associations and their vulnerability before the "party in power," something that CSCE observers emphasized, as well as the undeveloped state or almost complete absence of independent mass media in Kazakhstan. One example confirming the fairness of this analysis came up straight away on the first day of the conference's work. On the eve of its opening, 16 May, an unsanctioned rally of pickets, several of whom had declared a hunger strike, broke out on the square outside the building of the parliament, which had gathered for the latest stage of its first session. The militia drove the hunger strikers out of the square, and five of them were arrested. This was prompted by an actual parliament decision, which essentially outlawed not only obvious demonstrations, but also "other gatherings of citizens." The speech by V. Lyubushkin, a deputy from the North Kazakhstan Oblast Council [Maslikhat], devoted to the "Russian question," was a dramatic one. He cited "acts of intimidation to others," applied by the Kazakhstani authorities toward those who are trying to defend the rights of the Russian-speaking population, as a state policy distortion that is exacerbating this question. Thus, Boris Suprunyuk, chairman of the "Russian Community of North Kazakhstan" and a journalist, is under arrest. The conflictual situation arose during the work of the civil rights conference with the sociopolitical newspaper KAZAKHSTANSKAYA PRAVDA -- the most popular Russian-language publication in the Republic. Quite recently, the "Kazakhstan" television and radio broadcasting company was transformed into a corporation under the control of the authorities by being merged with a private television company -- and a presidential representative was appointed in it, in the person of press service leader I. Romanov. At IZVESTIYA's request, assessing the conference that has ended, Burkutbay Ayaganov, deputy chief of the internal policy section in the president's apparatus and the Kazakhstani Republic Cabinet of Ministers, who participated in it, called it "effective," and said it testified to democratic Kazakhstan's desire to become a rule-of-law state. When asked to comment on the cases of the violation of human rights which occurred in the past few days outside the conference hall, the deputy chief of section answered that he is not prepared to do so as he does not have sufficient information at his disposal.
