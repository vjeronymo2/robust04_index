BFN [Article by Kamil Bayalinov under the "Chinese Ceremonies" rubric: "Chinese People's Nuclear Tests Fall to the Lot of the Kyrgyz People"] [Text] The Kyrgyz Foreign Ministry has finally responded to this Chinese nuclear explosion with a "statement." The mild document, retouched by experienced diplomats, says that Kyrgyzstan..."welcomes the aspiration expressed by China for the complete destruction of and ban on nuclear weapons, as well as for the achievement of a universal ban on nuclear testing." The whole tone of the statement is couched in a spirit as if Kyrgyzstan were apologizing for its nuclear tests. However, at the end of the "memorandum," the diplomats got down to business: "In the space of not quite two years, China has carried out three nuclear tests. To us it is obvious that such tests have a negative effect on the environmental situation and the health of people in the region. Kyrgyzstan, as a state which borders directly on China, is experiencing justified and growing concern about this matter." Having carefully studied the document, the Chinese Embassy in Bishkek delivered its answer to Kyrgyz representatives. It reports that China unfailingly advocates "the complete destruction of and ban on nuclear weapons, and, in this context, the implementation of a universal ban on nuclear tests." But since China understands the concern of its neighbors perfectly well, then it is exerting every effort to conclude treaties on a universal ban on nuclear explosions.... Following such an answer, it evidently remains for the Kyrgyz to apologize for the trouble caused to the Chinese. The Chinese nuclear test range at Lop Nor is situated to the south of East Turkestan, which is now part of the Xinjiang Uygur Autonomous Region. The first explosion occurred in October 1964. By the late 1960's there had been a series of tests of hydrogen bombs. At first the test range occupied a modest area -- 56 km by 36 km -- but then it expanded considerably. The PRC nuclear research center is located here. Three operating sites for conducting explosions on land, underground, and in the air, are located on the territory of the test range. A test range for the study of the effect of radiation on animals and plants is situated near the city of Hotan. But the zone of the effect of radiation on people does not just include the entire Chinese autonomous area, but also one-half of Kyrgyzstan and part of Kazakhstan. According to figures from the "Nevada-Semipalatinsk" international antinuclear movement, after every hydrogen bomb test at the Lop Nor test range, the infant mortality rate in Alma-Ata [Almaty] and the area has gone up by 30-60 percent. It is interesting that at the time of the latest test at Lop Nor, east winds have been blowing over the test range (this is very rare in the area, incidentally), in a direction away from Central China and toward Kazakhstan and Kyrgyzstan. Following the nuclear explosion in May 1992, aerosol tests in Bishkek showed an increase in the activity of ruthenium and strontium radiation by a factor of 20. And after all, Bishkek is 1,000 km away from the test range! However, as far as radiation is concerned, there are no borders or distances. The Chernobyl tragedy is an example of this. In four months' time, the Lop Nor test range will be 30 years old. During this period, the Chinese military have conducted 22 surface and 15 underground tests of nuclear and thermonuclear weapons. Naturally, without informing either their own citizens or the governments of adjacent countries about it. The latest explosion resounded 10 June of this year. A powerful cyclone swept over northern Kyrgyzstan within several days. Information about an increase in the radiation level has not found its way into the press yet. The Kyrgyz leadership is adopting all possible measures to reduce anti-Chinese moods among the people, which are being intensified from one day to the next. And not only because of the nuclear tests being carried out by the mighty neighbor.
