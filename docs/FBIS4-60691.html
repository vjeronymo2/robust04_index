BFN [Text] Cairo, 26 May (MENA) -- Haydar Abu-Bakr al-'Attas, former Yemeni prime minister and 'Ali Salim al-Bid's envoy, left Cairo this evening after a one-day visit to Egypt. In a news conference held before he left, he paid tribute to President Husni Mubarak's efforts to end the fighting in Yemen. Mubarak's mediation is needed more now than ever before to save the Yemeni people from successive disasters, al-'Attas said. He stressed that the Yemeni people feel proud of and grateful to Egypt's president, government, and people. He praised the role played by the United Arab Emirates and Saudi Arabia to restore peace in Yemen. He said the recently formed government in Aden stresses its desire to stop the Yemeni war without any conditions and is willing for a direct dialogue with the northerners to find serious solutions to the crisis, ones that will take the interests of the people into consideration. The dialogue should be based on equality and frankness, and the north should not express any reservations about the participants, he said. Al-'Attas blamed Yemeni President 'Ali 'Abdallah Salih and the General People's Congress for aborting Yemeni unity. The dismissal of southern ministers and ambassadors means separation, and so does imposing unity by occupation, he said. Al-'Attas accused Sanaa of going back on the Pledge and Accord Document, which he said "enjoyed the support of all the Yemeni people." He said President Salih declared war against the Yemeni nation, took away freedoms, and exceeded his constitutional powers when he declared emergency rule and unilaterally abrogated all the minutes that preceded the proclamation of unity between the two parts of Yemen. He said the detention of a number of nationalists and the disappearance of known political leaders like 'Abd-al-Quddus al-Midwahi, Muhammad 'Abd-al-Mu'min, 'Abd-al-Habib Salim, and 'Ali Salih 'Ayyad strengthens the conviction that the authority in Sanaa is canceling its constitutional legitimacy. Mr. al-'Attas said Yemeni President 'Ali 'Abdallah Salih's decision to storm and loot homes of the leaders of the Yemeni Socialist Party and other parties and to use those leaders as human shields to protect his palace and his tribe from air raids indicates that his acts, including the war decision, were illegitimate. He said Yemen today is being subjected to a crushing war imposed by 'Ali 'Abdallah Salih and the elements allied with him, with military and financial support from Iraq and Sudan, to implement a joint scheme that Aden is determined to confront and foil. Asked about the possibility of restoring Yemeni unity if the battles stop, al-'Attas said in his news conference that such an action will not take the form of merger unity as in 1990. He explained that many issues need to be discussed before the restoration of unity, such as relations with Yemen and relations with Arab countries. Asked about the international efforts being made to arrange a cease-fire in Yemen, al-'Attas said that during his recent visit to Washington he sensed a sincere international desire led by the United States and the Arab countries to end the fighting. He blessed these efforts and noted Egypt's decision in the early sixties not to go to war against Syria when the latter decided to secede from the United Arab Republic. Al-'Attas ruled out the fall of Aden at the hands of the northern Yemeni forces. He stressed that Aden is a strong front that is difficult to occupy. If the aggression against Aden continues for long, he said, then "we will be compelled to request assistance from the fraternal countries." Al-'Attas said that irrespective of the results of the current battle in Yemen, it cannot determine the future of the country. He admitted that the northern forces made some gains and occupied some regions, but he described the military statements issued by Sanaa as exaggerated. He stressed that Sanaa supports extremists and fundamentalists to use them for political assassinations in Yemen. He pointed out that 'Ali 'Abdallah Salih has not yet signed a draft law on education because the Yemeni Reform Grouping, which is a religious party, is opposing it. Al-'Attas explained that the recently proclaimed Democratic Republic of Yemen in the south was meant to stop the bloodshed and to prevent Yemen from disintegrating into many mini-states. He said the new state does not need de jure international recognition. He said the government of this republic pledges to work with all national forces to strengthen the foundations of security and stability and to hold municipal elections within one year. It also commits itself to implementing all provisions of the Pledge and Accord Document. Al-'Attas said this republic is eager to establish strong relations with the Arabian Peninsula countries and with Egypt and Syria, and is now working to restore its right to membership in the Arab League, the Islamic Conference Organization, and the United Nations.
