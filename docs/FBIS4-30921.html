BFN [Text] Bratislava May 30 (CTK) -- Criticism of the Slovak Government by Miklos Duray, one of the Hungarian minority leaders, was today rejected by chairman of the Democratic Left Party (SDL) Deputies' Club, Milan Ftacnik. "(Ex-Premier Vladimir) Meciar and Duray need each other," Ftacnik said, commenting on the Egyutteles chairman's accusation this weekend that Jozef Moravcik's government was not fulfilling its promises towards the Hungarian parties. The SDL is part of the coalition government. Ftacnik denied the "endless speculations about the existence of secret agreements" with Hungarian parties which the present cabinet had allegedly concluded in return for a promise of support from these parties. The undertakings which the Moravcik government made when it arose were part of its manifesto, and were being fulfilled, Ftacnik said. Proof of this was the passing of the law on personal names last week by the Slovak Parliament (which allows Hungarians to register their names in the Hungarian form and not to use the ending -ova for womens' surnames) and the fact that on Wednesday [1 June] the "signpost law" on the bilingual names of towns and villages is to be passed. Other issues which the coalition government had promised to solve by the end of its temporary term were being worked on, Ftacnik said. However, at the weekend Duray had accused the government of hesitation over the promised approval of the European charter of local autonomies and minority languages. Ftacnik countered today that the government had only undertaken to create conditions for the future implementation of these norms. "The agreement did not read 'approve' but 'create conditions for'. It is unreasonable to tie a future government to models with which it would not necessarily agree," he said. On another demand of the Hungarian minority leaders, that a law be passed on the transfer of property with no known restituent into the ownership of the community, Ftacnik commented that it was "at first sight sympathetic and democratic," but that there could be no debate on it without the consequences of such a law first being analysed.
