BFN [Text] La Paz, 22 Jun (LOS TIEMPOS) -- The metaphor about the "milk and the cow" was thoroughly explained today by President Gonzalo Sanchez de Lozada, when he said that even if Bolivia gets a sovereign sea outlet -- it wants to take concrete steps on the sea issue with Chile -- it must also get several outlets on both the Pacific and Atlantic, with the full use of the port of Ilo in Peru as well as the Paraguay-Parana waterway and other ports in Argentina and Uruguay. "Even though the sea outlet claim is a valid issue, though problematic and difficult, we want to make progress in all fields -- cultural, political, and economic -- in all fields related to our great effort to have a sea outlet on the Pacific and outlets through the waterway," said President Sanchez at a news conference held today to talk about the positive aspects of his tour of the United States and Colombia. The controversial statement made by President Sanchez de Lozada after meeting with Chilean President Eduardo Frei prompted a wave of protests for an allegedly antipatriotic policy on the sea outlet issue. This policy was denied today by President Sanchez de Lozada, who said that the talk with President Frei was from any point of view very positive, because the sea outlet issue will be debated during the Rio Group summit next September. "We want to see concrete things, concrete steps to improve the Bolivian situation," said Sanchez de Lozada, who pointed out the work being carried out by Peruvian President Alberto Fujimori, whom, he said, was carrying out "concrete" work allowing greater binational contacts, one proof of which is the highway linking the port of Ilo with the Bolivian border. The explanation given by President Sanchez de Lozada on the metaphor he used after meeting with President Frei was mainly related to the fact that Bolivia has to use other "outlets" to the Pacific and Atlantic, and these would be the port of Ilo in Peru and the Paraguay-Parana waterway, because Bolivia is entering a stage of economic and productive expansion for which it is necessary to seek ports for exports. Sanchez de Lozada said: "Even if Bolivia gets a sovereign sea outlet -- and this is our great goal and struggle, which will not change in this government or in any other -- we must also find other ports that will not be Bolivian, but Chilean, Peruvian, and on the waterway of the River Plate Basin. In its projection abroad Bolivia must show that by negotiating with our neighbors we can get benefits without having direct ownership of those ports and their installations." In this regard, President Sanchez de Lozada said there is a feeling of solidarity in the neighboring countries (Peru, Argentina, Paraguay, and Uruguay) which makes him believe that Chile will also enter a new stage of relations for mutual benefit. Therefore, said Sanchez de Lozada, it is hoped that for the September meeting the Bolivian and Chilean Foreign Ministries will prepare a work agenda that will consider the issues dividing the two countries under a new spirit of friendly relations.
