Language: English Article Type:BFN [From the "Diplomatic Panorama" feature: Reports by diplomatic correspondents Dmitriy Voskoboynikov, Igor Porshnev, Vitaliy Trubetskoy, and others] [Text] The U.S. Embassy in Moscow has no information as to the whereabouts and plans of ex-President Richard Nixon who came to the Russian capital for a visit. As a high-ranking American diplomat told INTERFAX, the embassy does not monitor private visits. The ex-president came to Russia in a purely personal capacity. He has no contacts with the embassy; he has not informed it of his movements or further plans, the diplomat said. As INTERFAX learned from the travel agency Connect servicing Nixon's visit, the ex-president will stay in Moscow till about March 16th. According to a member of the agency directly concerned with the high-ranking guest, his arrival in Moscow is in no way connected with Big Politics or business. Richard Nixon simply likes to visit Russia, meet with Russian people and to see the changes in the country. His present trip to Moscow is neither the first nor the last, he said. The North-American Department of Russia's Foreign Ministry told INTERFAX that the ministry has nothing to do with Nixon's visit to Moscow. "The Russian Foreign Ministry would have monitored the ex-president's visit, had the American embassy been in charge of it" a high-ranking Russian diplomat told INTERFAX's correspondent. According to him, the visit is of "a purely scientific nature". "Nixon's ties with the Carnegie Foundation and with the Fund for Democracy are well known. Evidently the former American president is on a visit in Moscow in connection with these organizations," he said. Another Russian diplomat interviewed by Interfax's correspondent has a different point of view. "As a retired politician Nixon may allow himself what officials from the Clinton Administration cannot afford. He has already conferred with the Communist leader, Gennadiy Zyuganov, and with the former Vice-President, Aleksandr Rutskoy. Nixon makes no secret of his intention to meet with the Liberal Democratic Party leader, Vladimir Zhirinovskiy. All this reflects a striving to "sound out the situation," if not "build bridges," and to clarify what the Russian opposition is like, and whether it is capable of coming to power eventually," he said.
