November 6, 1990, Tuesday, P.M. Final

PEOPLE'S PHARMACY: ANTIBIOTICS, BISMUTH FOUND HELPFUL IN TREATING ULCERS

Question: I have been taking Tagamet for a couple of years to treat ulcers. Unfortunately, when I stop the medicine, my stomach soon starts to hurt and the ulcer returns.

I remember reading something in your column about a new approach to curing ulcers. My doctor is skeptical. But if there is really a way to cure ulcers, I'd rather be done with them once and for all than take Tagamet for the rest of my life.

Answer: A controversy over the causes of stomach ulcers has raged for several years. Many gastroenterologists doubted that ulcers could be infectious. However, a consensus is growing that many ulcers are due to bacteria.

At the World Congress of Gastroenterology, Helicobacter pylori was recognized as a major cause of ulcers. Treating with acid-suppressing drugs such as antacids, Tagamet (cimetidine), or Zantac (ranitidine) can help heal the lesion, but ulcers return in up to 80% of patients when the medicine is discontinued.

Treatment with antibiotics and bismuth (Pepto-Bismol) appears to be much more successful in eliminating the bug. Dr. Barry Marshall of the University of Virginia, quoted in Medical World News, says, "We think we have a cure for this bacterium and thus a cure for ulcer disease in probably 90% of patients."

Q: I used to take tryptophan to help me get to sleep, especially when I traveled. Ever since the ban I have had to rely on non-prescription drugs, which I don't like as well.

I am getting fed up with the government. It seems like a long time has gone by, and I haven't heard anything about the cause of the tryptophan problem. Will tryptophan become available again?

A: We too are disappointed that the investigation of this problem has gone so slowly. Over a year ago the first reports of a rare blood disease called eosinophilia myalgia syndrome (EMS) came to the FDA's attention. People developed fatigue, fever and severe muscle pain, and some died.

The outbreak was traced to l-tryptophan. Investigators have linked EMS to a new manufacturing process developed by one Japanese supplier, and the company has recently identified the component associated with the epidemic.

We don't understand why the FDA has not kept the public better informed about its progress in this investigation. If contamination from a single source was the cause of the problem, it seems reasonable to expect that eventually tryptophan could be sold to the public again.
