Language: English Article Type:BFN [Sidharth Kanengo commentary] [Text] Ties between India and Uzbekistan, which date back to ancient years, [break in reception lasting several seconds] the visit of the Uzbek president, Mr. Islam A. Karimov. The visit, resulting in a series of agreements and memorandum of understanding between both the countries, besides high-level deliberations, is bound to take their bilateral relations to new heights in the new year. The Uzbekistan, the land of the builders of India's historic Taj Mahal, offers a very lucrative mosaic of opportunities to Indian entrepreneurs, businessmen, and industrialists. It is time for Indian businessmen to give up inhibitions and invest in this former Soviet republic known for gas deposits, coal reserves, non-ferrous metals and gold. Now pursuing the path of a free market economy, Uzbekistan is witnessing considerable foreign investment flow. The changed global scenario definitely demands Indian businessmen to break the barrier and tap the available opportunities in the land where people also love Indian films and adore Raj Kapoor, the doyen of Indian cinema. As Mr. Karimov put it after his arrival in New Delhi on Monday, he would not like Indian businessmen to be left behind. Besides strengthening cooperation in economic areas, the two countries can launch a joint offensive against fundamentalism and extremism with India playing a greater role in Central Asian region in political fields as well. During his meeting with the Indian president, Dr. Shankar Dayal Sharma, the Uzbek president said that the disintegration of the Soviet Union had created a vacuum in Central Asian region. India has a special relationship with the countries of the region when Europeans and other countries are to get a foothold. [sentence as heard] The agreements and memorandums of understanding now being entered between the two countries involve cooperation in economic and other areas, including telecommunications, science and technology, culture, and transport and transit. The Uzbek president earlier held formal talks with the Indian prime minister, Mr. P.V. Narasimha Rao, and discussed bilateral issues with several Indian ministers. India had recently extended a credit of $10 million for setting up projects with Indian know-how and entrepreneurial participation in Uzbekistan. Projects worth $7.8 billion [as heard] for Uzbekistan have also been approved in addition. Uzbek personnel are being trained in India in disciplines such as diplomacy, banking, management and mass communication. In the area of joint ventures, the two countries are also producing electronic and brass wares. India's (Thapar Group) is also to set up a cotton yarn plant in Uzbekistan. Close interaction between the top leaders of both the countries has been on ever since the Central Asian Republics attained independence two years ago. While New Delhi was the first city that Uzbek president, Mr. Karimov, visited outside the Commonwealth of Independent States in 1991 after his country decided to break away from the former USSR, Indian Prime Minister Mr. P.V. Narasimha Rao reciprocated the gesture in May last year when he chose Uzbekistan as his first destination within the CIS as prime minister. Only last year, five agreements on cooperation in science and technology, tourism, avoidance of double taxation and cooperation between the foreign offices of the two countries were signed during the visit of the Uzbek deputy prime minister, Mr. (T.M. Miryakubov). Mr. Karimov, accompanied by a delegation from Uzbekistan, including the foreign minister and chairman of board of dealing with trade like industry, economic relations, civil aviation and tourism met leading Indian industrialists and entrepreneurs to explore possible areas of cooperation. This visit is likely to open a new era in India-Uzbekistan links with this Central Asian country creating conditions for mutually beneficial cooperation and encouraging ties with foreign companies, firms and financial institutions.
