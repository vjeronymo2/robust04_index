March 29, 1990, Thursday, Home Edition

FOOD AND COMMON SENSE: SUCCESS AT PHYSICAL FITNESS IS NOT LIMITED TO THOSE WITH IDEAL BODIES

In America today, two visions seem to be emerging simultaneously. One is of sleek, muscled bodies in expensive jogging shoes, colorful workout leotards or revealing swimsuits. Svelte yet strong, this contingent sips diet sodas or light beers, shuns fat and cholesterol and opts for slenderizing salads and bean-sprout sandwiches in between trips to the health club.

On the other side, we've got the queen-size, comfortable look, fast-food fries, crispy cooked chicken, burgers with everything and doughnuts baked fresh each morning. This group varies from the "plump-is-prettier, I-accept-myself-the-way-I-am" stance to the "I'll kill myself if I don't lose 40 pounds by next Wednesday, I'm going on a starvation diet now" approach.

What we're witnessing is partly encouraging, partly disturbing. In America today, 34 million of us are overweight. Of that group, 13 million are seriously obese. By contrast, only 30% of the population exercises regularly, and only some at a level that promotes cardiovascular fitness.

But in TV and print advertising, the image of the physically fit, healthy, happy individual is consistently held out as the ideal. Success is portrayed as a combination of youthful physical prowess, self-confidence and affluence.

In life, there is always a conflict between reality and perfection. Naturally, we think good health and physical fitness are worthy goals toward which we all should strive. It's healthier not to be overweight, and it feels good to be physically active. What's more, it's a sign of civilization's progress that the old view of women -- as weak creatures in need of protection -- is being replaced by a vision of strength and independence.

TV or magazine ads that show people jogging or lifting weights may spur some people into action. That's all to the good. Where we might run into trouble is when such a presentation of the small group of Americans who are at the pinnacle of fitness becomes not an inspiration but a source of depression to the others.

If you're a good 75 pounds overweight, seeing an ad for a slender celebrity working out at the spa could make you feel worse than ever. You may say to yourself, "I can't afford to join a health club, and even if I exercised for a million years I'd never look like a famous movie star, so why bother?" And then there's your hand, reaching for the doughnut box again.

Our message is a simple one: Set your own goals. In their desire to sell products, the media and the industries they represent create images we can never match in our own lives. But that doesn't mean we should give up and sink into sedentary "flabdom."

You don't have to aim for the body of celebrated muscle man or Wonder Woman to be healthy and strong. In other words, you don't have to try to re-make yourself to fit somebody else's vision of perfection -- a goal you probably can't achieve anyway. That might just mean setting yourself up for failure and frustration.

Think realistically, not on a grand scale. Ask yourself, "What can I do today to maintain or improve my health?" How about a 20-minute walk? Or a trip to the supermarket to buy fresh fruits and vegetables. Forgo the submarine sandwich at lunch and take something lighter; go to the store and price exercise machines; check a cookbook for a new, interesting low-fat recipe. Find out where in your neighborhood you can swim regularly; use stairs instead of elevators at work; go for an after-dinner stroll with your family instead of (or at least before) plunking down by the TV.

These suggestions add up to a general approach that can make a difference. You're likely to find that some of the changes turn out to be fun, not burdensome, as long as you're following your own agenda rather than forcing yourself into a prefabricated model.

The point is to think about what your own life is like and what your preferences are, and to incorporate into that a desire to be a healthier, more active person. The Greeks devised the concept of the "golden mean," which has remained valid throughout the span of centuries. The muscular actor or actress in the TV commercial whose body is sculpted to perfection and who seems to own the world may have about as much to do with body type and life as does the man in the moon.

But if that vision isn't attainable, the alternative doesn't have to be resignation and a size-large pizza. There is a place in between spending umpteen hours a day at a health club and growing so totally out of shape that you get winded running for the telephone. You need to find that place -- your own personal golden mean -- and use it to work toward good health and good times.

Slight Differences for Pink, White Grapefruit

Question: Are there any nutritional differences between pink grapefruit and white grapefruit?

Answer: The real difference between the two is that pink or red grapefruit contains some carotene, which the body converts to Vitamin A. Color is the key to how much it provides.

For example, figures derived from an average of pink and red fruits grown in California, Arizona and Florida indicate that half a grapefruit will provide, on average, about 6% of the U.S. Recommended Daily Allowance for Vitamin A. However, values for red grapefruit from Texas are more than twice that.

Beyond that, grapefruit are nutritionally similar. For a small grapefruit weighing about a pound, half will have approximately 40 calories. It also contains some iron and B vitamins. And it is an excellent source of Vitamin C; a small grapefruit usually provides 75% of the day's needs.

Q: I bought two bottles of olive oil several months ago. When I went to use the second one, I noticed a milky residue in the bottom. Does this mean it's unsafe?

A: No it doesn't. The fatty acids in olive oil are mainly monounsaturated. However, it also contains varying amounts of saturated fatty acids, which tend to be solid at room temperature. The milky residue you describe is made up of crystals of saturated fatty acids that have settled to the bottom of the bottle. You would observe the same phenomenon, perhaps even more extensively, if you stored the oil in the refrigerator, since cooler temperatures speed the process.

That doesn't mean you should avoid refrigeration. While olive oil can keep up to two years if tightly sealed and stored away from heat and light, it is sometimes recommended that to prevent it from becoming rancid, it should be stored in the refrigerator when the weather is hot and humid. As it comes to room temperature, or with slight warming, it will turn clear once again.

Q: We live in an area where there are many fine restaurants. Yet for anyone interested in a low-fat, health-conscious menu, eating out is very difficult. We've spoken to one young chef who says that if he had guidelines, he would add a low-fat selection to the menu. Can you give us directions in helping him?

A: Across the country, both fine restaurants serving elegant fare and those catering to more everyday needs increasingly are offering low-fat menus designed to please the palate and, at the same time, taste as good as richer dishes.

The basis guidelines are the same as those used by millions of Americans in their homes. They begin with heavy reliance on ingredients that are low in fat and light on salt, and then preparing them by methods that do not require adding much salt or fat, particularly saturated fat. And, of course, for flavor they should rely on the freshness of the food and on herbs and spices, rather than a heavy hand with the salt shaker.

For example, well-seasoned vegetables purees often replace rich cream sauces for chicken. Alternatively, a tart fruit sauce is used for lightly sauteed fish fillets or poultry. Pasta dishes with simple but appealing sauces prepared from several varieties of mushrooms carefully seasoned with herbs and perhaps a little smoked salmon are another choice. In place of (or in addition to) cream-based soups, restaurants are offering soups that make use of good fish stock, hot chile seasonings or the wonderful flavor of fresh vegetables.

Fresh fruit, attractively served, should be a menu mainstay, with some sorbet for those who want it, and perhaps cookies prepared with unsaturated fat. Some people are concerned about fat and sodium but not worried about their weight. For that audience, the chef might prepare elegant desserts, such as cakes prepared mainly with oil (not butter) and served with fruit slices.

To help the young chef get started, we have several basic suggestions. First, he might begin by searching among his repertoire for recipes that lend themselves to adaptation. Second, he should consider working with a registered dietitian who could not only offer specific guidance on modifying his recipes, but who could provide him with nutrient analyses with respect to fat, saturated fat and sodium. He can find one through his state's dietetic association.

We also recommend that the chef clearly identify those special dishes on this menu, and provide information about calories, fat and sodium to his customers. Experience suggest that all this is good for business.
