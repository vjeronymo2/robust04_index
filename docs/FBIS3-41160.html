Language: English Article Type:CSO [Text] Witnesses in drug cases in Trinidad and Tobago are so easily bought off that the Ministry of National Security is putting focus on the seizure of drugs outside of Trinidad and Tobago as a deliberate policy. This was stated by National Security Minister Russell Huggins in the Senate yesterday. Noting that there was criticism that the big drug busts were not taking place in Trinidad and Tobago, but outside, Huggins said the seizing of drugs outside of Trinidad was "an intentional act." "Witnesses (in Trinidad and Tobago) are being bought off as easy as it is to buy a soft drink in a parlour. We start a case, we have four state witnesses and by the time the matter comes up, you can't find any of them. They are in the United States living it up," he said. He explained that as a result of this the Ministry had taken a particular approach to the busting of drug deals. "When we find out about it (we arrange) to have it seized outside of Trinidad and not inside," he said, adding: "We will work with agencies outside of Trinidad and Tobago and I can tell you that it was largely as a result of our effort that a drug link between this country and Venezuela was destroyed recently." Huggins noted that one of the main perpetrators, a Trinidadian received a 10-year jail sentence within eight days of his arrest. "Now that is the kind of swift justice I will like to see here. But we have skilled lawyers who know how to delay justice when it suits them," he added. The Minister also referred to a recent bust "as far as Holland" in which both the narcotic units here and the OSS (Organisation of Strategic Services) played an important role in the discovery of shipment of drugs on board a British Airways craft leaving Piarco. Huggins noted that the task of preventing drugs from coming into Trinidad and Tobago was far more difficult. Those who import cocaine have a lot of opportunity to do so, he said, adding that the south-eastern coast, given the present size of our Coast Guard, was impossible to patrol on a full-time basis. He recalled that only recently the Coast Guard had acquired boats with the capability to chase the small fast boats used by drug traffickers. Turning to the Point Lisas Port, he noted that it was virtually impossible to search 1,000 containers. "The only way you can do it is by random checks and a container may slip by and the government and the security at Point Lisas are blamed," he noted. He said that while Point Lisas had put in a very good system to deal with that, it was virtually impossible to completely eradicate the problem. He stressed that good intelligence in detecting the drugs was still the best option and urged citizens to assist the authorities in this regard. Huggins said the U.S. Government had provided govenrment with US$303,000; the Canadian Government $1 million, the French Government had given technical assistance as did the Royal Canadian Mounted Police. But, he stressed, foreign assistance was extremely difficult to access. "Although public pronouncements are made that some governments are providing assistance, because of the bureaucracy involved, that assistance is extremely difficult to get our hands on," he said. He said on regional basis--Caribbean Financial Action Task Force--was established to deal with the problem of money laundering.
