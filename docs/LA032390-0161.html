March 23, 1990, Friday, Orange County Edition

HER EXPERIENCE WITH A STEPMOTHER IS NOTHING LIKE CINDERELLA'S

Not all stepfamilies have to struggle as they work toward love and unity. My own experience was happy from the beginning.

I'm sure that my natural mother loved me, and I her, even though I don't remember her very well. I was 8 and my sister 12 when our mother died.

But we didn't have the mother-daughter closeness apparent in some families. I don't remember listening to bedtime stories while being tucked gently beneath the covers or while cuddling on her lap, her cool hands stroking my hair.

When my father announced his plans to remarry a number of months later, I was surprised but not unhappy.

I still remember where we were and where we had eaten dinner, how the gold-flecked diamonds on the seats of my dad's truck felt under my hand as he told us.

Not having spent much time with my natural mother, I was ready to welcome a new mother who would be home when I went to bed at night and got up in the morning.

I don't remember too much from the first few months in our revised family. Some things do stand out -- getting over the awkwardness of calling a new woman "Mom," the Madame Alexander doll with her yellow dotted dress she brought me, and adjusting to a new schedule that, for the first time, included regular bedtime hours and church.

And all the new rules!

Having been used to spending a lot of time on our own, my sister and I weren't used to someone's telling us what to wear, what we could watch on TV and what to eat.

No more mornings of Pop Tarts and Cap'n Crunch. We were now awakened to a mother who served us oatmeal and wheat toast. The days of wearing shorts and flip-flops to school were definitely over. Mom made me some outfits that I hated to wear, and I didn't tell her because I didn't want to hurt her feelings. They were so out of style! Especially the green pantsuit with the gold trim! Junior high, as we all know, is a place where you have to conform or die of embarrassment.

All in all, though, changing a few habits and styles wasn't too much to ask of us, and it didn't take me long to adjust to having a new mother.

I'll never forget the time we were making chocolate chip cookies late one Saturday morning. In my eagerness to taste the batter, I leaned over just a little too far and got my hair tangled in the beaters.

She and sister were sure it was the funniest thing they'd ever seen. They made me stand, glaring, for a picture with my gooey hair twisted around the beater.

And the time our dog, Nemo, was killed by a car during a lightning storm. When I came home from school, Mom pulled me onto her lap to comfort me. I was crying uncontrollably. She was completely at a loss.

"I'll do anything, anything, if you'll just stop crying," she said.

"Don't . . . ," I sobbed, stuttering, "make me wear . . . that green pantsuit anymore. . . ."

Poor Nemo was temporarily forgotten. How we laugh about that now!

My sister had a tougher time adjusting than I did, but not really because of the stepmother situation. It was just that hard time of maturing, leaving childhood behind for the new challenges of being a teen-ager.

Looking back, not only did we gain a new mother, we also got a new sister (already married by that time) and lots of other wonderful new relatives.

I cherish this new family situation. It seems foreign to think of my mother and sister as stepmother and stepsister. They're simply family.

It was wonderful, and still is, to have a mother who cared for me as for her own. What makes a family, after all, is not merely blood ties.

To me, a real mother is the woman who brings a child up with care, love and respect -- regardless of whether she brought him or her into the world.
