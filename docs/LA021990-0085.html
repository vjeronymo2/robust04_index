February 19, 1990, Monday, Home Edition

DEALS / ALLAN SLOAN: MARKET WATCH;

THERE ARE PLENTY MORE STILL TRAPPED IN ROACH MOTEL

Just because the world didn't end in a puff of smoke last week when Drexel Burnham Lambert went belly-up, you shouldn't make the mistake of thinking that things have settled down in the junk bond market. Far from it. There is plenty of carnage yet to come. The apparent calm is deceiving.

The absence of a collapse wasn't because the market calmly absorbed the bad news about Drexel, which invented the modern junk bond market and was then consumed by its own creation. The reason the market didn't blow apart is that regulators and junk bond holders were trying to keep the market rising by the power of talk alone -- and because there weren't very many actual transactions taking place.

To understand why things in the junk market are actually worse than they look, you have to understand that the junk bond market isn't a real market. At one point, when Drexel Burnham and Michael Milken were flying high, parts of the market were probably real. But that was a long time ago.

In a real market, you can buy or sell sizable blocks of securities at something close to the quoted prices. In today's junk market, you can buy as much as you want at the quoted prices, or even lower. But except for a few issues -- such as some of the bonds issued in the RJR Nabisco leveraged buyout last year -- it's virtually impossible to sell significant numbers of bonds without driving the price way down. And even then, it's iffy.

Think of the junk market as a financial Roach Motel: You can check in, but you can't check out. Why has this happened? It's a chicken-and-egg problem. When people have faith in the junk market, the market functions, and the fact that it functions reinforces the faith. When people lose faith -- which has been the case for months -- the market doesn't function. When the market doesn't function, no one has faith.

Junk boomed in the early 1980s because Drexel Burnam Lambert, under the inspiration of Milken, the now-deposed junk bond king, brought new believers into the tent. Before Milken, who in his prime was a marvelous analyst and possibly the world's best salesman, junk bonds were shunned as being too risky and too tacky for most investors.

But as the gospel according to Milken gained adherents, normally conservative investors -- pension funds, traditional insurance firms, mutual fund buyers, even the occasional bank trust department -- began putting money into these strange, complicated securities. Because the supply of junk buyers expanded more quickly than Milken and his imitators at other brokerages could spew out more junk, the prices of these securities kept rising. While the occasional bond issuer might crater, a mania set in and everyone just had to put junk bonds into his or her portfolio or be laughed out of the bars where money managers go to drown their sorrows. Unlike the stock market, where brokers match sellers and buyers, the bond market is one in which the broker typically buys the bonds from you and resells them to someone else.

To make this go smoothly, brokerage houses had to carry very large inventories of bonds. They didn't mind doing this, even though they had to borrow billions of dollars on a short-term basis to carry the inventory.

The beauty of this, for the brokers, was that the interest rates on the bonds they carried in their inventory was higher than the interest rates that the brokers paid on their short-term borrowings. What's more, since junk prices were heading upward, the value of their inventory rose. A double profit. Pretty neat.

But what happens when junk prices begin to head downward? Funny you should ask.

When bonds are heading down, no one dares to hold a lot of them in inventory. Even if you are making a spread of four points a year by carrying Texas Air notes yielding 15% a year with borrowed money that's costing you 11%, you can get killed in an eye blink if the bonds fall five or 10 points in a day, which is the kind of thing that's been happening. Because there's not much inventory at the brokers, the market gets choppy. When the market gets choppy, everyone wants to sell -- but there's no one to sell to. Just as the cycle feeds on itself going up, it feeds on itself going down.

The most prominent victim of this downward death spiral is Drexel itself. One of the major reasons the firm went busto is that the value of its bond inventory rotted out and nervous lenders cut off credit. Even though Drexel tried to protect itself by cutting down its inventory, it got killed anyway.

The current collapse of junk prices was started last summer when Integrated Resources, one of Drexel's stranger creatures, announced out of the blue that it was defaulting on its debts. It seemed only fitting that Drexel and Integrated -- the creator and the creation -- filed Chapter 11 bankruptcy petitions the same day.

The fall of Drexel and Integrated is part of a historical process. The junk bond explosion of the 1980s is being replaced by the junk bond implosion of the 1990s, as the market collapses in on itself. Unless you are a professional willing to spend a lot of time and take a lot of risk, you will do well to take your money off the table and go home. Some junk bonds may, in fact, be so cheap that they're bargains now. But if history is a guide, they'll be a lot cheaper a year from now.
