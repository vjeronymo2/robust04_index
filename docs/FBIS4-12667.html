BFN [Text] The U.S. Deputy secretary of state, Mr. Strobe talbott, had a meeting in Islamabad with Prime Minister Ms. Benazir Bhutto during which they discussed matters of mutual interest to both the countries. Earlier, Mr. Talbott discussed bilateral matters, including Kashmir and nuclear nonproliferation, with Foreign Minister Sardar Asif Ahmad Ali. He also held talks with officials in Islamabad during which they reviewed entire spectrum of bilateral relations. Briefing newsmen about the talks, a Foreign Office spokesman said the two countries have agreed to the concept of multilateral talks on South Asian security and to hold consultations with each other in order to develop an appropriate framework. The spokesman said that the president and the prime minister reiterated Pakistan's desire for continued close and cooperative relations with the United States in the post-cold war period. The spokesman said during the talks consideration was given to the two nonpapers presented by the United States to Pakistan. These dealt with the proposal for multilateral talks on South Asian security, proposing a verifiable capping of the production of fissile material by Pakistan in the context of removing the impediments in the supply of defense materials. Consistent with its well-known position, Pakistan expressed support for the objectives of promoting nonproliferation regionally and globally. The two sides agreed to proceed with further consideration of this initiative with a view to evolving an agreed and equitable approach. Replying to a question, the spokesman said Pakistan made it clear that it would not accept any unequal and inequitable obligation as far as nonproliferation is concerned. Pakistan would not accept anything which places it at an disadvantageous position politically, militarily, and security-wise. It was ready to accept any nondiscriminatory and equitable proposal for this purpose vis-a-vis India, but not unilateral capping of its nuclear program and it remained our position even today. To another question, the spokesman emphatically stated that Pakistan is opposed to the deployment of ballistic missiles in South Asia. He said Mr. Talbott reiterated the desire of the United States to maintain the long-standing friendly and cooperative relationship with Pakistan. He informed the president and the prime minister that Pakistan was today as important as it was during the cold war era, both regionally and globally and it had a crucial role in the region. The spokesman in reply to another question said Pakistan's stand on Kashmir remains the same that the dispute should be resolved in accordance with the United Nations resolution that calls for plebiscite in Jammu and Kashmir. The United States, the spokesman said, has expressed its willingness to assist in the solution of the dispute if this was agreeable to both the parties. Addressing a news conference in Islamabad this afternoon, the U.S. deputy secretary of state underscored strong commitment of his country of maintaining good friendly relations with Pakistan. He said the two sides also agreed on the elimination of nuclear weapons from the entire South Asian region, especially through talks in a multinational forum. He emphasized that the American proposal for nuclear nonproliferation in South Asia qualifies to be even-handed and has a regional and trans-regional approach. Mr. Talbott said President Clinton and Secretary of State Warren Christopher had asked him to convey this commitment to Pakistani leaders that the United States was determined to champion and broaden this relationship further. He said his talks with the president, the prime minister, and the foreign minister covered supply of F-16's aircraft, capping of nuclear program, and other regional and global issues. He said his dialogue with Pakistan will continue to advance the goals of nonproliferation and to end arms race in the region. Mr. Talbott said they are aware that Pakistan Government is not going to accept any initiative unless the government and the people are convinced that the American initiatives serve the security interests of Pakistan. He said the United States is not asking for a roll back of the nuclear program, but is suggesting a verifiable capping. About the supply of F-16 aircraft to Pakistan, he said further discussion on this will take place.
