Language: English Article Type:BFN [By Peter Chen] [Text] Brisbane, March 16 (CNA) -- Australia and the Republic of China [ROC] are scheduled to hold talks in Melbourne later this month to discuss expansion of the current air links between the two countries, the Taipei Economic and Cultural Office (TECO) in Canberra said Wednesday [16 March]. TECO Representative Francias Li said the ROC delegation would be led by Sun Chao-liang, director-general of the Civil Aeronautics Administration, and his Australian counterpart would be Australia's representative in Taiwan. Officials from the Australian Transport Department, Australian Asia Airlines (AAA), Eva Air and Mandarin Airways will take part in the two-day meetings on March 24 and 25. Li said the ROC delegates are expected to arrive in Melbourne on March 23. "The purpose of the meeting is to discuss bilateral expansion of the current air links between our two countries," he said. He said the issue was first raised by the officials of AAA, a subsidiary of Qantas Airways, last year. Australia signed an aviation agreement with the ROC in early 1991. AAA inaugurated its flights to Taipei from Sydney and Brisbane on October 11 the same year. Mandarin Airways followed suit a week later with direct flights between Taipei and Sydney. Eva air started flying to Sydney and Brisbane from Taipei in September 1993. Under the 1991 agreement, airlines from the two countries are allowed to provide four weekly flight between Taipei and Sydney and Brisbane. Additional flights on this route have to be approved first by either Taipei or Canberra. AAA has maximized its services with four weekly flights while Mandarin and Eva provide two weekly flights each. But a faster-than-expected tourist flow from Taiwan to Australia last year has called for more flights and the airlines operating the route are very profitable, airlines officials said. Eva Air has been forced to offer an extra flight to meet growing demand, company representative in Sydney Peter Kwoh said in an interview. "All airlines operating between Taipei and Sydney and Brisbane are making profits thanks to a surge in the number of tourists from Taiwan," he said. The number of tourist arrivals from Taiwan going to Australia hit a record high of nearly 109,000 last year, up from 63,500 in 1992, according to the Australian Tourist Commission (ATC). Tourists from Australia going to Taiwan totalled more than 20,000, up only slightly from a year earlier. "The Taipei route has been known as the golden route for many years," Kwoh said, adding "because more and more affluent Taiwan residents like to travel overseas." "They are big spenders in Australia," an ATC official said. China Airlines, a parent company of Mandarin, said this month that its subsidiary would expand its current Taipei-Sydney flights to Brisbane later this year. "I believe all the airlines operating between the ROC and Australia will cash in on the growing tourist flow to share a bigger market pie," TECO's Li said.
