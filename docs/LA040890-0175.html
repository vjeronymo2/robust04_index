April 8, 1990, Sunday, Home Edition

THE BUTCHER: USDA PROVIDES DIFFERENT TECHNIQUES FOR CURING, DRYING, COOKING MEATS

DEAR BUTCHER: Your recipe for beef jerky, which appeared at least a year ago and which I have made often, produces excellent jerky. However, at some point in my Food Guide readings, I came across a discussion of "safe cooking temperatures" and so called the United States Department of Agriculture to ask them about the slow drying at 150-degrees method. To my dismay, they did not recommend cooking any meat at lower than 325 and further to cook the meat to an internal temperature of 160.

-- A READER

DEAR READER: Our government takes such very good care of us. If we listened to and adhered to all government recommendations, I'm quite sure that death would be optional, 160 degrees, indeed. I'll have my steak rare and 130 degrees, thank you.

Next time you call the USDA, try to talk to a food scientist about curing and drying, not cooking. Curing and drying are two of the oldest methods of food preservation known to man, and they have served us well. Enjoy your jerky.

For more good sound scientific information on the subject, I recommend: "A Complete Guide to Home Meat Curing" available from Morton Salt Co., P.O. Box 355, Argo, Ill. 60501. The price is $1 plus 25 cents, shipping and handling. Also recommended: "How to Dry Foods" by Deanna DeLong (HP Books).

-- MERLE

DEAR BUTCHER: This nutrition business certainly is out of hand and a pain in the neck. I am a retired old lady in my mid-'80s and terribly tired of hearing about cholesterol and what foods are good for you or terribly bad for you.

I remember how in years long ago conversation at the dinner table with regard to food was about how good it looked and tasted and good recipes, etc. Those days are gone.

-- A READER

DEAR READER: I hope not. The tide already shows signs of swinging back to some of the nutritional common sense of yesteryear. More and more these days I am hearing scientists and nutritionists, dietitians and such recommend pretty much the same thing Grandma used to: a well-balanced diet from the four basic food groups; a little moderation, and "get off your duff and go do the chores, the exercise will do you good."

The whole cholesterol issue is being hotly debated in scientific circles these days and one would hope that out of all the debate will come some truth and perhaps even some common sense. In the meantime, be grateful that there is still some conversation around your dinner table.

The latest for the younger set, for whom even fast food isn't fast enough, is the computerized ordering system now in place at several Arby's Roast Beef Shops. Just step right up to the computer and punch in your order. Faced with a barrage of complains about slow service, Arby's installed the system for customers who no longer have time to spend.

-- MERLE

DEAR BUTCHER: When serving my family stuffed cabbage, the meat is always a bright pink. I suspect this is caused by some color additive in the ground meat. Am I right?

-- A READER

DEAR READER: The cause of the pink color is nitrate, but it's not something added to the meat. Rather, it's the naturally occurring nitrate in the cabbage. Nitrate is added to meat products like ham and hot dogs and other sausage products to prevent botulism, which is why those products have a pink color. But green leaf vegetables also contain nitrates. According to Dr. George York, a good friend and one of the leading food scientists in the country, cabbage has 50 to 100 parts-per-million nitrates, more than most hot dogs and more than enough to turn the meat in a cabbage roll pink. If you would like an even pinker color, wrap your meat in spinach that contains 1,500 parts per million.

-- MERLE

DEAR MERLE: Why do I see so much seafood, pork ribs, beef ribs, etc., market with bright tags saying "Thawed, Previously Frozen"? Why in heaven's name don't they leave them frozen, omit the thawing?

-- A READER

DEAR READER: I don't know, but I suspect the reason markets thaw frozen meats is very likely because people won't buy them in the frozen state. And government regulations demand that we be told if the product has been frozen. If enough folks like you would ask for frozen seafood, pork and beef ribs, etc., you can bet markets would stop thawing them.

-- MERLE
