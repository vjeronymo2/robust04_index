920114 FT 14 JAN 92 / Ford workers warned that productivity must rise WORKERS at Ford's Fiesta production plant at Dagenham have been told that productivity must improve sharply if the plant is to be sure of having a role in building the present model's successor. Ford denied last night that it had issued an ultimatum to the workforce but acknowledged that it had set targets to improve performance. The company said that if those were not met, the 'ultimate sanction is that the business can be lost'. According to a confidential internal Ford memo the 7,000 workers employed on Fiesta production at Dagenham required 52.2 hours to build a car in 1990. That compares with 33.3 at Ford's Valencia plant in Spain and only 29.9 hours at its Cologne plant in Germany. Dagenham's figure for 1991 was forecast to improve to 40 hours a car, compared with marginal improvements at Valencia and Cologne. However, it is understood to have achieved only about 46 hours a car, resulting in the performance targets for this year in a series of briefings to employees by Mr Albert Caspers, Ford's vice-president of manufacturing. The company maintains that although wage rates are lower than in the continental plants, Ford's UK labour and overhead costs are substantially higher. Ford's present Fiesta model is only two years old and is unlikely to be replaced before the mid 1990s. Nevertheless, it has been indicated to Dagenham employees that fundamental decisions about how production of the replacement will be distributed are likely to be taken within the next 12 months. In spite of the productivity gap Ford has been relatively complimentary about the improvements achieved at the plant since Sierra production was withdrawn and it began producing only Fiestas two years ago. In 1989, for example, it took Dagenham 59.4 hours a car. The improvement has been sufficient for Ford to have resumed significant exports to the Continent. About half Dagenham's output of 223,000 Fiestas was exported last year.
