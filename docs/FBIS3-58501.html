Language: French Article Type:BFN [Second of two-part "exclusive" interview with UN Secretary General Butrus Butrus-Ghali by Franz-Olivier Giesbert, Jacques Jacquet-Francillon, and Charles Lambroschini; place and date not given: "Butrus-Ghali Denounces Bad Payers"] [Text] [LE FIGARO] What are you most proud of since you took over the post of secretary general of the United Nations two years ago? [Butrus-Ghali] We were successful in Cambodia and in El Salvador. The fact that we could offer aid to millions of Somalis is also a success. There is no longer famine in Somalia. This is to the credit of the United Nations. We are also helping two million Yugoslavian refugees. Two million is nothing to sneeze at. We brought 360,000 refugees back to Cambodia. That is something. We successfully kept the peace in Mozambique, where we obtained major results. Elections are to be held on 27 April in South Africa. You might say: "You had nothing to do with that," but that is simply not true. We contributed a lot to this operation by sending observers there. We are going to send another 1,700 in the coming weeks. These are positive elements that you can see on the ground. [LE FIGARO] Very well. So what was your big failure? [Butrus-Ghali] The thing I am least proud about is the fact that I have not managed to attract greater attention -- in the philosophic sense of the word -- from the member states as to the importance of the United Nations. The United Nations is a tool which they still hesitate to use. They say all the right things, and they make a lot of promises, but when the time comes to face up to reality, they hesitate.... [LE FIGARO] Out of all the UN member states, how many do not pay their dues? [Butrus-Ghali] At the end of last year, only 60 out of 184 had paid their contribution to the ordinary budget. [LE FIGARO] And the preceding years too? [Butrus-Ghali] It was even worse. [LE FIGARO] What about the peacekeeping operations? [Butrus-Ghali] We must owe about $1.2 billion out of a total of $3.6 billion. [LE FIGARO] Are there any bad payers on the Security Council? [Butrus-Ghali] Certainly, the United States and Russia. The United States is late, and Russia does not pay. This means that the good pupils, the ones who send us troops, are punished indirectly because we cannot pay them what we owe them for their contribution to the peacekeeping forces. [LE FIGARO] You cannot reimburse France? [Butrus-Ghali] We reimburse France with a delay of one year. We reimburse Sweden with a delay of two years. I have received complaints from Tunisia, Egypt, Uruguay, and Jordan, all of whom want to withdraw their troops. Just yesterday morning, I received telegrams from two countries which have not received their contribution to the operations in Cambodia that ended last September. As a result, these two countries -- Tunisia and Bangladesh -- are refusing to send additional troops to Somalia. We are going to end up with insurmountable problems on our hands because of this financial crisis. [LE FIGARO] One of the reforms of the UN Charter, which you mention often during your trips around the world, is the reform of the composition of the Security Council. This would essentially be a new allocation of permanent seats. How do you see things? How many new permanent members do you envisage? How would the veto right be allocated differently? [Butrus-Ghali] First of all, this is a decision which, in accordance with Article 108 of the Charter, must be made by the General Assembly. I play no role in it. [LE FIGARO] Really? [Butrus-Ghali] Following the initiative taken by the General Assembly, I sent a questionnaire to all the member states, to which I have received already some 70 replies. A consensus has emerged from these replies in favor of increasing the number of members on the Security Council, since when the United Nations was founded, it consisted of only 50 states, whereas it now has 184. It has also been emphasized that the expansion of the Security Council must be limited so that it can continue to operate well.

General Relaxation
[Butrus-Ghali continues] There is also emphasis on the fact that we should move quickly in order to take advantage of the celebration of the United Nations' 50th anniversary in 1995, and to carry out the reform. However, that is where the consensus ends. So we risk opening a Pandora's box, in that some 30 member states feel -- rightly or not -- that they deserve a permanent seat on the Security Council. From there we can indulge in academic exercises: We could envisage new permanent members which do not have the veto right, etc. [LE FIGARO] The same names always come up: Germany and Japan. In order to have the rest of the world better represented, are there any other countries, like India, Brazil, Argentina, Nigeria, etc.? [Butrus-Ghali] I do not want to make any comment regarding any of these countries. I do not wish to create problems for myself. [LE FIGARO] Surely India is pretty inevitable? [Butrus-Ghali] Everything depends on the vote, you know. There will be a political debate in the General Assembly, whose members are going to vote. However, do not forget that in the final analysis, we will still have to have the agreement of the five permanent members. Any revision of the Charter will only be valid after it has been ratified by the five permanent members of the Security Council -- all of whom have the veto right -- even if the resolution has been passed by a national assembly in which there is no veto right. [LE FIGARO] So...? [Butrus-Ghali] To my mind, what counts is not so much the reform, but the fact that via this reform I can attain greater participation by the most important member states in the role of the United Nations. [LE FIGARO] So now we are coming back to your initial remarks. [Butrus-Ghali] Yes, the lack of political will on the part of the large countries. [LE FIGARO] The lack of leadership...American leadership. [Butrus-Ghali] No, I do not agree with you on that. American leadership used to be counterbalanced by another leadership. We had a bilateral system, bipolarism. Once one of the leaderships disappeared and collapsed, we emerged into a system which we cannot control. Nobody knows what has to be done anymore. The fact is that the very big member states are not up to their importance. Whether in Latin America or Asia, member states are not playing the role they should be playing -- a role they could afford to play if they had the political will to do so. [LE FIGARO] In more general terms, do you mean that all the nations are less interested today than before in the affairs of the rest of the world? That everyone is withdrawing into their own problems? [Butrus-Ghali] Yes, I repeat: There is a kind of laxness. [LE FIGARO] How do you explain it? [Butrus-Ghali] The end of the Cold War created a general relaxation. We are confronted with new phenomena. [LE FIGARO] With a confirmed political will and a clearer vision of what we could have done, do you think that we could have stopped the escalation of hostilities in Yugoslavia by intervening just after the first confrontations between Croats and Serbs? [Butrus-Ghali] It is very difficult to answer that question. A political will must not be expressed just at the beginning. It must be constant. That is the true lesson. The problem of peace is like the problem of health. You cannot allow yourself to go on a diet for two weeks. If you stop, you will get sick again. There must be continuity in peacekeeping operations: before, during the conflict, and afterwards. You must not stop checking up on the patient. There must be permanent attention. It is like cleaning the streets of Paris.
