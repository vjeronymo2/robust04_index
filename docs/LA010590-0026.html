January 5, 1990, Friday, Home Edition

JUROR FAIRNESS WOULD BE ON TRIAL

Now that we have him, can we give him a fair trial? That's the question that lawyers around the country are asking on the heels of Manuel Noriega's dramatic surrender to American authorities.

There are no easy answers, because there is really no precedent for a jury trial of a deposed dictator charged with drug crimes.

Never before in American history has a jury of 12 ordinary Americans been asked to sit in judgment on a major foreign policy goal of a President. Brave soldiers died in order to bring Noriega to justice. Can we really expect jurors to decide that these soldiers died in vain if they conclude that Noriega is probably guilty but there is a reasonable doubt?

In previous situations where American foreign policy has demanded the conviction of deposed tyrants -- for example, at the post-World War II war criminal tribunals -- the cases were submitted to judges or to courts-martial, rather than jurors. The same was true of the trial of the murderers of President Abraham Lincoln at the close of the Civil War. It is expecting far too much from ordinary jurors to insist that they maintain a presumption of innocence when the President has put the prestige of our nation behind the claim that Noriega is a common criminal.

Noriega has the option of waiving a jury trial and asking for a judge, but he has the constitutional right to demand a jury trial that is fair. The Noriega case poses a challenging test for the American judicial system. Juries work best in small cases involving obscure defendants. They do not work well in highly publicized cases involving notorious defendants. And they are at their worst when their patriotism is pitted against the rights of the defendant.

Judges, who are given the protection of life tenure, are supposed to assure the independence of the judiciary and the separation of powers. The federal judge who presides over the Noriega case will have more than the fate of Manuel Noriega in his hands. The integrity of the American system of justice will be on trial in that Florida courtroom.
