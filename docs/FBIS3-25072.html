Language: French Article Type:BFN [Text] Following his inauguration last week, the new president of Burundi, Cyprien Ntaryamira, has just appointed his prime minister. He is Anatole Kanyenkiko, former minister of public works and equipment in the government of President Ndadaye who was assassinated last October. Kanyenkiko, who is married and father of three children, comes from the Tutsi ethnic group and is a member of the main opposition party, Unity and National Progress Party [Uprona]. He hails from Ngozi Province, northern Burundi. Before becoming minister of public works and equipment he was mayor of Bujumbura. Mr. Kanyenkiko replaces Mrs. Sylvie Kinigi, also a member of Uprona. However, it was not an easy task to make the appointment. Here is Celcius Nsengiyunva, reporting from Bujumbura: [Begin recording] [words indistinct] lasted 48 hours and finally at 1300, Radio Burundi announced the appointment of Anatole Kanyenkiko as the new prime minister. The choice of Mr. Kanyenkiko was not an easy one. The opposition did not make things easy for President Cyprien Ntaryamira. In the beginning, it was agreed that the president of the Republic would choose the prime minister after consultation with all political organizations and that the prime minister would come from the opposition party in parliament, that is Uprona, the former single ruling party. While in the government of Sylvie Kinigi, Anatole Kanyenkiko consistently stressed the fact that he was from Uprona party but after realizing that the new president was going to choose Mr. Kanyenkiko, the opposition [word indistinct] that Kanyenkiko had worked with the party in power and claimed that there had been a violation of the agreements and that there was a need to renegotiate terms. Once again, politicians met this morning at a meeting chaired by Bishop Bududira and with the UN envoy, the Mauritanian Ahmedou Ould Abdallah, as an observer. Anatole Kanyenkiko replaces Sylvie Kinigi and must form a government as soon as possible in which the opposition will take up 40 percent of seats while the ruling Front for Democracy in Burundi and its supporters will take up 60 percent.
