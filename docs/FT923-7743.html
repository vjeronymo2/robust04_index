920815 FT 15 AUG 92 / How To Spend It: Edinburgh's festival of bargains - Take a break from the arts and go shopping. Liz Joseph suggests a tour of the best Scotland's capital has to offer EVEN WITH its reputation for sound money Edinburgh actively discourages thrift in August with a range of attractive 'products' to tempt Festival shoppers. For traders, this is the pivotal point of the financial year - the make or break month. So, like the hundreds of thespians flooding into the city for the Festival and Fringe, the stores have polished up their acts. The city's shops are looking at their best and you could find that you and your money are easily seduced as that grey, douce old lady Edinburgh rouges her cheeks and hoiks up her skirts for one the world's greatest arts festivals. The discerning shopper will first visit Jenners, the oldest independent department store in the world, and then find that it pays to go off the beaten track into the many interesting by-ways. From the West End with its bustling clutter of tiny shops to the sadly rundown Royal Mile with its untidy litter of street traders, Edinburgh shows many faces. Explore the gorgeous Georgian New Town and visit Stockbridge on its northern Bohemian fringes, where so many shops specialise in the old, and then climb the Mound to the Old Town and the Grassmarket, where the new rubs shoulders with the antique. Begin in the New Town at Jenners, 48 Princes Street (031-225-2442), which has authentic tartans, cashmeres and lambswool, high fashion knitwear, as well as fine glass and china. Visit the splendid food hall which boasts Scottish shortbreads, preserves and mustards and, until September 7, A Taste of Scotland promotion offering the opportunity to taste some of the country's best foods and wines. Then head for the West End. William Street and neighbouring Stafford Street are renowned for their small delicatessens and wee shops specialising in everything from superb teas and coffees and designer labels fashions, to fresh fish and inexpensive toys, to chic little art galleries and restaurants. At the Better Beverage Company, 43 William Street (031-226-6617) David Halbert sells freshly roasted coffee from five continents, as well as a pot-pourri of teas and treats such as chocolate-coated coffee beans and teapots and coffee makers. Flying Colours Gallery, 35 William Street (031-335-6776) is Jane Houldsworth's lively gallery deals in work by contemporary Scottish artists, such as exquisite watercolours of Auld Reekie by Caroline Buchanan (until August 29) and Jane Rutherford's colourful gouaches and etchings of the Brazilian rainforest (September 2-30). Belinda Robertson Cashmere, 22 Palmerston Place (031-225-1057) creates covetable cashmere. Robertson drapes, folds and accessorises her cashmere designs into smart day wear and sophisticated evening wear. Cardigans cost around Pounds 185; matching leggings, Pounds 95; and a cashmere cape, Pounds 300; discounted styles from Pounds 35 are often available. Further east lie Thistle Street, with its antique shops and fashion clothes and shoe shops, and the elegance of George Street, where there are several fine art galleries. The Scottish Gallery, 94 George Street (031-225-5955) celebrates its 150th anniversary with an exhibition which includes work by Sam Bough, Orchardson, McTaggart, Crawhall, Peploe, Fergusson, and Bellany. The crafts gallery offers ceramics and wood, and jewellery by designers such as Cynthia Cousens, whose metalwork designs are currently on show. Across St Andrew Square at 15 North St Andrew Street is one of Edinburgh's oldest shops. Cunningham & Co (031-556-5142), the hatter, was founded in 1817. Here you will find trilbies, bowlers, caps, panamas, a bespoke tailoring service from a wide range of cloths including cashmere and Kinloch Anderson tartans, and scarves and ties. As you make your way down into Stockbridge visit Howe Street with its interior design shops selling Russian imports, tiles, fine linens, and, at InHouse (No 28, 031-225-2888), furniture and lighting, as well as Charles Rennie Mackintosh-style chairs and the Phillipe Starck toothbrush. The Kimono Company, 3 Carlton Street (031 332 1519), has antique kimonos, haori jackets and happi coats in silk or cotton. Prices range from around Pounds 25 to around Pounds 100. Montresor, 35 St Stephen Street (031-220-6877), is an Aladdin's cave of a shop. It houses a dazzling display of art deco and art nouveau costume jewellery, antique lighting, paste, cut glass and Bakelite designs by Chanel, Schiaparelli, Dior, Givenchy and Norman Hartnell. Galerie Mirages, 46a Raeburn Place (031-220-6877) has a huge selection of woven and embroidered textiles, old silks, kilims, wall hangings, antique furniture and boxes from Afghanistan, Indonesia, Nepal and Egypt. Until September 19 there is a selling exhibition of arts from Java, Lombok and Bali, including masks, puppets, musical instruments and antique carvings, as well as tribal jewellery and snake chains from India, Puntek beads from Burma, traditional Afghan jewellery, amber pieces from Arie Vardi and Shula Shek's intricate collection of silverwork. Prices range from Pounds 2.50 to Pounds 300. North West Circus Place offers antique shops where prices compare favourably with London. But the dedicated bargain-hunter will find many an art deco, and other, gem can be uncovered at the festival's Grassmarket Fair, 'behind' the Castle. The fair is on August 15, 22 and 29, from 10am to 4.30pm. Worth a visit while you are in the vicinity is Mallinson's old curiosity shop, whose mutton-chop whiskered proprietor is a noted source of coins, military bric-a-brac, old beer bottles and other eclectic delights, arranged in no particular order. Azteca, which has branches at 5 The Grassmarket (031-229-9368) and 16 Victoria Street, sells a range of Mexican folk crafts, including hand-blown glass, Peruvian and Mexican jewellery, Guatemalan textiles and clothes, and a superb collection of rustic Mexican furniture, intricately carved with traditional motifs. Telephone for details of mail order catalogues. The Arch Leather Company, 88 Grassmarket (031-220-6474) is all that is black and silver and desperately groovy. Recycled leather jackets lined with old scarves, from Pounds 99 to Pounds 275; suede shift dresses, Pounds 108; Fiorelli fun handbags, from less than Pounds 16; and Hi-Tek's amazing inside-out watches and silver jewellery. Robert Cresser, 40 Victoria Street (031-225-2181) is a brush maker bristling with ideas. Here you can buy everything from a tiny shoe brush or a natural bristle hand-made nailbrush for Pounds 2.90 to a three-foot-wide tennis court brush for Pounds 38. A D Mackenzie, 26 Victoria Street (031-220-0089) stocks leather bags, cases and portmanteau, all manufactured by Alan MacKenzie in his Isle of Arran workshop. Briefcases, Pounds 139-Pounds 189; Gladstone bags and soft luggage, Pounds 220-Pounds 305. Round the World, 82 West Bow (031-225-7086) sells home and personal accessories collected by owner Mike Gordon on his globe-trotting trips. Here you can find everything from a handkerchief to a shower curtain to wrapping paper (90p) to key rings, all bearing maps of the world. The latest accessory for the trendy is the slim-line Charles Rennie Mackintosh-style Italian CD rack, free-standing or to hang on your wall in black, chrome or silver finish, from Pounds 39.50. If you have the money and the energy left, then in the Royal Mile you will find super tartan silk blouses and skirts, dresses, jackets and tunics at Betty Davies Scottish Fashion International (51 High Street, 031-556-7518); also at 51 George Street for tailored styles and romantic evening wear; bespoke kilt outfitters, Hector Russell, 137/141 High Street (031-558-1254, also at 95 Princes Street and 509 Law Market); whiskies and smoked salmon at Royal Mile Whiskies, 379 High Street (031-225-3383); and playing cards, from a Chinese deck at Pounds 1.50 to a French 'antique' pack at Pounds 4.80 at R Somerville, 82 Canongate (031-556-5225).
