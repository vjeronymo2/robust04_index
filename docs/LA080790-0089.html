August 7, 1990, Tuesday, Home Edition

MORE DEFENSE INDUSTRY LAYOFFS IN THE WORKS;

AEROSPACE: GENERAL DYNAMICS WILL LET UP TO 2,000 WORKERS GO. IT ALSO PLANS TO CONSOLIDATE UNITS IN POMONA AND RANCHO CUCAMONGA.

General Dynamics said Monday that it will lay off 1,500 to 2,000 workers in the Los Angeles area by the end of 1991 and consolidate two divisions in Pomona and Rancho Cucamonga.

The St. Louis-based defense giant blamed the cutbacks on declining defense budgets and on the negative effects of a broad Pentagon procurement policy to seek second production sources for weapons.

Ralph Hawes, the firm's executive vice president, said Monday that the company may lay off 600 to 1,000 workers by the end of this year but has made no decision about closing any of its missile plants.

The firm's Valley Systems division in Rancho Cucamonga employs 3,500, and its Pomona division employs 5,700, including 1,600 out of state. Both divisions produce tactical missiles. The Pomona division already has laid off 560 workers this year and is down from a peak of 8,000 three years ago.

"It is very clear that there is going to be less business out there," Hawes said in a telephone interview Monday. "It is really beginning to hit home."

The aerospace downturn overtaking General Dynamics is expected to last at least through the mid-1990s, by which point the California economy would have lost an estimated 110,000 direct defense jobs, according to economic forecasters DRI-McGraw Hill.

In the past year, virtually every major prime contractor in Southern California has been forced to cut back employment, either because of a drop in business or because it wants to flee to lower-cost areas of the nation.

Hughes Aircraft eliminated more than 9,000 jobs last year. Lockheed may cut as many as 8,000 jobs over the next few years. McDonnell Douglas is slashing 8,000 jobs this year. Northrop is trimming 3,000 jobs this year. Only TRW and Rockwell International have yet to order sizable layoffs.

General Dynamics is the nation's second-largest defense contractor and has 103,000 employees at facilities stretching from a submarine shipyard in the Boston area to a rocket plant in San Diego.

Even though Pomona and Rancho Cucamonga are among the firm's smaller operations, they have accounted for about half of the corporation's layoffs this year.

The reason for the disproportionate effect is that tactical missile programs are being sharply cut back by the Pentagon, even as it seeks to preserve the $62.8 billion B-2 Stealth bomber program and other higher cost weapons.

In addition, the General Dynamics operations have been severely hurt by a broad Pentagon policy in recent years to seek second production sources for its weapons. Every major program at the two divisions was slated for second sourcing, Hawes noted.

The Navy selected Raytheon to compete on the Standard missile and General Electric to compete on the Phalanx shipboard gun. The Army selected Raytheon to compete on the Army Stinger missile.

Now, both Raytheon and General Electric are behind schedule in learning how to build those systems and have yet to be "certified" for production, even though the Pentagon is continuing to award contracts to the firms, Hawes charged.

"They are both substantially late in getting qualified," he said.

Moreover, even though the Pentagon has a new policy of curtailing second source competitions, it can not terminate the General Dynamics competitions until the second sources have been qualified so they can compete for a final buyout of production, Hawes charged.

"From a business point of view, you have to question the sanity of it," Hawes said.

As a result, Hawes said, General Dynamics is now attempting to cut its costs so it can eventually win the future competitions, assuming the competition learns how to build the products.

General Dynamics said about 600 of the 1,500 to 2,000 due to be eliminated will come from reducing duplication once the divisions are combined.

When General Dynamics decided to open the Rancho Cucamonga operation in 1985 in the heyday of the Reagan Administration defense buildup, it was to relieve the growth at the Pomona operation.

But the Pentagon decision to seek second sources left General Dynamics with excess factory capacity, since it would have to share production. In Phalanx guns, for example, General Dynamics tooled up for production of 10 systems per month, but under dual sourcing production is likely to be two or three per month.

It is not clear how the company will eliminate surplus factory space. Though Hawes expressed the sort of dissatisfaction with the California business climate that is now commonplace in the industry, he ruled out a total closure of the California facilities.

"I don't have any immediate plans for a wholesale move out of California, but I would certainly have to say that the trend is to move out of California on a long-term basis," Hawes said.

For one thing, the firm has invested tremendously in its Pomona plant over the years. In addition, it is partly owned by the Navy, which helps lower costs. The Rancho Cucamonga plant is owned by General Dynamics. The two divisions also operate plants in Camden, Ark., and on a Navajo Indian Reservation. Hawes said the company recently opened a small plant in the Imperial Valley, where wages are lower, to see if it can continue to do business in California at all. So far, 100 workers are located at the facility.
