April 28, 1990, Saturday, Home Edition

BLACK WRITERS OF SOAPS

I take exception to George Blaine Jr.'s April 7 letter concerning finding black writers for the NBC soap "Generations." Blaine seems to assume this should be easy.

"Call the Writers Guild," he suggests.

That's the problem. Call the Writers Guild and they will tell you -- there are no black writers in daytime TV.

When "Generations" became the first soap to establish a central black family, head writer Sally Sussman felt it was essential to include black writers on her staff. Finding them wasn't easy. Even experienced writers have trouble adapting to soaps. It's unlike writing for anything else, and it's relentless. Writers turn out five scripts a week -- 52 weeks a year. The scripts are 90% dialogue, and they require working from another writer's outline -- a major adjustment for any writer.

"Generations" is lucky enough to have two terrific black writers. Hopefully there will be more.

Most importantly, the group at NBC Productions has made every effort to provide jobs for blacks in every field. Just this year, under the aegis of the Writers Guild of America, "Generations" established a minority writers program to create more opportunities for black writers, addressing just this problem. And this month NBC, in association with the National Negro College Fund, instituted the "Generations" Scholarship, for young blacks interested in pursuing a career in the arts.

MEG BENNETT

Writer, "Generations"

Los Angeles
