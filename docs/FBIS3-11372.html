Language: Spanish Article Type:BFN [Text] Havana, 15 Mar (PL) -- An unexpected summit of the Anglo-Caribbean community Caricom [Caribbean Common Market] held this weekend clearly established the Antillean countries' hope of joining the North American Free Trade Agreement (NAFTA), either as a bloc or separately. The meeting, held on Saturday and Sunday in Kingston, capital of St. Vincent and the Grenadines, and attended by 13 Caricom heads of state, focused its attention on ways to join the Canadian, U.S., and Mexican trade agreement. For more than a year, Caricom has studied possible ways of joining NAFTA and also ways to keep the old Caribbean Basin Initiative (CBI) privileges sponsored by former President Ronald Reagan's administration in the 1980's. CBI accorded certain Caribbean and Central American products tax free access to the U.S. market. The 12-year deadline stipulated by the CBI for its implementation, however, is about to end and the benefits are threatened by the competition of similar products manufactured in Mexico, a NAFTA partner. The Kingston summit reached two conclusions. First, to officially ask U.S. Trade representative Mickey Kantor to report on Caricom's ability to join NAFTA, and second, to give Trinidad and Tobago and Jamaica the green light to negotiate their independent admission to the NAFTA. The latter conclusion, which seems to be the result of an aggressive campaign by U.S. Ambassador in Trinidad Sally Cowall, somehow contradicts the group spirit which has prevailed in Caricom negotiations with the world since its creation in 1973. The regional integration organization, although criticized for the slow implementation of its financial and commercial decisions, still maintains an unfailing unity among its members, and it has been the strength behind the Association of Caribbean States (CSA), whose constitution will begin to be drafted on Wednesday in Jamaica by a group of experts from the 38 potential members. The special summit, just two days before the Kingston meeting, seems to indicate there are still unexplored areas in Caricom's plans for a mechanism that will tie their fragile insular economies to those of other more developed Caribbean countries, like Cuba, and the so-called medium Latin American powers like Mexico, Venezuela, and Colombia. The Caribbean people are truly facing a situation akin to panic given the danger of losing CBI benefits; Latin American banana producers' questions concerning their advantages -- earned through their colonial heritage -- in the British market; and the real threat of NAFTA, which will significantly undermine access of their products to the United States. Nevertheless, history has shown the spirit of integration which prevails among the small Anglo-Antillean islands is deeply rooted and their leaders' unyielding resistance to Washington's pressure to exclude Cuba from the future CSA proves it. The future organization, designed to coordinate intergovernment cooperation in trade, culture, education, and ecology, and to sponsor a future market among its members, should include 38 countries from the Caribbean insular, basin, and coastal areas with different degrees of development and political tendencies. Stages of the project include preparing the constitution, which will be handled by experts gathered in Jamaica between 16 and 18 March; the foreign ministers approving it while in Mexico; and the heads of state and government, who will formally comprise the CSA, signing it at the Caricom summit in July in Barbados. Either way, NAFTA looms over any trade negotiation started by the CSA because it involves one of the member states -- Mexico -- and it significantly affects the other members. Whether Caricom manages to join NAFTA as a group or Jamaica and Trinidad forge ahead on their own is still being studied, and that decision will surely set the prevailing trend in future trade negotiations between the CSA and the United States, which always prefers to negotiate bilaterally with the Caribbean people given the saying, "divide and conquer."
