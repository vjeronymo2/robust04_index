Language: Bulgarian Article Type:BFN [Neven Kopandanova commentary: "BSP Has Monopoly"] [Text] On 21 February Zhan Videnov said that in addition to meeting the visiting Russian parliamentarians as Bulgarian Socialist parliamentarians, the two delegations also had unofficial contacts at the party level. Apparently next week there will be a meeting of Russia's leftist parties in which the Bulgarian Socialist Party [BSP] will take part. On the Russian side, the meeting will be attended by the Socialist Labor Party, Rybkin's Agrarian Party of Russia [APR], and the "Free Russia" People's Party [as published]. Our Socialists hope that other CIS countries will also participate in the meeting. The Socialists' active contacts with congenial parties in Serbia, Greece, and Russia, where those parties rule, could hardly be reduced to attempts on the part of the Bulgarian Socialists to overcome the difficulties along the road to Europe. There is a present danger that the BSP could monopolize a part of our foreign policy. This evokes an even greater concern against the background of the different views of the political parties in our country in anything related to foreign policy elements. It is true that National Assembly Chairman and the Movement for Rights and Freedoms [DPS] also received official invitations to visit Russia. On 21 February deputies of the Union of Democratic Forces [SDS] said that they also have been invited, but for the time being they consider their invitation as unofficial. In principle, the SDS was interested in strengthening relations with Russia, but for the time being there was no adequate program, [SDS deputy] Asparukh Panov said on 21 February. The main foreign policy expert of the blue coalition [SDS] did not hide his concern with the independent foreign policy tunnels the BSP is tracing. He noted that the BSP's foreign policy activity is becoming ever more intensive and that this hardly contributes to a uniform Bulgarian foreign policy. Bulgarian tax payers noted a long time ago that there is no uniform Bulgarian foreign policy. If the president is closer to the SDS doctrine, the premier undoubtedly professes the BSP's doctrine. However, since according to the Constitution, there are two foreign policy centers in Bulgaria, it is reasonable to expect their chiefs to clarify the direction in which the wind blows. One could hardly doubt the fact that we need good economic and political relations with Russia. The question is to what extend we will be able to defend our independence and equality in our relations with Russia, something that the two centers, or at least one of them, apparently cannot guarantee. It cannot be natural that the political force with the greatest influence in our country -- the SDS -- does not have direct contacts with a great power such as Russia. Such relations would certainly introduce a new tone in international relations, which will break out of the narrow party track in which the Socialists pushed them through their monopoly.
