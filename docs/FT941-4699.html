940310 FT 10 MAR 94 / Court to rule on review of councils The High Court will decide tomorrow on applications by three local authorities for judicial review of the Local Government Commission's proposals for council re-organisation in north-east England. A government decision on the area has already been delayed. The commission said it had failed to consider about 500 letters from local interests that were sent before the closing date for consultation. As a result an unprecedented supplementary report on the area will be published. It seems unlikely, however, that the supplementary report will make significant changes to the recommendations. Mr John Gummer, the environment secretary, must wait until the commission's reports are complete before announcing any decisions on the area. If the appeal for judicial review is successful, the process could be further delayed. Humberside and North Yorkshire county councils, and Ryedale, a district within North Yorkshire, are all dissatisfied with recommendations which would see the creation of five all-purpose unitary councils north of the Humber in place of the current two-tier system. They say the commission failed to consider properly the possibility of maintaining the two-tier status quo, and to carry out proper consultations. The Labour-controlled Association of County Councils said it was 'concerned' by the commission's 'worrying' mistake. It will instruct its members in areas not yet reviewed to check that all submissions have been considered by the commission. The cross-party Association of District Councils, whose members include Ryedale, discourages districts from resorting to judicial review, as the process is costly and delays re-organisation. Five counties - Avon, Cleveland, Derbyshire, Lancashire and Somerset - have already been allowed to challenge commission decisions in the High Court. The judicial reviews for Avon, Somerset and Cleveland will take place on May 23. In the following two weeks the commission, which is working to a timetable shortened by the government last September, must announce proposals for re-organising Bedfordshire, Berkshire, Leicestershire, Oxfordshire, Cambridgeshire, Suffolk, Staffordshire, Cheshire, Cumbria, Hampshire and Devon. Proposals for Kent and Devon may also be announced at the same time, if severe local disagreements in both areas can be overcome.
