920123 FT 23 JAN 92 / Agents report house sales still flagging THE DECISION to remove stamp duty on most house sales for eight months has so far failed to provoke a revival in the housing market, according to a national survey of estate agents published today, Andrew Taylor writes. The survey by the Royal Institution of Chartered Surveyors shows house prices continuing to fall in many parts of the country in the last quarter of last year. A high proportion of agents remains concerned about prospects for this year, saying the housing market is as deeply depressed as ever. Most expect no recovery until after the general election. They say cut-price sales of thousands of properties repossessed by mortgage lenders have continued to drive down prices, particularly in London and south-east England, where recession has been worst. Just over half of the 114 agents questioned said house prices continued to fall in the three months to the end of December. More than two thirds of agents in London said prices had fallen. Virtually no agents reported an increase in average prices during the three months. There were mixed views about the effects on the market of the temporary partial abolition of stamp duty. Some agents reported a slight increase in prospective buyers. Others said the rescue measures, promoted by the government and the Council of Mortgage Lenders, were unlikely to have any significant impact. Agents Copping Joyce in Islington, London, said: 'We are hoping the latest government measures will have some effect in the new year but are dubious that a dead battery can be quick-started.'
