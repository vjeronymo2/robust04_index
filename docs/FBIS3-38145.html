Language: Norwegian Article Type:BFN [Hugo Pedersen report: "First Oil Find in Barents Sea"] [Excerpt] Statoil has made a small oil find in the Barents Sea. It is the first time that such a find has been made in the area. The oil was found northeast of the North Cape only 40 km from land, in completely new geological formations. Next week Statoil will test the find to see whether oil production is possible. According to AFTENPOSTEN's information, the field contains only 4 to 5 million tonnes of oil. However, development in the Barents Sea needs a field containing 50 to 500 million tonnes of oil. But the fact that for the first time oil that can be exploited has been found in the region is very positive for the Barents Sea's future. "The drilling rig, Ross Rig, has found oil in block 7128/4 off the coast of Finnmark and we will now test this find to find out whether it is profitable. If the oil can be exploited, we are talking about a relatively small find," Statoil information chief Wenche Skorge said. "The most interesting thing is that the find is the first in the region, and the first that has been made using this search model. The presence of hydrocarbons has been demonstrated in a stratum of porous flint which is around 250 million years old. This is approximately 100 years [as published] older than rocks of the the Jurassic period in which oil has generally been found on Norway's continental shelf," she said. The oil find has been made in a well which will be drilled to a depth of 2,500 meters. Oil Directorate information chief Jan Hagland confirmed to AFTENPOSTEN that traces of oil have been found, but that it is thought to be marginal find. The find was made at the last minute, and in the very last drilling operation on this part of the Norwegian continental shelf. Most oil companies have abandoned their expensive exploratory operations in the Barents Sea, after decades of drilling without result. A total of over 50 exploratory wells have been drilled in the region, and hitherto only gas has been found. The Statoil find could therefore have major repercussions, particularly since the authorities are now in the process of completely changing the rules for drilling in the Barents Sea. "If there does turn out to be oil in the find, it comes at the absolutely right time. It tells us that we should not abandon the Barents Sea. There is still a lot of work to do in the region -- for example, several new exploration models must be tested," he said. Hagland stressed that this, together with the signals contained in the new Oil Report, could trigger great optimism about the Barents Sea, although we should continue to be prepared for disappointments. The Barents Sea is a very complicated area in which to search for oil. [passage omitted]
