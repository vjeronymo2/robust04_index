Language: English Article Type:BFN [From the "Focus on Africa" program] [Text] Liberian civilians are once again taking flight in fear as a result of fighting and bringing with them horrendous and vivid reports. Several thousand Liberians are said to be heading toward the port of Buchanan in the Grand Bassa County, trying to escape skirmishes between Charles Taylor's NPFL [National Patriotic Front of Liberia] and the organization calling itself the Liberian Peace Council. These skirmishes between the NPFL and the Liberian Peace Council have reportedly been going on for nearly three months in southeastern Liberia. This is in the midst of the stalled peace process and the continued ineffectiveness of the Transitional Council. From Monrovia, Nii Anartey Allison telexed this report. Cecil Gillaume, a medical coordinator of the French charity Medecin Sans Frontieres at Compound No. 2 in eastern Grand Bassa County, said the displaced were without food and safe drinking water. She added: Many die each day from diarrhea, malaria, and other diseases. She said that several thousand others were trapped in riverside villages by landmines planted west of the St. John River which crosses Grand Bassa County. Ms. Guillaume added that it was difficult to reach them with any aid though some of them are managing to arrive at Compound No. 2. Several thousand others fleeing fighting in Liberia Agricultural Company area have been trickling into Buchanan for fear of imminent fighting two weeks after the Liberia Peace Council overrun the area. More than 2,000 people have entered the port city of Buchanan in the past week. The city is already sheltering at least 10,000 displaced people. The refugees said they had to leave behind them several dozen others, mostly the sick and elderly as bullets whisked passed them. They said they had seen bullet-riddled bodies littering the bushes during their escape to Buchanan. People are very frightened, and one man told me that, on seeing an unused bullet near to his tent, he shouted, bullet, bullet, and other refugees rushed to the scene and used a shovel to dispose of it. Another refugee described the situation in the rebels' enclave as appalling and added that the two rebel sides were subjecting the people to harassment, flogging, and forced conscription. He added: Anyone who speaks good English or looks clean is a target. I was afraid to fight back because they would kill me, another panic- stricken refugee said, explaining how NPFL rebels had taken away his 13-year-old son. A local aid agency, Medical Emergency Relief Cooperative International, or MERCI, has posted emergency mobile teams at all seven refugee entry points to deal with the influx of refugees into Buchanan. We are now treating per day two to 300 cases of diarrhea, malnutrition, and bullet wounds. This was told to me by Dr. Mohamed Sylla, head of MERCI's Buchanan operations.
