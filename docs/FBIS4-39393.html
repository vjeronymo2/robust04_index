BFN [Aleksandr Shalnev report: "President's Directive Signed But Still No Clarity. Appointments of 20 Russian Ambassadors Delayed"] [Text] According to informed sources, the other day President Boris Yeltsin signed an internal directive on the procedure for appointing and approving Russian ambassadors abroad. This process came to a standstill without even starting, literally after the entry into effect of the new Russian Federation Constitution, whose provisions regarding the procedure for appointing ambassadors caused friction in relations between the Foreign Ministry and the Federal Assembly's international committees, which aspire to the role of active participants rather than passive observers of the consideration and solution of questions of this kind. The Constitution prescribes that appointments are made by the president by agreement with the appropriate parliamentary committees. The term "by agreement" has become a stumbling block: Does it mean that, for example, committees can block a particular nomination proposed by the head of state, or is it merely the case that the president will acquaint himself with the opinion of the committee members but will make the final decision exclusively at his own discretion? Behind-the-scenes disputes have turned into a situation whereby for the last five months or so not a single nomination has been submitted to parliament for agreement. Consequently, a number of key ambassadorial posts -- first and foremost in the United States -- remain unfilled. On the whole, according to information from informed sources, around 20 appointments are currently "pending." Without going into the substance of the president's directive in detail, in conversation with IZVESTIYA Deputy Foreign Minister Sergey Krylov gave an approximate outline of what the appointment process will be: Submitted nominations will be discussed in closed parliamentary hearings and only in closed hearings so that, the deputy minister said, "ethics are not violated and future ambassadors' names are not publicized before these ambassadors have received the host country's agreement." The Constitution, Krylov stressed, does not place limitations on the president in his final choice of a particular ambassador. In other words, even if the nomination is rejected by parliamentary committees the president is nonetheless fully entitled to approve it. Similarly citing diplomatic ethics, Krylov refused to say in which specific countries changes will take place in the near future, merely observing that, because a Russian ambassador had recently died in a Latin American state, there would naturally be a new appointment there. Meanwhile, in the State Duma I was informed that there could be talk only of open hearings, which will be closed to unauthorized persons only when it is necessary to consider confidential information. But the parliamentary committees' decisions, submitted directly to the president, will in fact be secret. "Can the president," I enquired, "insist that the hearings be closed?" "No," the Duma sources replied, "because the nature of the hearings is determined by internal parliamentary regulations which have already been approved." After talking to representatives of the Duma, Foreign Ministry, and presidential staff you are unlikely to conclude that even now, with the signing of the directive, all problems have finally been solved and the process of filling vacancies and "rotating" ambassadors will get into full swing, although this is what Russia's foreign policy interests demand.
