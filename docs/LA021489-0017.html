February 14, 1989, Tuesday, Home Edition

THE DRUG CLINIC: CRACKING THE USER'S FACADE OF DENIAL

"Find a need and fill it" is a wise business maxim. "Figure out what people need -- and they'll buy it." That sounds logical, but the nation's need for alcoholism treatment went unfilled -- until relatively recently.

In the late 1970s I was sitting in a think tank with alcoholism treatment marketers and TV advertising consultants. Research had shown there was definitely a need for treatment out there. The question was: How do we fill it? In spite of regular ads in medical journals only 5% of the patients in alcoholism clinics were sent by doctors. The other 95% were being brought in by the police, the courts or angry spouses.

"To educate doctors doesn't pay," the admen deduced. You can always tell a doctor -- but not much. Did we clinicians have any better ideas?

Well, yes. Why not take alcoholism education to the people who live with the problem?

Right! The ad men positively jingled. Sutton robbed banks because that's where the money was, right? The disease is all over so let's take our TV ads directly to the living rooms of America.

We know that the average family consists of 1.8 alcoholics/addicts and four co-dependents (non-alcoholics who suffer along with them). So how's this for a 30-second ad: An introduction showing signs and symptoms, such as personality changes and drunk-driving arrests. These things co-dependents already know. Then add some things they only suspect, like memory loss and decreased life span. Close the piece with hope ("treatment works") and flash the clinic logo on the 800 number.

Now the admen were really getting into it -- scribbling, sketching, even ad-libbing new terms. To rattle "heartland puritanism" and shape up "bicoastal sophistication," let's dramatize the ads with high-pressure soap scenes. That will crack what clinicians call "denial," the thing that doctors, co-dependents and alcoholics/addicts hide behind.

It worked with almost Pavlovian charm. After running ads for one month, America's alcoholics were hauled out of closets and dragged into clinics by guilty co-dependents. Why? Because the ads portrayed the co-dependents as contributing to the demise of somebody they loved -- or hated. The clinics filled up and multiplied. The nation went on a treatment binge. There definitely was a need and the treatment "industry" had found a way to fill it.

Not surprisingly, there were angry objections to this invasion of America's privacy. These ads, it was charged, were not just titillating -- they were actually traumatizing people's emotions; the ads were exaggerating "minor" family problems.

True, the ads that got the greatest response were the ones that evoked anger, fear and guilt. But, the ad guys countered, do you really think that these ads can scare an unhappy wife so much that she will label her errant husband an alcoholic, drag him to a hospital, spend their hard-earned money on expensive treatment -- and label herself a co-dependent, to boot? No, not unless there is a real problem.

Why do hospitals advertise? To fill beds. Why are the ads successful? Because there is a need. How do the ads achieve their goal? By breaking through the viewers' denial and making them aware of their need.

Let's face it. Alcoholism and drug addiction are different from other diseases. If you have a headache, rash or hearing loss, you'll feel it, or you'll see it, or somebody will make you aware of it -- and 500,000 doctors are willing to treat it.

Not so with alcoholism/addiction. That's a disease of denial: The addicts deny it, their families hide it, the neighbors suspect it, the bosses ignore it and the doctors mistreat it.

We may not like it, but we might as well accept it. The real reason the ads work is because they tell troubled viewers what others (family, friends, bosses, doctors) should tell them, but won't. They play on anger, fear and guilt because that's where our soft spots are. And they invade our living rooms because, to paraphrase bank robber Willie Sutton, that's where the alcoholics are.
