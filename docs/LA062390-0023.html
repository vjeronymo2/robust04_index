June 23, 1990, Saturday, Home Edition

DEALING WITH OIL TANKER ACCIDENTS IN COASTAL WATERS

Larry Stammer's article on the plight of the Gulf of Mexico really hit home with me (front page, June 15). I grew up in Rockport, Texas, 30 miles north of Corpus Christi and until this past month, I had not seen the gulf coast for 20 years.

When a returned to the gulf, I knew there would be changes along the vast virgin stretches of sandy beach where my friends and I had cruised. What I wasn't prepared to see was a coastal garbage dump.

Besides the flotsam and bird carcasses, what I also found incredible was the people in the area claiming that the pollution was coming from Mexico. Of all the hundreds of washed up plastics and glass bottles, I never tripped over one that had Hecho en Mexico written on it. What I did notice were dozens of offshore drilling projects along the gulf horizon that didn't exist 20 years ago.

Where does the debris on our beaches come from? The trash cans remain empty while most of the garbage lay washed up onshore. The offenders are the companies that are involved in the exploration and transport of oil found in these offshore drilling projects. Isn't it time that our state and federal politicians take a tougher stance on the regulatory practices of offshore drilling as well as stronger enforcement of laws that tolerate no leakage of oil from these projects?

Thanks to Stammer's article, I am no longer apathetic about the de-gentrification of our shorelines. I am ready to join the protest. Everyone agrees that it's time to clean up the crime in our streets. How about cleaning up the crime in our oceans?

JAY DAYS

Los Angeles
