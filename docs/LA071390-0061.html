July 13, 1990, Friday, Home Edition

MARKET BEAT / TOM PETRUNO: SOME ANALYSTS SEE AN ERA OF GROWTH FOR GROWTH STOCKS

Ed Kerschner hears the same moan about the stock market from a lot of big investors in a lot of different places: It's too late to get in.

And when the market has a day like Thursday -- when the Dow Jones industrials jumped 37.13 points to a record 2,969.80 -- the moaning about "being too late" just gets louder. Kerschner, Paine Webber Inc.'s 37-year-old chief investment strategist, believes that life is only going to get more miserable for those doubters.

Kerschner, in L.A. this week to visit with clients, is a member of a small but growing school of Wall Streeters who believe that growth-stock investing has entered a dramatic new phase. The market as a whole may finish just 10% to 15% higher -- or lower -- this year, he says. But the companies that are producing strong earnings growth are going to be stars for some time to come, Kerschner says.

Investors who insist that many of these growth stocks are already too high priced are forgetting the nature of market cycles, Kerschner says. When Wall Street begins thinking a new way, the cycle tends to run for much longer than anyone initially predicts. Remember how the takeover-stock cycle ran from 1984 to 1989?

And if growth-stock fans sound like their counterparts back in 1971-73 -- the days of the Nifty Fifty growth stocks that soared to outrageous heights before crashing -- well, so be it, Kerschner says. "The Nifty Fifty wasn't a conspiracy. It was a result of the investment process of the time," he says. The time will come to sell growth stocks in this cycle, too, he says -- but not yet.

Kerschner adds that growth investing doesn't have to mean chasing little-known companies. One theme that he stresses is the idea of "world-class" growth stocks -- companies that are major players overseas. In the new world unfolding, it's a simple fact that demand for many products and services will grow far faster overseas than in the United States, Kerschner says.

His list of 12 world-class stocks includes Boeing, Coca-Cola, oil services giant Halliburton and drug firm Merck. All have been hot stocks this year. And while some investors may be getting bored hearing the same names, remember: Early in stock market cycles, familiarity doesn't breed contempt; it breeds more buying by new investors who want to own names they can understand.

Kerschner presents his list as a core portfolio for any investor who wants to bet on a booming world economy in the 1990s. These are companies that already know how to do business overseas. Many of them now derive more than 50% of their revenue from overseas business (which Kerschner counts as sales by the firms' overseas subsidiaries, plus their exports from the United States).

A kicker for the group, Kerschner says, is the potential economic renaissance of South and Central America. Mexico, in particular, is undertaking bold economic reforms designed to bolster growth in the 1990s, Kerschner says. Instead of seeking new loans to paper over Mexico's problems, Mexican President Carlos Salinas de Gortari is trying to reenergize the economy by putting state enterprises -- such as banks -- back in private hands. Kerschner believes that many U.S. companies and investors haven't yet realized the long-term significance of the changes in South and Central America. Latin America "could be for us in the 1990s what Eastern Europe will be for Western Europe," he says.

Briefly: La Jolla-based Investment Quality Trends newsletter rates stocks based on their dividend yields -- specifically, the current yield's value relative to the stock's historical dividend norm. Among 350 blue chip stocks the newsletter follows, publisher Geraldine Weiss now rates 19% undervalued, 19% overvalued, 31% headed toward undervalued status and 31% headed toward overvalued status. That's what Weiss calls "a market in equilibrium, with as many good buys currently in the market as there are stocks to avoid." The figures also mean the market is "not vulnerable to a severe decline," she says. Before the 1987 market crash, Weiss says, 36% of the stocks were overvalued and 35% were headed toward overvalued status. . . . Another sign of a pickup in small stocks: NASDAQ over-the-counter trading volume equaled 89% of New York Stock Exchange volume in the first six months of this year, compared to 81% for all of 1989.
