BFN [Report by Roberto Reyes on interview with Honduran President Carlos Roberto Reina by unidentified reporters at the Government House in Tegucigalpa on 20 April -- recorded] [Text] We have remarks by President Carlos Roberto Reina referring to the Honduran Government's and Army's plan to modify the 40-year-old treaty dealing with U.S. military aid for Honduras. The president spoke about this initiative, which he said is not official. Asked why the Armed Forces rather than the executive branch took the initiative on this, he said the source of the initiative does not matter. Here is President Carlos Roberto Reina: [Begin recording] [Reporter, in progress] ...the Honduran Government's initiative to modify the Military Assistance Treaty with the United States. [Reina] Statements have been made. They are not official. The issue is going to be discussed by Honduran Government officials and also by the various government branches. These agreements were implemented at a time when there was a different situation in the world. It was the time of the Cold War and the polarization of states and minds. It is a phase that has been completely overcome. The new agenda is one of coming together in favor of mankind. At the right time, in agreement with U.S. Government representatives, we will sit down to review these agreements and treaties, some of which are very old, dating back 40 years. In this way, we will be able to update the forms of cooperation in all fields with the great democracy represented by the United States of America and its excellent president, Mr. Bill Clinton. [Reporter] When would this take place? We spoke with the U.S. ambassador just a few moments ago, and he said they are ready to begin reviewing these agreements. [Reina] If U.S. Government representatives, who are very honorably represented by Mr. Pryce, are ready, at any time you might hear news that we are sitting down to review the agreements to which some protocols were added at times of great tension in Central America and the world. [Reporter] What would [words indistinct]. [Reina] The subject is military. These are military agreements. Cooperation is now more directed toward human development. That is one way of beginning to address the problem of the military presence. [Reporter] The question the people are most interested in is the issue of the Palmerola military base. Will this base continue to operate in Honduras or will the U.S. troops leave? [Reina] We return to the same topic: the presence of that base, which is legally Honduran. Its name is the Soto Cano base. It is under the sponsorship of the Honduran Government. It was established at a time of great tension for Honduras and Central America. It is logical that we review and update the structure of cooperation. At that time, it was believed cooperation should be military, because tensions were so great. We think that at present cooperation should involve other areas, such as environment, health, education, human rights, and drug trafficking. For example, we must not allow this scourge known as drug trafficking to continue to hurt Honduras. In this regard, I will say this to the ambassador and to you: We are your ally in waging a frontal assault against scourges that cause so much damage to humanity. [Reporter] Mr. President, this has been interpreted as an initiative of the military institution and not of the executive branch, as it should be. [Reina] A military issue is involved. They are also part of Honduras. They are an institution, constitutionally speaking. They took an initiative. Now is the time to resume discussions without innuendoes and without pulling out cards from under the table. [Reporter] [question indistinct] [Reina] They proposed a review. The ambassador said he agrees, and I also fully agree. The Congress must review this. I wonder if you recall -- if you have a good memory -- that there was much discussion when the protocols came up. In my opinion, those protocols should have been brought before the National Congress, because constitutionally speaking, military issues should be (?discussed) at the National Congress. [Reporter] When will such a discussion take place? [Reina] They will take place in more calm times, in times of less confrontation and very sincere cooperation. [Reporter] [words indistinct] U.S. ambassador that there is a possibility that, once the Southern Command closes down, some soldiers from that base might be transferred to the Palmerola Base? [Reina] I think you are moving ahead of the negotiations and discussions. This is theoretical. This issue should be discussed and its approval should be in keeping with the interests of our power [nuestro poder]. [Reporter]) I would like to change the topic, Mr. President. How does the people's constant criticisms, justified or not, about the energy crisis facing the country harm or suffocate your government? [Reina] It is unfair to criticize the government for this crisis. The government, which is only three months old, did not create this crisis. We are trying to resolve it quickly. This crisis is the result of mistakes made by serveral past administrations. We will not take revenge by claiming only the previous government made mistakes. Any lack of foresight and problems linked to the Francisco Morazan Dam must be resolved by this government. It is completely unfair to criticize the government for trying to resolve these problems. [Reporter] [words indistinct] declare an emergency over this energy crisis, because the [words indistinct]. [Reina] I am not trying to lay blame. The chief of the ENEE [National Enterprise for Electric Power] is abroad looking for aid from friendly countries. Everyone must help resolve this crisis. Using this as a political tool against the government is totally unfair and absurd. We are the ones who will resolve this problem. We did not decree an emergency.
