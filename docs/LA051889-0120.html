May 18, 1989, Thursday, South Bay Edition

REDONDO RETAINS MODERATE COUNCIL;

PARTON LEADS 'CLEAN SWEEP' OF CONTROLLED-GROWTH CANDIDATES

In a runoff election that retains a moderate-growth alignment on the City Council, Redondo Beach voters have selected 28-year-old W. Brad Parton as the city's new mayor, reelected Councilwoman Kay Horrell to a second term in District 2 and brought in newcomer Terry Ward to represent District 4.

The outcome was a "clean sweep . . . (that) put the people in place that we need to accomplish a series of things," said District 5 Councilman Ron Cawdry, a member of the majority in power over the last four years.

Cawdry, who was not up for reelection, said he campaigned hard for the three winners. He introduced them to an exuberant crowd at Horrell's victory celebration at Pancho and Wong's restaurant late Tuesday night after the votes were tabulated.

The series of things to be accomplished, he said later, include completing a revised General Plan, developing more park space and rebuilding King Harbor and the Redondo Pier, which were devastated by floods and a fire last year.

The minority on the new council, after the runoff winners are sworn in Tuesday, will be Councilwoman Barbara Doerr, who won the District 1 seat in the March general election after serving eight years as mayor, and Councilman Stevan Colin, who was elected to the District 3 post in 1988.

There was a runoff because no candidates for the three posts at stake Tuesday received a majority of votes in the March balloting.

Parton, who owns a retirement investment firm, received 3,669 votes, or 55.7% of the total cast, while his runoff opponent, architect Frank Bostrom, got 2,914, or 44.3%

In the District 2 race, real estate agent Horrell won with 891 votes, or 52.4%, compared to 811, or 47.6%, for Michael F. Ford, a real estate appraiser. Ward, a data communications consultant, took the District 4 seat with 528 votes, or 52.2%, while school board member Valerie Dombrowski received 483, or 47.8%.

Dombrowski narrowly missed victory in the general election, falling only four votes short of the 50%-plus-one majority needed to be declared the winner.

She said her chances in the runoff were hurt by personal attacks on her family from Ward's supporters and by Ward's success in registering about 150 new voters who supported his candidacy.

"But I'm not bitter," Dombrowski said. "I'm not shattered."

At her victory party, Horrell faulted the losing faction for being "so negative and down on everything. . . . I think they scared some voters by their meat-ax approach" to the city's concerns about overdevelopment and traffic congestion.

In response to those concerns, Horrell said, the present council has "practically downzoned the entire city" over the past four years. She said she believes the new council "will continue to take a moderate approach that allows for quality, controlled growth."

Stricter Limits

The opposition faction, which advocated stricter limits on growth, blamed the outcome Tuesday on what Bostrom called "dirty political tactics" and on heavy campaign spending by the winners.

Parton, Horrell and Ward spent a total of about $60,000, compared to about $15,000 by the three opposing candidates, Bostrom, Ford and Dombrowski.

Mayoral candidate Bostrom, who watched at City Hall as his opponent seized a commanding lead in votes at the outset and maintained it to the end, blamed his defeat on a last-minute mailer sent out by Parton over the weekend.

"There's no question in my mind," Bostrom said, characterizing Parton's mailer as a "hit piece" that used "character-assassination" tactics. He provided copies of flyers that suggested Bostrom had a "hidden agenda" for development in Redondo Beach and that questioned statements in Bostrom's resume about his accomplishments as an architect and volunteer community worker.

The mailer asserted that Bostrom was one of three candidates "backed by the political machine."

Parton, who raised the specter of a political machine frequently during the runoff campaign, said in an interview last week that he was referring to a "tight, little group that attacks me in a variety of ways but doesn't get down to the issues." He declined to be more specific.

'Cardinal Error'

Bostrom said he had a response mailer prepared but decided not to send it out because the cost would have pushed him over a self-imposed limit of $10,000 on campaign spending.

"I probably made a cardinal error, politically," he said. "But I said, '$10,000 is my limit and I'm not going to spend another nickel.' "

He predicted that Parton, who depicted himself as an independent in the campaign, would buckle under pressure from developers during his tenure as mayor.

At a boisterous victory celebration at his home, Parton was busy making telephone calls to supporters, including his parents in San Jose and a close friend in New York. He defended his mailers as "all fact" and attributed his victory to hard work, which he said focused on walking much of the city door-to-door.

Voters also liked his independent stance, he said. "I was not part of any slate," he said. "I was out there running on my own."

On the campaign spending issue, Parton said he needed to spend more money than Bostrom because his opponent had been backed by politically well-known figures, such as Doerr and Dombrowski.

Parton also discounted Bostrom's prediction that he would be influenced by developers. "I've got my ethics and my integrity," he said.

Ward, who will replace outgoing Councilman Archie Snow in District 4, seemed a bit flabbergasted by his victory over Dombrowski, a longtime community activist and school trustee for 12 years.

"I came from behind (after the March election)," said Ward, a political novice. "But we managed to build momentum . . . the more I walked and talked, the better I fit in with how (voters) felt," he said.

Development Views

He said the voters he met generally agreed with his views on development. "I've said from the beginning that I want controlled, quality growth," he said. "But I don't think we can stop growth entirely, because if you stop growth, you die."

Tuesday night also was sweet for Snow, who lost his bid for the mayor's post in March and then went on to campaign for Ward. "I kind of like the way things turned out," he said above the clamor at Horrell's party.

Snow was viewed by some as the council majority's leader during his eight years in office, in part because of his frequent verbal clashes with former Mayor Doerr. The City Charter barred both from running for third terms in their respective posts.

Colin, who has been Doerr's only ally during his first year on the council, struck a conciliatory note Wednesday at the conclusion of the often-rancorous campaign.

"I look forward to working with Brad, and I think he will be a good mayor," he said. "I don't know a lot about Terry (Ward) yet, but I think he'll be fair. . . . Basically, we all want to do what's best for Redondo Beach."
