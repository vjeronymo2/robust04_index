December 7, 1989, Thursday, Home Edition

DEAR MISS MANNERS: 'ETIQUETTE ZEALOTS' MAY GO TOO FAR

DEAR MISS MANNERS -- Several times in your columns you have mentioned that if individuals do not act in a manner generally approved of by society, they have to suffer society's sanctions. Social control is an absolute necessity in any society, especially in as large and individualistic a society as ours.

But how is this to be accomplished? We now have such a fragmented culture that peer pressure is diminished, and rudeness and crime (which is extreme rudeness) abound.

To complicate matters even further, it appears to be "politically correct" to advocate a pluralistic, multicultural society (I don't support this), and perhaps one person's "bad manners" are correct in his culture.

If so, doesn't that negate the whole concept of manners in America, and manners especially as a form of social control, at least in principle?

GENTLE READER -- No. American manners draw on both a variety of traditions and the original ideas of the country's founders, who had a specific interest in developing an egalitarian etiquette that would be different from the aristocratic traditions they knew. But even allowing for the ignorance of newcomers and regional differences, we can all distinguish good American manners from bad.

As to how we punish the bad, that is a more difficult problem. Society's consequences for violating etiquette are not -- nor should they be -- what society's consequences are for mass murder or grand larceny. For this reason, etiquette zealots are often frustrated enough to take matters into their own hands, delivering rude punishments for rudeness, much as vigilante groups punish violations of the law by illegal means.

Obviously, Miss Manners condemns this. But she also believes it to be based on an underestimation of what she has been pleased to call society's sanction.

That sanction is social disapproval. Purposeful rudeness antagonizes those who are its victims, and they, in turn, treat perpetrators with disdain -- not return rudeness, Miss Manners hopes, but the coldness of distaste. Politeness does not require anyone to pretend to like being deliberately treated rudely.

While being generally disliked may not be as clear or severe a punishment as a jail sentence or fine, it is unpleasant enough to discourage most people from persisting in provoking it.

DEAR MISS MANNERS -- I have a group of female friends to whom I have been writing for quite a number of years. Several of these friends have married recently. I have never met their mates.

I would like to continue writing and exchanging gifts, but feel awkward now that these friends are married. May I continue to address the letters to my friends only, or do I now need to address letters to "Mr. and Mrs."?

We usually exchange Christmas and birthday gifts. Should I now include the husbands in the gift exchange?

GENTLE READER -- Although seasonal greetings are customarily addressed to both members of a couple, a lady does not lose her private mailing privileges upon marriage. Your letters and birthday presents continue to go to her alone, although it would be courteous to send greetings to her husband and perhaps the wish that you will someday meet. If your Christmas presents are for the household, they could also be sent to both.

DEAR MISS MANNERS -- This will be my first year of sending Christmas cards as a widow. I have a son living at home, who has always been part of our signature. How do I write my name and his so that no one will mistakenly think that he is my husband and not my son? I am also curious about how I should sign my name on other correspondence, "Mrs. Alan Hanlin" or "Mrs. Adele Hanlin"?

GENTLE READER -- Your signature is "Adele Hanlin," with the formal name ("Mrs. Alan Hanlin") beneath it in parentheses on business letters, so unknown recipients will know how to address you. A lady's name does not change on her husband's death, and an honorific is not part of anyone's signature. Don't let anyone tell you otherwise.

As for the Christmas cards, sign them "Adele Hanlin and Sean." Miss Manners assumes that you do not make the mistake of sending social greetings to people who know you so little as to be unaware that you are recently widowed and have a son. You therefore run little risk of having them believe that you have taken up with a gentleman too young to have a surname.
