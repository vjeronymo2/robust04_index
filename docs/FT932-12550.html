930428 FT 28 APR 93 / Knight sees end of media barons THE AGE of the dominant media baron was dead because of the fast-changing structures of modern communications, Mr Andrew Knight, executive chairman of News International, told the conference. Growing diversity of media ownership meant no one figure could possess overwhelming dominance, and the perception - and it was only a perception - of the media mogul belonged to the past. He said: 'In a market proliferating at such a rate, with access so easy and open to all comers, domination by any one group or individual would simply be impossible . . . The days of Lord Beaverbrook attempting to run the country through the pages of a newspaper are long gone, and will never return.' Modern life was too anarchic, rich, non-conformist and wild for a Beaverbrook, a Northcliffe or a Murdoch to dominate anything. Mr Knight, one of Mr Rupert Murdoch's closest lieutenants, took as his theme the strength and diversity of the media industry in the UK and the opportunities that lay ahead for it internationally. 'We in Britain can have a bite of a trillion-pound global business . . . but, if we are not careful, it could all slip from our grasp,' he warned.
