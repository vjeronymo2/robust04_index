941229 FT 29 DEC 94 / Tec chairman's contracts defended The awarding of contracts by a training and enterprise council to a company owned by one of its board members was defended yesterday by the Tec National Council. Essex Training and Enterprise Council awarded a contract to a business owned by Mr Roy Lawrence without going through competitive tendering, it emerged yesterday. Mr Lawrence, former chairman of Essex Tec, said that in 1991 Mr Philip Howard, then chief executive, appointed Raytel Insurance Brokers - a company Mr Lawrence then owned - to arrange insurance cover and pensions without accepting formal bids from other brokers. Mr Howard said that when the Tec could not find a 'suitable broker' for its insurance cover, Mr Lawrence 'offered the help of his broker manager'. The Tec decided to place its business with Raytel after talking to a few other brokers because 'the deal was by far the best'. Mr Lawrence said he did not take part in the decision and fully declared his interests. The Department of Employment, which provides about Pounds 2bn in funding to the 82 employer-led Tecs, which administer government-funded training in England and Wales, requires all contracts to be awarded impartially. In an audit of Essex Tec's bidding procedures last year, it said it found 'no reason to think any impropriety has occurred'. Mr Howard said that in late 1991 Mr Albert Gable, then head of finance, asked Raytel to arrange personal pensions for some staff members, again without putting the contract out to tender. Mr Gable left the Tec in late 1993, and yesterday refused to comment on the arrangements. In May 1993 Mr Michael Clegg took over as chief executive and reviewed the Tec's pension scheme. A number of brokers were invited to bid for the account, and Mr Clegg chose to transfer all 30 plans which were placed with Raytel to the Tec National Pension Scheme. Mr Chris Humphries, director of policy and strategy at the Tec National Council, said: 'The whole point of a Tec is that it is based in a community and requires you to have local people on your board.'
