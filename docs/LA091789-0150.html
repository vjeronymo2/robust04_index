September 17, 1989, Sunday, Home Edition

THE ILLNESS AND THE CURE

Nancy Mairs' review of Anne Stevenson's "Bitter Fame: A Life of Sylvia Plath" (Book Review, Aug. 20) brought to mind the tragic and yet successful life of the late Pulitzer Prize-winning poet Theodore Roethke (1908-1963). Subject to periodic attacks of manic depression, Roethke fought to overcome his possibly inherited and gene-based disease and to use the experiences of the attacks in his writings. As the biographer Allan Seager wrote: "The very qualities that made Ted a poet seem to have been the ones that made him ill -- his sensitivity and his energy."

KENNETH LLOYD LARSON

LOS ANGELES
