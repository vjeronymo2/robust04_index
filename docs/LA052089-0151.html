May 20, 1989, Saturday, Orange County Edition

'JERUSALEM'S' PLEA FOR END TO STRIFE;

SWADOS' ORATORIO DRAWS ON ARAB, JEWISH, CHRISTIAN, ARMENIAN MUSIC

Elizabeth Swados' oratorio "Jerusalem" makes a plea for peace in the strife-torn Holy Land by drawing on Jewish, Arab, Christian and Armenian musical traditions.

"It's meant to be a way of capturing the city through sound," Swados said in a recent phone interview from New York.

"Not just the dialects or the moods, but the passions that are so strong that the people could build magnificent places of worship and also kill each other. And I was always curious, what kind of passion is that?"

Swados, 38, has composed opera (including rock and pop operas), musicals, political revues and film scores. She said that "Jerusalem" incorporates the poems of Yehuda Amichai and echoes of the biblical book of Jeremiah.

Alan Weiner, cantor at Temple Bat Yahm in Newport Beach, will conduct the work Sunday at Loyola Marymount University in Orange and Tuesday at the University of Judaism in Los Angeles.

"A tourist comes to the land to warn the people or to learn from the people about the conflict," she explained, "and no one listens to this prophet and the city burns."

A timely subject?

"It's meant to be."

But she does offer some hope.

"The city burns, but the last thing is a prayer, which really implies that the next generation will make it better.

"And the piece itself is hopeful because of the work I had to do to get those people (the original multicultural cast) to sing together, to become friends who have done it many times together. . . .

"You see those people on stage working together -- that itself is a statement of hope -- whereas in the beginning they would never even sit next to each other."

Swados said the work got a "very mixed" reception when it was presented in Jerusalem in 1987.

"They interpreted it only politically, and, whereas my work is very popular there, some people felt I was trying to preach to them about not fighting within their country or something," she said. "So I got slapped around a little bit for that.

"Israelis really hate it when Americans do Israel-type shows, and in a way you can understand it," she added. "I don't know how I would feel if I saw a bunch of Israelis coming over here and doing some type of show about American girls from Buffalo. I don't know how I would feel."

Despite the controversy, Swados calls writing the work "one of the most positive things I ever did."

"I'm not grandiose. I don't think that my work has the power to effect social change or alter people's opinions. But I do think that you can create new forms that move the heart and wake people up to their emotions, and that is a very heady thing to do."

"Jerusalem," which had its premiere in 1983 at La Mama Annex in New York, grew out of a 6-month workshop underwritten by the Ford Foundation, according to Swados.

At first, she did not see "Jerusalem" as a work of art.

"First and foremost, I saw it as an experiment, a musical exchange that might evolve into a piece of work.

"All along the way, I was very conscious of any problems or toes I might have stepped on once I began to put the thing together. And everyone was very vocal about what would be right or wrong considering his or her religion.

"I respected everything that everyone said. I did it even if they were going too far with me, picking on me. There was so much to draw from -- they had given me so much I could write from -- it didn't matter. . . . I think that made the piece special because no one is uncomfortable when they sing it."

She said that she did not attempt to "balance" the differing viewpoints by allocating equal time to each.

"That's where music is very helpful," she said. "I don't think the intellect comes in there. You know, 5 minutes for the Jews, 5 minutes for the Christians. The piece balances itself musically. And there is a lot (of) stuff that is not anything, like sounds of a marketplace. . . .

"The music dictated its own distribution of time, and if something was too much, it didn't matter what (religious) quarter it was in, it was too long."

Swados said that she provides no translations of the 13 languages incorporated in the work. "I just let the sound tell you" the meaning, she said.

"I've always found that the most important ingredient in making my music work, besides technical expertise and energy, is intention. . . . What would hurt ("Jerusalem") would be somebody presented to be holy or a muezzin or an Orthodox priest who is (just) an actor. That would really be blasphemous and wrong."
