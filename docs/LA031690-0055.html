March 16, 1990, Friday, Home Edition

MUSIC REVIEW;

FRENCH ORCHESTRA AT AMBASSADOR AUDITORIUM

Don't try to tell anyone in the Orchestre National de France, or Lorin Maazel for that matter, that Beethoven is a greater composer than Saint-Saens. They might get angry. At least so it would seem if their Wednesday night concert at Ambassador Auditorium is any indication.

Maazel began with Beethoven's "Fidelio" Overture, in a polite, tidy rendition, notable more for nimbleness than weighty heroics. He followed with more Beethoven, the Symphony No. 4. It started promisingly enough, the opening Adagio taking on a sinister quality with the measured calm of Maazel's pacing and poised balances in the orchestra.

But thereafter it was as if Maazel and orchestra had switched to automatic pilot. He made graceful, sometimes cute, always dapper gestures; the orchestra responded with nonchalant neatness. The outer movements lacked robustness, moving along at times with a certain elegance, but with little feeling for their playfulness. The Adagio became a leisurely stroll, a pleasant rehashing of the notes.

This orchestra plays well; its strings are a unified body in tone and execution, and the woodwinds have a distinctive, grainy blend. And there were good moments: some telling rhythmic points in the Minuet, an alertly shaped trio and beautiful clarinet solos in the Adagio. But these remained just moments in a musical whole of cool indifference.

It was a different matter altogether with Saint-Saens' "Organ" Symphony. Here, the orchestra dug in with aggressiveness and fire, the strings in particular taking on a powerful, brilliant sheen. There were sweeping lyrical lines and big, rich-toned climaxes.

The ensemble played the Adagio with clear passion, and, in the lurid Finale, made a brassy, muscular noise, with the help of the guest soloist, Douglas Major, on the Allen Digital Computer Organ. Instant bravos at the end and a standing ovation -- but it was still only Saint-Saens. TIMOTHY MANGAN
