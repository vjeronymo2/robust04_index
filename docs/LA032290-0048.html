March 22, 1990, Thursday, Home Edition

THROW OFF THE SLAVERY OF PASSIONATE ATTACHMENTS;

DIPLOMACY: A WARNING FROM GEORGE WASHINGTON SHOULD BE HEEDED ANEW: IT IS OUR FRIENDS WHO COST US MORE DEARLY THAN OUR ENEMIES.

As Eastern Europe throws off its shackles, now's the time for the United States to shed its chains as well. For almost 50 years we have been in a bondage eloquently described by President George Washington in his farewell address of 1796. Usually dismissed as a caution against entangling alliances, his remarks actually warned of a much more insidious danger.

"Nothing is more essential," he said, "than that permanent, inveterate antipathies against particular nations and passionate attachments for others should be excluded. . . . The nation which indulges toward another an habitual hatred or an habitual fondness is in some degree a slave. It is a slave to its animosity or to its affection, either of which is sufficient to lead it astray from its duty and its interests."

Paradoxically, our allies more than our adversaries have been the ones who have led us away from our real interests. Our animosity to the Communist Bloc led us to see the world as a zero-sum game in which allies became counters in the win-lose equation. The more allies you had, the better you were doing. Realizing the power that gave them, many of our so-called friends became adept at using the threat of encroaching communism to bilk the United States at every opportunity.

Ten years ago, the Army War College conducted a study on the effectiveness of U.S. security assistance and found that, for all the billions expended, the United States had purchased few friends and precious little influence. Further, in many cases, reverse influence had developed, "making it difficult for the United States to disengage from a relationship which no longer serves U.S. interests." These nations had become, in Stanley Karnow's phrase, "puppets who pulled their own strings."

With the attenuation of the Soviet threat, now is the time to cut those strings and divest ourselves of "allies" who continue to beggar and enslave us. As Washington said, "a passionate attachment of one nation for another produces a variety of evils. Sympathy for the favorite nation (facilitates) the illusion of an imaginary common interest in cases where no real common interest exists and . . . leads also to concessions . . . of privileges denied to others."

Now is the time to substitute practicality for passion.

Using American interests as the litmus test, do we really need to put up with Spanish and Greek blackmail to maintain bases in the Mediterranean, bases whose usefulness is now in question? And do we really need to put up with the insults of Philippine officials and their unseemly demands for ransom for our bases there? With America's opening to the People's Republic of China almost two decades ago, the Philippines' strategic usefulness has passed. The days of extortion are over, and those who try it should be told to take the bases and do with them what they may.

The days of the United States as nattering nanny are over as well. Although then talking about Europe, George Washington could have been talking about the Middle East today when he warned "she must be engaged in frequent controversies, the causes of which are essentially foreign to our concerns. Hence, therefore, it must be unwise in us to implicate ourselves by artificial ties in the ordinary vicissitudes of her politics.

With his words in mind, why are we paying more than $3 billion in foreign aid each year to Israel and another $2 billion to Egypt, now that the chances of Soviet encroachment in the area are almost nil? The likelihood of the Soviet Union's arming Syria and Iraq for another Arab-Israeli war is fast diminishing. Why, then, is the Middle East peace process an American responsibility? After all the grief he caught over his recent remarks about East Jerusalem, George Bush ought to take the words of George Washington to heart and opt out, thereby encouraging the region to grow up and settle its own problems.

The first step in constructing a viable post-Cold War strategy should be to cut through the animosities and affections that have grown up in the course of that Cold War and to rediscover and build on our true national interests. But, as Washington pointed out, that will not be easy. We are still plagued by "ambitious, corrupted or deluded citizens who devote themselves to the favorite nation" and who have the "facility to betray or sacrifice the interests of their own country without odium."

We must wake up and turn a jaundiced eye toward our so-called friends. The rest of the world is throwing off its chains. It's time we Americans did so as well.
