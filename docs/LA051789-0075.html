May 17, 1989, Wednesday, Orange County Edition

KRAFT TRIAL PENALTY PHASE TO BEGIN JUNE 5, WILL COVER 8 MORE KILLINGS

The jurors who found Randy Steven Kraft guilty of murdering 16 young men in Orange County were ordered Tuesday to return June 5 for a death penalty hearing, where the prosecution will present evidence of eight more murders.

However, on May 24 Kraft's lawyers will raise a series of issues with Superior Court Judge Donald A. McCartin out of the jury's presence. Chief among them will be a renewed attempt to prevent the prosecution from showing jurors any more entries from a list found in Kraft's car. Prosecutors claim it is a "death list" that Kraft wrote to keep track of how many people he had killed.

Kraft, 44, of Long Beach, was convicted Friday of the 16 murders. Most of the victims, between the ages of 18 and 25, were mutilated or sexually abused, and dumped along or near freeways. Prosecutors claim they have linked Kraft to 45 murders altogether in what could be the worst serial murder case in the nation's history.

After Kraft's conviction for all 16 murders, Deputy Dist. Atty. Bryan Brown decided to narrow the number he would introduce at the penalty phase of the trial to eight. Six of those occurred in Oregon and two in Michigan.

The defense lawyers complained in the Santa Ana courtroom Tuesday that Brown owes them a better explanation of what evidence he will present next month. Brown responded that his case should be obvious to them, and that the list would be the primary evidence.

"It doesn't take a rocket scientist to figure it out," he said.

The Oregon entries on the Kraft list are all written in a row: "Portland ECK," "Portland Denver," "Portland Blood," "Portland Hawaii," "Portland Reserve" and "Portland Head." The entry that Brown claims represents two Michigan victims is "GR 2," written immediately below the Oregon entries. The two young men were last seen alive attending a horticulture convention at the same hotel where Kraft was staying in Grand Rapids, Mich., in December, 1982.

One of the young men found dead in Oregon was from the Denver, Colo., area, and another was from Hawaii. A third was in the National Guard reserves. Brown claims that a fourth victim had lost an enormous amount of blood, to explain the "Portland Blood" entry. While the other two entries have not been explained, Brown claims that they must represent the other two victims because the six entries match the number of unsolved murders in that state attributed to the same serial killer.

Another issue the defense intends to bring up is the media attention given the trial after last Friday's guilty verdicts. Kraft lawyer William J. Kopeny said he was "shocked" at all the publicity, and added: "There is a grave danger that this jury has been prejudiced by the publicity."

The judge did note that one juror sent a message to the court stating she had inadvertently seen a headline on the Kraft case in a newspaper Tuesday morning, but did not read the article. The judge admonished the jurors again not to read or listen to anything about the case until after the trial is over.

The judge asked the defense lawyers for an estimate of how much time they expect to take putting on their case in the penalty phase. But they told him it would be next week before they could say with any certainty. Prosecutor Brown told the court he expects to take about 2 weeks to put on the evidence of the Oregon and Michigan murders.

The judge met with the defense lawyers in chambers -- much to Brown's surprise -- to discuss any scheduling problems they might have during the trial. When they came out, the judge said he might permit a 2-week interruption during the penalty phase to accommodate the Kraft lawyers in scheduling witnesses.
