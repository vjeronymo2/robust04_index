August 6, 1989, Sunday, Home Edition

FICTION IN BRIEF: THE LINE OF THE SUN BY JUDITH ORTIZ COFER (UNIVERSITY OF GEORGIA PRESS: $19.95; 291 PP.)

Split into two equal and utterly different halves, Judith Ortiz Cofer's novel concerns the break in tradition that defines the first generation of a Puerto Rican family in New York. The narrator of the novel, Marisol, was born in the family's native village, Salud, but was brought to the mainland as a baby. In the first half of the novel, she tells the mythic tales of the Puerto Rican village where her father and her wild uncle Guzman grew up together. Cofer is a poet -- this is her first novel -- and in her descriptions of the small town, her eye for detail brings alive the stifling and magical world of village life: the seething hostilities carried over decades, the power of wives, the privacy that people manage to maintain even in crowded shacks. A pious brand of Catholicism puts the priest nominally at the center of village life. But spirits are also a serious force to be contended with; cleansing and protecting rituals are practiced daily, and the dead are as much a part of the village as the living. Mama Cielo, mother of Guzman and Marisol's mother Ramona, receives a rocking chair as an inheritance from her mother-in-law, but "not trusting her motives even in death, Mama Cielo had always kept the rocker out on the porch."

From childhood on, Guzman is called "the devil's child" because of his uncontrollable, wayward habits. Although his mother's favorite, he brings her nothing but grief. At 15, he flouts all of Salud's conventions by running off to live with La Cabra, a village outcast who is consulted in secret for love potions but is openly scorned as the lost woman of the village. Guzman and his best friend, Rafael, make plans to escape the village. For poor Puerto Ricans who have never traveled beyond the nearby market town, the other place in the world is Nueva York, "la tierra de nieve" -- the land of snow.

In the telling of these stories, Marisol is all but absent, surfacing only occasionally in the identification of characters as "my uncle" or "my father's family." The second half of the novel consists of Marisol's life in El Building, a tenement in New Jersey where Rafael has brought his wife and baby. Rafael is in the Navy and determined to become Americanized. On his rare visits to his family, he speaks only English to his children and rarely reminisces about his childhood. Marisol's knowledge of Puerto Rico comes mainly through tales told by her mother of her wild brother Guzman, who has not been heard from in years but continues to be the main topic in letters from Mama Cielo back in Salud. When Guzman suddenly surfaces at El Building, Marisol finally confronts a part of her family's past.

Cofer's narrative is a bit jerky, occasionally repeating itself, as if many sketches had been strung together. But many of the passages are beautiful, particularly the scenes where Guzman wanders Salud at night, taking leave of the village he plans to abandon forever. The lasting impression from Cofer's novel is the gulf that separates poverty-stricken life on the island and the trapped, tenement life on the Mainland. Although all the immigrants say they will return to Puerto Rico one day, funerals are the only thing that draw them back. For the rest, their desire to return to the village only with all the outward signs of success -- a big car, lots of money -- keep them as inextricably bound to the life of El Building as they were in the native village.
