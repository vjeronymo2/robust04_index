March 14, 1990, Wednesday, P.M. Final

PEOPLE'S PHARMACY: MOST INNOCENT OF MEDICINES COULD IMPAIR MOTORIST

The officer was convinced the elderly woman who had run off the side of the road was drunk. She couldn't walk a straight line, her speech was garbled and she seemed confused. He arrested her on a charge of DWI (driving while impaired) and took her in for a breath test.

To everyone's surprise she passed the test with flying colors -- no alcohol in her system. But she was still impaired, not from illicit drugs, but from the medicine her doctor had prescribed, a combination of a nerve pill and an antidepressant.

Millions of people who would never think of drinking and driving are out on the roads endangering themselves and others because of something as simple as an over-the-counter (OTC) allergy medicine or cold remedy.

Law enforcement agencies in this country are finally beginning to recognize this problem. In California police can pull you over for "driving under the influence" even if you haven't had a drop of alcohol. Physicians, pharmacists and patients have generally underestimated the impact of both prescription and OTC medications on motor coordination and driving ability. Many people confuse impairment with drowsiness. But it may be difficult to tell when you are impaired.

Someone who has had a few beers may feel all right and still be a hazard behind the wheel. Equally, an older person who takes flurazepam (Dalmane) to get to sleep may not realize she is weaving in traffic the next day.

A Dutch study found that the antidepressant amitriptyline (Elavil) at normal doses "had a more detrimental effect on driving performance than the intoxicating dosage of alcohol."

In another test, police officers who took a therapeutic dose of the popular anti-anxiety agent diazepam (Valium) had difficulty keeping their cars in the proper lane.

How can you tell if your medicine will affect your driving ability? Unfortunately, warnings about this problem are often buried in the fine print on the package of an OTC medication. Many consumers overlook cautions and warnings or fail to take them seriously.

Many prescription antianxiety agents and antidepressants have warnings to avoid driving, but doctors may sometimes fail to impress their patients with the danger.

Before you get in your car, review every medication you take.
