BFN ["Special article" by He Chong (6320 3095) ] [Text] Hong Kong, 28 Apr (ZHONGGUO TONGXUN SHE)--Former U.S. President Richard Nixon has already been laid in his final resting place. At the state funeral, President Clinton and former Presidents Ford, Carter, Reagan, and Bush, as well as many other dignitaries were present. It was an occasion of glory and of mourning. Nevertheless, Americans at home and abroad have varying opinions about Nixon's historical achievements and faults. Although Watergate disgraced him, his three major diplomatic achievements were visible for all to see, namely that he ended the Vietnam War through talks, opened the door to the establishment of Sino-U.S. relations by his personal visit to China, and defrosted the Cold War by reaching an arms reduction agreement with the Soviet Union. These three major events have had worldwide influence and will surely be recorded in the history books. Moreover, today, U.S. China policy, which he designed himself, continues to influence the White House and has a positive part in Sino-U.S. relations. Nixon always viewed Sino-U.S. relations from a strategic plane and with foresight. Since stepping down, he has always opposed pressuring China by linking most-favored-nation [MFN] trading status with the human rights question. In his last book "Beyond Peace" he wrote: "Today, China's economic power makes U.S. lectures about human rights imprudent. Within a decade, it will make them irrelevant. Within two decades, it will make them laughable. By then, the Chinese may threaten to withhold MFN status from the United States unless we do more to improve living conditions (of the poor) in Detroit, Harlem, and South Central Los Angeles." Obviously, Nixon predicted from the historical angle that China would become a first-rate power within two decades and that it would be so strong as to get tough with the United States over human rights issues. However, he may have forgotten that China has repeatedly declared that it "will never seek hegemony." Even though China may become strong, it is certainly not going to pressure others using human rights or any other issues. In all events, Nixon never changed his view of China's world status and international role even when Sino-U.S. relations were at a stalemate and going through the most difficult of times. For example, in the wake of the "4 June Incident" in Beijing in 1989, the U.S. administration and other Western countries jointly imposed "economic sanctions" on China, leading to the possible crisis of renewed confrontation in Sino-U.S. relations. In response to this, Nixon, who had stayed out of public life for a long time, publicly stood up to show his disapproval of "sanctions against China" and energetically urged maintaining a channel of dialogue between China and the United States. In late October that same year, despite criticisms from the government and the public, he paid a visit to Beijing, bringing a message from former President Bush to Deng Xiaoping and acting as a broker between them. Afterward, he paid another three visits to China, contributing to the restoration of normal relations between China and the United States. Nixon's firm stance on maintaining Sino-U.S. friendly and cooperative relations was derived from his profound analysis of the international situation. In the 1960's, he was vice president in the Eisenhower administration and was a prominent anticommunist figure. Later, after touring all over the world, his horizons were broadened and he began to realize that it was impossible for the United States to solely dominate the world and nor was it possible to control the world in collaboration with the Soviet Union. At that time the Soviet Union had a wilder ambition to solely dominate the world than the United States. In the meantime, China had already broken through U.S. encirclement and had stood up in the East. In 1968, Nixon won the presidential election. He appointed Henry Kissinger as White House security adviser, revised the foreign policy strategy together with him, pursued the "world triangular balance strategy," and admitted that the world had not only two poles but a third one, namely China. In 1971, he resolved to establish diplomatic relations with China and China also wished to usher in new diplomatic prospects and expand economic and trade exchanges with the West, thus it was high time they established diplomatic relations. The Chinese top leadership pointed out that Sino-U.S. relations were founded on common interests, not on ideologies or social systems. Nixon agreed with this viewpoint. As the 1990's unfolded, drastic changes took place in Eastern Europe and the Soviet Union disintegrated. Some Americans said that "China's role has weakened" and the "CPC will not stand" and that the world has only one pole, the United States. Therefore, they were publicly exerting pressure on China using the human rights issue in an attempt to bring about the "peaceful evolution" of China. This is the root cause of the numerous disputes between China and the United States in recent years. Because whoever starts the trouble should end it, so the Clinton administration should act on Nixon's China policy, face China's speedy economic development squarely, and separate the MFN status issue from the human rights question. Only by doing so can Sino- U.S. relations further develop.
