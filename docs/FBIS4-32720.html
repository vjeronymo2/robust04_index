BFN [Text] Bucharest, ROMPRES, 28/6/1994 -- For a week the city of Cluj-Napoca (North-West of Romania, inhabited by a large Hungarian minority) has been living in a state of tension created by the conflict between the public authorities and the local branch of the Democratic Union of Hungarians in Romania (UDMR). The conflict, which is not something new, because such situations arose several times in the capital of the historical Romanian province of Transylvania, was sparkled by the intention of the local authorities to authorize, at the request of the Transylvanian History Museum, archaeological diggings in the central square of the city where the monument to Hungarian King of Romanian descent Matthias Corvinus is located. The local branch of UDMR is accusing the City Council, and Mayor Gheorghe Funar in the first place, of using the digs as a pretext to move Corvinus's statue to another location. "The archaeological diggings planned to start in the Union Square of Cluj-Napoca are about 100 meters away from the statue of King Matthias Corvinus," the city mayor Gheorghe Funar told CRONICA ROMANA. "The UDMR misleads its members, supporters, and the whole world, pretending that the unearthings pursue to create a state of conflict in the town," Gheorghe Funar also says. His opinion is that the tension aroused in the past few days is "artificially created, has a political connotation and was generated by the UDMR's actions intended to prevent the archaeological investigations in the Union Square." He specified that the initiative comes from the History Museum of Transylvania of Cluj Napoca, which has obtained the approval of all the competent fora, and has allotted 10 million lei to the project. The mayor himself suggested the local council to approve another 30 million lei to sustain the project. Despite assurances from local authorities that Matthias Corvinus's statue will have nothing to suffer, "the UDMR representatives said they would stage demonstrations and urge to civic disobedience, and termed the matter a political issue," Gheorghe Funar said. Should any acts of civic disorder appear in the town, Gheorghe Funar said he would be the advocate of "firm enforcement of the law." The daily ORA carries a commentary in its yesterday's issue qualifying the case as part of "the Hungarian concerted strategy aimed at having the Trianon Treaty canceled or revised." The Treaty of Trianon permitted Transylvania to reunite with the mother country after Habsburg and Hungarian occupation was abolished. The article in question remarked that "the plan is absolutely the same for all the countries having a Hungarian minority," and that "from this point of view, UDMR is one of the spearheads of this intelligent plan, obstinately followed by Budapest." The archaeological investigations due to start next week in Cluj-Napoca intend to bring to light the forum of the ancient city of Napoca, a Roman colony on the territory of ancient Dacia, the cradle of the Romanian people and language. The city of Napoca used to be the capital of the Roman Province of Dacia Porolisensis, and Cluj-Napoca is celebrating this year 1870 years from its first documentary attestation.
