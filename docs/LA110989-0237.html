November 9, 1989, Thursday, Home Edition

L.A. EIGHTH-GRADERS IMPROVE TEST SCORES DESPITE STRIKE

State schools chief Bill Honig owes teachers union president Wayne Johnson a dinner.

Last April, just as eighth-, sixth- and third-grade pupils were taking the annual California Assessment Program tests, Johnson, head of United Teachers-Los Angeles, was gearing up for a strike in the state's largest school district.

"You're going to have real problems" with the CAP scores because of the looming strike, Supt. of Public Instruction Honig recalled warning Johnson. "No way," Johnson shot back, and the bet was on.

This week, when the state Department of Education released the test results, Honig prepared to pay up. Los Angeles Unified School District eighth-graders' average scores showed improvement in every area: They climbed six points over last year's scores in reading, five points in math, eight points in social studies and six points in science.

The district's younger pupils did not fare quite so well. Third-graders dropped slightly: one point in reading, two points in writing and one point in math, continuing a pattern of slow decline in all topics since 1985-86. Sixth-graders went up two points in reading, slid three points in writing and stayed even in math. This was the second year in a row that sixth-graders showed improvement in reading.

While the district remained well below statewide averages in all subject areas, education officials said the results are far from bad news. Honig said the district's improvement in eighth-grade scores shows officials "are responding" to a mandate to improve student achievement.

On Monday, the state released average scores but did not provide individual schools' results or scores for each test subject area until later in the week.

Paul Possemato, associate superintendent in charge of instruction for Los Angeles Unified, said district officials are "very pleased" with the across-the-board improvement in eighth-grade scores but remain unsatisfied with its students' continued lower-than-average test results.

The Los Angeles district has a rapidly increasing proportion of pupils with little or no proficiency in English -- 16.6% of the eighth-graders who took the CAP tests last spring, 23.4% of the sixth-graders and 41% of the third-graders.

"Sometimes our students who are very capable in a subject matter can't deliver as well on the test," Possemato said. "That's an issue, but that's not an excuse.

"We've got to provide a full program that leads to achievement by our students, and we're going to do it," Possemato said, adding that district staff members are prepared to scrutinize school-by-school results over the next month to identify problems. He expects that it will take several years to bring the district up to statewide averages.

Students in the Glendale and La Canada unified school districts continued to do well on the tests.

Glendale's eighth-graders, for example, had a combined score of 278, well above the state average of 263. Its three-year growth in test scores averaged just one point, usually a cause for worry for education officials. But Honig, singling out Glendale as a high-achieving district, said its consistently good test scores should overshadow any concern about a low growth rate.

Although scores in some La Canada schools fell from last year, they were still high. The lowest score earned by any group of La Canada pupils in any subject area was 306.

HOW TO READ THE SCORES

These are results of the California Assessment Program test for third, sixth and eighth grades.

State officials say the scores are intended to provide a way to assess schoolwide academic performance. Students do not receive individual scores.

Schools are listed alphabetically by district. Scores are scaled between 100 and more than 400, and may be compared to district and state averages. For example, if the third grade at your school scored 295 in math, and the state average is 278, children at your school are performing at a higher level than most third-graders statewide.

In general, a fluctuation of 10 points or less should not cause alarm, although state education officials say that the margin of error varies depending upon the size of the school.

The state has eliminated the written expression portion of its eighth-grade multiple choice test and replaced it with an essay test. The results of the essay test will be released next month.

Dashes indicate that tests were not given or that scores were not available.

Schools in this section generally are in the central Los Angeles area and Glendale. Additional scores are in the appropriate suburban section.

Sample Question

Illustrative social studies passage:

In 1860, a Midwestern stagecoach company let people know about an exciting new plan which would affect our entire country. The company planned a faster mail service to California -- the pony express, which would deliver the mail in ten days or less.

Strong young men were the riders. Buffalo Bill was one of the famous ones. These men had to have courage. Every day they faced many kinds of danger. They had been told, "Remember, you travel alone and the country is wild and rugged. You may be cold, hungry, wet and tired, but you must go on! The mail must go through!" And it did go through!

Over the saddle of the Pony Express horses was hung a leather blanket or mochila. In the four pockets of it were the tissue-thin letters. Five dollars was the usual price for sending a letter.

The route cut directly across from Missouri to Sacramento. Each rider rode nonstop for about 100 miles, and then a new rider took over. The horses used were chosen for speed and were changed every 10 miles. Quickly the mail was thrown over the saddle of the fresh horse, much like a relay race. Horses were saddled and ready to go at every station. With a leap, the rider was in the saddle -- and off like the wind!
