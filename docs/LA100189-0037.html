October 1, 1989, Sunday, Home Edition

WALL STREET / JOHN CRUDELE: DAVIS FAMILY HASN'T LOST ITS TASTE FOR TAKEOVERS

Without the Davis family, things would have been very boring on Wall Street these past few months. Marvin Davis, the Los Angeles investor and oil man, tried and failed to acquire both NWA Inc. and UAL Corp.

Both airline companies are being acquired by someone else, but Davis' forays have made money for him and for traders on Wall Street who would have spent the summer doing crossword puzzles had it not been for Davis.

Last week I asked John Davis, Marvin's older son and partner, if the family thought that the economic environment was still favorable for attempting takeovers. I would have asked Marvin himself, but he doesn't talk to the press.

"There are some opportunities we still see in this country," says John Davis, who thinks an economic slowdown that would make doing deals risky probably won't begin for 12 to 18 months.

Even after the slowdown starts, Davis says, his family may keep their takeover machine running by stepping into situations where previously accomplished leveraged buyouts are failing. Davis, of course, wouldn't be specific as to what companies, or even industries, that the clan is looking at.

And, Davis says, Europe could offer some opportunities. Some European conglomerates, he says, "are not rationalized as efficiently as U.S. companies." Take as an example BAT Industries PLC, which is being forced to sell its retail operation, Davis says.

In Europe, Davis says, "there are different obstacles, but different opportunities." Davis claims that he's looking at certain situations in Europe, but hasn't yet decided to act.

"It looks to me like Europe is where the U.S. was in 1980 in terms of inefficient conglomerates that could better realize their economic potential as individual business units instead of diversified, unrelated groupings of companies," says Davis.

On a more controversial matter, Davis says his family was "absolutely" serious about wanting to acquire NWA and UAL, despite critics saying Marvin Davis was simply trying to force NWA and UAL stocks higher so that he could add to his billions.

John Davis says they had followed the airlines for two years and determined that the industry, and particularly those two companies, had good prospects for the next decade.

If so, are there any other airlines in the Davis' future? "I'm not sure what we are going to do. We took a shot on the two most attractive properties," he added.

The roar of Harley-Davidson's stock the past two weeks could have drowned out the sound of one of its famous motorcycles.

Shares of Harley-Davidson were selling at slightly over $30 at the end of August. But this week the stock breached the $40 mark as trading soared to 10 times its normal level.

Too bad that the takeover rumors that caused the increased trading activity probably aren't true.

"No one has been knocking on our door," Harley president Richard Teerlink told me. He adds that his company isn't interested in being acquired, and "we have all the appropriate takeover defenses."

The main rumor stirring up the situation was that Ford Motor wanted to acquire Harley-Davidson. Ford wasn't commenting, but executives at Harley, which is based in Milwaukee, were quickly and quietly telling Wall Street analysts that there was no truth to the gossip.

If you don't like that rumor, there was another one -- a standby, so to speak. Harris Associates, a Chicago investment firm that already owns nearly 13% of Harley, was said to be buying more stock.

But even if Harris was adding to its holdings, and its president Roger Brown wouldn't comment, it doesn't mean that Harley is being chased by a shark. Harris Associates has never attempted a hostile takeover, and sources say the investment firm is so meek that it calls Harley executives any time it buys more stock.

"There is nothing in Harris' background to indicate they are hostile," says Teerlink.

Even if Harley isn't going to be acquired, there may be another compelling reason why its stock should be going up.

Harley, the only major American manufacturer of motorcycles, is doing unbelievably well in a business that once was nearly monopolized by foreign competitors.

Brown of Harris is one of the stock's biggest boosters. He's been buying shares regularly for months and his representatives have had meetings with Harley's chairman, Vaughn Beals, and Teerlink. "We like the management and the value we see in the stock," is about all that Brown will say. "We're always cordial. They are very talented people."

Beals and Teerlink have other admirers on Wall Street. Recently, two brokerage firms, Robert W. Baird in Milwaukee and Dean Witter Reynolds, spoke glowingly of Harley. Daniel Kapusta, the analyst at Baird, says he sees Harley's stock climbing to between $48 and $51 a share within the next year. "On a fundamental basis, the stock looked cheap for a long time," says Kapusta.

Harley's business is "fantastic," says Kapusta. "We think they will have record earnings this year."

Analysts are estimating that Harley's earnings could reach $4 a share this year, easily topping 1988's record of $3.41 a share. And in 1990, profits could rise a full dollar to $5 a share.

Probably more important, say the experts, is the fact that demographics are working in Harley's favor. As the nation's population ages, the number of men in their upper 30s who can afford to indulge in motorcycles is growing. And that's the biggest market for the company's products.

And the loyalty of Harley's customers, the experts say, is unparalleled. "Do you know any other company where customers tattoo the name of the company on their body?" asks one expert.

Harley's only problem, it seems, is the takeover rumors. Not that the rumors will come true. That's not the problem.

The biggest problem is what will happen to Harley's stock a few weeks from now when the rumors don't pan out and Harley becomes just another company that's doing well.

Investors had better be wearing their helmets.

Is F. W. Woolworth Co. really a takeover candidate? The gossip around Wall Street last week was that some raider was about to go after the downscale retailing chain. But several smart traders -- the type who won't buy a stock unless a rumor makes sense to them -- were wondering out loud why anyone would go after a retailer like Woolworth when chains like Bloomingdale's, Saks Fifth Avenue and Marshall Field's are up for grabs . . .
