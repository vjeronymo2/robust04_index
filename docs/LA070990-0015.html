July 9, 1990, Monday, Home Edition

COLUMN RIGHT;

AMERICA IS FREE TO GO HOME;

A SOVIET-GERMAN MARRIAGE OF CONVENIENCE LEAVES LITTLE ROOM FOR U.S. ROLE IN EUROPE.

From the time he was appointed general secretary of the Soviet Communist Party, Mikhail S. Gorbachev has had three principal goals vis-a-vis the West: to prevent the resurgence of a German threat in the heart of Europe, to break the Soviet Union out of its technological and economic isolation and to eliminate a major U.S. presence in Europe.

If Gorbachev stays in power, he has a good chance of achieving all three in this current season of change and reconfiguration in Europe.

For such success -- if he achieves it -- he will be beholden not only to his own undoubted ability but, above all, to the extraordinary diplomatic skill and activity of West German Chancellor Helmut Kohl and his foreign minister, Hans-Dietrich Genscher. For their trouble, they will also have achieved a united, sovereign Germany and left the Soviets, the French and the Americans each feeling that the country whose interest most closely coincides with their own is Germany.

It promises to be a stunning achievement for both Gorbachev and Kohl.

Soviet officials have made no secret of their fear of resurgent German power. What a surprise, then, to hear Soviet Foreign Minister Eduard Shevardnadze say, as he did a few months ago, that the security of Europe is better served by the reunification of Germany than by its division, providing that it takes place in the context of an appropriate pan-European settlement.

The European settlement that Gorbachev has had in mind is one that would "overcome" the division of Europe into blocs and hostile alliances; would integrate the Soviet Union and the East into a common European family; would give the Soviet economy access to Western technology and financial assistance and, of course, would almost as a byproduct eliminate a significant U.S. role in Europe.

It is not wholly clear at what point Gorbachev realized, as Shevardnadze put it, "it is necessary to utilize in depth the potential of a German-Soviet economic cooperation." By the time the Soviets understood that the Germans could help solve their economic problems, the Germans had already understood that there would be no reunification without Soviet support. A marriage of convenience was negotiated that promises to help both partners achieve their hearts' desires.

Kohl and Genscher made themselves representatives of increased Soviet inclusion in international arenas. In addition, Bonn offered financial assistance highly favorable to Moscow. It guaranteed a $3 billion credit for the Soviet Union and agreed that East Germany would pay approximately $750 million toward the cost of supporting 360,000 Soviet troops and their families in East Germany in the last six months of 1990, providing Moscow hard currency at a highly subsidized rate.

Now Kohl, with French President Francois Mitterrand, has sponsored a proposal for $15 billion in Western aid to the Soviets. Next week, Kohl will travel to Moscow to report to Gorbachev on progress so far and to appeal for the Soviets to drop opposition to German membership in NATO -- which the United States and Western Europeans want as their price for supporting German reunification.

But the Soviets' price is likely to be high and some of its terms may be ultimately incompatible with meaningful German membership in a meaningful NATO.

Mitterrand has been a willing and active participant in the Gorbachev-Kohl policy. His own quasi-Gaullist vision of a stable Europe without the blocs coincides with Gorbachev's in some crucial ways. They also share a barely articulated desire to reduce the role of the United States and NATO in Europe and to enhance the Conference on Security and Cooperation in Europe.

Like Gorbachev, Mitterrand looks toward a Europe for the Europeans.

The desired American contribution to the Gorbachev-Kohl design is now clear -- it is for economic aid and a "flexible" policy toward NATO. Where the Bush Administration will come out is not yet clear. My own sense is that a meaningful long-term role for NATO will not survive the current reconfiguration of Europe -- if present trends in the Soviet Union continue -- and that Bush cannot join Kohl and Mitterrand in a major financial aid program when the Soviets are still spending nearly 20% of their gross national product on the military.

But a "no" will not matter that much to the emergence of the new Europe.

Europe is once again absorbed with itself, creating its own complex correlations of power. And America is free to go home.
