Language: Japanese Article Type:BFN [Editorial: "The U.S. Administration Which Turns Its Attention More Toward Domestic Affairs"] [Text] U.S. President Bill Clinton, who has entered the second year since his inauguration, gave his State of the Union address in Congress on 25 January. A major portion of his address dealt with domestic issues and made little reference to diplomatic issues. President Clinton, who became President by advocating his plan "to rebuild America," originally indicated that he planned to place emphasis on domestic affairs. On top of this, off-year elections are to be held this coming fall. As a result, he has apparently made it clearer that he intends to turn his attention more toward internal affairs. The President's State of the Union address includes an outline of the U.S. Government's domestic and diplomatic policies over the coming year. Particularly since this was Clinton's first such address, it has attracted the world's attention. However, it could not avoid being rated as unimpressive due to its domestic affairs-oriented content. In his address, the President praised himself for "making a good start" concerning his first year at running the government. According to research by a periodical specializing in Congressional affairs, over 84 percent of the bills reaching Congress during Clinton's first year as President were passed. Since the periodical began such research in the early 1950's, this figure ranks third after former Presidents Dwight D. Eisenhower and Lyndon B. Johnson. It shows Clinton's extraordinary leadership over Congressional steering. When Clinton was first inaugurated, critics charged that his policies lacked focus and were inconsistent. However, supporters praised his uncompromising realism. Generally, experts give high ratings to Clinton for his accomplishments during his first year as President. His declined support rating has resurged to the 60-percent level he enjoyed at the time of his inauguration. Recovery of the U.S. economy has been the greatest contributing factor in Clinton's regaining his popularity. Whether the President can also make dazzling achievements during his second year depends on whether he can ride on the favorable winds of the time. As prime priority policy goals for his second year, the President presented three pillars: fight against crime, health care system reform, and welfare system reform. Concerning the fight against crime which is of the greatest interest to the American people, Clinton has already sent a comprehensive crime prevention bill to Congress that includes recruiting 100,000 more policemen. As part of the crime prevention plan, a law on hand gun control was enacted last year. It is hoped that greater gun control will be promoted, such as introducing a system requiring gun licenses. Concerning health care system reform which aims at having every American covered under health insurance, President Clinton hopes that Congress will pass the bill worked out under the leadership of Hillary Clinton, the First Lady. Tentatively, the focus of attention will be on the contest between this bill and a number of other bills proposed by some Congressmen which Congress is currently examining. The President has decided to continue with his plan to give priority to problems of the people and boldly deal with social issues which tend to be ignored. The greatest task facing President Clinton is that of solidifying his foundation for reelection by having his party win in the off-year elections. The President hopes to form a majority that encompasses a broad range of the population by winning over the middle class in addition to traditional Democratic supporters. He will need to patch up relations with leaders of the Democratic Party and labor unions with whom he clashed regarding ratification of NAFTA. He also needs to quickly regain public confidence that has been lost due to consecutive scandals involving women and the Whitewater scandal. Regarding diplomatic affairs, a task has emerged for the President to reconstruct his team in charge of diplomacy and national security, as this team has been considered a weak point in the Clinton administration. Bobby Inman, who at first agreed to succeed Les Aspin as secretary of defense after the latter announced his resignation, decided not to accept his nomination. Finally, William J. Perry, deputy secretary of defense, was just promoted to fill the post. There has also been deep-rooted criticism against Secretary of State Warren Christopher and Anthony Lake, assistant to the President for national security affairs. In his State of the Union address, President Clinton indicated U.S. leadership is essential to global stability. For the United States to exercise its leadership without repeating the mistakes it has made in Somalia, Haiti, and former Yugoslavia, it is indeed necessary to promptly form a strong team to handle diplomacy and national security.
