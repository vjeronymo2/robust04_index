BFN [Interview with Ahmed Dogan, leader of the Movement for Rights and Freedoms, by Mehmed Hodjov; place and date not given] [Excerpts] [Khodzhov] Mr. Dogan, by what formula can the government survive today? [Dogan] This is the first time since the DPS [Movement for Rights and Freedoms] assumed the mandate to form a government that the government has faced such an extremely tricky situation. Last week, the main thing that worried us was that the BSP [Bulgarian Socialist Party] was doing everything possible to get its own representative into the government. We rejected this on principle, because the situation on the basis of which the so-called government of experts was formed at the end of 1992 is quite different from the present political realities. In our view, the fact that the BSP has 100 deputies in parliament does not constitute a position in the political sense. This party was in power for half a century; it is affrontery, to say the least, for it to make claims for power now, when we are making a transition and want to change the very thing that the BSP created. Professor Berov, who has very great power and has to bear the responsibility for a definite program that parliament approved, should not try to be a balancing force in the National Assembly and exert pressure on parliament. In my opinion, this is where his mistake lies. At present we are really considering a parliamentary agreement between the different political forces inside and outside parliament. [Khodzhov] This agreement is in the context of the proposal made by the president in his address to the nation, is it not? [Dogan] First, I support the proposal the president made to the nation, but I would naturally like to point out that I am not convinced that this parliamentary agreement is possible, given the extremely complex situation that exists in the country at present. Second, if, on the basis of this agreement, parliament approves a government that is authorized by the president to act as a caretaker cabinet, this will be a new precedent in our Constitution. [passage omitted] [Khodzhov] Mr. Dogan, when you were in Kurdzhali you said that elections will most likely be held in October. What sort of representation does the DPS count on having in the next parliament? [Dogan] The DPS will have a very decent representation in parliament. One does not comment on the predictions, but if you write down what I tell you, you will see that we may even get more seats. A party should have untapped reserves, and we are working on them. Naturally, we will have a completely different platform, updated to take account of the social problems arising from unemployment and the economic program. The great disadvantage of our cadres is that many lightweight people have taken key positions. We are starting right now to select cadres for the next elections, on the basis of certain criteria. [passage omitted] [Khodzhov] Mr. Dogan, do you believe that the Turks are united in Bulgaria? I am thinking of [ex-Chief Mufti] Nedim Gendzhev's DPS [Democratic Party of Justice, with the same initials as the Rights and Freedoms Movement] and of Mehmed Hodja leaving your movement. [Dogan] It would be stupid for the Turks to be united. I would not agree to represent an organization formed on an ethnic basis. We do not need armed soldiers, but we do need thinking people with similar ideas. If Nedim Gendzhev is such clever fellow, why is he forming a Democratic Party of Justice instead of calling it the Party of Free Democrats or some other name? Everyone hovers around the DPS, because it is very important as a symbol. However, whereas earlier it was the former repressions that united us, what must unite us now is our economic interests -- this is the great change in the DPS program. The people will sense this transformation from now on. One cannot create a party only on the basis of rights and freedoms. The man in the street is now banging the table and saying: I am out of work, I have nothing to eat. Previously, we did not appreciate this fact, but it is already knocking and even hammering on our door. It seems that from now on the market mechanism even postulates a fragmentation of society, but let us not discuss this issue now.
