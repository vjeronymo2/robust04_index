July 29, 1990, Sunday, Home Edition

COLUMN RIGHT;

'THE MARKET' IS BLAMELESS THIS TIME;

THE S&L MESS WAS ENGINEERED, AND LARGELY BY DEMOCRATS.

At the Arbatov Institute in Moscow last week, the Soviet expert twitted his guests about American savings-and-loans: "We have had difficulties in our public sector, you have had difficulties in your private sector." What? Private sector?

The people at Arbatov, otherwise known as the U.S.-Canada Institute, are as bright as people can be who have served a Marxist government for so many years, from which one concludes that the myth is widespread that the collapse of the thrifts is a free-market disaster.

Of course, it is not. The $300 billion we hear about every day or two would not exist as a liability but for government. There is nothing more clearly a part of the public sector than a government guarantee, and it is government guarantees that cause the S&Ls to be a noticeable economic phenomenon.

The way the Communists and the Democrats would like to put it, the story goes roughly as follows: Deregulation was a goal of the private sector, motivated by greed. Under the Reagan Administration, the thrifts were deregulated, pursuant to Republican cupidity. Result: The taxpayers are out $300 billion.

That's roughly how Democratic polemicists want to parlay the thrift story, perhaps nudging Neil Bush up as president ex officio of S&L Inc. Anybody who believes that ought to work for the Arbatov Institute.

Deregulation is a good idea, and much of it was done under the Carter Administration. It was then, for instance, that airplane deregulation was done.

What became visible during the 1970s was that there was a credit crunch stifling the thrifts. This was the result of the deregulation of commercial banks, which began then to make loans at commercial rates of interest. The S&Ls pointed out that unless they could compete with the banks, they too would become extinct. You cannot entice depositors by offering them 4.5% in interest when the bank next door is offering 7.5.

The most dramatic moment in the history of the S&Ls came when, in the last year of the Carter Administration, the ceiling on deposit insurance was unobtrusively raised from $40,000 to $100,000. Four legislators, according to an eyewitness, met calmly in a back room during a temporary adjournment and decided to up the insurance. Three Democrats (Sens. William Proxmire and Alan Cranston and Rep. Fernand St. Germain) and one Republican (Sen. Jake Garn) simply decided that 100 grand was more like it, and the next thing we knew, that became the law.

That was a significant raise, which immediately caught the attention of Wall Street money brokers who were happy to place $100,000 chunks of loose change with whatever thrift in town was offering the highest interest. What was there to lose, with Uncle Sam agreeing to guarantee the safety of the deposit? Of course, the S&L offering the highest interest was the S&L in the greatest trouble, the thrift that desperately needed cash.

In due course the thrifts turned to Congress with a complaint: We are free to pay whatever interest we choose to pay to our depositors under deregulation, but we are not free, under existing legislation, to deregulate our "assets." In non-banking lingo, they were saying: Certain things we can't lend money on, and therefore can't make a profit on, so please give us the liberty to move away from staid old home mortgages, and let us swing like the bankers across the street. Whereupon the thrifts went chasing after office buildings, fast-food restaurants and amusement parks. St. Germain, the ever-accommodating House Banking Committee chairman, went further, with a bill authorizing the S&Ls to lend up to 100% of an asset's appraised value, never mind that the actual purchase price was lower. In the words of economist Edwin Rubenstein: "S&Ls could now lend $2 million on a $1-million project, so long as an accommodating appraiser could be found. Speculators and swindlers moved in. Their motto: 'Heads I win, tails FSLIC (Federal Savings and Loan Insurance Corporation) loses.' "

By the time Ronald Reagan took office, 3,300 of the 3,800 thrifts were losing money. In 1986, Treasury Secretary James A. Baker III tried to get through the Democratic Congress a $15-billion bailout plan -- 5% of estimated current federal liabilities. It was torpedoed by House boss Jim Wright and our old friend Fernand St. Germain.

A classic case of the feds making possible an economic catastrophe.
