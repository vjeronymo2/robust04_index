BFN [Maksim Yusin article: "U.S. Administration's Attitude to Russia Could Change If Russian Authorities Follow Opposition's Foreign Policy Recipe"] [Text] Washington-Moscow -- "The day when Russia joins the `Partnership for Peace' program will be a great day for Europe." Bob Graham, Democratic senator from Florida and a close friend of President Clinton, is convinced that Russia will continue to be one of the administration's main foreign policy concerns in the foreseeable future. During a trip to Washington organized by the U.S. NATO mission for journalists from 22 European countries, the subject of Russian-U.S. relations overshadowed all other issues, with the sole exception of the Bosnia crisis. Officials and experts from the State Department, the Pentagon, the National Security Agency, and the Congressional Research Service made no secret of the fact that many points of concern to Washington have recently emerged in Russian policy. First and foremost there is Moscow's skepticism about the "Partnership for Peace" program. Russia's unexpected decision to delay joining the "Partnership" indefinitely was a nasty surprise for the Americans. When nationalists and Communists in the Duma oppose military cooperation with the West, that surprises nobody, but it is quite another matter when the president and defense minister start adopting that stance.... Washington finds the arguments put forward by Moscow against "Partnership" less than convincing. "Russia is demanding special status in relations with NATO. But it will have such a status," was the assurance given by a high-ranking Pentagon official who was speaking to journalists, like most officials, anonymously. "Perhaps not everyone in Moscow realizes that `Partnership for Peace' is actually not one partnership but many. Each side implements its own cooperation program with NATO. When Russian colleagues tell me that they do not want to be playing the same role as Albania, I say: `Look at the map. Yours is a huge country with a very powerful army, you are a nuclear power. Who would think of comparing you with Albania?' Naturally, we will have an entirely special relationship with Russia -- a closer and a detailed relationship. I see no serious reason why Russia should renounce participation in `Partnership' and deliberately condemn itself to isolation." In the Americans' opinion, Moscow's vacillation over both cooperation with NATO and the much-troubled Russian-U.S. joint maneuvers is due to domestic factors. Following the reformers' failure in the December elections, Yeltsin has changed the emphasis in foreign policy, espousing some of the opposition's ideas. These include -- however unpalatable it may be for Washington to recognize it -- the arbitrary anti-Americanism of the Russian "patriots." Researchers from the "security policy research center," who regularly send reports to the White House, Congress, and the State Department, are convinced that since December the Russian authorities have lost their reformist potential, that a rapid retreat into the past has begun, and that the world is on the threshold of a new era of confrontation between Washington and Moscow. Frank Gaffney, one of the center's leading experts, believes that it would be a mistake to involve Russia in "Partnership for Peace" and that the program in general is of no value to the West -- the functions of restraining Russian expansionism could be successfully handled by NATO. Former Secretary of State Henry Kissinger and former National Security Adviser Zbigniew Brzezinski are of a similar opinion. The White House is in no hurry to follow their recommendations. Washington's policy toward Moscow is determined to a considerable extent by Strobe Talbott, deputy secretary of state, who is a Yeltsin supporter and believes that Russian reform will succeed. It is premature to talk about a revision of the U.S. approach to Russia. In the opinion of most of the specialists to whom I talked during the trip, the Clinton administration has staked too much on Yeltsin to suddenly deprive him of support and recognize that the tone in Russia is now set by the nationalists rather than the democrats. "We are proceeding on the basis that the Russian Government is keeping faith with the reform policy, does not plan to implement an imperial policy toward its neighbors, and is geared to cooperation with the United States," a high-ranking State Department official stated. Concern over Russia's relations with the other republics of the former USSR was repeatedly evident during my meetings with U.S. officials. They clearly indicated that, if Yeltsin and his entourage follow the hard-line opposition's recipe in their foreign policy (above all in resolving territorial disputes with neighbors), the Clinton administration's approach to Russia could change. The Americans consider the Kremlin's increasing pressure on the Baltic states and the troop withdrawal delay unacceptable, and, in the event of any infringement of Ukraine's territorial integrity, Senator Graham has promised to employ "effective economic and political measures." But as far as the stepping up of the Kremlin's role in the Transcaucasus and Central Asia is concerned, none of the people I spoke to condemned Moscow -- as if tacitly admitting that the states which have taken shape there form part of its sphere of special interest. No longer seeing Russia as the strategic enemy of the United States, U.S. diplomats clearly prefer Moscow's influence in these explosive regions to be strengthened in preference to that of fundamentalist Iran or other Islamic states.
