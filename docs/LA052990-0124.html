May 29, 1990, Tuesday, Orange County Edition

PSST -- YOU CAN READ THIS, BUT DON'T PASS IT ON;

COPYRIGHT: NEWSLETTERS ARE HAVING A BIG PROBLEM WITH SUBSCRIBERS' MAKING FREE PHOTOCOPIES OF THEIR PRICEY PERIODICALS. ONE CORPORATION JUST AGREED TO PAY A $100,000 SETTLEMENT.

Don't even think about making a copy of this story to pass around the office.

In what may be the largest settlement of its kind, an unidentified major corporation has agreed to pay more than $100,000 to an Arlington, Va., newsletter publisher for violating the publisher's copyright by circulating photocopies of a newsletter to its employees.

The publisher, Washington Business Information Inc., declined Thursday to name the offending company. Washington Business Information publishes 11 newsletters, including Product Safety Letter, Hazardous Materials Transportation, Plastic Waste Strategies and Devices and Diagnostics Letter.

Washington Business Information also would not say which of its newsletters was involved nor discuss the size of the settlement, citing a confidentiality agreement included in the settlement. But the publisher said it had received a sum in six figures, and others in the industry said they couldn't remember a settlement that large in other, similar cases.

Photocopying is one of the banes of the newsletter industry. Subscribers frequently attempt to evade subscription prices that can run hundreds of dollars a year by buying a single subscription to a newsletter and making dozens of photocopies.

"It's not unusual for major industrial companies or major law firms to take one copy of a newsletter and make 30 or 40 copies of it and pass it around," said David Swit, president of Washington Business Information.

"It's self-evident that a newsletter consisting of relatively few pages is particularly vulnerable to unauthorized copying," said Arthur Scheiner, a Washington attorney who has handled copyright-violation cases for some members of the Washington area's huge community of newsletter publishers. "I'd guess that it's rather frequently done."

Some publishers estimate that unauthorized copying cuts potential revenue by as much as one-third. Most newsletters include warnings against copying, and some publishers even offer rewards for information about illegal copying. Federal law allows statutory damages of up to $100,000 and legal fees for unauthorized copying, plus possible criminal liability.

"Copying of newsletters is possibly the largest problem in the newsletter industry," said Albert Warren, founder and head of Warren Publishing, a Washington newsletter publisher whose newsletters include Communications Daily. Warren, who founded the Independent Newsletter Assn., a trade group, in part to combat newsletter copying, is awaiting the outcome of a 5-year-old, $3-million suit against the U.S. Telephone Assn. for alleged unauthorized copying of Communications Daily.

As is common in many such cases, Washington Business Information was tipped off to the violation by an employee of the company that was doing the copying. Swit said the company was making "umpteen" copies rather than paying the subscription price, which is $500 for the first copy and $250 for each additional copy. Swit said his company negotiated a settlement without filing a lawsuit.

Industry officials say most violations of copyright laws are settled, usually with companies making cash payments and agreeing to buy additional subscriptions. Warren said his company has entered into several hundred thousand dollars worth of settlements over the years.

"It's astonishing what organizations will cheat that way," Swit said. "Strangely, some of the most egregious violators of our copyright are lawyers."
