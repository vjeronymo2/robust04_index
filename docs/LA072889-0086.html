July 28, 1989, Friday, Home Edition

THE GALLERIES: WILSHIRE CENTER

In 1903, when photography was still knocking timidly on the imposing front door of art, Alfred Stieglitz began publishing Camera Work. In its 14-year lifespan, the quarterly magazine would make a big case for the aesthetic possibilities of the medium, aided by the most scrupulous printing methods available and far-ranging essays on art and photography.

The images in the journal, displayed individually in this exhibit, are hand-pulled photogravures usually made from the original negatives and printed on Japanese tissue to retain the delicate gradation of tone. Robert Demachy -- whose 1904 photograph, "Speed," shows a roadster disappearing down a soft gray road lined with trees -- noted wryly that "For the initiated or perhaps for the insane . . . there is a most exquisite pleasure in the contemplation of fine shades" of black "independently of form."

Form in the works on view generally takes on a dreamy aura, bearing out the Photo-Secession group's belief that a photograph ought to possess the evident artistry of a painting rather than utilitarian sharp focus. (Stieglitz's views on this matter changed radically by 1917, when he was a champion of "straight" photography, but the show emphasizes Camera Work's early years.)

Karl Struss' "The Landing Place" peers down at a gentle summer scene: a graceful stone balustrade and shady tree overlooking a lake with a lazy rowboat and barge. In "Ploughing," Stieglitz finds a glorious palette of grays in the sky above a farmer and the black and white flanks of his horses. Theodore Hofmeister's "Dachau" brings today's viewer up short with a placid view of white houses and tall trees. (The photograph predates by 29 years the concentration camp established on the outskirts of this picturesque German town.)

Along with nature, young women were favorite subjects. Stieglitz's "Miss S.R." introduces a moodily attractive femme fatale in a pert hat. As an emblem of "Spring," he choose a toddler in a bonnet and flaring coat, planted in profile on the grassy meadow like a giant flower. In George H. Seeley's "The White Screen," a slender woman in a long gown and flowing shoulder wrap is bathed in light-dappled shadow.

Other Camera Work photographers whose work is sampled here include Baron Adolf de Meyer, a specialist in luminous portraits and still lifes, and the mid-19th-Century duo of David Octavius Hill and Robert Adamson, revered by members of Photo-Secession as early masters of the photographic portrait. (Tobey C. Moss, 7321 Beverly Blvd., to Aug. 12.) CATHY CURTIS
