Cooperation 94WB0031B Mexico City EXCELSIOR in Spanish 13 Mar 94 pp 1-A-3, 4-A-3 94WB0031B Mexico City EXCELSIOR Spanish CSO [Article by Ignacio Gutierrez A.] [Text] In fighting drugs, unilateral action is insufficient for dealing successfully with the phenomenon. Therefore, international cooperation is being sought as the only means of consolidating an effective barrier against a supranational type of problem. This statement was made yesterday by Raul Campos Rabago, commissioner of the PGR's [Office of the Attorney General of the Republic] [National] Institute To Combat Drugs. He was participating for the second time in the sessions of the Inter-American Committee for Control of Drugs and Drug Abuse [CICAD], being held in Mar del Plata, Argentina. Addressing representatives from North, Central, and South America, and the Antilles, the Mexican official specified Mexico's achievements in this respect during 1993, describing them as a record in the battle against the drug traffic. He reported confiscations of nearly 46 tons of cocaine, 129 kg of opium gum, 27 kg of morphine, 26 kg of heroin, and a ton of poppy seed. He also cited approximately six tons of marijuana seed, 500 tons of marijuana, 23,000 units of psychotropic substances, more than 2,000 land vehicles, 24 aircraft, and six vessels seized. Moreover, five clandestine laboratories for processing and refining various illicit substances or narcotic precursors were dismantled. Over 200,000 marijuana plantations and approximately 160 poppy crops were seized as well; and 18,000 nationals and 75,000 foreigners involved in committing crimes against health were captured. In the juridical-penal area, Campos Rabago remarked that the most significant action taken in the context of the national antidrug policy was the amendment to the regulations of the Organic Law for the Office of the Attorney General of the Republic. It created the National Institute To Combat Drugs, assigning a sector of the Federal Judicial Police to that decentralized agency, to achieve an ethical, professional police force devoted exclusively to fighting the drug traffic. At the same time, he gave a reminder that, early this year, several articles of the Federal District Penal Code pertaining to both the common jurisdiction and the entire republic were amended, given additions, or repealed. Including Articles 193 to 199, and 201, regulations pertaining to crimes against health were established, to provide for more severe punishment. Addressing the representatives of the CICAD's 24 member nations, and those of the observer states, he stressed that all the national and international action taken to combat drugs is reducing the power of the criminal associations. Nevertheless, he noted that, to deal with the problem with modern, really effective methods, "it seems essential to us that all countries have a juridical frame of reference, a national program, and a central coordinating and executive organ. Hence, we take the liberty of urging those who have not yet done so to ratify and, if pertinent, sign the United Nations Convention of 1988 against the illicit trafficking of drugs and psychotropic substances." He explained that, with that expedient, "many of our nations have taken steps to harmonize their legislation, which is an essential factor for dealing with the problem on a regional and international scale, in a coordinated manner. This will make it possible to accrue increasingly positive results in this battle, which can be won only through the political will and concerted action of all nations." Raul Campos Rabago emphasized that, in the case of the Mexican experience, "our country has always stressed that the causes and not merely the effects of the drug traffic must be attacked." In this forum, he reiterated Mexico's conviction that it was imperative to promote alternative development of underprivileged rural areas, where extreme poverty has fostered the illicit crops as a means of subsistence. He continued: "In my country, great importance is attached to suitable planning and regional coordination of the strategies for combating drugs and drug addiction. The aim is to reduce consumption and, hence, the demand for and illicit trafficking of drugs and psychotropic substances, as well as the associated activities. "It is highly desirable to promote the establishment of coordinated action in the interior and among the various geographical areas comprising the American Continent, always based on the principle of respect for national sovereignty and jurisdictions."
