January 14, 1990, Sunday, Home Edition

VICE VERSA: WEAKNESS INTO ART;

T. S. ELIOT AND PREJUDICE BY CHRISTOPHER RICKS (UNIVERSITY OF CALIFORNIA PRESS: $25; 290 PP.; 0-520-06578-6)

In 1933, the poet Thomas Stearns Eliot gave a series of lectures in Virginia, eventually to be published under the title "After Strange Gods." The lectures focused on the importance of a cultural tradition, specifically the American tradition in the Southern states, but the lectures will be remembered more for furthering the charges of Eliot's anti-Semitism than the cause of tradition.

Controversy centers on a passage about the need for stability in a society developing a tradition: "The population should be homogeneous. . . . What is still more important is unity of religious background; and reasons of race and religion combine to make any large number of free-thinking Jews undesirable. . . ."

There also are early poems, such as "Burbank With a Baedeker: Bleistein With a Cigar," that express anti-Semitism: "The rats are underneath the piles./The Jew is underneath the lot."

Combined with these, the statement about "free-thinking Jews" has made anti-Semitism as lasting a part of Eliot's legacy as his receipt of the Nobel Prize for Literature in 1948. But Christopher Ricks reconsiders the controversy.

In Eliot's prose and poetry, Ricks finds a sensibility struggling to rationalize its own prejudice and the nature of prejudice in general. "Eliot is from the beginning preoccupied with prejudice because it constitutes an intersection of philosophy, psychology, politics, and art." It also is a struggle that Eliot did not always win.

Ricks does what is needed to an issue that has sat, growing in its own sensationalism, for the last 50 years. There is no doubt about his appreciation for Eliot's mastery as a poet, but he does not excuse Eliot's anti-Semitism either. What Ricks offers are explanations, acknowledging their incompleteness, that may help us toward an understanding not only of Eliot's biases but of prejudice in general.

This position has been challenged by Leon Wieseltier, and Ricks addresses Wieseltier in the book. Wieseltier says the horror of the Holocaust cannot be explained. The victims of that brutality are innocent; "it came unbidden from the outside." To understand the issue would mean assigning the victim a part in the cause, but Wieseltier argues "when the victim is an accomplice, evil is -- banal."

The brutality of the Holocaust certainly makes discussions of anti-Semitism difficult in this century. But if we accept Wieseltier's argument, Ricks says, prejudice becomes irrational, and this abolishes "the chances of the rational reduction of prejudice." To treat it rationally, no matter what the pitfalls, is to maintain the chances for change.

But understanding prejudice is easier in theory than in practice. Ricks devotes a long chapter to the nature of prejudice. What is discussed, supported with the work of Edmund Burke and William Godwin in the 18th Century, is the necessity of prejudice.

Ricks draws on many interesting sources for his information. William James' observation on the conformity of young professionals shows, gratifyingly, that my generation is not responsible for the yuppie. Allan Bloom, sounding controversial but actually making good sense, says he tries to teach prejudice to his students. For him, it is important that his students believe in something before they learn to doubt.

The enormity of the issue creates an attitude in the book similar to "He that is without sin among you, let him cast the first stone." What Ricks is doing is deflating our tendency to sit in judgment of another person's supposed guilt.

Is the tone of the lines "In the room the women come and go/Talking of Michelangelo" from Eliot's "The Love Song of J. Alfred Prufrock" obvious? Literary critics Hugh Kenner, Helen Gardner and Grover Smith are assured that the speaker is condescending. Gardner and Kenner hear whiny feminine voices, while Smith says the women talk "no doubt tediously and ignorantly." Ricks shows that these assurances are unfounded; the lines are actually very toneless, and open to many interpretations. It is a lesson to us that even our critics, trusted for their explanations, are not infallible.

This ambiguity is not accidental, nor is Eliot unaware that he is loading the title of his first volume of verse with a clunker, "Prufrock." He knew what the name would do to people's expectations of a poem called a "love song." It was Eliot's way of showing how easily we make judgments, even on the basis of a name; "how so little information controls so much behavior." Already with his first volume, Eliot was winking at the public's predictability, and making satire of it.

One convincing argument concerning anti-Semitism in Eliot's poetry is treating it in relation to content. An error that readers make, and Eliot exploits, is to assume the printed word always reflects the true feelings of the author, when it may be only the dramatic persona's.

The speaker of "Gerontion" is a sick man with a "dry brain," living in a decaying house (house so often is a metaphor for the body). A person in such a rotting condition may foster a questionable perception of the world. When we read that "the Jew squats on the window sill, the owner," we must remember the questionable mental state of the speaker. In fact, in much of the poetry cited for instances of anti-Semitism, Ricks says, the narrator's authority is questionable.

Other explanations are connected to Eliot's relationship with his father. Eliot grew up in a Unitarian household, the son of a strict father, and William Empson interprets Eliot's anti-Semitism as a symbolic repudiation of his father and his father's religion.

Empson takes Eliot's opinion that the Jewish religion, liberalized, becomes a "colourless form of Unitarianism" and says, "If you are hating a purse-proud businessman who denies that Jesus is God, into what stereotype does he best fit? He is a Jew. . . ."

Ricks' own opinion is more tactful. One of the more offensive poems, "Dirge" ("Full fathom five your Bleistein lies/Under the flatfish and the squids"), was a poem Eliot never published -- it happened after his death -- and he never permitted a second printing of "After Strange Gods." Ricks sees in these actions a recognition of his mistakes. And this is the premise that guides the second half of the book, taking him from "The Waste Land" to "Four Quartets."

In doing this, Ricks takes us and the concept of prejudice into the mechanics of poetry. Eliot's meticulous care for the formation of lines is discussed in relation to his influences, Shakespeare and Dante. Ricks recognizes Eliot's debt to them, while still maintaining the innovation Eliot made in 20th-Century poetry.

Ricks' careful attention to Eliot's poetics is a joy to read. He enhances the poetry for those who, when they read poetry, whip through it as if it were a faster-format newspaper.

It would not be inappropriate if his book were subtitled something like "A study in the art of making poetry," because he illustrates the poet's tools better than one would expect (due to a prejudgment, no doubt?) in a book about prejudice.

It is clear from this study that poetry, in a sense, requires prejudice. Poetry is the synthesis of the poet's words with the reader's comprehension. Most often with drama, the artist deliberately leaves mood up to the actor. An expression like "Ah, soldier!" from Shakespeare's "Antony and Cleopatra" has many interpretations. The woman utters it after Cleopatra's death, and then she dies too. So is this a cry of pain? Or does she mourn the queen's death?

Eliot's struggle with prejudice is connected to his belief that weaknesses could be turned into art. He once said of Dostoevsky that he studied his epilepsy and hysteria in order to open up a "personal universe." Ricks says the same of Eliot. And this self-contemplation of human spirit, Ricks reminds us, also includes "human personality at its lowest." The low points for Eliot are when anti-Semitism finds expression in his poetry.

In the entire study, the area excluded, surprisingly, is Eliot's anti-Irish sentiment. "Apeneck Sweeney," a stereotype of the violent, uneducated Irishman, appears many times in Eliot's poems, but is overlooked here. However, Ricks says more biographical inquiry into Eliot's life is needed, and maybe this will be a part of the next study to penetrate even further into Eliot's "Gioconda smile."
