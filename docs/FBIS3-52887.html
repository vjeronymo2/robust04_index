Language: English Article Type:BFN [Text] New Delhi, Jan. 2 (PTI) -- India and China will soon start talks on reducing troop-levels on both sides of the line of actual control (LAC) in pursuance of an agreement signed during the Prime Minister P.V. Narasimha Rao's visit to Beijing last September, Chinese ambassador Cheng Ruisheng, said in an interview here. "Both sides may have already reduced some troops, but I am not aware of the facts. Both sides want to divert their resources from military to economic development," Mr Cheng said. The Chinese ambassador said that an experts group of diplomats and military personnel, set up as part of the agreement on maintenance of peace and tranquillity along the LAC, would hold its first meeting in New Delhi next month. He said the experts group would tackle the issue relating to the redeployment with a view to reduction in military forces in the areas along the LAC. The experts group, which will have about 10 members from each side, shall advise the Foreign Secretary-level joint working group (JWG) on the resolution of differences between the two sides on the alignment of the line of actual control. Replying to wide-ranging questions, Mr. Cheng said there was no Pakistan factor in China's development of relations with India. Describing "stability and overall improvement" as the main characteristics of the Sino-Indian relations in 1993, he said if China used India or Pakistan as a card for developing bilateral relations, it would "complicate matters very much." He emphasised that India and China had achieved a breakthrough in exchanges in the military field. The visit of the Chinese vice chief of Army staff to India last month came after a gap of over 30 years. Like-wise, the Chinese naval ships visited Bombay nearly after 40 years. India and China had problems in 1962 but that was part of history now. "We are trying to catch up with the lost time," the Chinese envoy said. He emphasised that there was no such gap in the development of China's relations with Pakistan. Mr. Cheng agreed with the questioner that the human rights issue was being exploited by the west. The Chinese envoy said India and China had identical views on the human rights issue. "We are of the opinion that differences on the human rights issue should not be linked with bilateral relations including trade or economic ties," he said. Replying to a question on the nuclear issue, Mr. Cheng referred to differing perceptions between India and China. China had already signed the nuclear non-proliferation treaty. India was not prepared to do the same, he observed. Mr. Cheng said his country was in favour of a nuclear weapons free zone in South Asia as there was no nuclear power in the region. China, he said, felt that the two major world powers should reduce their nuclear weapons. Asked about the Chinese military assistance to Pakistan, he said this did not pose any threat to India. He made the point that China was involved in commercial sales of military hardware and India could also be a buyer if it so desired.
