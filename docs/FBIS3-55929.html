Language: Russian Article Type:BFN [Article by Yelena Ovcharenko and Leonid Timofeyev under the "Ambassadorial Rumors" rubric: "How Diplomats Are Packaged"] [Text] The resignation request from Russian First Deputy Foreign Minister Anatoliy Adamishin was submitted to President Yeltsin even before the New Year holiday. The reason for this was his election to the State Duma as a representative of the Yabloko bloc. And he will be replaced as deputy minister by the current Russian ambassador to Spain, Igor Ivanov. He is well-known in the Foreign Ministry, as he headed that department's general secretariat for a long time. An additional two ambassadors will occupy offices in the skyscraper on Smolensk Square. They are Aleksandr Panov, ambassador to South Korea; and Albert Chernyshev, who has represented Russia in Turkey for six years now. The Russian Foreign Ministry has made it known that all these reshuffles are of planned nature and are in line with the principle of staff rotation. They do not hide their satisfaction there that leading posts will be occupied by career diplomats. The fate of the ambassador in London, Boris Pankin, will probably be different. For the time being he remains there, but the decision to replace him was made long ago. According to our information, the Tetrapak company, which intends to pool its efforts in the near future with the French company, Alfa-Laval, on learning that Boris Pankin was about to leave his post, immediately offered him a job as a consultant in "any country" of his choice. He has supposedly opted for Sweden, where, we remind you, he was once ambassador. Tetrapak produces packaging, including cartons for milk. Some are moving into commerce, others returning to big-time politics. When we rang Washington, [Ambassador] Vladimir Petrovich Lukin was upbeat. In masterly fashion he parried our probing question, "Is it true that you are down to be deputy speaker?" "I am ready for anything? Let's see when we get there. Incidentally, the job is not that attractive, it's pure anxiety and turmoil, particularly if they decide to turn the speaker into a lightning rod." Vladimir Petrovich said. "I don't envy the speaker--he will have a tough job." While observing that he knew several suitable people who could occupy the vacant ambassadorial posts, Lukin was reluctant to give their names so as to avoid, as we understood it, inadvertently doing them an ill turn. On the other hand, he gladly told us about the attributes which they, according to the diplomat, should possess. In Washington he would like to see someone with considerable experience and knowledge of both the language and the country. The Washington Embassy throughtout has been a source of alternative information for the president, the government, and the Foreign Ministry. A valuable quality for those who know how to avoid being in thrall to "convenient" assessments.
