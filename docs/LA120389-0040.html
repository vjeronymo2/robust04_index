December 3, 1989, Sunday, Home Edition

ANTIQUES: AROUND HOME;

COLLECTING SNUFF BOXES

THE TAKING OF snuff reached its height in Europe during the 18th Century, when it was a habit almost universally enjoyed, particularly in elegant and sophisticated circles. Some indication of its popularity can be seen from the many delightful snuff boxes available to the collector today.

Snuff got started in the Western world with the North American Indians, and its introduction to Europe was attributed to the 16th-Century French ambassador to Portugal, Jean Nicot. Its use quickly spread from one country to another, reaching England by way of Ireland and Scotland. Indeed, the carved and painted figure of a costumed Highlander in the act of taking snuff was once the common sign outside a snuff-seller's shop.

By the 18th Century the habit was an important part of daily life, and snuff was found everywhere. The ordinary man carried the powder in boxes made of horn, copper or brass, wood, tortoise-shell, papier-mache and enameled copper. The aristocracy, on the other hand, had far more elaborate containers: gem-encrusted gold boxes, silver, ivory or anything equally exotic. The snuff box became an article of men's jewelry, and it was said of a certain Lord Petersham that he owned so many of them that he was able to select a different one for each day of the year, and for his changing moods as well.

The most extravagant boxes were undoubtedly those made by the French during the eras of Louis XV and Louis XVI. The Paris goldsmiths used colored gold, enamels and precious stones in which some of the costliest designs are reminiscent of feminine jewelry of the day. Often, boxes were decorated with miniature portraits, and some were furnished with false lids concealing erotic subjects.

Snuff boxes come in all shapes and sizes. Generally, lids were decorated, often painted, and sometimes inset with representations of famous castles ("castle tops" regularly showed views of Windsor Castle). Some have compartments for different grades of snuff, and others have watches built into them. Examples from present-day auctions range from a Scottish silver-mounted cowrie-shell box, c. 1810 ($175), to a Victorian castle scene, 1838, by Nathaniel Mills ($700), or a George III silver-gilt fox-mask box, c. 1807 ($4,320).

By the 19th Century it had become traditional for monarchs to present snuff boxes as marks of honor to distinguished foreign visitors, and the custom continued long after snuff ceased to be popular. Today a collection of such boxes adds a charming accent to the top of some 18th-Century or contemporary table.

Look for snuff boxes at Richard Gould in Santa Monica; Frances E. Klein in Beverly Hills; Speidel Antiques in Pacific Palisades; Green Dolphin Street and Jay's Antiques, both in Pasadena; Snooty Fox in Ventura; Richard Yeakel and Roberta Gauthey, both in Laguna Beach; Castle Antiques in Costa Mesa, and Mission Gallery and Hood Antiques, both in San Diego.
