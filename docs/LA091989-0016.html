September 19, 1989, Tuesday, Home Edition

FIGHTING THE WAR ON DRUGS

The Times should be lauded for publishing an unpopular alternative drug plan ("Anyone Care What Works?" by Ethan Nadelmann, Op-Ed Page, Sept. 6). The trouble with Nadelmann's plan is that it does not go far enough.

Heretofore, it may be true that government has respected free enterprise and just taxed and regulated "sins." But drugs are such a big "sin" that government will have to get directly involved.

The best way to kill this "blue chip" $110-billion business is to have the U.S. government take it over. Set up government drug stores, register users and sell drugs at a fair market price which will drive illegal dealers out of business. And if it doesn't, cite them as we now do bootleggers and bookies. Define drug abuse and addiction as social-mental diseases requiring treatment and hospitalization. Circulate lists of users to employers, schools, military services, government agencies, etc. Inform parents of use by minors, and require that parents try to stop drug abuse in the home.

With the profits that even government would somehow manage to make, for the first few years at least, we could treat addicts, and build and fund special hospitals and halfway houses. We would free penitentiary cells, court calendars could be cleared and the police and military could go back to chasing muggers. And maybe the gangs would go back to spraying graffiti and just shooting one another.

For the "less fortunate" who cannot afford drugs, we could give drug stamps and clean needles. Perhaps then our persons, homes and cars would be safer.

Am I serious? As serious as were Voltaire, Mark Twain and Will Rogers.

LAWRENCE M. FISCHBACH

Claremont
