May 17, 1989, Wednesday, Home Edition

'VOICE' OF THE PHILHARMONIC TO BECOME CBS RADIO FILM CRITIC

Gail Eichenthal, the "voice" of the Los Angeles Philharmonic broadcasts for more than 11 years and the first woman to announce the national radio broadcasts of a major symphony orchestra, will leave her post at KUSC-FM (91.5) to become a film critic for CBS Radio.

The 35-year-old Eichenthal has hosted the KUSC concert series -- distributed by American Public Radio and heard on 95 stations -- since 1978 and in 1983 began producing the show.

"I've been involved in concert music as a broadcaster and producer virtually all my adult life and I love it passionately, but I've felt the need to stretch myself in new directions for some time," she said.

Eichenthal's last live Philharmonic broadcast will be in June, but her authoritative and informative broadcasts will be heard for several months thereafter as the season's shows are repeated through December.

Though she is excited about her new endeavor (her reviews will be heard nationwide), she is not without "a tremendous amount of sadness" in leaving KUSC.

"This is like leaving home," she said. "In a lot of ways I grew up here."

Wally Smith, KUSC's general manager, said: "Gail (and her production team) have set a new standard in the sound, production and quality of writing in live concert music radio programming," Smith said. "Gail has been the linchpin in that effort."

Her decision to leave KUSC was propelled, she said, by "a hankering for the real world."

A self-described lifelong "news junkie," Eichenthal will also take on newswriting and reporting assignments for KNX Radio, the local CBS affiliate.

The decision to move into other journalistic arenas has been several years in the making, Eichenthal said.

KNX's news director Robert Sims has long been a fan of Eichenthal's. "She's a particularly gifted writer with a wonderful sense of radio," Sims said. "That kind of talent is quite a find regardless of whether the person is involved in music or news."

Sims said Eichenthal's film reviews, which premiered April 17 and air Mondays through Wednesdays, have been "very intelligent, thoughtful and insightful."

Sims first called Eichenthal in 1985 and persuaded her to produce a series of entertainment reports for his station.

When the film critic's job became available, he urged her to try out.

"I didn't think I had a chance," she said. "I was up against some real film critics."

But, Sims said, Gail had that special spark, a rare ability to communicate warmth and insight. "Gail's efforts had the sound of someone with something special to say, despite her rather thin film background," he said.

Though a music major at UCLA, Eichenthal says she has been a film buff for years.

"Films have been a great release for me," she said. "I go to movies incessantly."

In a recent movie review, Eichenthal praised a small, low-budget film: "Right under our noses the most marvelous mysteries are going on every moment if only we realized it. That's the refreshing premise of 'Some Girls,' a terrific romantic comedy with a French-Canadian flavor." She describes a house in the film as "a Gothic masterpiece filled with ancient tapestries and spooky Art Deco antiques," and a pack of relatives living in it as "so bizarre they make the Addams family look like they fell out of a Norman Rockwell poster."

She would like to "keep a hand in classical music" and hopes to work on special projects with KUSC and the Philharmonic.

"I'm so in love with the Philharmonic that if there's any way to continue that association I will do whatever it takes," she said.

Among the highlights of her dozen years with KUSC and the Philharmonic was her first national broadcast on Oct. 26, 1978 -- a date indelibly etched in Eichenthal's memory -- the four foreign tours she has taken with the orchestra and the opportunity to work with former Philharmonic music director Andre Previn, who resigned April 25 because of dissatisfaction with the organization's management.

Touring with the orchestra enabled her to learn more about the day-to-day life of the musicians.

"Part of my job has been to impart a sense of what it is like for the musicians," she said. She said she has endeavored to persuade listeners that classical music is "not for an elitist audience," and has tried to humanize the orchestra by giving listeners an idea of what is involved in being a concert performer.

"I have a tremendous amount of pride in being associated with an orchestra of that caliber," she said.
