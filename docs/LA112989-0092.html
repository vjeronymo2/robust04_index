November 29, 1989, Wednesday, Home Edition

BAY AREA REAL ESTATE SHAKEN UP;

HOUSING: SALES OF SINGLE-FAMILY HOMES WERE DOWN 26.7% IN THE MONTH OF OCTOBER. BUT THE POPULAR VIEW IS THAT THE MARKET WILL BOUNCE BACK ONCE THE QUAKE DAMAGE IS REPAIRED.

For the seller of one house in San Francisco's posh Marina district, it was a nightmare.

The Oct. 17 earthquake gave a buyer cold feet, and a $950,000 deal fell out of escrow. But another buyer from Hong Kong immediately stepped in, and the house sold a few days ago for $900,000.

In hard-hit Santa Cruz, one broker is selling a corner lot for $175,000. The five-unit, yellow clapboard apartment building that had stood on the site -- and might have commanded a price of $300,000 if it had not rattled apart in the quake -- lies in a heap.

Throughout the Bay Area, real estate brokers and would-be home sellers and buyers are feeling economic aftershocks from the devastating 7.1-magnitude temblor. Sellers have lowered prices. Buyers have backed out of deals. Escrow closings have been delayed and brokers are suffering reduced sales commissions.

Numbers released Tuesday by the California Assn. of Realtors gave the first firm indication that the quake, indeed, had a negative effect on property values. Bay Area single-family home sales fell a sharp 26.7% in October from the year-ago levels. But perhaps more telling, the median home price dropped 1.4% from the previous month.

But brokers -- facing lost or reduced commissions -- attempted Tuesday to cast the figures in as favorable a light as possible.

After all, they say, one can blame some of the decline on the normally slow holiday season. Moreover, after a long period of torrid sales and price increases, housing activity in the San Francisco area and elsewhere in the state had tapered off, putting buyers in the driver's seat and keeping a lid on prices.

And, brokers point out, some housing transactions that were disrupted by the quake to allow buyers time to inspect properties anew are simply taking longer to work their way through escrow.

In the 15 business days after the quake, escrow closings on single-family dwellings in the Bay Area declined an average of 12% to 15% from the 15 business days before the temblor, according to John Karevoll of Dataquick Information Services, a data company in La Jolla. San Francisco County showed the sharpest drop, 26%. Last year, he said, there was no significant difference between the first half and the second half of the month.

Ann Scherbert, resident sales manager in the San Francisco office of Coldwell Banker, a real estate brokerage firm, blames much of the October decline on the season. "And we're in a buyer's market," she said. "People have been denying it for quite some time, but we're clearly in it."

Figuring the temblor would scare off some buyers, Coldwell Banker advised many would-be sellers to hold off a bit before putting their houses on the market immediately after the quake.

"It takes some time to recover," said real estate broker Pius Lee, a Marina resident. "When you repair (all the damage), the market will come back. I still have a lot of confidence that the houses will continue to go up in the neighborhood."

Lee's California Realty & Land Inc. represented the Hong Kong buyer of the $900,000 home in the Marina, in a prime location across from the Palace of Fine Arts. The buyer had actually made a stand-by offer of more than $900,000 when the home was in escrow with the original $950,000 bid. After the earthquake, the original buyer halted the deal, and the Hong Kong bidder stepped up, asked to view the property, then negotiated the lower price because of some quake damage.

The seller, Lee said, was "very happy" because it would have been difficult to capture a $900,000 price otherwise.

Lee expects housing prices to remain soft for at least another month or so, with 5% to 10% declines typical in the Marina and other heavily damaged areas.

He figures that his agency's business is off by 15% to 20%. At least one $800,000 apartment sale on Post Street near the Tenderloin was lost because of the quake. "My saleslady felt bad," he said. No wonder. She lost out on a $40,000 commission.

Marina sales activity undoubtedly suffered from the presence of barricades erected after the quake to keep out gawkers as utility workers and contractors went about the business of shoring up damaged buildings and restoring power.

Until last Thursday, only residents who showed identification with their home addresses were allowed past guards. Power and water service were only recently restored to some residences; many Marina residents have had to shower in trailers set up in the middle of streets by the Department of Water and Power.

Broker optimism aside, clearly it will take some time for the memory of crushed neighbors, falling-down buildings and flattened cars to fade. On several corners in the Marina district, apartment buildings that came crashing down had to be demolished. In all, 60 homes and apartment buildings were lost in the district. One apartment building hung a triangular green sign reading "Safe."

Scherbert of Coldwell Banker, also a Marina resident, said most buildings that tilted off their foundations have been pushed back into place "so they don't look so cockeyed." Sidewalks are being repaired, and things are largely getting back to normal. Now that non-residents are once again strolling the neighborhood, Scherbert expects business to pick up.

In Santa Cruz County, 63 residences were destroyed by the quake. Throughout the picturesque college town of Santa Cruz, the streets echo with the sounds of hammering and buzz-sawing as repair workers fix up damaged homes.

At the corner of Center and Elm streets, a sign for Susan Liebl Properties has been hung on a window of a collapsed 50-year-old apartment building. It stood on poorly compacted soil in the so-called liquefaction zone, where shaking was magnified and many buildings suffered extensive damage.

The San Francisco owner put the property on the market immediately after the quake. Liebl, whose firm is based in nearby Aptos, said she hopes the deal will close about year-end, 2 1/2 months after the temblor. Meanwhile, the owner is losing rental income.

"He is recouping close to his investment (in the property)," Liebl said. However, "he just has to eat" the loss of appreciation.

Liebl noted that she got about 30 calls on the listing.

"Buyers are thinking they can pick up properties for a song," she said. "But I don't see a lot of people jumping to put houses on the market. You don't see a lot of panic selling.

"I don't think the earthquake truly scared anybody away except my relatives in Kansas."
