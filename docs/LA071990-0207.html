July 19, 1990, Thursday, Orange County Edition

POP BEAT: O.C. BLUES DUO FINDS SUCCESS AFTER FINDING ITS OWN STYLE;

WILL BRADY AND JODI SIEGEL GAIN ACCLAIM AFTER GIVING UP AN UNCOMFORTABLE, HYBRID MUSICAL PERSONA AND DOING WHAT COMES NATURALLY?

For a while during their 10-year musical partnership, Will Brady and Jodi Siegel tried to be songsmiths, crafting would-be hits that they imagined being recorded by big-name pop stars.

There also was a time when they would play Beatles songs, figuring that was what the people in the Orange County pubs and restaurants they played wanted to hear.

Eventually, though, Brady and Siegel decided that they had to please themselves before they could be a hit with anybody else. The way to do that was by playing the blues.

"We were trying so many styles. We were doing whatever it took to keep the gig," Siegel said as she and Brady sat in a Laguna Beach restaurant Tuesday night, recounting how Brady & Siegel turned from an uncomfortable hybrid into an assured electric-blues duo. "People could see it wasn't true to who we were. They know when it's not authentic, or when you're not liking it."

"You become non-recognizable, lose your identity," Brady chimed in without missing a conversational beat. Brady and Siegel have been working together long enough for their speech patterns to become as well-synchronized as their interwoven guitar lines.

Brady, now 45, and Siegel, 34, found the answer to their musical identity crisis while noodling around between sets during those unsatisfying pop-hybrid days. Every time they had a few minutes to kill, they would unthinkingly start playing the blues.

If that's what came naturally, the two guitarists decided, that's what they would be best off doing all the time. It was a smart decision: In the six or seven years since it was defined by the blues, Brady & Siegel has evolved into an uncommonly fiery act that can reach back to the country blues tradition of Robert Johnson or leap forward into modern modes that echo such blues-based rockers as Little Feat, Bob Seger and Chuck Berry.

Playing the blues didn't turn out to be a bad career move either. Instead of having to worry about keeping steady gigs, Brady and Siegel have settled into a regular routine on the local bar scene. Every Thursday night, they play at the Old Dana Point Cafe -- a gig they've held for 2 1/2 years -- and every Tuesday night for the past three months, they've been appearing at the Marine Room Tavern in Laguna Beach. In 1989, Brady and Siegel jumped into a Hyundai and took their blues around the country on a five-week tour, an experience that Brady said "gave us a feeling that it is possible to make all this happen" -- namely, stepping out of the small rooms of Orange County and onto a national stage.

Opening for Albert King recently at the Coach House in San Juan Capistrano, Brady, a tall, affable man with a furrowed, balding pate, and Siegel, with a tight tangle of flaming-red hair framing sharp facial features, proved that they can win a crowd of deep-blues aficionados. When they were introduced, silence greeted the two guitarists' names. It was Freebo, their sideman for the night, who drew a ripple of applause in recognition of his past tenure as Bonnie Raitt's bass player.

Before long, though, the audience was hollering approval for Brady's clean, stinging Telecaster solos, delivered with back-arching body English, and for Siegel's unfettered raucousness on slide guitar. Between liberal solo trade-offs, both members showed a convincing, intense way with a blues vocal.

"You can't play little, polite blues," Brady said of the duo's aggressive method. "You have to go in there slugging. It keeps you honest."

"You can't fake the blues," Siegel echoed. "Either you can play it or you can't. It has to be part of your makeup. I get up on that bandstand, it's my fun zone. I'm on vacation."

Siegel grew up in Chicago, the world's blues capital, where Duane Allman's signature solos for the Allman Brothers Band inspired her to take up slide guitar. But most of her public performances as a teen-ager centered on folk and pop.

She moved to Orange County at 19 to attend Orange Coast College. Gravitating to the local club scene, she ran into Brady. The guitarist had been in on the ground floor of the Orange County music scene, running the lights at the Golden Bear in Huntington Beach during the folk boom days of 1964-65, when Roger McGuinn and David Crosby would drop in to play. In the mid-1970s, Brady played bass for three years with Honk, the only Orange County rock band of that era to make a national name for itself. (He still takes part in Honk's periodic reunion shows.)

The two played together occasionally, then struck up a close musical partnership when Siegel and her blues-aficionado husband, Sid Shultz, took a top-floor apartment in a Laguna Canyon house where Brady was the downstairs neighbor.

After years of performing together, the duo's first bid for wider recognition was its 1989 debut album, "Beer & Donuts," which was produced by Freebo and financed largely by Patrick Farrah, a wealthy benefactor who would show up at Brady & Siegel gigs offering $100 for every Jimmy Reed song they would play.

The album, available at Brady & Siegel shows, includes only one original song. Entitled "She Takes What She Wants," it's a saucy number that allows Siegel, a temperate, low-keyed woman offstage, to step into the hot and rowdy onstage mode she often favors.

"The next (album) will have a lot more original songs," Siegel said. "If you want to make it in the blues world, you've got to have some originality, your own sound that distinguishes you from everyone else."

As a redhead who plays a mean, traditional-sounding blues slide guitar, Siegel knows that it will take some effort for her to establish a distinct identity.

"People come up to me and say, 'Bonnie Raitt, right?' " she said. "I say, 'No, I'm not Bonnie Raitt.' I guess it's inevitable. I'm honored to be compared to a four-time Grammy winner, but I don't want people to think I'm trying to be her, because I'm not. Any female blues player who grew up in the '70s (had to be) influenced by her. But I was just as much influenced by Freddie King and Duane Allman and Etta James."

While waiting to take their next career step, Brady, who still lives in Laguna Beach, supports himself primarily as a guitar teacher; Siegel, now a Dana Point resident, works days in a record store.

The point, Siegel said, is to play for the enjoyment of it and let any future career ascent flow from that. "We'd be doing this anyway," she said. "We'd be doing it for our own personal therapy."

"Any musician has a dream about being recognized by your peers and making enough money so you can relax a bit and not worry about where your next dime is coming from -- which is the way it's been all my life," Brady said. "But I'd be doing it if I were on a desert island."

Siegel's deadpan response: "That's our next gig. I got a call from Gilligan."

OFF-TANGENT: A show next Wednesday by Agent Orange, the Swamp Zombies and Medicine Rattle will be the finale for Club Tangent, the weekly alternative-rock showcase at the Marquee in Westminster.

However, Kitty Bash and Octavious, the two young promoters who have been putting on Club Tangent shows since last summer, appear to have found a new outlet: Starting next month, pending final agreement on a deal they have negotiated with owner Ray Camacho, they will book Thursday and Friday night shows at the Pink Cadillac in Anaheim under the moniker Tangent Productions.

Club Tangent's four-month run at the Marquee is ending because it wasn't generating enough money, according to L. Trojan Tabak III, co-owner of the 452-person-capacity club.

Bash said the Marquee's identity as a haven for hard rock and heavy metal made it difficult to draw fans from the alternative-rock scene. Lacking a budget for advertising, she said, Club Tangent was not able to reach a potential clientele that normally wouldn't think of going to a heavy-metal club. Tangent drew an average of 80 to 100 concert-goers at the Marquee, Bash said, although in recent weeks, shows by Tender Fury and Caterwaul had boosted attendance to 180 or 200.

"We usually broke even, with sometimes a little extra (profit)," Bash said.

From a club-owner's perspective, Tabak said, the Tangent crowd had a serious drawback: an inadequate thirst for bar drinks.

"We served more water than anything else," he said, noting that the alternative-rock fans spent an average of $4 each on drinks, only half of the per-customer bar revenue on other weeknights. Tabak said he is considering offers from other promoters who have proposed booking blues acts, hard-rock bands or deejay dance nights on Wednesdays.

Bash said Camacho invited the Club Tangent team to help him promote shows at the Pink Cadillac even before they learned that they were losing their slot at the Marquee. The Pink Cadillac already has established an alternative-rock identity with shows by some of the same bands that Club Tangent featured.

"I'm kind of sad, because I think the Marquee was a really neat venue, but we're going to be picking up where we left off," Bash said. She said that she and Octavious expect to sign a contract to book Thursday nights in August at the Pink Cadillac, then expand to Thursdays and Fridays at the end of the month, beginning with an Aug. 31 concert by Firehose.

COLOR IT ORANGE: Club Tangent's last concert will mark a homecoming for Agent Orange, which hasn't played its native county since a 1987 show at Night Moves. Now based in Los Angeles, the band features a splendid new lineup, with founding singer Mike Palm backed by Brent Liles and Derek O'Brien, the bass-and-drums team from Social Distortion's early days.

According to manager Moss Jacobs, Agent Orange has negotiated a long-sought release from its recording contract with Enigma Records. "We're going to examine a lot of possibilities and look at taking the band to the next step," said Jacobs, who is general manager of Avalon Attractions. As part of its severance agreement with Enigma, Agent Orange will record a live album of songs previously released on the band's studio albums and EPs. The recording takes place Saturday night at the Roxy in Hollywood, where Agent Orange will play two shows with the tape rolling.

T.S.O.L. GETS L.O.S.T.: Also cut free from Enigma -- but not nearly so happy about it -- is T.S.O.L. The long-running local hard-rock band was among the acts whose contracts were dropped in a major shake-up last week at the Culver City label.

"It was quite a shock to all of us," said T.S.O.L.'s manager, Mike Zoto. The band's current album, "Strange Love," was to have been the first in a multi-album deal reached last year. "After you let it sink in and release your emotions, it could be OK," Zoto said. "You can't sit and dwell on it. You've got to go out and make something happen. But it's something very new and has to be pondered for a while." Currently on a national tour, T.S.O.L. is scheduled to play Aug. 11 at Jezebel's in Anaheim.

The Enigma shake-up has producer Jon St. James feeling nervous about the possible impact on Bardeux, another Orange County act on the label's roster. "It doesn't affect our (recording) deal, but it affects everyone at Enigma" in terms of the label's ability to promote current releases, St. James said. "I understand (Enigma) cut the staff considerably," he said. With a new Bardeux single, "Shangri-La," surfacing with dance-club play, he said, "I hope the company is there to back it up."
