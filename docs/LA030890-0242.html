March 8, 1990, Thursday, P.M. Final

LIZ SMITH: MADONNA GETS IT 'ALTOGETHER' FOR VANITY FAIR

"I SLEEP a certain number of hours every night, then I like to get up and get on with it. I set aside the hours I have to exercise. Then I set aside the hours for creativity. Yes, I can summon my creativity!" says Madonna to Kevin Sessums in the sizzling new Vanity Fair.

This April issue hits it big with Madonna on the cover and on nine pages inside, photographed seductively by Helmut Newton (who else?). Of course, there is an obligatory Newton-type picture of Madonna baring one breast. This is the first look at a part of Madonna's "altogether" since those early nude shots (made when she was little more than a young girl) surfaced in the skin magazines.

But there is a lot more to Madonna's interview than tantalizing glimpses of her famous flesh. The star talks candidly about her fame: "Listen -- how can you say I'm a private person if I'm in show business? Just because I conduct myself in a particular way, you couldn't straight-facedly call me a private person." On Warren Beatty, her "Dick Tracy" co-star: "He's been an icon for years . . . obviously, somebody who hasn't experienced it would be more threatened by my fame than he is. . . . We're just perfect together." Madonna breaks into tears while discussing her ex-husband, Sean Penn. She says the divorce was "like a death to deal with. It's very, very difficult." And, of course, Madonna addresses the Sandra Bernhard did-they-or-didn't-they question. They didn't, says Madonna. It was all in fun, a hoot, but, "It got out of hand and then I didn't want to do it anymore."

But typically, Madonna's most potent remarks concern AIDS and the Catholic church. "I want to do anything I can to promote AIDS education. . . . Aside from Hitler, AIDS is the worst thing to happen in the 20th Century. The sad thing is that it makes people even more bigoted. It gives people a reason to vent their true feelings about homosexuality." And how does the Catholic superstar view the Catholic church's stand on homosexuality? "I think it's disgusting. I think it's hypocritical. And unloving. It's not what God and Christianity are all about."

MADONNA ASIDE, the most fascinating article to me in this particular issue is Gerri Hirshey's interview with Judith Campbell Exner. It is the heart-wrenching story of how Judith has been reunited with the son she gave away at birth (an effort to protect the child from all the FBI surveillance, torment and media furor that came about after she was revealed to be a John F. Kennedy mistress and a messenger between the President and mobster Sam Giancana).

This is the first story ever to do justice to the miseries of Exner, who has been painted as a kind of scarlet woman because she dared to sleep with a married man. To make matters worse, many were scandalized that Judith resembled Jacqueline Kennedy.

About the son -- his father is a well-known movie executive. He has been advised of the boy's existence but wants nothing to do with him. Exner's child, when he became an adult, sought her out and recently found her. She is mortally ill with cancer, but he visits her frequently and is devoted. The Judith Exner story is not a pretty one but, at least, it has the glimmer of a happy ending.
