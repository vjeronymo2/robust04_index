April 8, 1990, Sunday, Home Edition

HINTS FROM HELOISE: FOR EASTER, DECORATING HOLLOW SHELLS IS BEST WAY TO AVOID WASTING EGGS

DEAR HELOISE: My children like to decorate Easter eggs but I hate to waste three or four dozen good eggs. Any suggestions?

-- JEANNE, Kansas City, Mo.

DEAR HELOISE: One of my favorite traditions here in San Antonio is cascarones -- decorated hollow egg shells. They're simple to make and should be just the thing you're looking for.

Whenever you use fresh eggs, don't crack the shells -- blow them out instead. This takes a little more time than just cracking them, but it's easy to do. Be sure the egg is clean, then poke a pin hole in the small end of the shell. Next, using a knife or spoon, gently crack a small dime-size circle at the other end and carefully remove it.

Be sure to remove the inner "skin" that lines the eggshell so that the egg can come out. Hold the egg over a cup or bowl and blow into the pin hole, forcing the egg out the broken end.

Run water gently into the empty shell to rinse it, blow out the water and set it aside to dry thoroughly.

The empty shells can be dyed, painted or decorated with markers the same as hard-cooked eggs. Just be sure to handle them gently. Close the open end by pasting a small piece of colored tissue paper over it.

Traditional cascarones are filled with confetti before the end is sealed. Breaking the egg and sprinkling the confetti on someone is supposed to bring good luck.

Start several weeks ahead to make sure you have plenty of eggshells, knowing that you can have your Easter eggs and eat them too.

-- HELOISE

DEAR READERS: Easter will soon be here and so will the traditional Easter-egg hunts. Children will be searching here and there, Easter baskets in hand, for those colorful Easter eggs.

Adults should be aware that special precautions need to be taken when storing hard-cooked Easter eggs before and after the hunt.

Hard-cooked eggs should be cooled and refrigerated immediately or they just may become a source of food poisoning. This is also true for foods containing eggs, like egg salad, deviled eggs and potato salad. If the Easter eggs are destined to become a side dish, make sure they are not left at room temperature for more than two hours.

If you hide hard-cooked eggs for an Easter-egg hunt or display them in a centerpiece, either follow the two-hour rule or do not eat them.

Here's to a happy and safe Easter for you and yours.

-- HELOISE

DEAR HELOISE: How can I get spaghetti-sauce stains out of my children's vinyl place mats?

-- CHERI, Houston, Tex.

DEAR CHERI: Soak the place mats in an enzyme stain-remover bleach mixed with a little detergent and warm water. Follow the directions on the box for amounts. After soaking, scrub the spots with a brush and then rinse thoroughly.

If the stains remain, put the place mats outside on a hot, sunny day and let the sun bleach them out.

-- HELOISE

DEAR HELOISE: As a young, working homemaker I've learned to cook quick, easy meals for my family but I still have one problem -- planning interesting, well-balanced menus. I've been getting ideas, though, by looking at the frozen dinners in the supermarket.

Each dinner has a meat, a starch and one or two vegetables. I make a note of the different combinations, then duplicate them on my own. This helps me avoid repetitious meals and my family enjoys the variety.

-- SALLY, Rockwall, Tex.

DEAR HELOISE: This may sound trivial, but my children and I would really appreciate some help. Is there any way to keep peanut butter and jelly sandwiches (their favorites) from getting soggy in their lunch boxes?

-- PAT, Oxnard

DEAR PAT: Not trivial at all to a true peanut butter and jelly lover. Spread peanut butter on both pieces of bread, then add the jelly. The peanut butter will keep the jelly from soaking through.

I'm sure other peanut butter and jelly lovers have hints too, so please share them.

-- HELOISE
