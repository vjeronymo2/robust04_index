September 14, 1989, Thursday, Home Edition

FOOD AND COMMON SENSE: WRONG MIX OF MEDICATION, FOOD CAN LEAD TO NUTRITIONAL DEFICIENCIES

Many of us try to choose our foods to promote good health. Some of us also take medications -- either over-the-counter or prescription -- for the same reason. But what happens when the two are at odds?

Research has shown that use of certain drugs can lead to nutritional deficiencies. And by the same token, diet can block the effectiveness of some medications.

It makes sense that foods and drugs interact within the body. Yet the chemical intricacies of how they do so remain a gray area. Meanwhile, medications on the market multiply every year, widening the problem's scope.

Some facts are known about the effect of drugs on nutrition. Digitalis, used in the treatment of heart disease, has been found to depress appetite. Many other drugs can upset the stomach, causing you to eat less. Over a long period of time, such a side effect might result in significant weight loss and depletion of nutrients.

Nutrient Absorption Blocked

Some drugs impair the body's ability to utilize nutrients. One of the first to be shown to do so was mineral oil, once commonly used as a laxative. In some people it hampers absorption of vitamins A, D, E and K. Laxatives cut down the length of time foods stay in the body, reducing the opportunity to absorb the nutrients they provide. And some laxatives also can affect the lining of the intestine. Likewise, antibiotics alter the intestine, affecting intake of glucose, cholesterol, sodium, potassium, calcium, iron, carotene and Vitamin B-12.

Another group are the antacids containing magnesium or aluminum hydroxide. Binding themselves to phosphorus, they make phosphorus hard to absorb. They also interfere with absorption of folic acid by making the gastrointestinal tract more alkaline. And potassium chloride, which is used to protect potassium supplies, raises the acidity of the intestine, reducing uptake of Vitamin B-12.

Other drugs can hasten the destruction of vitamins. Patients on long-term anticonvulsant medication may develop a bone disease called osteomalacia, possibly because the drug stimulates enzymes that inactivate Vitamin D, which is essential to bones.

Still other drugs cause nutrients to be lost through urinary excretion. Taking aspirin may increase excretion of Vitamin C. Isoniazin, a drug used to fight tuberculosis, binds with Vitamin B-6, which is then excreted by the kidney.

With all the drugs we've been discussing, the extent of the nutritional problem caused will depend on the length of time involved, rather than on the dose. In other words, occasional use of medications won't jeopardize your nutritional status, but long-term use could open the door to nutritional deficiencies.

The flip side of the coin is that as drugs can alter nutritional processes, so food may affect the action of drugs. If you drink milk when you take the antibiotic tetracycline, calcium will block the drug's absorption. Other foods affect other medications differently, and the effect is not always negative. For instance, anti-fungal medications are absorbed more readily if taken with a high-fat meal. And while fruit juices may interfere with absorption of some antibiotics, the acid environment they create is helpful when it comes to taking iron supplements.

Some foods contain ingredients that counteract the effectiveness of a medicine. Foods high in Vitamin K (essential for blood clotting) such as broccoli, beef liver, cabbage, asparagus and spinach may inactivate anti-clotting medication. And if you're taking the anti-depressant monoamine oxidase inhibitors, you should stay away from foods rich in tyramine such as Cheddar cheese, avocado, bananas, soy sauce and chicken liver. These foods can bring about serious complications. Also, megadoses of Vitamin B-6 work against L-Dopa, used to treat Parkinson's disease.

So, what's a body to do when medications are necessary over the long term? The wisest step is to read directions on the bottle or package to find out just when (before or after a meal) and how (with water, milk, etc.) any drug should be taken. It's also important to consult with your physician about what dietary course to follow.

When it comes to mixing drugs and food, you should give yourself every chance to make the mix both effective and safe.

Updated Figures Give Eggs Lower Cholesterol Content

Question: Are eggs today lower in cholesterol than they used to be? I heard that on the radio and wonder if it's really true.

Answer: Updated figures for the cholesterol content of eggs are considerably lower than those first published by the U.S. Department of Agriculture more than 20 years ago. Indeed, the results of a nationwide study conducted jointly by the USDA and the egg industry indicate that the average large egg contains 213 milligrams of cholesterol, down 22% from earlier estimates of 274 milligrams. These results are based on analyses of samples representing more than 60% of the eggs produced in this country.

A major reason is the availability of superior methods of laboratory analysis, making more accurate determinations possible. But there may be real differences in the amount of cholesterol in eggs now available. It has been suggested that changes in feed and increased production of eggs by hens result in eggs that contain less cholesterol.

But even at these lower levels, eggs remain the main source of dietary cholesterol for most Americans.

Q: Is it true that one out of five adults in this country is overweight?

A: Alas, the figure is higher. The most recent estimates come from data obtained by the Behavioral Risk Factor Surveillance System (BRFSS) for 32 states and the District of Columbia in 1987. Conducted by state health departments and coordinated by the National Centers for Disease Control, the surveillance system's telephone surveys have been providing valuable self-reported behavioral and personal-health practices information since 1981.

That survey found the prevalence of overweight to be 21.8% for men and 21.1% for women. Prevalence varies considerably by region. It is lowest in the West (17%), followed by the Northeast (19.8%), the South (22%) and the Midwest (23.1%).

But if anything, these estimates, based as they are on self-reporting, err on the side of underestimation.

In the second National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey, where individuals were weighed and measured, the prevalence of overweight was 24.2% among men and 27.1% among women. Given the increased risk of a number of diseases associated with excess weight, even the lower figures are too high.

Q: Can honey cause botulism poisoning in infants?

A: Honey has been linked to increased risk of botulism in infants and is currently not recommended as a suitable food for babies under 1 year of age, and especially not before they are 6 months old. But the occurrence of infant botulism cannot be explained by the use of honey alone. In fact, while as many as 29% of the affected subjects in early studies had been given honey, in later studies that figure had dropped to about 20%.

In an attempt to better understand the infection, which has been diagnosed in 600 infants since it was first described in 1976, the Center for Infectious Diseases of the Centers for Disease Control recently conducted a study of all known cases that had occurred over a two-year period in every state but California between April 1985 and March 1987. The objective was to identify factors common to babies who got botulism but absent from a similar group who did not.

Risk factors were found to change with age. For infants less than 2 months old, living in a rural area or on a farm was the only consistent factor. For those 2 months and older, however, the condition was more likely to occur among those who were breast-fed, among those who had a history of decreased frequency of bowel movements for at least two months, and among those who had been given corn syrup, which has occasionally been found to contain low levels of botulism spores. The use of honey also remained a factor, although only 11 babies in the case studies had consumed it.

These data do not provide a cohesive approach to preventing botulism. The report's authors concluded that while avoiding honey does make sense, the relationship with corn syrup requires further clarification. And the higher prevalence of botulism among breast-fed infants should not be viewed as a deterrent to breast-feeding. The reason for the association with breast-feeding remains a matter of conjecture. Moreover, botulism is rare, and the benefits of breast-feeding far outweigh any possible increase in risk.

Infants who do not have a fever but who show a reduced ability to feed, and in whom no other infection can be demonstrated, should have a stool specimen submitted for detection of the toxin, especially if there has been a change toward less frequent bowel movements.
