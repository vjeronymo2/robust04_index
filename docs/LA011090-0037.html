January 10, 1990, Wednesday, Home Edition

CONSUMER VIEWS: CONSUMERS;

GUIDE FOR THE DISGRUNTLED MAY HAVE YOUR NUMBER

Question: Like a lot of people, I frequently feel the need to complain to someone -- anyone -- when I feel that I've been ripped off. But where do I start? Writing to a department store, for instance, is like dropping the letter into a big hole. Any suggestions? -- G.M.

Answer: Even with a Rolodex filled with consumer-oriented contacts, I know and share your bafflement. You should find a new publication by San Francisco-based Consumer Action invaluable, however. It's the 1989-1990 edition of the group's 16-page pamphlet, "Consumer Services Guide," and it includes the addresses and telephone numbers of regulatory and law-enforcement government agencies, business-sponsored complaint resolution offices, nonprofit information and referral organizations and agencies that offer free, or low-cost, legal assistance. I use it constantly.

You can get a copy by sending $1 for postage and handling to: Consumer Action, 116 New Montgomery St., San Francisco, Calif. 94105. Free copies of the guide are available in bulk to community-based organizations.

Q: I was deeply disappointed in the answer you gave recently on the way the California Lottery publicizes its large prizes -- a deceptive and fraudulent practice. Instead of condemning it for the deception that it is, you condoned it, and you further muddied the waters with a discussion of taxes.

Perhaps the most basic concept in all of economics is the notion of the time value of money. A dollar to be paid tomorrow is not worth as much as a dollar paid today. The only way you can call that lottery prize "a million dollars" is to assume that interest rates are zero.

The quote that a lottery representative gave you: "the money we spend to pay a $1-million prize is exactly half that," is a bald-faced admission of that lie. Your newspaper could end the deception tomorrow. All it would take would be for you to agree that whenever you run a story about a lottery prize, the number in the article and the number in the headline should be the present value of the award. -- K.G.M.

A: I wouldn't presume to argue with a university professor of mathematics even if I didn't agree with you in principle.

I quite understand the concept of the value of money and I am not condoning the lottery or the people charged with conducting it. I explained the state's rationale for doing it the way it does it -- to keep public interest in the lottery at a fever pitch. I don't recall saying that I approved of this fun-with-figures way of presenting it.

I agree with you -- any state lottery is a piece of fluff right from the time you buy a $1 ticket, only 50 cents of which goes into the "pot" in the first place. And, with odds of 14- to 16-million-to-1, this is "gambling" in about the same way that diving blindfolded, and armed with a spear, into an Olympic-sized swimming pool containing two guppies is "going fishing."

This is pure fantasy. The state knows it and the lottery players know it and, speaking of odds, I will lay odds that the next person hitting a "$10-million" top prize doesn't give a diddly what the "present value" of that $10 million really is -- he sees 20 annual payments of (before taxes) $500,000.

Q: Last April, American Continental Corp. declared bankruptcy. I am 76 years old and my wife is 67. We had invested $45,000 in this company. It was money earned with hard work to provide an extra income to supplement our Social Security. About 25,000 other investors were involved, and the company used the money to buy luxury cars and houses.

We don't know where to turn. Is there any law in this country to protect the little investor? -- Z.L.

A: I wish we could be more optimistic here, but it's going to take a long time for the federal regulators to muddle through the complex, overlapping affairs of American Continental and its many subsidiaries -- the principal one being Lincoln Savings, which did, indeed, sell about $200 million in bonds to individual investors. And most of those investors, unfortunately, are in the same boat as you. Bondholders like you fall under the heading of "unsecured creditors" and, in the pay-back line, are behind the other creditors holding so-called "secured" claims on the company.

"Laws to protect the little investor," alas, don't really work. With any investment there must be some degree of risk. There are laws, of course, requiring that the degree of that risk be fully disclosed. And this is where tightening up is called for. All you can really do, I'm afraid, is hope that enough of American Continental's assets can be sold off to get the bondholders at least a portion of their (your) money back.
