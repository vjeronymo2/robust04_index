BFN [Text] San Jose, 29 Mar (ACAN-EFE) -- Costa Rica has abandoned the "banana war" it was waging with other Latin American nations against the European Union (EU) and accepted an offer shortly after the defeat of Costa Rican Foreign Minister Bernd Niehaus as OAS secretary general. Roberto Rojas, Costa Rican foreign trade minister, told ACAN-EFE that this agreement, negotiated last week with Rene Steichen, EU commissioner for agricultural matters, "has nothing to do" with Colombian President Cesar Gaviria's election as OAS secretary general. Rojas assured that the largest banana producers in the Caribbean, such as Belize and Jamaica, voted for Costa Rica on Sunday in Washington and that the agreement with the EU safeguards the advantages it has always granted to these small nations. At the OAS election, some Caribbean nations, as well as Panama and Ecuador, which had committed their votes to Costa Rica, broke their pledges at the last minute and voted for the Colombian president. This gave Gaviria the victory to lead the OAS for the next five years. These events caused strong misgivings in Costa Rican diplomatic and political circles because, according to Rojas, vigorous U.S. pressure in support of Gaviria included the use of the banana issue as a negotiating tool. Nevertheless, the Costa Rican minister assured that the "banana war" did not have a bearing on changes in OAS votes, although there was an attempt to link the "banana war" to Foreign Minister Niehaus' bid to become OAS secretary general. Neihaus lost to Gaviria by a vote of 20-14. The European commissioner today appeared satisfied with the agreement which, besides resolving the dispute, "assures that the EU's objectives in regulating banana imports will continue to be respected." For Steichen, this guarantees respect for the interests of EU banana consumers and producers, as well as of the producers of the fruit in Africa, the Caribbean, and the Pacific. Costa Rica was the most ardent challenger -- followed by Colombia -- of the EU restrictions that have been in force since July. Costa Rica now will benefit the most by this agreement. The agreement grants Costa Rica a 23.4 percent share (25 million cases) of the quota established for all banana imports from Latin America. Of the 2.1-million ton quota for this year and 2.2-million tons for next year, Colombia will obtain 22 percent, Nicaragua 3 percent, and Venezuela 2 percent. The agreement also includes a reduction in (import) tariffs from 100 ECU's [European Currency Units] ($112) per ton to 75 ECU's ($75), as well as granting 70 percent of the import licenses to the producing nation. This was one of the most controversial issues in the Latin American-EU dispute. Europe imposed a quota system on Latin American banana imports on 1 July, reducing its purchases from 2.5 million to 2 million tons. This unleashed charges from the Latin Americans -- led by Costa Rica -- before the GATT, which ultimately ruled in favor of the producers, claiming that the EU system violated basic GATT principles. The other four nations -- Ecuador, the world's largest producer that will have a smaller quota to export to the EU; Panama; Honduras; and Guatemala -- will continue their struggle before GATT. Of these nations, only Guatemala is a full member of this trade agreement, while Honduras is in the process of joining. After a meeting with the Costa Rican producers, Rojas said this agreement will allow Costa Rica to arrive feeling satisfied at the signing of the Final Document of GATT's Uruguay Round on 15 April in Marrakesh, Morocco. Rojas stated: "This agreement will create market and price stability while resolving this very difficult controversy for Costa Rica and the producers." At the same time, Rojas highlighted the importance of the European market for his country. Rojas assured that his country will remain a member of the Union of Banana Exporting Countries and will attend all meetings in which this matter is studied. Nevertheless, he made it clear that the bloc that had negotiated with the EU has been dissolved. In this regard, Rojas said Costa Rica will attend the meeting of producing nations to be held in Panama on 8 April to explain the reasons for and the terms under which Costa Rica accepted the EU's proposal.
