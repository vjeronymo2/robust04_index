Language: English Article Type:BFN [By Kevin Barrington] [Text] KONG KREAL, Cambodia , March 6 (AFP) -- Cambodian officials said Sunday the Thai military helped Khmer Rouge [KR] guerrillas, backed by tribesmen, recapture a major base in northern Cambodia which was taken by government forces early last month. "Thai military helped fresh Khmer Rouge troops come through Thai soil from Pailin," General Khin Yean said referring to the Khmer Rouge's main headquarteres in northwest Cambodia. The general said there was no doubt the guerrillas were helped by the Thai military in retaking the base at Anlung Veng which served as the Khmer Rouge's headquarters for guerrilla operations in north and central Cambodia. "I am 100 percent sure, not 99.9 but 100 percent sure," the general said, speaking at this rear control base about 50 kilometres (30 miles) west of Anlung Veng. All around the base troops were carrying out repairs on tanks and bringing up supplies for what government officers said would be an imminent government counter-attack. The general was emphatic in saying he did not blame the Thai government for Phnom Penh's defeat at Anlung Veng. "But we blame the Thai military and Thai businessmen who co-operate together to make money from logs," he said. The fresh Khmer Rouge troops, who numbered up to 500, were joined by small groups of guerrillas from surrounding areas and by several hundred hill-tribe fighters, the general said. Government troops said they were frightened by the hill-tribesmen. "We shot at mountain people but they kept on coming, it's like they were not afraid to die," said one soldier, who retreated through the jungle for two days without food and water. "I think the Khmer Rouge gave them some special medicine and they get drunk. They came at us like they didn't seem to care, like life was not important," private Penn Nouvat said. Some of the Montagnards were in Khmer Rouge uniform but others were dressed in civilian clothes, government soldiers said. General Khin Yean said the Montagnards were from the Kouy tribe who came from Cambodia's remote northern and northeastern provinces. Ta Mok, the notorious one-legged Khmer Rouge general and commander of Anlung Veng, hired them as mercenaries and was paying them handsomely, he said. The Khmer Rouge were not short of money, he said, as they sold concessions to log in the rich forests surrounding Anlung Veng to Thai business interests, many of them controlled by the military. A government official in the provincial town of Siem Reap said the Thais were helping the Khmer Rouge as they had a lot of money tied up in logs that had been cut but were still lying on the ground around Anlung Veng. "There was about 150,000 cubic meters of wood lying there when we went in and a cubic meter (35 cubic feet) of top quality timber sells for around 80 US dollars," the government official said. Government officers confirmed reports that trucks crossed over from Thailand to start collecting the wood soon after the base was recaptured by the Khmer Rouge on February 24. Government officers said the guerrillas were equipped with sophisticated anti-tank weaponry, which they believed to be German made "Armburst" anti-tank missiles. "I think that Thailand sold this kind of weapon to the Khmer Rouge because we have never seen them use it in action before," tank commander General Ben Hort said. But a government military source in Phnom Penh said the weapons may have been given to the royalist FUNCINPEC [National United Front for an Independent, Neutral, Peaceful and Cooperative Cambodia] forces and the non-communist Khmer People's National Liberation Front (KPNLF) and then sold to the Khmer Rouge who were their allies in the resistance war against the Phnom Penh regime. The war ended with the signing of the 1991 Paris peace accords which paved the way for United Nations supervised elections and the formation of a coalition government made up of the Phnom Penh regime, FUNCINPEC and the political wing of the KPNLF.
