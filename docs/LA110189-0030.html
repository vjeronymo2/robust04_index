November 1, 1989, Wednesday, Home Edition

'ISRAEL DIVIDED ONCE AGAIN'

The answer to "Israel Divided" may rest with Israel's president, Chaim Herzog. Mainline American Jewish organizations should press him to intervene in bringing about the necessary peace negotiations. The longer they are delayed by Shamir's tactics, not only will Israel suffer, but support for the Jewish state will be fragmentized.

Herzog's views, made public in the Oct. 1 Opinion piece by Tad Szulc, were that the situation had reached a point where he (Herzog) had to " 'step over the line' of presidential prerogatives to protect the national interests." And as Szulc notes, Israel's president has the background, temperament and public trust to bring the peace process -- which Herzog insists is "irreversible" -- into being.

Yet, at a subsequent meeting at which the Los Angeles Israeli consul general was the speaker, his response to a question as to the powers of the Israeli president, in light of Szulc's article, was surprising.

He had read the article, but questioned its authenticity as he had yet to see an "official" transcript, and from his personal knowledge of Chaim Herzog, he doubted the president would inject himself into the current Israeli political stalemate.

The Conference of Presidents of Major Jewish Organizations, while staying clear of Israel's "politics," however, can inject itself. Peace and American interests in the region are issues that are the agenda of all its constituents and their respective memberships. It remains central to Israel's future as a nation and link to Jewish life in the United States and elsewhere.

HYMAN H. HAVES

Pacific Palisades
