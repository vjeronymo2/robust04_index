November 20, 1989, Monday, Home Edition

VAN TO VAN COMBAT;

IT'S CHAOS, SOMETIMES, AT LAX AS MORE THAN 30 FIRMS VIE FOR PASSENGERS.

It was smooth going in front of the Los Angeles International Airport one recent Wednesday as two Department of Airport officers policed the movement of airport shuttle vans in and out of Terminal 1. But once the officers left, the usual chaos resumed.

Within minutes, 12 airport shuttle vans from five different companies descended on the passenger island. About half of the drivers jumped out and began trying to solicit passengers to share a $10 van ride downtown or pay up to $50 each to go to such faraway destinations as Newport Beach. Inevitably, arguments broke out as fully loaded vans pinned to the curb battled empty vans still seeking passengers.

"It's pure hell out there," said John Taylor, who has been driving vans for Supershuttle since 1986. "If you are not fighting other vans, you are fighting the (rental car) buses, the hotel buses and passenger cars."

With thousands of passengers arriving each hour at Los Angeles International Airport and the relatively low cost of acquiring a vehicle, van shuttles may look like an easy, no-risk business. But the proliferating number of airport shuttle van companies has produced cutthroat competition, airport congestion and almost daily confrontations between drivers at the world's third-busiest airport, officials say.

Some 30 privately operated van companies with 650 vehicles now serve the airport, compared to just two companies in 1971, according to airport officials. Competition has increased so much that Airlink, a Southern California commercial bus operator, cited the skyrocketing growth of vans when it shut down its 19-mile-long airport-to-downtown bus service last year.

Even industry leader Supershuttle, with about 200 vans and estimated annual revenue last year of nearly $50 million, is feeling the pinch. Last year, it took out a $14,000 newspaper advertisement to tell Department of Airport officials that Supershuttle and its customers had " . . . the most to lose from chaos and irresponsible practices at the airport."

"We are all having problems with the number of shuttle van companies serving the airport," said Rod Ramsey, chairman of the Shuttle Van Assn. of Southern California and president of City Shuttle in Van Nuys. "We are a young industry, and the potential for growth is tremendous. (But) we have a few bad apples we need to weed out."

In response to the complaints, the Department of Airports imposed a moratorium in September on new van operators and is considering new rules that would limit the time that drivers could solicit passengers during each stop and even force van operators to install devices that would electronically track how many trips the vans made to and from the airport.

"There's too much wheeling and dealing going on on the curbs," said Tom Drischler, transportation coordinator for the Department of Airports' unit that oversees ground transportation. "Drivers spend more time on the ground hustling up business than they do driving their vans."

Almost unheard of a decade ago, shuttle vans now carry tens of thousands of passengers who used to drive to the airport or relied on taxis and buses to transport them. Profit margins for operators are razor thin, typically less than 2%, several shuttle van companies say. But transportation experts say vans have become popular because they offer a compromise between a taxi, which is faster but costs more, and a bus, which is less expensive but slower.

On the airport-to-downtown route abandoned by Airlink, for example, cab fare is a flat $26.50 and van service is $9 to $11, compared to the $6.50 that Airlink had charged last year.

"Basically, you have got economics accounting for the growing popularity of vans," said Martin Markovich, transportation planner for the Southern California Assn. of Governments. "But you also have demographic changes as well."

Markovich noted that there is a greater percentage of single households or dual-income families in which husband and wife hold jobs and, therefore, don't have time to transport household members or guests to and from the airport.

"Another demographic change," Markovich said, "is that most people don't go downtown anymore." With urban sprawl, he said, more passengers arriving at LAX are going to a wider variety of destinations -- stretching from Orange County to the San Fernando Valley. As a result, he said, large commercial carriers such as Airlink have trouble filling up a 50-passenger bus that goes to a single destination.

By contrast, vans operate in a much more flexible manner: They pick up passengers by appointment at their homes or businesses (very much as taxis do), but unlike taxis, vans may make one or more stops to pick up additional passengers along the way. Travelers can hail vans, just as they do taxis, at designated points outside airline terminals.

It is this last aspect of van service, however, that has made van competition especially fierce in California, experts say.

In many states, door-to-door airport vans may only pick up passengers who have arranged for the service in advance -- normally by at least a day. But California regulators operate under "a very loose interpretation" of what constitutes advance notice, said Ray A. Mundy, a transportation professor at the University of Tennessee who is executive director of the Airport Ground Transportation Assn.

If a customer calls from the airport or hails a van from the curb in California, that constitutes advance notice, Mundy said.

But while the short-notice rule can be convenient for those seeking transportation on the spur of the moment, it can be frustrating for passengers who want to be delivered to their destination in a timely fashion.

Craig Stack, a customer service representative supervisor for Los Angeles-based TTMC Co., which helps manage van shuttle passenger relations, tells of passengers who have been driven around the airport for 20 minutes or longer while the driver seeks more customers to fill up his van.

And the Department of Airport's Drischler said his unit recently discovered that some van drivers were trading away passengers like chattel to other fully loaded vans headed for the same destination because the drivers didn't deem it financially worthwhile to make a trip with fewer than three customers.

"The only way we found out about it was that one of the passengers left his luggage in the van he was forced out of," Drischler said.

Surprisingly, such incidents have generated few complaints to regulatory authorities, said Ebi Esule, a transportation engineer with the state Public Utilities Commission, which regulates van and bus service within California.

"My impression is that the public feels they (vans) fill a very vital public need," said Esule.

The van explosion began in 1981, when the PUC opened up a market then dominated by cabs and buses. According to a Department of Airports' study completed two years ago, vans shuttled less than 3% of airport passengers, versus 54% using private cars and 16% using taxis. (Buses, rental cars and other forms of transportation also had slices of the market.)

Today, however, most observers believe that vans have increased their share of the estimated 44 million travelers who use LAX each year.

They have been aided in their expansion by the PUC.

Before 1981, the PUC granted few licenses to new airport bus or van companies. "If there was a bus line that already served the area, the commission would say no," said PUC spokeswoman Carole Kretzer.

But the commission changed its philosophy as federal officials continued deregulating transportation among the states in the early 1980s. The watershed year, experts say, came in 1981 when the commission allowed the Trailways Bus Co. to operate along Greyhound routes.

"Now it's very open," said Kretzer. "We encourage more competition because, theoretically, it leads to better service and lower prices."

Van companies now serve virtually every neighborhood in Southern California. One company -- Seoul Shuttle -- specializes in serving Koreatown. Passenger rates have barely risen among shuttle operators in the past five years, experts say.

But the free granting of licenses to new van operators has upset Department of Airport officials and industry leaders, who banded together earlier this year and formed the shuttle van association to help lobby for stricter van operating rules.

"If they throw this thing open to anyone," groused Mitchell Rouse, president of Supershuttle, "they are going to get the lowest common denominator of van operator, who doesn't have the burden of having to provide a system to get customers to the airport . . . (but) only flags them down at the curb in front of the terminals."
