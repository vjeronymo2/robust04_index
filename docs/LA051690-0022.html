May 16, 1990, Wednesday, Home Edition

LIBERTY GOES BEGGING;

LAW: A FEDERAL JUDGE CONSIGNS PANHANDLERS TO A CLASS OUTSIDE THE PROTECTION OF THE FIRST AMENDMENT. WHAT MORE CAN THE HOMELESS LOSE?

A few of those hissings and slitherings that intimate the presence of the snakes of meanness of spirit are rather too audible in the cadences sounded in U.S. Court of Appeals Judge Frank X. Altimari's affirmation of the New York City Transit Authority's ban on panhandling on its subways.

We might wish that Judge Altimari was up to a little more respect for the decencies of hypocrisy than he showed in the fleeting nod he gave to the altar of our ritual pieties about suffering humanity in general before he dismissed one of its most abject segments in the particular.

In essence, the Court of Appeals ruled that individuals cannot beg in the subways, but that organized charities can. Judge Altimari's distinction -- it scarcely amounts to a difference -- is that solitary beggars convey no message except their desire "to extract money from those they accost," while respectable, presumably because organized, solicitors communicate information and disseminate views and ideas. The homeless beggar's want of shelter therefore extends to the First Amendment because his conduct fails to meet the standards for "variety of speech interests" that qualify a candidate to lodge under even its roof.

Altimari may or may not have been often reduced to the exigencies of subway travel, but there is small chance that he could have and escaped the too-frequent experience of the passage by some ragged stranger mutely holding forth his paper cup, and then one's sudden halt to turn and drop a puny mite. If a face that can thus abrade the laggard conscience is not a kind of speech, then what are we to take communication to be?

It is Altimari's view that, "while there is no doubt that giving alms is virtuous, in the Western tradition there is also no doubt that the virtue is best served when it reflects an 'ordered charity.' " This is a precis of Western tradition that some of its historians may find lamentably shortened. The Franciscan order would be the last to suggest that it is purer than in the days when its saintly founder went barefoot on the byways begging bread for the poor and, of necessity, also for himself.

Altimari supports his preference for "ordered charities" by citing the Supreme Court's opinion that it was unconstitutional for Maryland to limit fund-raisers to 25% of the proceeds of their appeals. The plaintiff was a professional solicitor, and while we might go too far if we called a man a hustler for keeping for himself a quarter of what he has gleaned for those in need, we have to recognize him as indeed the stateliest of beggars.

It is hard to dispute the Court of Appeals' judgment that the panhandler is a nuisance. Reminders of suffering humanity are always nuisances, but there are altogether worse ones attendant upon the companionships enforced in public transit. Of course, beggars now and then accost, but the run of them are too beaten by circumstances and the shame to which they have been reduced to be up to exertions beyond standing all but unconscious of the faces that tithe for them.

Altimari gives special weight to a Transit Authority survey that "revealed that, in fact, two-thirds of the subway ridership have been intimidated into giving money to (beggars)." It would not be easy to conceive a greater excess of frivolity in the judicial temper than this certification of a study of beggars bought and paid for by their declared enemies. After all, the conservative canon embodied by this judge has no article more sacred than the one that abjures courts from substituting what the public might say for the strict prescriptions of the Constitution. The subway beggar is indeed unique; he is condemned to the first class whose First Amendment credentials are disqualified by a poll.

Talk about nuisances. You cannot walk through an airline terminal without being afflicted by devotees of the martyred Lyndon Larouche bawling the noxious notions of a convicted swindler and unrepentant caterer to paranoid fantasies. No one would suggest that their right to ventilate their views could fairly be tested by a public-opinion poll that included the rational and civilized majority of citizens.

But the First Amendment remains the guaranteed protection of every sort of nuisance except the beggar who appeals to nothing worse in us than our pity and is now consigned by a circuit court to a station lower than a four-letter obscenity's.
