Language: Spanish Article Type:BFN [Report by Juan Carlos Tellechea] [Text] Dresden, 4 Feb (DPA) -- Ricardo Alarcon, president of the Cuban National Assembly who is in this city today at the end of his official three-day visit to Germany, has said that U.S. President Bill Clinton has moderated the policy on Cuba that the George Bush administration handed down to Clinton through the Torricelli Law. After expressing to his German hosts measured optimism about the possibility of the U.S. embargo against Cuba being eased during the latter half of Clinton's current term, or his second term should he be reelected, Alarcon told DPA that Havana has observed Washington becoming more flexible in the practical enforcement of the strict embargo. Alarcon, a former foreign minister and deputy foreign minister, was until today guest of Rita Sussmuth, president of the German Federal Parliament (Bundestag). Alarcon met with representatives of the government of Chancellor Helmut Kohl, federal deputies, and members of the regional parliaments of Bremen and Saxony in the former East Germany. Alarcon believes an eventual easing of the U.S. embargo against Cuba, which has lasted more than three decades, can take place in Clinton's second term, because the issue is still too heavily loaded, ideologically speaking, and the U.S. Administration still does not have enough latitude to make a political decision of this nature. Alarcon, who did not conceal from his hosts Cuba's interest in the current PRC model of evolution toward political liberalization by way of cautious economic reforms, nevertheless said that so far there has been no direct official contact between the U.S. and Cuban governments. [sentence as received] According to Alarcon, Havana observes "the U.S. Government's language is changing." For example, Cuban authorities have taken notice of "a reiterated statement by the U.S. State Department and Assistant Secretary of State Watson, saying that U.S. territory will not be permitted to be used for terrorist activities or violence against Cuba." Alarcon said: "This is what the American Neutrality Act states, and this is normal in international law. But the United States never said this during the past 12 years of Republican administrations. This is something we cannot overlook." He added that "the U.S. Government's position during the neuritis epidemic in Cuba" must also be considered in this regard. Washington "did not object to several U.S. scientists from National Institute of Health traveling to Cuba." The U.S. scientists "cooperated with Cuban authorities and made very unbiased remarks about Cuba when they concluded their work." The experts "returned to the United States, but kept in touch with Cuban researchers. The reason for this is still being discussed and pondered. It is interesting to note this, because the neuritis epidemic can occur elsewhere." Alarcon declined to comment on the lifting of the U.S. embargo on Vietnam after 20 years. He also elected not to imply that he can base his moderate optimism concerning Cuban-U.S. relations on this decision. The Clinton era represents for the United States a great leap ahead, since the generation that served in World War II and experienced "McCarthyism" until the time of the young men who either fought or refused to enlist in the Vietnam war. [sentence as received] The personalities who were active during the administrations of John F. Kennedy and Lyndon Johnson have disappeared or retired, while some who belonged to Jimmy Carter's time are still alive. Nevertheless, most members of the Clinton team are much younger people, unrelated to active politics since they were in the opposition during the quarter century of the Republican Party's several terms in the White House -- since Richard Nixon in 1968. All this can explain to Cuban authorities the whys and wherefores of the transformations under way today in the United States, and why the Clinton administration has established a policy allowing women and Hispanics to hold positions of responsibility.
