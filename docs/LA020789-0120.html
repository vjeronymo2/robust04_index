February 7, 1989, Tuesday, Home Edition

PARAGUAY RULER DENIES LINKS TO DRUG TRAFFIC

Gen. Andres Rodriguez, Paraguay's new military ruler, vowed Monday to crack down on drug trafficking and swore that he has never been involved in drug dealing. He dismissed allegations to the contrary as the work of enemies out to eliminate him.

Relaxed and smiling, Rodriguez answered questions from groups of reporters for more than two hours. Meetings with the press were a rarity during the 34 1/2-year regime of former President Alfredo Stroessner, who flew into exile Sunday after being deposed in Rodriguez's coup two days earlier.

Rodriguez repeated his commitment to respect human rights and bring democracy to Paraguay, but at the same time he indicated that political change would be neither sudden nor profound. For example, he said that Stroessner had not been a dictator and that past elections had not been fraudulent.

He also said that if he was asked, he would accept the nomination of the ruling Colorado Party in the May 1 election to fill the remainder of Stroessner's term until 1993.

Anticipated Questions

Rodriguez, commander of the 1st Army Corps, who had been Stroessner's second-in-command, clearly anticipated a grilling on reports published in the United States that he has been linked to shipments of drugs through Paraguay.

He said in a prepared statement that he considers drug-trafficking "one of the worst scourges that flay humanity" and that his government will ensure that "the laws are more severe and energetic to suppress it."

Responding to questions, he said: "I detest drugs. I swear as a (Roman) Catholic and as the head of a family, I swear on my sons, that I am not involved in drug trafficking."

Rodriguez said that he would cooperate with the U.S. Drug Enforcement Administration and all other international agencies in the fight against narcotics.

Former U.S. Ambassador Clyde D. Taylor Jr. has charged that senior Paraguayan officials were involved in drugs, but he did not accuse anyone by name. That earned him scorn from the pro-Stroessner news media.

There have been unverified reports in the American press that Rodriguez has played a major role in the smuggling of drug shipments through Paraguay. The U.S. government has often complained about drug smuggling through that country, and although U.S. officials have not publicly linked Rodriguez to the drug trade, they are known to believe that he is deeply involved.

In his news conference, Rodriguez, a multimillionaire with several business interests including money-changing and imports, said he has been to the United States many times, including official visits to the Pentagon, and "they never even said anything to me."

'Shining Opportunity'

"I happily thank you for giving me this shining opportunity to say to you that never in my life have I had any friendship or connection with any person with respect to drug trafficking," he said. "I know that there was an elimination campaign against me. I don't know by which opposition party. . . . It would be someone with bad intentions interested in eliminating me."

Rodriguez said that the number of dead and wounded in the six hours or so of fighting that ended Friday with Stroessner's ouster did not exceed 50. Foreign diplomats had estimated the death toll at about 300.

The Paraguayan political opposition had reacted jubilantly to Stroessner's downfall and to Rodriguez's initial public statements in defense of civil rights and the need for full democracy, which everyone outside the ruling Colorado Party agrees has never existed in Paraguay.

But there was immediate criticism of Rodriguez's announcement at the final meeting with reporters Monday that elections for president and Congress will be held May 1. This will be less than 90 days after the coup. The short campaign period gives a huge advantage to the Colorado Party, which has a vast nationwide organization and demands the support of public workers, civic groups and organized labor. The opposition will have to start from scratch.

Rafael Saguier, a member of the still-banned Authentic Liberal Radical Party, considered to be the primary opposition force, said: "Gen. Rodriguez knows that this short period is insufficient. This is a trick played on the opposition. He knows that we have not been able to organize for nearly 35 years and that all public servants and all army members must be Colorado Party members.

"And he defends past national elections as fair. This has the makings of another dictatorship. Rodriguez wants to be another Stroessner. They are going to change what they have to change to keep things the same."

Rodriguez said that all four opposition parties, presently outlawed, could compete in the elections and that only Communist parties were prohibited. Paraguay has no active Communist Party. "Ex-President Stroessner was not a dictator," he said. "He was freely elected in elections that had outside observers."

Asked why he had overthrown Stroessner if the latter was not a dictator, the 64-year-old general replied, "In recent times, President Stroessner was not producing good results for the party that had placed its trust in him."

Despite an expression of support for free news media by the new leader, Rodriguez's government censored from outgoing foreign television news reports any references to the death toll in the coup or to a possible Paraguayan role in drug dealing. There was no attempt to censor radio reports or newspaper or news agency articles sent abroad.
