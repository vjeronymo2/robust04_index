BFN [Editorial Report] The following is a compilation of reports on Mexican economic developments monitored through 19 April. In the past three years, revenues earned by the privatization of nonstrategic public organizations totaled $21.8 billion pesos, the equivalent of 8.88 percent of international reserves. Foreign investment for the past four years totaled 77.7 percent of the international resources the country obtained. (Mexico City EL FINANCIERO in Spanish 12 Apr 94 p 3A) The Chiapas government spent approximately 30 million pesos in the first quarter of 1994 to buy 10,000 hectares of arable land from ranchers and small landholders in an attempt to alleviate the conflicts prompted by the excessive demand for land. (Mexico City LA JORNADA in Spanish 12 Apr 94 p 3) The flow of foreign investment in Mexico in March was 50 percent less than the investment levels obtained in January and February, months during which investment totaled approximately $1 billion. According to officials of the Commerce and Industrial Development Secretariat, this decline resulted from recent political events. (Mexico City UNOMASUNO in Spanish 13 Apr 94 p 17) Mexico signed two credit agreements with the international financial community on 11 April. The first agreement, totaling $30 million, was signed with the Spanish Government; the second one, for $169 million, was signed with the Inter-American Development Bank. The loans will be assigned to infrastructure and education projects. (Mexico City UNOMASUNO in Spanish 13 Apr 94 p 18) According to Alberto Marin, Northern Chiapas Cattle Union president, the livestock sector has suffered a loss of approximately 300 million pesos, particularly in such areas as the highlands, the jungle, and the north, as a result of multiple peasant invasions. Marin has said the government's buying of lands has prompted the invasions to continue. (Mexico City UNOMASUNO in Spanish 13 Apr 94 p 9) The Foreign Trade Secretariat has revealed that $5 billion were reportedly invested in Mexico during the first quarter of 1994. This amount is a 150-percent increase over foreign investment earned during the same period in 1993. (Mexico City NOTIMEX in Spanish 1453 GMT 13 Apr 94) The National Bank of Mexico-Accival Financial Group has said in order to achieve the expected level of economic growth and employ the available labor force, Mexico will need at least $25 billion in foreign aid in 1994 due to an insufficient rate of savings, which ranks below 10 percent. The bank's experts have said in the past two years more than 70 percent of foreign investment was in stocks. (Mexico City EL FINANCIERO in Spanish 14 Apr 94 p 7) Mexican and Taiwanese businessmen have earmarked $7.5 million to start a pioneering industrial project in Fresnillo, Zacatecas, where shoes will be made for export to the United States and Canada. (Mexico City NOTIMEX in Spanish 2043 GMT 15 Apr 94) The National Foreign Trade Bank has placed on the international markets the lowest-priced issue of bonds in the past 12 years for the amount of $250 million. Foreign investors' purchase of these bonds will inject security and stability into the Mexican economy. (Mexico City UNOMASUNO in Spanish 15 Apr 94 p 16) Mexico and New Zealand have signed an agreement to strengthen ties in trade and investment and to grant the agricultural sector easier access to both countries' markets. (Mexico City EL FINANCIERO in Spanish 15 Apr 94 p 21) An EL FINANCIERO study indicates interest payments on the foreign debt in the private sector will increase by $100 million in addition to the estimated $2.1-billion debt pending for 1994. This increase results from a rise in international interest rates and an 8.5-percent devaluation of the peso vis-a-vis the U.S. dollar in recent weeks. Data provided by the Bank of Mexico, the Mexican Stock Market, and local and foreign experts show the leading Mexican companies, total of 36, incurred substantial foreign debts. (Mexico City EL FINANCIERO in Spanish 15 Apr 94 pp 1, 4, 5) The Finance Secretariat has said Mexican exports during February reached $4.4 billion, a 17.4-percent increase compared with February 1993. According to a Finance Secretariat communique, nonpetroleum exports increased by 23.7 percent. (Mexico City NOTIMEX in Spanish 1516 GMT 16 Apr 94) High-ranking government officials have confirmed that thus far, $980.7 million from the Contingency Fund have been allocated to support the Mexican peso in the exchange market. The Finance Secretariat has said the $6 billion granted by the United States to counter any pressure to devalue the peso are still available. (Mexico City UNOMASUNO in Spanish 18 Apr 94 p 16) According to the Bank of Mexico, its foreign indebtedness increased by 115 percent from 1989 to this date and currently totals approximately $21 billion. In addition, banks' foreign indebtedness represents 51.2 percent of the total debt contracted by the public sector abroad and equals 17.6 percent of Mexico's total foreign indebtedness. (Mexico City LA JORNADA in Spanish 18 Apr pp 64, 54) Mexican Petroleum has earned $482 million in revenues for Mexico by selling an average of 1.3 million barrels of crude per day to its clients in America, Europe, and Asia. The United States, 72.3 percent, Spain, 14.1 percent, and Asia, 14.4 percent, were Mexico's leading clients during January. (Mexico City NOTIMEX in Spanish 0119 GMT 19 Apr 94)
