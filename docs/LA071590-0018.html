July 15, 1990, Sunday, Valley Edition

THE PERFORMERS WANT TO PIERCE THE CONSCIENCE OF THE AUDIENCE

Things start out innocently enough for Brenda Varda's character in Timberlake Wertenbaker's "The Love of the Nightingale" (L.A. Theatre Works at the Edgemar Complex in Santa Monica). But then her brother-in-law takes a brooding fancy to her during a long ship's voyage.

His lying that he's suddenly single does little to arouse her ardor. Nor does his murdering her beloved. When all else fails, he rapes her. Later, when she confronts him with her desperation and fury, he cuts out her tongue.

"The more distance she has to go from innocence, the more horrible that moment is," said Varda, who is stunning as Philomele, from a giddy, fresh-faced teen-ager to a middle-aged madwoman. The moment after the attack -- when Philomele is reeling in agony and disbelief, choking on her blood -- "gets very silent in the audience," she said. "But the way we do it isn't nearly as violent as it'd be if you really had your tongue cut out. The amount of blood would be insane. And you can't swallow; you can't breathe."

The story (set in ancient Greece and freely adapted from ancient myth) is indeed less concerned with graphic bloodletting than with larger issues of chauvinism, censorship, sisterhood, slavery, rape and revenge.

"It should be disturbing," the actress said of the violence upon her character, "because that kind of thing is not something that doesn't happen today. There are death squads in innumerable countries that eliminate people or make them silent or cut off their hands. So what we're doing here is not divorced from the real world. It also has a lot of resonances with what's happening now with the National Endowment for the Arts, our artistic voices being silenced."

Director Peter Mark Schifter recalls that upon reading the play, his immediate response was: "Intelligence! Ideas! Theatrical power! Not commercial!"

"I did it because it's an important play," he said. "It has important things to say for contemporary society. And Brenda brings a real ingenuousness to her part -- and a piercing intelligence."

Varda believes that intelligence applies to the piece as well, taking exception to some of the criticism, which labeled "Nightingale" heavy-handed and didactic.

"Sometimes the force of words and images that shock you and scream at you, that say 'Are you listening?' are necessary," she said. "This is no museum piece. Most women have been subjected to some sort of harassment. If you live in New York, chances are you've been mugged. I lived in Chicago; I got mugged, my apartment was broken into, my car was stolen. Now those aren't necessarily crimes against women," she added, laughing, "but since I'm a woman and the crimes were against me. . . ."

The ability to slip-slide from serious to silly is one of the actress's most charming qualities. Lissome and long-limbed, she swears to having been an A-1 nerd growing up ("thick glasses and frizzy hair"). She entered the University of Minnesota as an engineering major but switched to acting after playing piano for the theater classes, figuring, "Hey, I can do that." She also studied journalism and interned in television news and on a morning radio show.

"I have stopped acting many times," said Varda, who returned to the university for her master's in communications ("doing research on creativity, how to stimulate people to be more creative"), but left before graduating. Distractions included tours with the St. Louis Repertory and Chicago's Second City, managing an art gallery, serving as creative consultant for a prank-playing company in Palo Alto, and -- when times were lean -- paying dues in waitressing and the secretarial pool.

"The activities seem sort of disparate," she said. "But I like to think of it as a continuum; things always end up feeding each other. Some of what I've done on the business side has been totally invaluable in creating an acting life in Los Angeles."

Not having a regular job, she says, is liberating: "I like to do different things. I write, I play piano, I sing, I've written a couple of musicals. It doesn't do wonders for your pension, but as far as what I get to feed myself with, it's worth it."
