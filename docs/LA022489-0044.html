ART REVIEW;

BILLBOARD EXHIBIT LEAVES VIEWERS COMPLETELY FLAT

Talk about missed opportunities! On hearing the title of the current exhibition at the Municipal Art Gallery in Barnsdall Park, "Inside Outdoor: The Art and Technology of Outdoor Advertising," the mind does a cartwheel. Billboards, you say? Burma Shave! The Coppertone kid being pants by her pup! The mechanized Camel Cigarettes billboard that blew smoke rings! Sunset Boulevard's corridor of glorious billboard excess that stretches from Crescent Heights to San Vicente! The entire history of 20th-Century America as seen from the wheel of a car!

But alas, rather than the colorful stroll down memory lane that this unsung genre of folk art deserves, rather than shedding any light on the American psyche as reflected in its relationship with the open road, we're given a series of turgid and technical how-to photographs chronicling the creation of a modern billboard -- just in case we're interested in doing a billboard at home in our spare time. Touting itself as "an exhibition focusing on the contributions of the billboard artist to the Los Angeles culture and landscape," "Inside Outdoor" fails miserably on every score.

Sponsored by Los Angeles Contemporary Exhibitions and the Patrick Media Group, the show features as its centerpiece a billboard that will be completed over the course of the show, which continues through March 5. So, if you want to see someone actually painting a billboard, the show does offer that. It also offers a very spotty pictorial history of billboards drawn from archival photographs, documentation of three conceptualist billboards recently completed by L.A. artists, explanations of computer-generated billboards and other modern technologies, and a video montage of current billboards -- just in case you want to see how they look on TV as opposed to on the street.

Other than that, the show leaves most questions unanswered. How, for instance, do billboards differ regionally? What things are taken into consideration in designing a billboard? How have they shaped the urban landscape? What makes them an effective way to sell a product? Have billboards had a golden age, and if so, when was it? The show fails to address any of these questions, nor does it mention painter James Rosenquist, Pop art or Photo Realism, all of which drew considerable inspiration from outdoor advertising.

Horribly installed, the show combines vast stretches of empty space with monotonous sequences of tiny photographs and a few chunks of actual billboards. This exhibit looks boring, turns out, on close examination, to be boring, and communicates none of the wonderfully crass spectacle of the art form it seeks to illuminate.
