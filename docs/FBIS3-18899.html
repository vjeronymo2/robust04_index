Language: English Article Type:BFN [Text] Paris, March 10 KYODO -- Yoshiyasu Sato, the newly elected chairman of the International Energy Agency's [IEA] governing board, says the 23-nation agency is searching for a new role following the end of the Cold War. Sato said the IEA, a forum for oil consuming countries, will continue to engage in dialogue with the oil producing countries. "But there is also concern over environmental problems caused by high energy consumption accompanying high growth rates in China and other Asian countries," Sato said in an interview. "And there are a lot of other issues like the fall-off in production in Russia which used to be the world's largest oil producer." The 59-year-old Sato, who is also Japan's ambassador to the Paris-based Organization for Economic Cooperation and Development (OECD), said guaranteed stability of energy is decisively important to the economy. "Especially for Japan, which doesn't have oil resources domestically, energy security is a pillar of its economy and diplomacy," Sato said. The IEA this year celebrates its 20th anniversary since its founding during the oil shock of the 1970's. It attracted international attention when it was set up in 1974 as a body representing the interests of consumer countries as opposed to the Organization of Petroleum Exporting Countries (OPEC). But recently it has assumed a lower profile as crude oil prices have stabilized. Sato has a wide network of personal contacts in high places. At the time of his election as the IEA governing board chairman, the U.S. representative nominated him and the German representative seconded the nomination. "Both were old acquaintances," Sato said. "It was good to have those relationships." The IEA will hold its first meeting since Sato assumed the post in Kyoto in April at which it will hold seminars on policies to carry it through to the end of the century. Sato is a former head of the Foreign Ministry's Economic Affairs Bureau. He served for three years from 1989 as chief secretary to the foreign minister. Since July 1992, he has served as Japan's ambassador to the OECD.
