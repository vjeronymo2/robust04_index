January 3, 1990, Wednesday, P.M. Final

PEOPLE'S PHARMACY: MEDICATION COULD BE THE KEY TO IMPOTENCE PROBLEM

Question: My husband has had a problem with impotence for years, but we got by somehow. Now he is not interested at all. He doesn't even come near me and yells if I approach him.

Is there a medicine or a vitamin or anything that can bring him to life? I don't want to give up sex yet. We are in our 50s.

Answer: You shouldn't have to give up sex in your 50s. First, your husband needs to have his doctor check his medications to make sure one of them isn't to blame. A surprising number of drugs can cause impotence or lower sex drive.

Next, a urologist may be able to tell if there is a biological problem. There are now several prescription medications that may actually help restore erections, including yohimbine or papaverine injections. Surgery is even possible in some cases.

Finally, psychological or marital counseling can provide the kind of support he needs to maintain a more loving relationship with you.

Q. You recently suggested the FDA approve 12-minute strep tests for home use. As a pediatrician, I question your understanding of the situation.

All of the quick strep tests on the market are inaccurate compared to a 24-hour culture.

An inaccurate, negative 12-minute strep test done at home could give a false sense of security and should never replace evaluation of the child's other symptoms, or the parent's intuition -- which I have found very reliable.

A. You are right -- the quick strep test is not perfect. If it indicates a strep throat, our consultants assure us that is highly reliable and deserves immediate antibiotic treatment.

The problem arises when the test shows no strep. About one in 10 of those are "false negatives." These children actually have strep which can be cultured in 24 hours.

If every sore throat were seen by a doctor, we wouldn't advocate home testing. But many children don't get their throats cultured and home testing might assist parents in making better decisions. A positive quick strep test would allow the doctor to call a prescription in. Unfortunately, a negative test would probably still require a follow-up visit to the doctor.
