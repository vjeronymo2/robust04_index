BFN [Text] Paris, June 23 (AFP) -- France is hoping for "more active support" from the European Union for its intervention in Rwanda at the EU's two-day summit in Corfu starting on Friday [24 June], French European Affairs Minister Alain Lamassoure said on Thursday. "We need the support of the whole international community, we got it with the United Nations Security Council vote -- at the same time we expect much greater support from our European partners," Lamassoure said in an interview on France Inter radio. Meanwhile Pierre Lellouche, foreign policy advisor to Gaullist presidential hopeful Jacques Chirac, criticised alleged lack of support from Germany for the Rwanda operation. In a reference to President Francois Mitterrand's invitation to German troops to take part in the annual Bastille Day military parade next July 14, Lellouche said he would prefer "that the German units do a detour via Kigali to carry out this peace mission before parading in Paris." For his part, French Foreign Minister Alain Juppe denied that France was isolated over Rwanda. Juppe told a press conference that Paris had won a pledge of "logistical support" from the United States and backing from its European partners and French-speaking African countries. Juppe, speaking after briefing the Senate foreign affairs committee, said other countries might also offer logistical support and mentioned Italy, Belgium, Portugal and Spain. The foreign minister, who played down Organisation of African Unity opposition to the Rwanda operation, also said there was no conflict with the rebels of the Rwandan Patriotic Front (RPF), which has vowed to fight the intervention. "There is agreement to pursue dialogue. We shall maintain contact (with the RPF) during the operations," Juppe said, insisting that one of the objectives was to "save Tutsi lives in the western part of Rwanda." The Tutsi-dominated RPF mistrusts France for past backing of its Hutu rivals. Juppe refused to answer questions on the progress of operations and to say whether French troops had entered Rwanda. Lamassoure said that although there would be "technical support within the framework...of the EU," France wanted "other Europeans to take part in the military operation." He said "There is a duty to come to the aid of a people in mortal danger" and "it is in this spirit that we are going there." Lellouche, who was speaking on Radio Monte Carlo, said the "Europe as a whole will have to be reviewed" if Germany could not be counted on when "major political and strategic issues" came up. He said that if Europe consisted of "the Germans looking after the Deutschmark, the British looking after trade while we are the mercenaries called upon to spend our money and have our young men killed for the stability of our periphery, then this Europe cannot work." Lellouche added that the US had stabilised Latin America, the Japanese had contributed to political and economic stability in their region and "Europe's duty is to do this job in the south."
