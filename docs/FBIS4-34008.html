BFN [Text] President Fidel Castro broke away from the agenda of the summit today and addressed the Ibero-American Summit after the session for each of the participants to speak had ended. Castro warned democracy must be understood within the context of a great discussion among Latin Americans, and he demanded solidarity for the people of Cuba who are suffering due to the U.S. blockade, which he described as criminal. Castro requested the floor from Bolivian President Gonzalo Sanchez de Lozada, who was presiding over this afternoon's session. Castro said: [Begin Castro recording] In conclusion, I will say I respect your ideas. I sincerely respect your ideas, concepts, and thoughts about democracy. We would have to hold a summit to discuss what democracy is, what participation of the people is, and why millions die in this hemisphere each year from disease and hunger when they could be saved. [end recording] Following Castro's speech, Bolivian President Gonzalo Sanchez de Lozada responded, saying even though there is still much to study and understand about the Cuban experience, he trusts that democracy will come to the island and that Castro will be at the head of a second Cuban revolution. [Begin Sanchez recording] Possibly [words indistinct], but I know a change will occur, and we all wish you [words indistinct] respect that all this [words indistinct] big river, big ocean [word indistinct]. [end recording] This was the second time Castro addressed the summit. His first speech was scheduled, the second was not. We asked Colombian President Cesar Gaviria Trujillo for his views regarding Castro's speech. Gaviria said: The speech Cuban President Fidel Castro made follows the pattern of words the Cuban Government uses at international meetings. They never sidetrack from this pattern. President Gaviria was also asked what he thought of the fact that Castro was wearing civilian clothes. Gaviria said: We were accustomed to seeing him in his olive green uniform, but we saw him wearing a guayabera today. It is an encouraging change, Gaviria said, adding there are also encouraging signs for Latin American democracy.
