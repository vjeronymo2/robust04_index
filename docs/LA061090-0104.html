June 10, 1990, Sunday, Home Edition

A PAPER GUY TO CALL HER OWN;

REACHING TIN RIVER BY THEA ASTLEY (G.P. PUTNAM'S SONS: $18.95; 224 PP.; 0-399-13532-4)

There is no landscape like the landscape of childhood, terrain at once vivid and terrifyingly vague. Thea Astley understands this well, how the sounds of teacups being refilled, a grandfather's mumbled comments, or the smell of a handful of gum leaves remain fixed in the brain years later as if they were the most important moments in one's life, the strongest memory of love.

At first, "Reaching Tin River" seems as if it's going to be a novel about this -- a childhood in the magical kingdom of Queensland, North Australia. Not that life is idyllic there on the burnt, raw land. "I want love," her small heroine, Belle, sobs, and we think it's because she's been uprooted from her beloved home and sent to a boarding school; also because she's a fatherless child, reared by aging grandparents, a daffy drum-banging mother and a piano-playing aunt who form an all-women's band, a duo "welded into a partnership stronger than marriage, a kind of twinhood sanctified and integrated by cornball rhythms," much more concerned with thumping out "sugar-candy tunes" than attending to a small girl's needs.

The novel ends up being about a much different thing, however, namely the adult Belle's obsessive search for a "center," a quest that causes her to go off the emotional deep end and adopt a "paper lover," a figure out of history named Gaden Lockyer whose face she first discovers in the archives of a library where she works. Yet the childhood that failed little Belle out on Perjury Plains (the telling name of the family property) in the final analysis seems immutably connected to her fractured, distressed adulthood. For one thing, she never stops looking for love.

Thea Astley -- three-time winner of the Miles Franklin Award, Australia's highest literary honor, and author of 10 previous novels and a collection of short stories -- is a most original writer whose sentences seem charged with incendiary power and whose insight into social mores -- especially the relations between men and women -- have a dead-on rightness. Reading her last book to appear here, "Two by Astley," a volume containing two novellas originally published in Australia in the 1970s, one couldn't help being deeply impressed by the energy and intelligence of her writing. The first novella: "A Kindness Cup," the story of an aboriginal massacre and its effect years later on the inhabitants of a small town, seemed the better work, though it was in "The Acolyte" that Astley's social wit and her pyrotechnic flare for language was most evident.

Unfortunately, neither talent -- not the story-telling ability nor the beautifully charged language -- is employed with the same success in "Reaching Tin River." The novel feels hurried, as if written in a rush. Its tone is rather conversational.

Not that Astley is capable of writing badly. The wit, the unerring social observations, still come through, and the novel has wonderful vignettes -- a girl in a bathing suit tantalizing an older man; a mother weaving "red setter" place mats out of her dog's hair. But the well-crafted plot of "A Kindness Cup," the controlled character revelations found in "The Acolyte" -- these have been scrapped for a breezier, faster sort of book about the obsessional Belle, a character who at the book's end has gone to extremes that don't make sense.

Belle's American serviceman father, Huck, knew her mother, Bonnie, only long enough to conceive a child and take her off to San Diego for a few months before Bonnie packed up and headed back to Queensland, though why the marriage didn't work is never clear. The legacy of the brief union is Belle. When Belle marries a fellow librarian named Seb, she gains little more man than she lost in her father. Seb's a self-inflated bore, and worse, he cheats on her. By the time Belle realizes her marriage is done for, she has already latched onto her next lover, Mr. Gaden Lockyer.

Gaden Lockyer -- no flesh-and-blood man -- is a figure out of history, a banker, farmer and politician who lived in Queensland at the end of the last century. Not handsome, but compelling in looks, he so intrigues Belle that she must know everything about him. Her obsession with Gaden grows, until she is fully engaged in "reconstructing or revivifying him."

She has a huge photographic blowup made of his picture so that she can talk to him at night alone in her room and insert herself into the "picture" by obliterating the image of his wife. Belle sets off to track down places where he's lived, assuming a variety of identities until, Belle in tardy pursuit, he finally reached Tin River, Gaden's last abode.

What she's really searching for is a "center." That much she makes clear. But what kind of a center? One within, or one without? She hopes such a center won't revolve around men, and maybe by creating a "paper" man Astley feels she's liberating her heroine, at least giving her a stepping stone.

"Philosophically, I suppose," Belle muses, "there is little difference between the human center that I seek and the abstract. Modern self-indulgent psychology proposes that one's self is one's center, but I find the theory unsatisfying. I need a center in which I can merge. An alter-ego center. Isn't that what we all look for?"

It's an interesting idea -- using the novel to ask this question: Where is the center? But the answers, the whole investigation, is too diffused here; some underlying fascination with Euclidean geometry is favored over an obligation to real emotional depth in characters.

When Belle finally tracks down her long-lost father, making a hasty trip to the States to meet him in New York, their reunion is described in such a way that the players seem artificial. And later when Belle tells her mother she's located her father, there's hardly an indication of a reaction on her mother's part.

Surely such a reunion would matter to both women -- matter deeply -- and a reader can't help but long for the emotional truth at such moments.
