940120 FT 20 JAN 94 / The FT500 (23): Devaluation helps Swedes and Finns - Recession continued to dog the region but stock markets rose sharply as interest rates fell and signs of recovery began to show / Country Analyses - Nordic Region After a dismal, recession-driven slump in the rankings in 1992, Nordic companies have rebounded significantly this year. Ten new Nordic entries - or re-entries - outweighed five departures (two of which were due to takeovers), compared with a net loss last year of 11. The turnaround took the total of Swedish, Danish, Finnish and Norwegian companies in the list to 45. Nordic companies also made their presence felt among the more eye-catching performers. Skandinaviska Enskilda Banken, Sweden's leading commercial bank, vaulted back from a loss-driven share collapse to re-enter the top 500 at number 199. Its rival Svenska Handelsbanken topped the list of big risers among established FT500 companies by surging from 465th place to 223rd. Astra, the Swedish pharmaceutical group, held its 1992 ranking of 32, the highest of any Nordic company. It also ranked as Europe's seventh most profitable company, with a 49.8 per cent return on capital. Another prominent Nordic re-entry to the FT500 was Nokia, the Finnish telecommunications group, which is riding a tide of growth in its mobile telephone division. It came in at number 301. Meanwhile, Norsk Hydro, the petroleum and fertiliser group that is Norway's biggest stock-listed company, registered Europe's biggest increase in profits, showing a rise of no less than 7,715 per cent. Recession continued to dog the Nordic region in 1993 - especially in Sweden and Finland. But stock markets in the four countries rose sharply as interest rates fell and signs of recovery began to show in the latter part of the year. In Sweden and Finland, companies also benefited greatly from the big devaluations in the Swedish krona and Finnish markka over the year, which boosted profitability. Finland's representation in the FT500 was quadrupled to eight from 1992, reflecting a near doubling of values on the Helsinki stock exchange over the year. The top Finnish company, forestry and engineering group Repola, improved from 387th place to 255th. Finnish newcomers, apart from Nokia, were forestry groups Enso-Gutzeit and Metsa-Serla, respectively at 387 and 471; Outokumpu, the mining company, at 482; and the banks Unitas and Kansallis-Osake-Pankki. Unitas came in at 440, while KOP edges in at 500. Six of Denmark's 10 FT500 companies also improved their positions. The main reverse was Carlsberg, the brewer, which was toppled from Danish top spot as it slid from 158th place overall to 229th. It was overtaken by AP Moller, the shipping group, whose joint parent companies, D/S Svendborg and D/S af 1912, moved up to 148 and 149 on the overall list respectively. Closer inspection of the rankings, however, reveals that stock market performance tended to be well ahead of bottom-line performance. Eleven of the 25 European companies showing the biggest swings from profit to loss were Swedish, Finnish or Danish. Prominent among these were banks and insurance companies. Most of the region's financial stocks rebounded strongly in 1993, as banks and insurance groups took the first steps back to health after crippling loan losses in 1992. But they are still carrying the burden of those losses: Unitas suffered Europe's 12th biggest swing to loss from profit - Dollars 445.5m. Others in the list of crashing profits were Swedish insurers Trygg-Hansa SPP and Skandia, S-E Banken, Den Danske Bank and Unidanmark. Traditional Swedish industrial powerhouses also put up a mixed showing, at best. Three Swedish industrial departures from the FT500 - Trelleborg, the troubled mining and metals group; Industrivarden, a mixed investment group; and Korsnas, a pulp and paper company taken over by Kinnevik - balanced new financial sector entries S-E Banken, Skandia and Trygg-Hansa SPP, to leave Sweden's total representation at 21. Of these, only seven improved their rankings.
