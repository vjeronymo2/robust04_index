September 19, 1989, Tuesday, Home Edition

HATCH COMMENTS ON IMMIGRANTS

I am concerned and disturbed by the statement quoted in a Times' article "Heavily Asian Town's Mayor Holds Tight to Controversial Views," by Berkley Hudson, (Metro, July, 16) on Barry L. Hatch, outgoing mayor (and current city councilman) of Monterey Park.

The statement quoted in the article stated that "new Asian immigrants have told him, 'We don't care about your culture, your traditions and your history. We're here to make money.' " Hatch has not only denounced the new Asian immigrants, but entire ethnic people groups.

If this statement was allegedly made, it certainly does not reflect the attitude of the entire new Asian immigrants or the Asian population. Those sweeping generalizations made by Hatch attach derogatory connotations to a particular group. The contents of the article touches upon a grave matter for the Asian people in the United States.

At the present, many Asian people continue to daily confront racial stereotypes and expectations that society has perpetuated. Many erroneous judgments and opinions originate from a single experience which does not give a true representation of the whole. It is an unfair and a wrongful assumption to single out and blame particular people groups on the present ills of society such as the "overburdened streets, schools, jails and hospitals of Southern California."

The culture and language barrier have alienated many people on both sides of the conflict. There are many Asian and Asian Americans who have successfully maintained their cultural identity and assimilated into American society, but this is masked by the visible group who is ignorant on how to or, unfortunately, choose not to. The vast majority of Americans have descended from or are immigrants from foreign countries, yet why is there a present outcry against those who come to the United States to seek a better life?

A constructive alternative to finger-pointing and increased animosity is an educational program to assist those new immigrants and others in assimilating into the American culture and society. This problem needs to be recognized and addressed. We can start by practicing the virtue of brotherly love and mutual help.

IRVIN R. LAI

Immediate Past National President

Chinese American Citizens

Alliance

San Francisco
