July 9, 1990, Monday, P.M. Final

PEOPLE'S PHARMACY: TANNING PILL WITH CANTHAXANTHIN MAY DAMAGE VISION

Question: With all the warnings about the dangers of getting too much sun, I was intrigued by an ad I recently saw for a tanning pill. It contains something called canthaxanthin which is supposed to be natural, harmless and approved by the U.S. Food and Drug Administration.

These tanning pills sound too good to be true but they aren't cheap. What do you think?

Answer: Canthaxanthin is a food coloring that is chemically related to beta carotene, the orange color in carrots. The amount of this agent used in food, however, is minuscule compared to the levels found in tanning pills.

Such products do not produce a golden glow that resembles a natural tan. The skin tends to turn more an orange color, and the stool can turn red. More alarming are reports of golden yellow crystals that show up in the retina of the eye. Prolonged use of these products may even lead to visual damage. The FDA has NOT approved canthaxanthin for use in tanning pills, so these products are illegal. If the FDA discovers them being sold, they are seized and removed from the market.

You are right to avoid a "natural" tan, but tanning pills don't appear to be risk-free either. Another alternative is dihydroxyacetone, a tanning lotion found in products such as Clinique Self-Tanning Formula, Coppertone Sunless Tanning Lotion and Estee Lauder Self-Action Tanning Creme.

Q: I just picked up a new prescription and the pharmacist told me not to store the pills in the bathroom. I like to keep my medications in the medicine chest so I remember to take them first thing in the morning. If the lid is on tight, what difference does it make where they are stored?

A: Check your bathroom mirror. If it fogs up when you take a shower or bath, there is too much humidity in the air to be good for drugs. Child-proof caps can't seal that moisture out. And even regular lids let humidity in every time the bottle is opened.
