February 19, 1989, Sunday, Home Edition

ON AGING: OFFENDERS MAY OUTGROW LIFE OF CRIME

The reason there are so few reports about older criminals is that, with age, most give up the trade. According to the U.S. Department of Justice, one-half of all people arrested for property crimes such as theft and robbery are 19 or younger. By contrast, only 19% are 30 or older.

Why is aging -- and the changes that accompany it -- such an effective deterrent to criminal behavior? Like many non-offenders, aging criminals "begin to take stock of their lives and their accomplishments," sociologist Neal Shover writes in "Aging Criminals." He reports on interviews with 50 offenders who were 40 and older.

In the process, many gradually view their youthful criminal identity as "stupid," and reshape once-grandiose aspirations into more modest but attainable goals. They grow more aware of time as a limited resource and, consequently, are more reluctant to spend it in prison. The development of satisfying relationships and ties to legitimate employment also encourage a more conventional life style.

Despite having learned to avoid trouble in prison, "older criminals will say that the time gets harder to do," says UCLA sociologist Jack Katz, author of "Seductions of Crime: Moral and Sensual Attractions in Doing Evil." He attributes this in part to "a loss of fascination in the myths and dramas that young people act out in crime. . . . In a hedonistic way, the seductions (of crime) become less attractive."

Noting that many offenders go straight in their 20s or 30s, Katz speculates that withdrawing from crime is a life-cycle phenomenon akin to outgrowing adolescence -- for persistent criminals, a prolonged one.

Successful older offenders who have prospered financially from crime are least likely to give it up, according to Shover. Nevertheless, he writes, many eventually mend their ways "in large measure because of real or imagined infirmities of age."

Question: On Christmas Day, my 73-year-old mother called paramedics because my father was ill. The individual who answered the phone apparently refused to take the call seriously. Fortunately, my brother dropped by and noticed that my father's breathing was very shallow.

He called the paramedics and, within one-half hour, my father was in the emergency room. Why didn't they pay attention to my mother's call?

Answer: Although most paramedics respond to emergencies appropriately, there have been cases where the paramedic operator failed to "pick up" on the seriousness of the call.

Emergency operators must make quick decisions with little information. Often when people call 911, they fail to transmit vital information because they are so overwhelmed with concern for the victim.

Another possible explanation is that the operator responded to your brother because it was the second call about the same individual. And though it rings of ageism and sexism, it may be that your brother's voice commanded more respect from the operator than your mother's.

According to Dr. Marshall Morgan of UCLA's Emergency Medicine Center, callers should give as specific information as possible. For example: "My father is having trouble breathing, has pain in his arm and has a history of heart problems." A specific description helps the emergency operator make an immediate decision without needing to ask many questions.

To improve emergency-operator responses, many paramedic programs are developing guidelines to help determine real emergencies.

Q: I'm a 52-year-old bride-to-be who will be tying the knot the second time around this spring. Can you advise me on what to wear?

A: Most second-time brides prefer pale colors or off-white. If you are planning an informal wedding, a knee-length suit or silk dress would be a good choice. If your wedding is more formal, you may want to wear a romantic dress of lace or chiffon with a mid-calf or long skirt. One advantage of a suit is that it can be worn again with a different blouse or buttons.

Shopping options include bridal departments and boutiques, securing the assistance of a personal shopper or having an item made-to-order. Some wedding-dress manufacturers have a collection geared for second marriages.

Most bridal experts recommend wearing a hat, bow or flowers, but not a veil.
