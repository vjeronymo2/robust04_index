930929 FT 29 SEP 93 / Labour troubles boil up at foreign plants in China WORKERS at foreign-funded factories in the Chinese port of Tianjin have gone on strike over low pay and bad conditions at least 10 times this year, according to an official newspaper, Reuter reports from Beijing. The Beijing Youth News, in a rare report on labour unrest amid China's economic boom, said the Tianjin strikes had hit mainly South Korean enterprises, although one Japanese company was also affected. A spokesman at the Tianjin Municipal Foreign Affairs Office confirmed he had heard of some strikes but declined to provide detailed information. The rash of strikes had prompted concern at high levels of the central government, including a special study meeting at the Ministry of Labour, the newspaper said. It said one strike now under way 'is being handled by the relevant authorities' but gave no details. It did not name any of the foreign companies involved. China has worked hard to play down the threat of strikes at its foreign-funded factories, which have been among the best performers in the economic upswing. Foreigners putting money into China have been promised a compliant and nimble workforce and a government-controlled umbrella union. A son of China's supreme leader Deng Xiaoping predicted yesterday his father's policies would survive after his death because they represented the wishes of China's population, AP reports from Beijing. 'I believe that China, under his leadership, has embarked on an irreversible course of reform and opening up,' the Chinese leader's eldest son, Deng Pufang, said at a news conference. His comments come soon after publication of his sister's biography of their father - part of a campaign to glorify the 89-year-old Deng and ensure his pragmatic modernisation policies survive his death. Asked about his father's health, he said: 'He is enjoying very good health.'
