Language: English Article Type:BFN [Text] Bucharest ROMPRES, 25/2/1994 -- "Any government in Romania must be a government for reform, and this necessarily implies a wide political and social support," President Ion Iliescu of Romania told the daily newspaper ADEVARUL. Asked whether he had any preferences for the government to be appointed after 1 March, the president of Romania said that he, personally, had no preference, and that he would like "a government enjoying some stability and enough parliamentary support." He underlined the distinction that should be made between "a political agreement between the political parties forming the parliament" and the "possible structure of a government." In this latter case a government, the president opined, "could be formed solely of technocrats or of representatives of a single political party, but only based on a political formula of a wider parliamentary support." About the much vented version of holding early elections, Romania's number one said that such an alternative would not be the best for Romania, because an election campaign at the moment would only "disturb public life." However, theoretically speaking, early elections "could be a solution unless others are found." President Iliescu's opinion is that in 1994 the Romanian society should concentrate its efforts on surmounting the crisis and on economic reconstruction. "No matter what governments will succeed one another, Romania's problems will be the same," the president of Romania said. As already widely reflected by the press, President Ion Iliescu had consultations with all the parliamentary parties with the aim of reaching a political pact aimed at reinstating a climate for dialogue between the political forces and at building a minimal common platform for taking the economic reform further. The first round of consultations ended in mid-January. Their resumption depends on the joint agreement of all parties involved.
