September 1, 1990, Saturday, Home Edition

MAHONY'S LETTERS TO SHEINKMAN

I write in response to the John Dart/Bob Baker article ("Mahony's Religious Suggestion to Jewish Unionist Stirs Anger," Religion, Aug. 25), and to express my puzzlement and my dismay.

My exchange of correspondence with Jack Sheinkman (president of the Amalgamated Clothing and Textile Workers Union in New York) began in February when I and other Catholic leaders across the country wrote to all the members of the Executive Council of the AFL-CIO urging them to take a neutral stance on the abortion question. These letters had nothing to do with the past cemetery workers dispute in the Los Angeles archdiocese.

I am puzzled why your reporters could not simply state these facts. Rather, they attempt to create division between me and Sheinkman, a man whom I respect greatly.

I was pleased that the result of these letters from many of us to the AFL-CIO actually resulted in the union taking a neutral stance on the abortion matter.

What shocks me is the obvious attempt by your reporters to inject a Jewish-Catholic rivalry in this entire matter. In Sheinkman's June 5 response to my May 21 letter, he makes absolutely no mention of interpreting that letter in the negative fashion described in the article. In two meetings with Sheinkman's general counsel later in June, his chief attorney also made no such inferences. In fact, it was only then in late June that I learned that Sheinkman was Jewish.

Virtually all of my letters written during the Easter season (April 15 to June 3) end with similar Christian expressions of joy and blessing. For a religious leader to extend blessings upon others does not reflect a disregard for their own religious faith or practices.

But your reporters have inferred a religious rift and apparently concocted this story to create a division between the Catholic and Jewish communities. I reject their assertion out of hand, and I am offended by their lack of knowledge concerning the outstanding relationship which has long existed, and continues to exist, between the Catholic community and the Jewish community in Southern California.

Your reporters cannot seem to state the facts clearly with respect to the past dispute between the archdiocese and ACTWU. The second election among our employees was held because they did not want anything to do with the union. The one-year election agreement had expired. But the purpose of the election was to give the employees their voice. And all evidence continues to show that our cemetery employees have absolutely no interest in belonging to ACTWU or any other union.

MOST REV. ROGER MAHONY

Archbishop of Los Angeles
