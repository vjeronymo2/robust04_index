June 2, 1990, Saturday, Home Edition

CONTROVERSY OVER NEA FUNDS FOR ART

The NEA isn't the stock market, no matter how much Fein and Meese would like it to be. It's no wonder that 25 years of NEA funding seems interminable to people who are always looking for a fast buck and higher return on their investments.

They expect masterworks to be produced and acknowledged in that short space of time, when the examples they cite have been around for decades, even centuries. The idea that great visual art is what garners the highest bids "at Southeby or Christie auctions" is yet another symptom of the malignant Reagan era money mentality: the measure of quality is in the price tag.

The proposal to shift NEA funds to education is admirable but redundant. All art is, by its very nature, public, inclusive, revealing, enlightening -- in other words, educational.

NEA grants aren't "government protection against free consumer choice." Rather, they are seed money for artistic gardens which, with diligence and luck, will prosper and perhaps, given years of critical and popular consideration, bloom into the accepted masterworks of the world and give joy and stimulation to everyone. And for the notion that art experts are "feasting off taxpayers' dollars" -- well, let's just say that no single artist is getting rich from NEA money. The price the government has paid certain military suppliers for a small bag of bolts and a toilet seat or two could float an entire colony of artists for years.

DAVID DRUMMOND

AUSTIN GRAY

Los Angeles
