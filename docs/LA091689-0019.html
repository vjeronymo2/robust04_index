September 16, 1989, Saturday, Home Edition

'FINE-TUNING' THE MEDICARE SURTAX

It's not enough to "fine-tune" the Medicare Catastrophic Coverage Act, as The Times suggested in its editorial defending this law ("All It Needs Is Fine-Tuning," Sept. 11). This new program needs a major overhaul.

As a member of Congress who voted against this bill, I have argued from the time its passage was imminent that we should scrap it and start over. Our original goal was right: to protect older Americans from the financial devastation that a major, long-term illness can cause. But we never achieved that goal. Instead of providing help with long-term nursing home or home health care -- the costs of which are feared the most by the elderly -- Congress provided a number of less essential benefits. It was no surprise that beneficiaries objected to paying very substantial fees for them.

The only truly catastrophic benefit of this new law is the unlimited hospitalization. The other principal new benefits are help with physicians' fees and prescription drug expenses. Large bills in both cases can cause hardship, but rarely are the costs "catastrophic," $2,000 worth of doctors' or pharmaceutical bills in a year is a modest expense compared to $30,000 for a yearlong stay in a typical nursing home, which really is catastrophic for all but the most affluent elderly.

Furthermore, many of the elderly have private insurance to pay for the hospital, physician, and prescription drug expenses that Medicare does not cover, but virtually none have insurance for long-term nursing home and home health care.

The existence of this new Medicare program has made it all but impossible for Congress to address the need for help with the costs of long-term care. Because of the huge expenses involved in nursing home or home health care, Congress cannot create a federal program to pay for them without asking beneficiaries to bear part of the cost. But we will also be hard-pressed to ask recipients to pay even higher health care fees after imposing such large premium increases and a surtax on the elderly to pay for some of these other, less urgent benefits.

Congress will soon be deciding whether to repeal or merely adjust the financing of this new law -- possibly in a way that would be detrimental to low- and middle-income elderly. Repeal is the right course: If we go back to square one and provide, instead, long-term care benefits -- the true "catastrophic" benefits that the elderly need and want the most (and probably thought they were getting with this bill) -- we're certain to see a much greater willingness on their part to help pay for them.

REP. ANTHONY C. BEILENSON

D-Los Angeles
