Language: Spanish Article Type:BFN [Text] Brasilia, 22 Mar (EFE) -- Brazilian Foreign Minister Celso Amorim stated today that his country endorses a consensus candidate for the election of the OAS secretary general to be held in Washington on 27 March. Amorim said that neither formal nor informal consultations were made on the OAS vote during the two-day meeting of the 13 Rio Group foreign ministers that ended today. Brazil has promised to vote for Colombian President Cesar Gaviria, contending that he would bring prestige to the OAS because of his successful record as president of his country for the four-year term that will end on 7 August. The foreign ministers and other government officials who represented their countries at the Brasilia meeting, which focused on the search for formulas to eradicate poverty and unemployment and on reconciling positions for the continental summit scheduled for December in Miami, sounded their preferences for the 27 March vote. Ambassadors from the 35 OAS member countries will elect the candidate to replace former Brazilian Deputy Foreign Minister Joao Baena Soares, who has served as OAS secretary general for two consecutive five-year terms since 1984. In addition to Gaviria, the other two candidates are Costa Rican Foreign Minister Bernd Niehaus and Venezuelan Foreign Minister Miguel Angel Burelli. Five positions have been identified within the Rio Group, the main forum for joint political and economic actions in Latin America. They are the positions of the three groups supporting each of the three candidates, the one advocating a formula for a consensus candidate, and Ecuador's undecided position. Seven Rio Group member countries -- Mexico, Argentina, Brazil, Bolivia, Paraguay, Colombia, and Peru -- support Gaviria; Chile, Guatemala, and Trinidad-Tobago support Niehaus; and Venezuela supports its foreign minister, Burelli. Uruguayan Foreign Minister Sergio Abreu today reaffirmed to EFE his 21 March proposal of seeking a consensus candidate, who could be any of the three candidates or someone else. The initiative to seek a consensus candidate, Abreu said, "may appear to be vague, but it is a responsible one" in light of the atmosphere of confrontation between the candidacies of Gaviria and Niehaus. Abreu did not give further details of his proposal. Abreu added: "An OAS election cannot be held in an atmosphere of confrontation. If we let matters take their course, we would be starting a new term of the secretary general with divisions already among its members."
