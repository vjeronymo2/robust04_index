Language: English Article Type:BFN [From the "Diplomatic Panorama" feature: By correspondents Dmitriy Voskoboinikov, Igor Porshnev, and Vitaliy Trubetskoy] [Text] The head of Russian diplomacy, Andrey Kozyrev, believes that the incident involving the cancelled meeting between President Boris Yeltsin and the former American President, Richard Nixon, as the recent "spy scandal connected with the Ames case", "must not be projected on political relations." The ex-president's aides are to blame for the incident, said Kozyrev in answer to Interfax's question after his Thursday's meeting with his Italian counterpart, Julio Andreatta. According to Kozyrev, "Nixon's first mistake was to neglect to address Russia's Foreign Ministry." "This is the usual practice, the more so as we have known each other for a long time. We would have oriented the ex-president correctly from the very beginning, in regard to meetings with the opposition he was seeking, too," Kozyrev said. "We, too, have to deal with the opposition every day, and know it well. Had Nixon asked me for a friendly advice, as a professional in the area of international relations, I would have advised him to confer with President Yeltsin first, particularly in view of the fact that they have known each other for a long time, and find acceptable and tactful ways to meet with the opposition leaders only afterwards. There were no obstacles for such meetings." He does not blame the American ex-president for what happened, Kozyrev stressed. "We blame his aides, who ought to have organized his trip with the help of their good friends in Russia, who would have oriented Nixon professionally. Now his aides have only themselves to blame," the foreign minister said. According to him, "there is no point in politicizing the errors of aides who did not manage to organize everything on a proper professional level." "The blunders of Nixon's aides must not cast a shadow on Russian-American relations," Kozyrev pointed out. With reference to his talks with the head of Italian diplomacy, Julio Andreatta, Kozyrev said that they had focused on the ways to organize the next G-7 summit with Russia's participation. It's common knowledge that currently Italy is the G-7's chairman. "We focused our attention on how to move toward turning the G-7 into G-8 at least in regard to political issues, where to do without Russia is impossible," Kozyrev said. According to him, the two sides also discussed the activity of the CSCE, since Andreatta is the chairman of that organization. They also considered such topics, as developments in Bosnia-Herzegovina, the situation connected with Russian troops withdrawal from the Baltic countries, and the observance of Russian-speakers' rights there. Former U.S. President Richard Nixon has no intention of curtailing his visit to Russia and will remain in Moscow until March 16, officials close to Nixon, who is on a private visit in the Russian capital, told Interfax. On Wednesday [9 March] President Yeltsin said neither he nor any of the Russian leaders would see Nixon following his meetings with opposition leaders -- Communist Party leader Gennadiy Zyuganov and former Vice President Aleksandr Rutskoy. "We will not seek meetings with Russian officials," one of Nixon's aides told Interfax. He said Nixon would attend a formal reception at the U.S. Embassy late on Thursday and speak to embassy officials at 1200 on Friday. He will later meet with Russian deputies including Irina Khakamada. Asked if Nixon was planning to meet LDP [Liberal Democratic Party] leader Vladimir Zhirinovskiy, one of his aides said "no agreement has been reached, though Zhirinovskiy's representatives were in contact with the American side." The aide said that although Nixon arrived in Moscow in a private capacity, this did not prevent him from maintaining contacts with the U.S. Embassy here. A meeting between a representative of another state and the opposition leader in a democratic country "must not be viewed as a provocative act," the leader of the Democratic Party faction in the State Duma, Nikolay Travkin, told Interfax in comments on President Yeltsin's refusal to meet with the former U.S. president, Richard Nixon. As Travkin said, the fact that "somebody came and conferred with the opposition must not irritate the head of state who claims the role of democrat and builder of democracy."
