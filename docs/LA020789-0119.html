February 7, 1989, Tuesday, Home Edition

KITTY DUKAKIS IS TREATED FOR ALCOHOLISM

Apparently despondent over her husband's failed Democratic presidential campaign last year, Kitty Dukakis has entered a treatment program for alcoholism, Massachusetts Gov. Michael S. Dukakis announced Monday.

Dukakis said his wife, who spoke repeatedly during the campaign about her successful battle in 1982 to quit a 26-year dependency on diet pills, had begun drinking at home in the three months since the election.

"Until shortly after Election Day on Nov. 8, Kitty had never had a problem with alcohol," Dukakis said in a statement released in Boston. "Unfortunately, a combination of physical exhaustion, the stress of the campaign effort and the post-election let-down all combined to create a situation in which, on a limited number of occasions while at home, she has used alcohol in excessive quantities."

Facility in Newport

The governor said his wife was admitted Sunday evening to Edgehill Newport, a highly regarded, 160-bed residential treatment facility in Newport, R.I., for alcoholics and chemically dependent men and women.

Dukakis said his wife voluntarily decided to seek treatment because she has recognized since her 1982 treatment that "she is chemically dependent and . . . that her recovery is always at risk." He said he and his family supported her decision.

Cathy Damakos, an Edgehill spokeswoman, said the treatment typically begins with one to seven days of close medical observation, followed by a 28-day rehabilitation program involving one-on-one counseling, group therapy, plus family sessions. A Statehouse spokeswoman in Boston said Dukakis would participate in his wife's therapy sessions.

Mrs. Dukakis announced in July, 1987, that she had been treated five years earlier at the Hazelden clinic in Minnesota for a 26-year addiction to amphetamines.

"As she has now discovered, whether it comes in a bottle or in solid, if you're chemically dependent, you're chemically dependent," Dukakis told a Statehouse news conference. After her 1982 treatment, "she thought they were two different things," he said.

Mrs. Dukakis campaigned frequently before students last year to cite her own story in moving terms and warn about the dangers of drug and alcohol abuse. Like many recovering addicts and alcoholics, she said in an interview with The Times last May that she still had to "struggle" with her former dependency.

Effusive Campaigner

Often described as a fiery complement to her husband's icy demeanor, Mrs. Dukakis was a warm, wisecracking and effusive campaigner.

Mrs. Dukakis, 52, began a nationwide speaking tour on Jan. 11 with a United Jewish Appeal reception in Beverly Hills, and has signed a contract to co-author a book about her experiences in the campaign. But since the election, "she has been very sad," said Sandy Bakalar, a lifelong friend who often traveled with Mrs. Dukakis during the campaign. "I think she's been grieving. This loss has been very, very difficult."

Bakalar said Mrs. Dukakis felt "sad and empty" when the campaign ended. "For a year and a half, she would wake up, people would set her schedule, they would tell her where she was going, a car was waiting and she was gone," she said. "She loved it. She was going 1,000 miles a minute, and then on Nov. 8, it was suddenly over. It was like someone died."

Dr. Al Peters, a recovering alcoholic who is Mrs. Dukakis' brother-in-law and who advised her to seek treatment, said her alcoholism was being treated "at the earliest stages." He praised her for going public with the problem.

"It takes an awful lot of courage to recognize and to admit one's chemical dependency, be it alcoholism or whatever, and to seek treatment," he said. "Hopefully, this may send a message to other people to have the courage to admit their problem and to seek appropriate treatment. Alcoholism is a treatable disease. The only shame in alcoholism is when it's not treated."
