May 15, 1989, Monday, Home Edition

PARENTS PLAN VARIED ROLES IN STRIKE;

SOME TAKING SIDES, PITCHING IN OR TRYING TO PLAY WATCHDOG IN CONFLICT

Westchester parent Sherry Otsap says she'll be walking the picket line with her son's teachers today. Barbara Bronson Gray, a Van Nuys parent, says she's going to teach. Pam Bruns of Pacific Palisades says she's going to watch.

As strike conditions take hold in schools all across the Los Angeles Unified School District today, parents will be taking sides, pitching in or trying to play the watchdog in a conflict that threatens to turn many schools, bereft of regular teachers, upside down.

No matter what side they're on, most say they're worried about whether the schools will be safe, whether their children will be properly supervised, whether they will get any meaningful education -- or whether school will just turn into a big baby-sitting operation.

About 20 parents and children held a rally Sunday outside United Teachers-Los Angeles headquarters near downtown to protest the planned walkout.

Even district officials admit that it's going to be tough out there. About half of the district's 32,000 teachers voted to go on strike, and the district estimates that it will have about 1,000 substitutes and administrators available to cover classes.

Monitoring Project

"We're hoping that we're going to see conditions that will satisfy our parental anxieties," said Bruns, who is the co-founder of a Palisades parent-teacher group that is sending observation teams onto several campuses today. "But we just don't know what's going to happen."

Bruns spent Mother's Day in feverish preparation for today. Early Sunday morning, about 20 Palisades parents gathered at her home to make final plans for an ambitious monitoring project that will cover the coastal community's seven public schools, which together enroll about 6,000 students.

Teams of two to four parents armed with checklists and badges identifying them as "Parent Observers" will visit each school at three times during the day, Bruns said. They will look to see whether students are receiving instruction, whether they are in their regular classrooms or herded together in gyms or auditoriums, what kind of personnel -- regular teachers, substitutes or volunteers -- is manning the school, and whether safe conditions prevail.

Bruns said that although the parent observers all have strong opinions on both sides of the teacher dispute, they have agreed to keep their views in check as they visit schools.

"We will only be there to observe. We are not going to teach or supervise. Our main concern is the safety of students during this tension-ridden strike period, and we hope teachers on the picket line will understand our mission and not be obstructive," she said.

On the other hand, Gray, the Van Nuys parent, said she plans to be anything but neutral.

"I'm frustrated with teachers," said Gray, whose child attends kindergarten at Kester Avenue School. "A lot of parents out there will be happy to cross the picket line and do whatever they can."

Gray said her principal expects that most of the school's 28 teachers will honor the strike. About 20 parents plan to show up today to help operate the school.

One mother who is an artist plans to set up an "art station" that groups of students will rotate through, every hour or so. Other parents will tell stories in the library and supervise the lunch hour. One parent who works in alcohol rehabilitation will give a lesson about alcoholism.

Gray, who is a nurse, said she plans to bring her stethoscope to class and give a lesson about cardiac physiology.

"So there will be some enrichment going on," she said.

'Far Too Dangerous'

Otsap, the Westchester parent who plans to walk the picket line, said she believes the teachers have legitimate gripes. But she will not let her son, who is in the eighth grade at Palms Junior High School's gifted magnet program, attend his classes during the strike. She's heard rumors of a gang fight today and is afraid that the school will be unsafe.

"How in the world can we possibly put our kids in school when in the best of times there is violence?" she said. "Now they're going to be berserk. I don't dare put my child in school. It's far too dangerous."

Another Westside parent said she, too, is keeping her child at home during the strike. But, unlike Otsap, she said she feels no sympathy for the teachers and plans to cross the picket line.

"I promised I'd volunteer," she said. "But I might get cold feet. If we go up to the picket line, is it going to be like the Continental (Airlines) machinists? Are they going to leap up and drag us to the ground? I think a lot of parents are going to take their children to see what it's like, but if they see their child's teacher on the picket line and she snarls at them, they'll go home."
