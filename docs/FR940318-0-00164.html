The blend offers an alternative with significantly less potential to deplete ozone than CFC&hyph;12. Users must be in compliance with section 610 Nonessential Products Containing Class II Substances. (d) HFC&hyph;134a. HFC&hyph;134a is acceptable as a substitute for CFC&hyph;12 in extruded polystyrene boardstock and billet foam. HFC&hyph;134a offers the potential for a non-ozone-depleting alternative to CFC&hyph;12 blowing agents in extruded polystyrene boardstock and billet foam. HFC&hyph;134a, because of its low flammability and encouraging performance in toxicological testing, exhibits definite advantages from the standpoints of environmental risk and worker and consumer safety. However, HFC&hyph;134a has relatively high thermal conductivity, is costly, and has the potential to contribute to global warming. In addition, the compound has poor solubility in polystyrene polymer, which could limit its usefulness as an alternative blowing agent from a technical standpoint. HFC&hyph;134a also has a relatively high global warming potential compared to other available alternatives. (e) HFC&hyph;152a. HFC&hyph;152a is acceptable as a substitute for CFC&hyph;12 in extruded polystyrene boardstock and billet foam. HFC&hyph;152a offers the potential for a non-ozone-depleting alternative to CFC&hyph;12 blowing agents in extruded polystyrene boardstock. However, the high flammability of HFC&hyph;152a when combined with its properties of high thermal conductivity, low solubility in polystyrene polymer, and high permeability through polystyrene limit the extent to which HFC&hyph;152a is likely to replace CFC&hyph;12. Plant modifications may be needed to accommodate the flammability of HFC&hyph;152a, and foams blown with HFC&hyph;152a will need to conform with building code requirements that relate to flammable materials. (f) Saturated light hydrocarbons C3&hyph;C6. Saturated light hydrocarbons C3&hyph;C6 are acceptable as substitutes for CFC&hyph;12 in polystyrene boardstock and billet foam. Of the Saturated Light Hydrocarbons C3&hyph;C6, pentane, isopentane, butane, and isobutane have been demonstrated as feasible blowing agents in polystyrene. In fact, saturated light hydrocarbons C3&hyph;C6 have been used for years in the manufacture of extruded polystyrene sheet products. However, saturated light hydrocarbons C3&hyph;C6 have several disadvantages as blowing agents in extruded polystyrene boardstock and billet foam. Replacement of CFC&hyph;12 blowing agents with Saturated Light Hydrocarbons C3&hyph;C6 may reduce the insulating efficiency in this end-use. Controlling the flammability of saturated light hydrocarbons C3&hyph;C6 may entail significant investment in plant conversion to accommodate them as alternatives to CFC&hyph;12. Foams blown with saturated light hydrocarbons C3&hyph;C6 will need to conform with building code requirements that relate to flammable materials. Finally, saturated light hydrocarbons C3&hyph;C6 are VOCs and must be controlled as such under Title I of the CAA. (g) HCFC&hyph;22/Saturated Light Hydrocarbons C3&hyph;C6.
