940215 FT 15 FEB 94 / UN backs Nato over Bosnian air strikes The United Nations Security Council yesterday threw its weight behind the Nato threat to launch air strikes around Sarajevo if Bosnian Serb and Bosnian government troops fail to place their artillery under UN supervision. Security Council members sent a clear message to the Serbs during a debate that the UN meant to act in total unison with Nato. US ambassador Ms Madeleine Albright told the Security Council: 'Co-operation between Nato and the UN is essential - not only for the citizens of Sarajevo and other safe areas in Bosnia, but also for the precedent it will set for the future of collective security.' The statement came as the British commander of the United Nations Protection Force (Unprofor) in Sarajevo said he had no doubt that by the time the ultimatum was up the Serbs would have complied totally with the weapons demand. But Lieut Gen Sir Michael Rose told reporters that he would have 'no hesitation' in calling in airstrikes after the ultimatum expired if they were warranted. Nato has said it will strike at positions held by either Serbs or Bosnian government troops if heavy weapons located in the exclusion zone are not handed in by midnight GMT on Sunday. Last night, French Prime Minister Edouard Balladur reaffirmed that there would be no extension of the Nato ultimatum. Mr Balladur told France-2 television: 'The ultimatum will not be extended by a day.' Earlier, Gen Jean Cot, the French commander, admitted there were very few weapons being handed over despite attempts by UN forces to speed up their collection so that Nato air strikes would not be necessary. Sir Michael has already spoken to senior Nato officials about the need for much greater back-up on the ground to collect and supervise heavy weapons. He acted to quash reported differences between Nato and UN officers over what constitutes UN control over the Serb weapons. Sir Michael Said: 'Nato and the United Nations are completely aligned as to what our plans are.'
