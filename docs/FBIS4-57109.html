BFN [By Alonso de Contreras] [Text] Santo Domingo, 12 May (EFE) -- The illegal appointment of Emile Jonassaint as Haitian president places the Dominican Government in a new, truly uncomfortable position -- a vantage point from which it has decided not to meddle in the internal affairs of its neighboring country. Today, the Dominican Government distanced itself from international criticism of the Haitian coupists for appointing Jonassaint as the new Haitian president. The government declared this was "an internal matter" and a new twist in a crisis the Haitian people must resolve themselves. In statements to EFE, Dominican Foreign Minister Aristides Taveras Guzman today said: "We understand the appointment... is a move by the Haitian politicians, and, as a country that abides by its government's policy, we respect the sovereignty and integrity of the people." With the backing of the military that seized power in a September 1991 coup, Supreme Court Magistrate Emile Jonassaint assumed the presidency of Haiti on Wednesday, drawing criticism from the international community and causing fears of armed intervention from abroad. Aristides Guzman said: "Although we have endorsed OAS and UN efforts to achieve the democratic transition of the (Haitian) people, we have always stressed, in one way or another, that the Haitians should be the sole protagonists in resolving their problems." This position by the Dominican Government, according to what Foreign Ministry officials admitted privately, reflects their impotence in face of a Haitian crisis that has no good or acceptable resolutions from a Dominican standpoint. The Dominican Republic is fated not to agree with but to comply with UN and OAS sanctions against the Haitian military -- sanctions designed to return deposed President Jean Bertrand Aristide to power. The Dominican Republic is hoping the crisis will calmly die on its own, preferably by the end of the constitutional term of the Haitian government-in-exile, which is in February 1995. All current developments in the Haitian crisis are threatening the Dominican Republic. Aristide was criticized because of his anti-Dominican remarks during the seven months he was in power. The military government cannot be accepted, however, because of international opposition, and the same is true for the illegal appointment of Emile Jonaissant. The economic embargo imposed by the United Nations and the OAS has not resolved the problem and has discredited the Dominican Armed Forces, which have been unable to prevent smuggling across the border. The new embargo, which will be implemented on 21 May, puts more responsibility on Dominican surveillance and will require additional spending. The number of Haitian exiles and refugees are increasing in the Dominican Republic, while the Dominican Government is resisting pressure to install refugee camps near the border. The Dominican Republic also has been obliged to accept international observers, who will arrive this week to oversee the fulfillment of the embargo. All of these problems were outlined to EFE today by Deputy Foreign Minister Fabio Herrera Cabral, who confirmed the acceptance of international observers, but he reiterated that "the embargo violates the Haitian people's human rights. He also said the embargo harms the Dominican Republic in view of international accusations of "alleged violations" and by the flow of Haitians. Herrera Cabral said the Dominican Government dedicates virtually 25 million pesos ($2 million) a month to maintaining border security. All of these inconveniences have been heightened in the past months by the campaign for the upcoming general elections, which will be held this coming Monday in the Dominican Republic. U.S. diplomatic sources, who chose to remain anonymous, confirmed to EFE that the Dominican Government "suggested" to the United States that it refrain from making "any move" on Haiti before the Dominican elections.
