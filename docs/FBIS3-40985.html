Language: English Article Type:BFN [Text] Podgorica, Yugoslavia, Feb 1 (AFP) -- A deadly Russian secret "laser" weapon which zaps its victims' brains will be tested Wednesday [2 February] in Serb-held northern Bosnia, Russian ultra-nationalist leader Vladimir Zhirinovskiy said Tuesday [1 February]. Zhirinovskiy told journalists in the Montenegrin capital that "my officers (soldiers of the Russian army close to Zhirinovskiy's party) will carry out a test of the weapon tomorrow in a confined space in Brcko." Speaking after a meeting here with Svetozar Marovic, secretary general of the governing ex-communist Socialist Democratic Party, Zhirinovskiy said: "Russia has several types of secret weapon. Their two-pronged effect is impossible to survive." The weapon to be tested Wednesday "acts directly on the brain, has the power of a laser and is not radioactive but ecological," he added. The announcement echoed claims made by the leader of the Liberal Democratic Party in December that Russia possessed a secret weapon called the "Elipton" that was more powerful than a nuclear bomb and could wipe out the whole of humanity. The claim was dismissed at the end of December by Russian Defence Minister Pavel Grachev. News of the test raised eyebrows in Belgrade as even if the weapon existed, the Russian volunteers known to be fighting in the former Yugoslavia are generally young men rather than officers or technicians. Zhirinovskiy later told a crowd of several thousand people gathered in the centre of Podgorica that "Russians and Serbs have two enemies, the Catholic religion in the west and Islam in the east." Russia had therefore to "play the major role in liberating the Slav peoples who are under threat of being converted to Catholicism." Zhirinovskiy, who is on a whirlwind tour of republics of the former Yugoslavia, was also due to meet with Montenegrin opposition representatives here. He visited Serb-held regions of Croatia and Bosnia Monday and was expected to return to Trebinje, in the self-declared Bosnian Serb republic, later Tuesday. Montenegro and Serbia, which form the present rump Yugoslavia, are subject to UN-imposed international sanctions for backing Serb forces in Bosnia-Herzegovina.
