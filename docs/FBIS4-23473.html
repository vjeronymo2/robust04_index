BFN [From the "Focus on Africa" program] [Text] There were signs of growing disagreement among leaders of Malawi's Aford [Alliance for Democracy] opposition party, which came second in last month's elections won by the United Democratic Front [UDF] of President Bakili Muluzi. Yesterday, on this program, one of Aford's founder member, Victor Ndove, criticized the party's leader, Chakufwa Chihana, both for his personal style and for his handling of negotiations with the UDF over the formation of the new government. He even accused the Aford leadership of holding talks with the ousted Malawi Congress Party, the MCP. On the Line to Mangochi, Chris Greene asked Aford's Secretary General Dr. (Mucky Mtewa) how he responded to Victor Ndove's criticism. [Begin recording] [(Mtewa)] First, of course, on a personal note, I have tremendous respect for my good friend Victor Ndove. In responding to the concern of the good founder, is that, of course, the position taken by President Chakufwa Chihana, on behalf of this party, in wanting to preserve the integrity of his members in parliament, and of course, definitely, the intricate structure of consultation within the Alliance for Democracy, was to make sure that everything else, as much as possible, was democratically conducted and that we were giving away the party or delivering our members in parliament to the UDF government, without knowing precisely the conditions and terms which would be acceptable to our members within the party. [Greene] But, in fact, you failed to negotiate yourselves into the governing coalition and you are now just a minor opposition party. [(Mtewa)] No, that is not...[pauses] I think I must have said about 48 hours ago that the negotiations and the talking still continue and nothing has been terminated yet, contrary to those perceptions. [Greene] Is it true that you are also negotiating with the former ruling MCP party? [(Mtewa)] Negotiating would be the wrong term. I am not sure what we are negotiating about. I think we are talking to all individuals in the country, as Malawians, given the (?need for) the preservation of the delicate balance of democracy in this country. On matters of common interest, should those happen to coincide at all in the debate on legislation introduced by government, we will be expected to consult and talk to those other members of the other party on how best to approach those issues of common interest. Talking now, of course, is purely a matter of friendship. They are both friends and that they were never enemies. They just happened to be members in the government at the time, as, I am sure, the UDF gentlemen happened to be members of that government for many years. [Greene] That is a very conciliatory language from you, given the sorts of things that Chakufwa Chihana said after he came out of prison? [(Mtewa)] Definitely, also conciliatory in terms of the personal life of this secretary general. For the record, of course, both my parents are crippled by the Malawi Congress Party government. I think you can say, at least, in politics we grow fast and we learn the hard way. [end recording]
