February 26, 1990, Monday, Home Edition

DEALS: MARKET WATCH;

HOW SMART DEALING, BORROWED BUCKS MADE BIG PROFIT FOR BASS

When you're a very smart billionaire, even deals that seem a little crummy turn out to be very, very profitable.

Consider, if you will, the tale of Robert M. Bass, the Ft. Worth zillionaire, and his takeover of one of the country's biggest troubled savings and loan associations, American Savings Bank of Irvine. American, you may remember, was owned by Financial Corp. of America, the nation's largest S&L, now a bankrupt hulk.

When American disclosed its 1989 earnings, those of us in the financial voyeur business began to worry about Bass, who has parlayed a large inherited fortune into an enormous fortune by successfully playing corporate takeover games.

Bass seemed to have made less than 40% last year on his American Savings investment. True, the bank earned a nice $215 million on its $16 billion of assets. But because Bass owns only 70% of American -- the Federal Deposit Insurance Corp. owns the rest -- his share of the profit was a crummy $150 million. Divide that by the $400 million that Bass invested in American, and he made a mere 37.5%.

For most of us, of course, 37.5% isn't mere. But for the Basses of the world, that's passbook savings money. Especially considering that Bass made his deal with M. Danny Wall, head of the Federal Home Loan Bank Board, who was holding a garage sale in late 1988, shoveling dead S&Ls and income tax benefits out the door.

Fear not for Bass. When you figure out what he really made on his real investment, he earned more than 150% on his investment last year. That's more like it.

Bass' paper profit last year, being very conservative, was $223 million to $310.5 million on an investment of $150 million. Bass earned this bonanza despite having been shafted by the government, which decided after he had plunked down his dough that he would have to add $240 million to American's capital. The government going back on its word to Bass is one reason that no one with money has been buying dead S&Ls at the government's current remnant sale, run by the Resolution Trust Corp.

But despite everything, Bass has still managed to make a fortune on American. His secret: smart dealing and borrowed money.

Bass, it turns out, borrowed $250 million of the $400 million that he put into American. When you slog your way through all the numbers, you discover that American paid out $75 million of its 1989 profit to a Bass holding company to cover interest on the borrowed money and to give the FDIC its agreed-on 75% of Bass' income tax benefits.

That left a $140-million profit. Bass' 70% is $98 million -- a return of 65% on his $150-million investment.

But wait, there's more.

Bass, remember, owns 70% of American, which is a very profitable institution now that the government has taken responsibility for the bad loans and idiotic interest rate gambles that sank the old American.

The Bass people, who would rather the world didn't recognize how good their American Savings deal is, didn't want to talk to me about their deal. No one wants to be hauled in front of the House Banking Committee, which let the S&L problem fester for years, and now wants to pillory anyone who has made a dime from it.

If you take the numbers that Bass' people have given members of the financial community -- numbers that I feel are too low -- you see that American Savings ought to net at least $125 million a year after making its income-tax-savings payments to FDIC.

In today's market, the shares of such an S&L should sell for at least six or seven times annual earnings -- or $750 million to $875 million.

Give Bass his 70% share of that, do the math I've done in the table accompanying this article, and you see that Bass' stock is worth $125 million to $212.5 million more than his investment. Plus, of course, there's his $98-million share of the 1989 profit.

But Bass is a piker compared to what Ronald O. Perelman, the New York-based takeover tycoon, has made on his S&L investment. Perelman made an $800-million paper profit last year on a $160-million investment.

The difference involves income taxes. Because the payments the government makes to cover losses on the bad assets that Perelman and Bass inherited in their S&Ls are tax-exempt, both of their S&Ls have huge tax losses. Perelman can use those losses to shelter income produced by Revlon and other companies that he owns. I think Ford Motor Co.'s S&L deal works the same way.

But Bass' deal, as best I can tell, allows him to shelter nothing but American Savings' profits. He can't shelter Bell & Howell's profits with them. So, even though Bass racked up $1.2 billion of tax losses by my estimate last year, he could use only $215 million of them. He may be able to use the rest of the losses someday, but I haven't counted them in my calculations.

Even though Bass has made a huge profit from the S&L debacle that we and our descendants will pay for until the end of time, you shouldn't be angry with him. He bid for American Savings, fair and square, against other potential buyers, and no one made the government deal with him. You should remember, too, that Bass was willing to risk taking an $8-billion promissory note -- now one of American Savings' assets -- from a federal agency that auditors had said was insolvent.

You can't blame the Basses, the Perelmans and the Ford Motors for making good deals for themselves. That's their job. What the federal government's job is, God only knows.

BASS NETS NICE HAUL

Robert M. Bass has done very nicely with his investment in American Savings Bank, a big, troubled S&L that he took over in late 1988.

American's 1989 profit was: $215 million

Less interest, expense and FDIC payments of: $75 million

Left this for Bass to split with the FDIC: $140 million

Bass' 70% share was: $98 million

Which gave a profit of: 65%

But that doesn't count the value of Bass' stock in American Savings. Add the stock's estimated value and you get the following:

His stock is worth at least: $525 million to $612 million

Plus his share of 1989's profit of: $98 million

Less borrowed money of: $250 million

Less his investment of: $150 million

Leaves him this far ahead: $223 million to $310.5 million

Which gives him a profit of: 149% to 207%

Source: Estimates from public filings.
