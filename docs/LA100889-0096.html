October 8, 1989, Sunday, Valley Edition

NIGHT MOVES: AT BEOGRAD, ROBUST FOOD, REVELRY TRANSLATE INTO ONE WORD: YUGOSLAVIAN

You might not know Marshall Josip Tito from Archduke Francis Ferdinand.

Perhaps you've never entertained the notion of pljeskavica or raznjici.

But consider, for a moment, Yugoslavia's robust wines, filo dough, stuffed meat pastries and Serbo-Croatian folk songs crooned by the Balkans' answer to Frank Sinatra.

The proprietors of Beograd, a restaurant and nightclub in North Hollywood, have given all of these Yugoslavian amenities a great deal of consideration. The bland exterior of the restaurant, in a mini-mall near Magnolia and Cahuenga boulevards, gives little indication of the Slavic delights that lie within.

The food is tasty, hearty and reasonably priced. The service is friendly without being overbearing. And where else in Los Angeles can you and your loved ones get serenaded by Zvomko Mitrovic, a pinstripe-suited Yugoslavian who puts his big Croatian heart into the songs he belts out to packed crowds each Friday and Saturday night?

In the year since Ljijana Javorina and Vojkan Kalnic opened Beograd, the nondescript corner has turned into a little slice of Yugoslavia on weekend nights, attracting Americans as well as Montenegrans, Serbs, Croats and other representatives of Yugoslavia's six republics and two autonomous provinces who come to rekindle memories of their homeland.

Don't be surprised if some of your fellow diners jump to their feet in the midst of their chicken Paprikash, link hands and begin dancing an ethnic fast-step that takes them in a half-circle around the room.

Although Beograd opens at 5 p.m, things don't really kick into gear until about 10:30 p.m. Sometimes the revelry extends late into the night. One recent Sunday morning at 1:30, the musicians were still singing to a packed (and rapt) house.

Javorina and Kalnic used to own Yugoslavian Village, a now-closed restaurant on Barham Boulevard near Cahuenga. Kalnic, who often plays accordion in the house band, is in contact with musician friends he left behind in Belgrade 17 years ago. As a result, Beograd is a showcase for visiting Yugoslavian musicians, who come to Los Angeles for six-week stints. The revolving door ensures that singers know the newest Slavic pop songs in addition to more traditional fare. And there's no cover.

One night, an accordion player from Belgrade coaxed unusual, vibrato-like sounds from his instrument, and Mitrovic's vocals evoked the quavering plaintive singing one associates with Islamic music. The band can switch to rousing polkas at the drop of a hat. Drum machines are verboten, and Mitrovic works the room like a celebrity entertainer in Las Vegas, maneuvering from table to table, trailing a long microphone cord, and trading jokes with the crowd in Serbo-Croatian.

Since Yugoslavia was cobbled together after World War I from the republics of Bosnia and Herzegovina, Montenegro, Macedonia, Slovenia and Croatia and two autonomous provinces, Vojvodina and Kosovo, Yugoslavian cuisine, like its music, is really a melange of influences. But dieters be warned -- this is not low-cal food.

Dinners run from $9.95 to $13.95 for specials and include a tomato-based spicy fish soup or salad that comes with "mysterious Yugoslavian dressing," according to a waitress. This translated into a salad of crisp romaine with what tasted like a Caesar dressing.

Chicken breasts came stuffed with feta cheese, rolled in nutty-tasting bread crumbs and fried, accompanied by sauteed peppers, carrots, rice and potatoes.

And the pljeskavica? That's a mixture of ground pork, beef, lamb and bell peppers served on a bed of onions.

Among the desserts, palacsinka -- stuffed crepe suzettes topped with a chocolate sauce -- stands out.

Beograd also offers strong, 12%-alcohol Yugoslavian beer for $3.50, mineral water, Turkish coffee and a variety of "Jugoslavian" wines at $11.95 a bottle.

Javorina, who has worked in restaurants for 25 years and does most of the cooking, says she plans to get a Yugoslavian publishing house to start sending over some Belgrade newspapers to make her Slavic customers feel at home.

But whether you're a Serb or an Angeleno, "When you come here, you feel like you are in Yugoslavia."

Beograd, 10540 Magnolia Blvd., North Hollywood, is open from 5 to 10 p.m. Wednesday, Thursday and Sunday; from 5 p.m. until 2 a.m. Friday and Saturday. Visa and MasterCard accepted. (818) 766-8689.
