August 15, 1990, Wednesday, Home Edition

PERSPECTIVE ON THE PERSIAN GULF;

WE'VE GONE AS FAR AS WE CAN;

U.S. DEPLOYMENT: THE WEST'S OIL IS SECURE, SO LET ARAB FORCES TAKE OVER THE TASK OF PUNISHING IRAQ.

The problem now is the notorious difficulty of sitting on bayonets. President Bush says that the objective of the United States in the gulf is "restoration of Kuwait's legitimate government." That also is what the United Nations Security Council demands.

The President says that U.S. forces in Saudi Arabia are not there "to drive the Iraqis out of Kuwait." Why, then, should the Iraqis leave?

The Security Council intends them to be forced out by economic and political pressures. Hence the U.N.-mandated embargo on commerce with Iraq.

However, the list of successful embargoes is short. This one has near-universal support from the United Nations' member governments, but it is opposed by millions of individuals in the Middle East who believe that Iraq's Saddam Hussein is the man to lead them out of poverty and end the humiliations Arabs have experienced throughout modern times.

Iraq has land borders with Jordan, Syria, Turkey, Saudi Arabia and Iran. Turkey and Saudi Arabia are committed to the embargo, the latter with little enthusiasm but no choice, as the victim of the piece.

Jordan is trapped, and King Hussein would surely be grateful if Saudi Arabia and Israel, his other neighbors, and the Western powers at sea, would enforce the U.N. embargo for him, which they are in a position to do.

Syria's is a complex and brutal game of regional rivalry. Its president, Hafez Assad, has joined the embargo and is sending a contingent to the Arab forces in Saudi Arabia to advance Syrian interests and check his enemy, Saddam Hussein. Syria, though, is heavily engaged in Lebanon, where enthusiasm for the Iraqi leader is incandescent; and Lebanon is not a place today where one can be optimistic about enforcing embargoes. In Lebanon nothing is enforced.

Iran's is a complex game, too, as it has every reason to wish its old enemy Saddam Hussein destroyed, yet every reason to wish him success in provoking a high international price for oil -- which is what he was doing when the United States stepped in.

The Iraqi president now is waging a successful political struggle by merging Islamic militance with pan-Arab nationalism. In modern times there have been two movements for pan-Arab renewal and "liberation," both utopian in intellectual and emotional appeal, the two in conflict with one another. One wanted theocracy, re-creation of the society that existed when the caliphs ruled an Islamic empire reaching from southern India to Spain. It includes Iranians and Islamic fundamentalists everywhere. The other movement is pan-Arab nationalism, which originated among Syrian and Lebanese intellectuals between the two world wars. One version has become the ideology of the dictatorships in both Syria and Iraq. Another was that of the most important modern Arab nationalist leader, Egypt's Nasser. His is the mantle Saddam Hussein wants to assume -- or usurp.

Hussein went to war against Iran a decade ago to fight Islamic fundamentalism. Today he proclaims that he is defending Islam's holy places against American and Western infidels -- and he is taken seriously. (It is a politically incendiary fact that the American build-up takes place not far from Mecca and Medina, while Jerusalem, Islam's third holy place, is in Israel's hands.)

If the U.N. embargo and Anglo-American blockade do not make Saddam Hussein disgorge Kuwait -- and they are not likely to do so -- the United States confronts a prospect either of military action in increasingly difficult political circumstances, or eventual withdrawal of its expeditionary force with whatever grace as can be managed.

If the oil market stabilizes, as a result of increased Saudi and gulf production and of resumed measures of fuel substitution and conservation in the West, military action to free Kuwait will run against U.S. domestic political opposition to anything that risks U.S. casualties. And there is no way to take Kuwait away from Iraq by force that will not produce substantial American casualties.

This being so, the American military buildup seems increasingly disproportionate both to the current threat to Saudi Arabia and to the policy options before the United States. Forces now reported on the move or on alert include the 82nd and 101st Airborne Divisions, the 7th Light Infantry, the 24th Mechanized Division, two Marine Expeditionary Brigades, a battleship, four aircraft carriers, plus 50 or so other major fleet units, B-52s, Stealth bombers and fighters, F-111s, etc.

Are they all eventually to be redeployed home with nothing accomplished?

If the United States is not prepared to take Kuwait away from Iraq, the Arab League's decision to put troops into Saudi Arabia should have been accepted as evidence that the problem now was in Arab hands. The U.S. fleet could continue to monitor the embargo and the threat to Saudi Arabia, but American ground forces come out as Egyptians, Syrians and Moroccans go in.

A considerable test has arrived of the seriousness, steadiness and policy competence of the Bush Administration. President Bush has made his point about America being a reliable ally. Saudi oil is secure for the present, and the international community is mobilized against Saddam Hussein. What now is the American aim? If it is the liberation of Kuwait, is this really within the Bush Administration's -- or the country's -- political means? Or will this huge and continuing deployment end on a dying fall, an exhibition without effect?
