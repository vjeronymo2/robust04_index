February 20, 1989, Monday, Valley Edition

PIERCE STUDENTS BRING PETS TO KEEP VET PROGRAM ALIVE

Shortly after county officials removed animals from Pierce College last month, Angela Daniel volunteered her pet cocker spaniel to fellow students practicing veterinary skills.

When Ebony's turn came up last week, six students and an instructor from the college's veterinary assistants training program practiced cleaning the small, black dog's teeth as she lay anesthetized, her legs splayed and her pink tongue lolling.

Ebony and the pets of 14 other students have become the program's sole source of animals since officials from Los Angeles County's Department of Animal Care and Control removed 14 dogs and cats from the school's kennels.

County officials decided to return the animals to the county's Agoura Hills shelter about four weeks ago after animal rights activists objected to their use in teaching Pierce students clinical procedures such as insertion of catheters and intravenous tubes.

"We're not saying the animals we removed were in pain or traumatized," said Bruce P. Richards, assistant director of the county department, when he announced the decision last month. "But we never agreed to let the students use them to learn how to insert catheters or intravenous tubes, or take X-rays or blood."

Student Experience

The county's removal of the animals -- lost dogs and cats that were claimed by no one and that were scheduled for euthanasia by the county -- has left Pierce in a quandary. Phil Des Marteau, director of the college's animal health technology program, said that unless the college finds a larger supply of dogs and cats soon, students will not get enough hands-on experience to become competent veterinary assistants.

Des Marteau said he hopes shelter officials will change their minds and eventually resume supplying the program with animals. He said the animals are sedated to make the procedures, such as drawing blood, as painless as possible.

But animal rights activists say stricter regulation of the college's use of animals, including banning procedures that are not necessary for the animals' health, should be established first. They said students get enough practical experience working with animals during internships at animal hospitals and other facilities.

"We do not have the right to cause the slightest unnecessary pain, discomfort or fear to sentient beings, especially when knowledge can be gained in other ways by using a little ingenuity," said Aaron Leider, a spokesman for the North Hollywood-based animal rights group, Last Chance for Animals.

Clear Understanding

Brian Berger, director of Animal Care and Control, said the earliest the county would resume supplying the college with animals would be next fall. He said that before that happened, he "would want an understanding so clear that there was nothing that could be perceived by anyone as misuse of animals."

Throughout California, officials at public and private animal shelters have become more hesitant in recent years to lend animals to teaching institutions, say veterinary instructors at schools statewide.

As a result, the six community colleges and several private vocational schools that train veterinary assistants have been increasingly forced to rely on the willingness of students to volunteer their pets for laboratory sessions. Sometimes they call on other sources, such as program supporters willing to donate animals.

"The climate today is very tense," said Joe S. Gloyd, assistant director of the American Veterinary Medical Assn., which accredits 62 animal health technology programs in the United States. "The number of animals used for instructional purposes has really diminished as a result. The bottom line is students come out less qualified to care for clients' animals because they've had less practice."

Strict Guidelines

In rare cases when agencies do lend animals, strict guidelines govern their use. For instance, the Pomona Humane Society lends Mt. San Antonio Community College in Walnut about 25 dogs per year, said Chuck Hewitt, coordinator of the college's agricultural sciences department. The agreement allows the school to perform only necessary procedures, such as spaying and neutering, and animals cannot be kept longer than overnight, Hewitt said.

"We do take some heat from students and the industry that our graduates aren't as proficient at inserting intravenous catheters as they would be if they had more chances to practice," Hewitt said. "But I'd rather take the heat than have people tell me we're cruel to animals."

The agreement between Pierce and Los Angeles County was not as specific about allowable procedures and time limits as the pact at Mt. San Antonio.

The county's agreement, spelled out in a 1982 letter from Des Marteau, explained the treatment of animals the college had formerly received from the Veterans Administration hospital in West Los Angeles. County officials inferred that cats and dogs from the shelter would be treated in a similar manner, said Richards.

Treated for Ailments

Animals from the VA were treated for any ailments they had, the letter said. Students "give them their vaccinations, and blanket them with loving attention." In return, the letter said, students "learn how to give injections, how to restrain properly, and how to make critical evaluations of the animal's health."

At the end of an animal's stay, which ranged from three weeks to three months, students in the program would try to find them homes, the letter said. It also said that "at no time are the animals experimented on or are they used for any research project."

County officials said they did not realize until last month that animals were being put under general anesthesia about once a month so students could practice such procedures as inserting catheters, drawing blood and giving injections.

Des Marteau said that a dog might be injected five to 10 times in the course of a lab session, but said that "there is no trauma" because the animal is sedated. However, county officials withdrew the animals after learning of the procedures through citizens' complaints. Techniques practiced at the college are "not medical experimentation, but we have the public's confidence to consider, in addition to the fact that they're not doing what's stated in the 1982 correspondence," Richards said last month at a press conference announcing the removal.

Student Upset

The withdrawal of the animals upset Daniel, 22, of Ventura, who said she is studying to become one of about 18,000 veterinary assistants in the nation because "I love animals, not because I want to hurt them. If there was anything wrong with what we were doing -- if the animals felt anything -- I wouldn't let them use Ebony."

In the search for alternatives to animals that come from shelters, some programs have purchased animals, said Paul F. Cook, a veterinarian and president-elect of the Assn. of Veterinary Technician Educators.

But Pierce lacks the funds to spend $200 to $500 per animal, Des Marteau said. Enrollment in the college's program, and consequently funding, has declined in recent years, although the demand for veterinary assistants has increased, he said.

Fewer people are studying to become veterinary assistants because it is not a lucrative field, said Gloyd of the American Veterinary Medical Assn. Annual salaries for veterinary assistants, most of whom are female, range from about $11,000 to $25,000, depending on experience, Gloyd said.

School-Owned Animals

Some programs, such as the one at Cosumnes River College in Sacramento, house groups of school-owned animals on campus and find homes for them at the end of each school year. The animals are donated by program supporters because using strays can present legal problems, school officials said.

At Cosumnes River College, for example, strays were used after they had gone unclaimed for two weeks until a local animal rights group objected on grounds that the animals' owners might still be trying to find them, said Cheryl Buch, an animal health technology instructor at the college. The animals were sent to shelters after the group objected, she said.

"I think it's a total waste to send those animals to the shelters where most of them will be destroyed," Buch said. "When they join our colony, they are only used four times a year and are adopted out in the summer."

Des Marteau also pointed out that one effect of the Pierce program is to extend the lives of animals that otherwise might be destroyed at county shelters.

Quality of Life

But Barbara Fabricant, president of the Humane Task Force, a private, nonprofit organization based in Canoga Park, said: "An animal is not an inanimate object to be practiced on. I strongly believe it's not life, it's the quality of life that counts."

The city of Los Angeles also would rather see animals put to death than have them used in Pierce's program or for any other medical purpose, said Walter E. Ziegler, chief veterinarian for the city Department of Animal Regulation. In fiscal year 1986-87, shelter officials put to death 60,671 or about 68% of the 89,357 live dogs and cats they received, Ziegler said.

But Gloyd disagreed, saying "waking up from anesthesia is better than not waking up at all."
