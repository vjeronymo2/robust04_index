June 18, 1990, Monday, P.M. Final

LIZ SMITH: WILL REGGIE JACKSON PUT ON THE PINSTRIPES AGAIN?

WHAT I wonder about today is the rumor that George Steinbrenner is going to bring back none other than baseball ace Reggie Jackson to manage the Yankees next season. Well, that's what Reggie-ites are whispering on both coasts.

NBC'S LATE-NIGHT show, "Later With Bob Costas," has the CBS titan Dan Rather on for two nights -- Tuesday and Wednesday -- and the anchorman rates his competitor by saying: "Costas -- he's a hell of an interviewer!"

BEING BOTH a hick and an incorrigible fan, I've never entered those hallowed gates of New York's "21" without seeing somebody special inside. The other night, the frisson of thrill came from looking up from the table I was sharing with Shirley "20/20" Clurman and her writer husband, Richard, and seeing Frank Sinatra standing there in black-tied glory. He had just come from his Radio City Music Hall concert and left a big table with his wife, Barbara, tycoon John Kluge, pal Judy Green, and that Hollywood glamour couple, Jilly and Tom Selleck, to come say hello.

Wearing the Presidential Medal insignia in his lapel, Sinatra looked fabulous. In fact, he seemed to be more fit than I've seen dear Francis Albert in ages. Those of us who thrilled to his music in the '40s and followed his saga through the '50s, '60s, '70s and '80s, always know he is a very special talent and still the biggest star of the '90s.

THE TOP-SECRET filming of spring-summer is taking place now on a hermetically sealed set at Pinewood Studios, outside London. The Universal movie is a contemporary English romantic comedy about the aristocracy, and director David S. Ward has quite a cast of dandies and eccentrics working for him -- Peter O'Toole, the British phenomenon who has progressed from being merely brilliant while drinking to being even more brilliant while sober . . . "Roseanne's" now less-hefty husband, John Goodman . . . and the fabled actor John Hurt, who has joined the "King Ralph I" cast in the role of the villain, Lord Percival Graves. . . . Walt Disney has signed the black British funnyman Lenny Henry to a major two-picture deal and (speaking of mega-stars), they hope to put him right into the big leagues with Eddie Murphy. . . . Producers Don Saxon and Robert Blume are working on a Broadway musical about the TV uncle of us all, Milton Berle, and Uncle Miltie is their consultant. (But I thought they already made a musical about Milton, titled "Top Banana.")
