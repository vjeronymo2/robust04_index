December 7, 1989, Thursday, Home Edition

SYNAGOGUE

Readers taking a close look at the photographs (Religion, Nov. 25) of Sukwon Kim examining his work on the Korean Philadelphia Presbyterian Church's restoration project may have wondered why the characters on the Ten Commandments in the top photo were in Hebrew, not Korean.

The reason is that the Korean congregation (at Fourth and New Hampshire), worships in a former synagogue, the home of Sinai Temple from its dedication in 1926 (not 100 years ago, as indicated in the caption) to 1961, when Sinai moved to its current location in Westwood.

The building, designed by architect S. Tilden Norton, is City of Los Angeles Historic Cultural Monument No. 91. As the Western outpost of the Conservative movement in Judaism, it housed the first classes of the University of Judaism in 1947.

Incidentally, Sinai's first building, dedicated in 1909 at 12th and Valencia, and the oldest extant synagogue structure in Los Angeles, has been the home of the Welsh Presbyterian Church since 1925. It was also designed by Norton and is Historic Cultural Monument No. 173. The Welsh congregation has lovingly preserved the building through the years and is now engaged in a construction project to comply with earthquake code requirements.

We are indebted to these congregations for keeping alive visual reminders of our city's rich multicultural heritage.

STEPHEN J. SASS

President Jewish Historical Society of Southern California
