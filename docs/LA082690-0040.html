August 26, 1990, Sunday, Home Edition

REAL ESTATE Q & A: OBTAIN WRITTEN MARKETING PLAN TO ENSURE PROMISED SALES CAMPAIGN

QUESTION: Several months ago when we put our home on the market for sale, we followed your advice and interviewed three agents before listing our home for sale with the best one. She came highly recommended, even by one of the other agents we interviewed. Her references were impeccable.

However, we neglected to insist on a written marketing plan for our house. Due to the slowdown in home sales in our city, her brokerage is cutting back on its advertising and promotion. As a result, our home was barely advertised.

When it became apparent that we had chosen the wrong agent, after 60 days of virtually no promotion and much hostility from the agent, we canceled the listing.

Then we listed our home with one of the other agents we interviewed. He gave us a written marketing plan and showed us examples of how his firm sells houses even in a slow market. As a result, after about three weeks he brought us a purchase offer that we accepted.

Why don't you include on your listing checklist a requirement the agent provide the home seller with a written marketing plan for the home?

ANSWER: Thank you for that valuable suggestion. The best realty agents specify in writing how they plan to sell the home. As you probably know, advertising, signs and open houses often are not enough.

I recently heard of a major brokerage chain in Miami that recommends its home sellers obtain a professional appraisal of the house before a listing will be accepted.

If a truly objective appraisal is made, this sounds like a good idea, providing the appraiser does not "low ball" the valuation to get the house listed for sale at a low price, which is easy to sell.

A Costly Mistake in Buying a Vacant Lot

Q: My wife and I bought a vacant lot in a very desirable neighborhood. It was being sold by an estate. We presumed we would be able to build a house on it, since it is a single-family neighborhood. However, when we contacted an architect about designing a house, she said the lot is below the minimum size for a house.

We applied for a zoning variance, but were turned down. Our lawyer says we can appeal to the courts, but that can be very expensive. We were thinking of suing the estate for not telling us the lot is unbuildable. But our lawyer refuses to take the case. He says the estate cannot be held liable for non-disclosure of the lot's small size. Do you think we should appeal?

A: Your situation is a classic example of why buyers of vacant land should first check to learn what can be built on the property. Apparently there was a very good reason why that lot remained vacant in an otherwise built up neighborhood.

However, I wouldn't give up. If your lot has no practical use, and the city refuses to grant a zoning variance, you may have a damage claim against the city. I suggest you find a lawyer who is experienced in zoning matters. The local Board of Realtors can give you the name of their lawyer who probably will either be interested in taking your case or can recommend another lawyer who specializes in zoning litigation.

When Not to Use a Wraparound Mortgage

Q: You said it is better for a home seller to carry back a wraparound mortgage instead of a second mortgage. But when I showed your article to our real estate agent she said that only applies if the existing first mortgage does not have a due-on-sale clause. Since we expect our buyer to get a new first mortgage, wouldn't we also be better off with a wraparound rather than a second mortgage?

A: Not necessarily. The best time for the seller to carry back a wraparound instead of a second mortgage occurs when there is an existing low-interest rate first mortgage without a due on sale clause.

For example, suppose your home sells for $200,000 and it has an existing $100,000 VA mortgage at 8% interest that is assumable. If the buyer makes a $40,000 cash down payment, that leaves a $60,000 finance gap. You can fill it by carrying back a $60,000 second mortgage at perhaps 10% interest. But you would be better off, and the sale would be more attractive, to the buyer if you instead carry back a $160,000 wraparound mortgage at perhaps 9.5% interest. You then earn 9.5% interest on $60,000 plus the 1.5% interest rate differential on $100,000.

Another advantage is the buyer makes one payment to you and then you use part of that money to keep up payments on the old first mortgage.

However, if the buyer will be getting a new first mortgage, it doesn't really matter if you carry back a second or a wraparound mortgage. But do not carry back a wraparound loan if the first loan is an adjustable-rate mortgage. The reason is you could become the "stuckee" if its interest rate is adjustable, but your wraparound loan has a fixed interest rate.

Can Home Seller Deduct a Loss?

Q: We bought our home about five years ago. Since then the state has upgraded a nearby road into a very noisy freeway. To make matters worse, they built an off-ramp exit that pours traffic down the street in front of our house. It is so bad it often takes me 10 minutes to find a break in the traffic, so I can back out of my driveway.

As a result, houses on our street have lost considerable market value. The homeowners are suing the state and the city, but I doubt we will ever collect for the lost value of our properties. Due to a job transfer I have to sell. My employer hired a relocation company that had our house appraised, but the value came out about $14,000 less than we paid for it. We have decided to accept their offer. Can we deduct our loss on our income tax returns?

A: No. Losses on the sale of your personal residence are not tax-deductible. However, you could have turned this situation into a tax-deductible loss if you had converted the house into rental property before selling. Losses on the sale of investment or business property are tax deductible. It's too late now for you, but perhaps other readers can benefit from your mistake.

Get Right Form for Power of Attorney

Q: I am a title officer with a major title insurance company. It would be a big help if you would clarify for your many readers the difference between a general and a limited power of attorney form. We have many people who bring us a limited power of attorney form, usually prepared by an attorney, but we need a general power of attorney where a person is representing an absent seller such as a spouse.

Often there is not enough time to get a general power of attorney form signed and notarized before the scheduled closing date. Any seller or buyer who plans to use a power of attorney should consult the title insurance company well in advance to be sure they have the correct form.

A: A power of attorney form can be very valuable when a buyer or seller is unable to be present for the sale of a property. With a power of attorney someone else, such as a spouse, can sign the papers for the absent person.

A limited power of attorney form appoints an attorney in fact just for a limited purpose, such as to sign papers necessary for the sale of a house. But most title insurance companies now insist on a general power of attorney form, so there is no question that the attorney has power to sign whatever forms are required for the sale or purchase. By consulting the title insurer well in advance of the closing, the right power of attorney form can be prepared and signed, so there are no last-minute problems.

Rent Run-Down House With an Option to Buy

Q: We bought our home almost three years ago. It was a fixer-upper then and it still is. Our plan was to fix it up, but then my husband lost his job and we had some illness. Now we have to sell as we can't afford the mortgage payment. A fine realtor has been trying to sell it for the last four months but, buyers are turned off by the need for renovation, which we can't afford to do. Any ideas?

A: Rent with an option to buy. Your house should appeal to bargain hunters who lack much cash for a down payment. You will be amazed at how lease-option tenants start fixing up a run-down house because they know someday they will exercise the purchase option and own it.

There are so many lease-option advantages for both owner and tenant I can't list them all.

Is Refinancing Better Than a Credit Line?

Q: I have about $100,000 equity in my home, and I would like to use part of it to get started investing in houses, probably fixer-upper houses. Do you think I should refinance my first mortgage even though it has an 8.75% fixed-interest rate or should I take an equity credit line which would be at 10% as I write this?

A: Unless your first mortgage is very small, I would leave it undisturbed and go for the home-equity credit line. The best feature of credit lines is they don't cost anything until you use them.
