941104 FT 04 NOV 94 / Spy chief rejects US-style agency Mrs Stella Rimington, the director-general of the security service MI5, yesterday hit out at the creation of a US-style national counter-terrorist agency, saying that recent experience had shown that a 'large number of agencies and organisations can co-operate effectively'. She said that the ceasefires in Northern Ireland had been brought about by political progress achieved 'in the context of a wider counter-terrorist strategy'. Speaking to police chiefs, Whitehall officials and government lawyers including Sir Nicholas Lyell, the attorney-general, Mrs Rimington promoted her agency as an efficient counter-terrorist organisation which had provided useful advice to the government on political issues such as Northern Ireland. Her views are in contrast to some experts and police officers, who have been critical of the fragmented state of Britain's law enforcement agencies and have called for a single national organisation similar to the US Federal Bureau of Investigation. Her move comes as the future of her agency and others is uncertain because of budgetary pressures and shifting political priorities. The secret services are to have their spending, administration and policies scrutinised by a parliamentary committee for the first time later this year. Mrs Rimington said that while the Ulster ceasefires were welcome 'after so many years of violence we must remain cautious'. She referred to attacks by Middle East terrorists, and said: 'Compared with the traditional certainties of the cold war era, with its frame of reference apparently fixed for all time, the service's responsibilities, and the environment in which we operate, are today all in a state of flux.'
