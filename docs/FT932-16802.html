930403 FT 03 APR 93 / World Stock Markets (Asia Pacific): Nikkei rises 1.8% to three-year high INDIVIDUALS, foreigners, and dealers placed heavy buying orders, and the Nikkei average rose 1.8 per cent in volume of 1.5bn shares, the largest since November 29 1989, writes Emiko Terazono in Tokyo. The index closed up 347.85 at 19,446.94, 2.1 per cent higher on the week and a new high for the year. It opened at the day's low of 19,136.03, and peaked at 19,750.93 before declining on late profit-taking. Volume surged from Thursday's 685m as some Japanese institutional investors, who had previously refrained from buying, started to purchase shares. Advances overwhelmed declines by 886 to 227 with 66 unchanged, the Topix index of all first-section stocks jumped 55.12 to 1,526.31 and, in London, the ISE/Nikkei 50 index rose 8.69 to 1,200.65. Nippon Telegraph and Telephone once again led the rise. The issue closed up Y23,000 at Y1m, climbing above the Y1m level for the first time since April 1991. Securities houses were strong, pushing the sector up to the day's top position with a rise of 11.9 per cent. Nomura Securities soared Y220 to Y2,190 and Daiwa Securities rose by Y150 to Y1,270. Large lot buying in Nippon Steel, the most active issue of the day as it gained Y60 to Y405, boosted other steels and shipbuilders. NKK rose Y55 to Y340 and Mitsubishi Heavy Industries advanced Y29 to Y659. Some exporters were weak on the stronger yen. The dollar closed down Y0.54 at Y114 after briefly trading on the Y113 level during the morning session. In Osaka, the OSE average climbed 763.04 to 21,070.88 in volume of 60.1m shares. The index regained the 21,000 level for the first time since May 21. Roundup Some strong performances were recorded in Pacific Rim markets. SINGAPORE blue chips were in demand by foreign investors and the Straits Times Industrial index rose 12.8 to a record closing high of 1,688.96, 1.7 per cent higher on the week. With the end of the corporate reporting season, investors were said to be taking a positive view of longer term prospects. In particular, the government's plan to privatise Singapore Telecom, and its forecast of stronger economic growth this year, are expected to continue to help investor sentiment. AUSTRALIA overcame an uncertain start and rallied to its highest close in 10 months on futures-related trading and positive economic news. The All Ordinaries index added 14.3 to 1,683.1, 0.5 per cent higher on the week. Investors welcomed the statement by Mr John Dawkins, the treasurer, that arrangements which effectively allow companies to pay tax-free dividends were safe, as well as his commitment to reduce the budget deficit. A big premium on futures and the strong performance in Tokyo also encouraged buyers. June share price index futures jumped 10.2 to 1705.0 on the Sydney Futures Exchange, giving it a 21.9 point premium to the physical market. SEOUL ended convincingly higher in brisk trade in response to a batch of recent indicators which showed an improvement in economic activity. The composite index closed 15.76 higher at 681.37, 3.3 per cent up on the week. HONG KONG, closed on Monday for a local holiday, recovered most of its early losses in a late round of bargain hunting by foreign investors. The Hang Seng index ended 4.44 lower at 6,404.44, 1 per cent up on the week, after lagging by more than 25 points for most of the day. Turnover was a slack HKDollars 3.15bn. BOMBAY moved ahead on renewed buying support from investment institutions when trading resumed yesterday after Thursday's holiday and the BSE index rose 30.92 to 2,311.44, a 1.2 per cent decline on the week.
