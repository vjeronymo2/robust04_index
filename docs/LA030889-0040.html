March 8, 1989, Wednesday, Home Edition

SUPREME COURT ON CHILD ABUSE

I was fortunate to be able to hear oral argument in Deshaney vs. Winnebago County Dept. of Social Services on Nov. 2, 1988, and to read David Savage's article on the case ("Rehnquist Court Restricting Constitutional Protections," Part I, Feb. 24). Counsel for Deshaney argued that the child had been taken from the father by the county and in court proceedings the child was returned with the agreement that the social worker would make frequent checks on the child's safety. She did not do so. She entered in her notes that she strongly believed the next time she saw the child he would be dead or seriously injured. This came to pass. Under questioning, the state's attorney admitted that if the child were in a foster home, the state would be liable.

No matter the labels we attribute to Chief Justice William Rehnquist and the court's majority, we are departing from the political theory our Constitution was founded upon. Thomas Hobbes, John Locke and Rousseau wrote on the social contract as the underpinnings of an orderly society. We gave up some of our liberties to the state in exchange for protection and safety. This was a higher level of civilization from a state of nature, or anarchy, where some lived outside the law. Their teachings were endorsed by Thomas Jefferson and carried over into our Constitution.

How strange to hear the chief justice expound that "The 14th Amendment was intended to prevent government from abusing its power. . . ." Is it not an abuse of power for the government to operate a police force, as its sole monopoly, to protect its citizens and refuse to do so?

BRUCE M. STARK

Long Beach
