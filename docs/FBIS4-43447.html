BFN [Report by Kostas Venizelos] [Text] The Cypriot Government submitted seven proposals to the United Nations on the future of the Cyprus issue. Foreign Minister Alekos Mikhailidhis is presenting this document during his U.S. meetings. According to reports, the foreign minister notes that this policy will create the appropriate climate for a solution, not the confidence-building measures as the United States is suggesting. On the other hand, an effort by Turkish Cypriot leader Rauf Denktas to appear as if he is accepting the confidence-building measures fell apart following a statement-denial by Gustave Feissel, the UN secretary general's alternate special representative in Cyprus. UN Secretary General Butrus Butrus-Ghali and the Security Council will decide whether Denktas' clarifications are satisfactory. Reports say that the occupation leader listed a series of conditions on the document that cancel out its acceptance. Government Spokesman Ioannis Kasoulidhis confirmed that a document has been sent to the UN Secretariat but avoided revealing its contents. Turkish Cypriot newspaper KIBRIS yesterday referred extensively to the document, and diplomatic sources confirmed to I SIMERINI its validity. The document reiterates Nicosia's decision not to resume talks on the confidence-building measures and Nicosia's warning that it will withdraw its support for the 21 March UN document and promote its own reservations. The seven proposals are as follows: - First: The demilitarization of the island. - Second: The issue of guarantees in a future solution. - Third: A UN Security Council reconfirmation that it will not accept any decision but one based on one sovereignty, nationality, and international image. - Fourth: An issue of demographic changes and a population census. - Fifth: The Turkish troops issue. - Sixth: An appeal for Turkey to return Famagusta according to UN Security Council resolution 550. - Seventh: An appeal by the five UN Security Council permanent representatives for European Union representation in the talks. In any case, questions have been raised about yesterday's meeting between UN officials and Denktas. In his statement, Denktas appeared to have accepted everything. He said: "We have accepted the UN demands and they promised to incorporate what was agreed in Vienna into the document." Clarifying on which positions he had changed, Denktas referred to the issue of access to Famagusta's fenced-off area, which remained one of the basic obstacles. Denktas said he agreed to move the Turkish troops 1 kilometer according to UN wishes, but noted by doing this (the Attila) will approach the National Guard positions by 200 meters. At the same time he agreed to move the "police" station from the eastern part of Dherynia Road and that the international organization will cover the cost. New York will evaluate Denktas' clarifications (which Butrus-Ghali demanded) and decide whether these positions constitute an agreement for the talks to continue. In any case Feissel described Denktas' clarifications as positive, but as soon as his statements became known he was quick to say that it is Denktas' personal view that he has accepted the document. He even clarified that he made no promises. Feissel had a telephone conversation with the president and briefed him on the meeting. According to our information, Kliridhis did not want to be informed about Denktas' positions; he only wanted to know whether or not he had accepted the 21 March UN document. Yesterday the government spokesman said that Denktas' remarks do not constitute an acceptance of the document and added that our side was not told that Denktas' positions constitute satisfactory answers. Yesterday, Feissel sent the UN secretary general a report on his meeting with Denktas.
