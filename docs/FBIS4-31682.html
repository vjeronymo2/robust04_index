BFN [Editorial Report] Budapest Duna TV in Hungarian at 1920 GMT on 13 June carries an unscheduled 30-minute compilation called "Help Yourself, the West Will Help You Too." The program, which deals with the political and economic conference held in Innsbruck on 11 June, includes a 20-minute exclusive interview with Gyula Horn, chairman of the Hungarian Socialist Party, MSZP. Jozsef Laszlo hosts the program; the place and date of the interview are not given. Laszlo outlines the agenda of the Innsbruck conference and introduces its participants. Video clips are shown of Gyula Horn's arrival at the venue; there are extracts from his interview given to Austrian Television. Also shown are an extract from an interview with German politicians Hans-Dietrich Genscher and Rudolf Scharping, who both express their trust in Hungarian democracy and Gyula Horn's premiership. The interview with Gyula Horn follows next. Horn reiterates his criticism -- outlined at the Innsbruck conference -- of the European Union, EU, for not having concrete programs on cooperation with the Visegrad countries. He repeats his arguments in favor of an urgent change. He confirms Hungary's readiness to repay its debts and urges the EU to draw up a timetable for Hungary's process of joining the EU. Horn also confirms the three main elements of the MSZP's foreign policy. Asked about relations with Hungary's neighbors, he says: "There is a basic treaty with Ukraine, as well as an agreement of about 20 pages on cooperation in minority matters. I have to say that the question of whether the Ukrainian side indeed observes the agreement also has to be examined, since there have been reports that this is not happening exactly the way it should." Horn adds: "There have been problems in its implementation, and what is happening is not exactly what had been promised by the other side, in Kiev." Horn is then asked about relations with Slovakia. He replies: "When I met [Slovak Prime Minister Jozef] Morvacik in Bratislava about one and a half months ago, I said that we should try to free the entire Danube matter -- the Gabcikovo matter -- from its political overtones and hold substantial talks on it. We cannot reconcile ourselves to the situation they created, which would mean the eventual implementation of variant C. We consider unacceptable any kind of divergence from the water distribution system recommended by the EU, which would be disadvantageous for Hungary. We regard this as a key question, and Moravcik promised that they would revise the decisions, the position, taken by them [Slovakia]." After further elaborating on the subject, he says: "As far as the Office for Hungarians Beyond the Border is concerned, I do not know whether there is any need for it or not. I am personally convinced that there is no need for it. I have to say -- although the reports may be exaggerated, or perhaps they are not well founded -- that I have heard a lot of negative things about its activities. However, there are people even within the party who say that this office should be maintained. We will examine this question. We will not make a hasty decision on it." At the end of the interview Horn outlines the most urgent tasks of the new government: a detailed review of the economy, decisions on the world exhibition and the Gabcikovo project, and solutions to the most serious problems in health service. He says: "I am convinced, finally, that the Hungarian Government has to prepare an overall program -- which should be handed over, first and foremost, to the EU and, in addition, to the United States and Japan as well -- that would serve as a basis for Hungarian modernization. I mean complete modernization of the infrastructure, including roads, transport, telecommunication and all large networks in connection with this, since modernization cannot be started without this. And I am thinking of a revision of all the rules, regulations, laws, and decrees that prevent a larger influx of foreign capital into the country."
