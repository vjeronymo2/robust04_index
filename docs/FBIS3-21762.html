Language: English Article Type:BFN [Text] The need for the general public to cooperate with and assist law enforcement agencies in checking drug abuse, peddling, and exportation, has again been raised by the law enforcement agencies. The concern was expressed by Mr. Robert Ayalingo and Mr. F.K. Amuzu of CEPS [Customs, Excise and Preventive Service] at a seminar organized by the International Council on Alcohol and Addictions on drug smuggling by Ghanaians. George Lawson's report is read to you. [read by announcer] There is increasing anxiety by the international community over the drug menace which is causing human destruction, threatening the security and future of many nations. Mr. Robert Ayalingo pointed out that the greater percentage of violent crimes such as murder and armed robbery are committed by people under the influence of drugs. Mr. Ayalingo noted with regret that Ghana has been identified as one of the transit points for cocaine and heroin arriving in Europe and North America. Mr. Ayalingo said from 1990 to 1992 the police seized 13.76 kg of cocaine and 24.87 kg of heroin. According to him, figures of 1993 are not available because the exhibits are yet to be submitted to the Ghana Standards Board. Mr. Ayalingo disclosed that another drug called crack has recently surfaced. He said it is an addictive form of cocaine prepared by combining cocaine with sodium bicarbonate, which is commonly called baking powder. He pointed out that it is cheap but very dangerous. The assistant training officer of CEPS, Mr. F.K. Amusu, said because Ghana is being used as a transit point in the international narcotic trade, local consumption of heroin and cocaine is rising sharply. He said CEPS with its limited resources has been able to make a number of arrests and will continue to fight against illicit drug trade. Miss Jessica Vapneg, a U.S. lawyer attached to the Faculty of Law, University of Ghana, Legon, said the couriers from Africa account for 20 to 30 percent of heroin and cocaine smuggled into the United States. Miss Vapneg said from 1989 to 1992, 164 Ghanaians were arrested in the United States. The vice chairman of the International Council on Alcohol and Addiction in Ghana, Mr. Noah Akolatse, appealed to NGO's [nongovernmental organizations], communities, and individuals to assist the group in their fight against drug abuse.
