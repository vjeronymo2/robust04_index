Language: Spanish Article Type:CSO [Excerpts] Following a charge implying the existence of appalling deals between federal agents and drug traffickers, the residents of the Indian communities in Juarez Valley have chosen to remain silent because of their fear. "This is a very critical matter that is being treated superficially.... I refuse to say anything": That comment was made by the Zaragoza community commissioner, Porfirio Silva, who, six months ago, publicly charged that "in the region it was easier to obtain a kilo of marijuana than one of tortillas." The movement of drugs (claimed in the accusation as occurring in most of the 17 Indian communities comprising the valley) is an activity not confirmed thus far by any evidence gathered by Federal Judicial Police [PJF] agents during their raids in that area. In its crackdown on the drug traffic, the department has not managed to seize marijuana or cocaine shipments after several raids reported to have taken place since July. Those negative statistics represent a strong motivation for the community residents, from Zaragoza to Guadalupe, not to opt for making any new charges regarding such activity. However, State Judicial Police [PJE] officials and municipal employees operating in those municipalities have also been remiss in issuing any further reports. For example, the state agents have not repeated their intervention in any operation, nor reported any unusual movement on trails or in settlements since August, when one of their fellow officers was captured by PJF members. "Since then, there has never been any kind of cooperation; and, besides, we represent an agency dealing with common crimes. We have devoted ourselves to that alone," declared the PJE commander in Guadalupe, Octavio Frescas. The federal police arrested one of his agents in August, accusing him of having protected the landing of a small plane presumably loaded with drugs: something that could never be proven. [passage omitted] According to the town hall secretary, Eduardo Ramos, Guadalupe, regarded for years as one of the municipalities used by drug traffickers for landing their planes, is, nevertheless, a safe place now. "It is so peaceful here that we do not even know the federal police," he remarked. [passage omitted] The commissioner of the Juarez Reforma Indian community, Jesus Olivo, who resides in the village of Caseta, a site notorious for drug trafficking, even asserted: "Movements of that type have never occurred here." Another community representative, Antonio Flores, from San Isidro, also claimed that the drug traffic in the region "is a thing of the past," although he was among those who witnessed, and did not deny, the public charge submitted to Mayor Francisco Villareal. Described by Silva as a region "serving as a springboard for drugs," Juarez Valley was explored by PJF agents weeks later, after the mayor had met with the PGR [Attorney General of the Republic] representative, Teresa Jardi Alonso, according to reports issued at the time. The agents' invasion was announced in August by the assistant representative of the Attorney General's Office, Samuel Terrazas. However, no results have been reported from the maneuvers to date. At the PJE office in Guadalupe, Commander Frescas indicated that, throughout their tours, countless trails and settlements "known in their entirety only to the natives" were detected. He added: "There are very long and varied trails, but it cannot be claimed that they are being used by `narcos.'" According to the residents themselves, the PGR helicopter flights have been intensified in that area during the past few days. Nevertheless, there has been no report to date of the discovery of landing strips, trails, or drug seizures involving considerable volumes of drugs.
