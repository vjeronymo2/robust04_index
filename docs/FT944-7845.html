941123 FT 23 NOV 94 / Aitken and Portillo hit out at EU One of the cabinet's leading Eurosceptics last night attacked the economic performance of the European Union as the government fought to contain a threatened backbench rebellion over an increase in UK contributions. Mr Jonathan Aitken, Treasury chief secretary, giving the Nicholas Ridley memorial lecture in London, said that Europe was 'trailing badly in the slow lane' of world economic growth. Mr Michael Portillo, the employment secretary, said in a speech to the Institute of Directors that Europe was underperforming because of 'interventionist and centralising' policies. Both ministers distanced themselves from opponents of membership of the EU, saying that job creation and trade prospects could be improved by deregulatory policies. But their comments risk increasing antipathy to the European Communities finance bill, which rightwing backbenchers have vowed to oppose in spite of Mr John Major's threat to call a general election if the bill is defeated. Mr Aitken, while warning against EU complacency, was careful to endorse Britain's continued membership of the EU as a means of developing Continental trade. He rebuked rightwing Conservative backbenchers by calling for an end to time wasting 'infighting' about EU institutions. Mr Aitken said there was too much introspective debating about 'theoretical political concepts' such as 'variable geometry', the idea of a multispeed EU developed by Mr Douglas Hurd, foreign secretary, as a means of mollifying the Tory party's pro and anti-European wings. But he said the union was responding sluggishly to the challenge of a historical shift in economic power to the Asia Pacific region. 'The reality of what we have to face is not a two-speed Europe but a two-speed world, in which Europe is trailing badly in the slow lane,' he said. Mr Portillo said that too many people in Brussels sought to protect those already in work rather than tackling unemployment. He called for a deregulatory 'jobs agenda for Europe'. Echoing his Eurosceptic speech at last month's Conservative party conference, he said: 'I cannot accept that a Europe which excludes 20m people from sharing in its prosperity is a Europe which is working.'
