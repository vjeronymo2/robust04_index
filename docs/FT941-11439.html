940204 FT 04 FEB 94 / Film institute's budgeting rapped The British Film Institute overspent by Pounds 108,000 at its London Film Festival last year because of poor budgetary control, says a report released by the National Audit Office today. It warns that audiences are perilously low at the National Film Theatre and the Museum of the Moving Image, both in London. The report says that the institute's senior management agreed to unrealistic budgets for the festival and officials were not monitored sufficiently. Mr Wilf Stevenson, BFI director, said: 'I think the report is a little unkind to us. We have to decide what films we are going to screen in advance of knowing whether we are going to get audiences or not. 'The festival is by definition risky and experimental and it is in its nature that you are bound to have uncertain spending patterns.' He added: 'The expenditure overruns will not happen again because we have put in better controls.' The report pinpoints the National Film Theatre and Museum of the Moving Image as vulnerable parts of the institute's operation. It warns that the institute will have to take 'difficult decisions' if attendance levels at the theatre and museum do not improve. Last year the National Film Theatre filled 31,000 fewer seats than budgeted. The report cites the recession, the theatre's South Bank location and increasing competition from television as the reasons for the decline in ticket sales. Mr Stevenson said: 'We want to take account of the changes in viewer behaviour and are making changes in our programming now. We will try to offer what TV can't - what we call 'live cinema' - bringing in film-makers to introduce their movies.' Visitor figures for the Museum of the Moving Image have fallen from 536,000 in 1989-90 to 368,000 in 1992-93. Audience levels are now at about half the level needed to break even. The audit office urged the institute to monitor its marketing strategy closely. Mr Stevenson said: 'A recent visitor survey said the Museum of the Moving Image was the most popular attraction in London so we obviously have a good product.'
