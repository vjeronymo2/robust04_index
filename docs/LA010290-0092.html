January 2, 1990, Tuesday, Orange County Edition

PEOPLE: TRUMPETER SPREADS THE JOY OF MUSIC

Douglas Thompson has spent most his life playing music for the love of it.

Financially, "I'm not doing too bad, but then again I'm not doing too great," said Thompson, who favors the trumpet.

Many times, "When I'm at a low ebb, I've had to find jobs outside music to survive."

The Huntington Beach resident decided at age 21 to become a professional entertainer. Later he wanted to be a professional trumpet player. He finally settled on being a band leader and director.

While he currently works in customer relations at a car agency, Thompson is the director of the Businessman's Band, although not all the players are businessmen. In fact, the band has had several names including Music Man's Band, Fountain Valley Community Band and Band X.

In 1983, Thompson went to Washington as a member of what he said was the world's largest non-marching marching band.

"We didn't march, we just played march music," he explained.

"I get a lot of joy working with my music and with people in music," said trumpeter Thompson, who sometimes switches to piano, trombone or drums. "I've always said if you do music for the money in it, then you're not doing music."

Added Thompson: "In fact, serious musicians are strictly in it for the creativity, love and sharing of music and that's the 100% feeling most of us have."

But while there is love for music, "a musician has to keep his financial life on an even keel and that's why I have to take a number of different jobs," he continued. "It goes with being a musician."

And he knows of what he speaks.

"When I was in college (Cal State Los Angeles) I titled my term paper "Earning a Living as a Musician," which he said addressed the problem of keeping body and soul together while being an artist.

He also attended Orange Coast College and Huntington Beach High School.

These days, Thompson is attempting to develop a 200-member community band that he hopes will consist mainly of businessmen, but that is open to "anyone who can read music and play," as he put it.

The current 32-member band rehearses Thursday nights at Costa Mesa High School and plays at Orange Coast College football and basketball games as well as other community functions.

Thompson, who once was assistant director of a U.S. Army band in West Germany, complains about the small number of community bands in Orange County as well as the limited number of places for adults to learn and perform music.

"I don't want to see a complete dry-up in community performing arts training," moaned the one-time church choir singer who said he was part of 13 performing arts groups when he was 17.

After his Army discharge, Thompson went to Sacramento in hopes of persuading lobbyists and legislators to push for more funding for the performing arts.

"There wasn't a whole lot of performing arts training going on and the future of musicians was at stake," he said. "It was difficult getting the attention of the right people."

But later, besides his work with community bands and orchestras, he also got the attention of Boy Scouts.

"In 1974 we formed Band Explorer Post 808," said Thompson, who was the post leader. "The Boy Scout Council said it was the first (such band) ever formed in the western half of the United States."

Some scoundrel stole the American flag from the Garden Grove home of Lu and Paul Bole, who have flown the flag atop a 25-foot pole 24 hours a day for the past 10 years.

Word about the theft reached Mac McCullock, Americanism co-chairman for the Garden Grove Elks Lodge.

Within a day he presented the couple with a flag that had been flown over the Capitol in Washington.

"Flying the American Flag is an important part of the Elks Americanism program," he said.
