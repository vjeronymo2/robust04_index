Language: English Article Type:BFN ["Pool" item] [Text] Novi Sad, March 12 (TANJUG) -- President of Montenegro Momir Bulatovic has stated that the Federal Republic of Yugoslavia (F.R.Y.) is the factor that cannot be bypassed in establishing peace in the Balkans so the respect of its state and national interests would help stabilize the region. "Policies which do not respect the real state and national interests of Montenegro and Serbia, or the Federal Republic of Yugoslavia (F.R.Y.), cannot secure peace and stability in the Balkans," said Bulatovic in an interview to the NOVI SAD DNEVNIK weekly published in the Serbian province of Vojvodina. Hoping in a soon end of the "isolation, sanctions, and international oppression being applied to the F.R.Y.," Bulatovic said that "those wanting to exhaust ourselves totally can no longer justify to their general public the senselessness of everything that has been undertaken against the F.R.Y." In May, 1992, the U.N. imposed comprehensive rigorous sanctions on the F.R.Y. for its alleged involvement in the civil war in Bosnia-Herzegovina, where, however, there have never been any Yugoslav soldiers. "Today it is clear to everybody that the Bosnian question is being used in an attempt to solve the fate of NATO, relations between America and Russia, global unity within the European Union (E.U.), and open pretensions of Japan and Germany to become permanent members of the U.N. Security Council," said Bulatovic. He pointed out that the road now followed by the Yugoslav republics Serbia and Montenegro was neither easy nor entirely certain, but this was the only possible road "in keeping with the historical, state and national interests." He reiterated a joint stand by Montenegro and Serbia that the Serb people outside the F.R.Y. were entitled to exercisinig their freedom and equitable position that presumed also the right to self- determination which had already been used by other nations in the former Yugoslavia. Denying the right to self-determination to the Serb people in the forcibly seceded ex-Yugoslav republics was one of the principal causes of the current war in the areas of former Yugoslavia.
