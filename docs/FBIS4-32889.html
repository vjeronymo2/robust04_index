BFN [Text] Caracas, 30 May (EFE) -- Today, Venezuelan President Rafael Caldera carried out his first cabinet change, only 117 days after the start of his government and in the mist of a profound economic crisis. Caldera, who assumed the presidency on 2 February, appointed Alberto Poletto, Luis Carlos Palacios, Ciro Zaa, and Carlos Bernandez as ministers of Development, Central Office of Coordination and Planning [Cordiplan], Transport and Communication, and Venezuelan Investment Fund, respectively. Caldera's restructuring of his cabinet has been interpreted by local political experts as a sign that the president is prepared to make a switch to an economic policy less populist than the one he proposed during the elections campaign and closer to the neoliberal policy implemented by former president Carlos Andres Perez. Apparently, Caldera, who was one of the most severe critics of the free market policy promoted by Perez, has been unable to structure an economic formula out of the neoliberal guidelines. Venezuela is living through a difficult economic situation, with a fiscal deficit estimated at more than $4.5 billion, a severe depreciation of the bolivar, and an explosive social situation fueled by a significant fall in the purchasing power of the people. President Caldera put Carlos Bernandez at the head of the organization in charge of privatizing state enterprises. Bernandez has always been linked to the local banking sector and he has been president of the Bank of Venezuela, which is one of the most important banks in the country. Bernandez has offered to increase the privatization process. The local media has described his appointment as a victory for the financial sector, which was able to produce the departure of Abdon Vivas Teran, a politician very close to Caldera and to his electoral offer for an economy with a human face, from the Venezuela Investment Fund. Luis Carlos Palacios, the new Cordiplan minister, replaces Enzo del Bufalo, a dissonant voice in Caldera's economic team. The new minister is closer to the free economy concepts that the Caldera administration apparently wants to adopt in this new phase. Also, Alberto Poletto, president of the Foreign Trade Institute, will be development minister, which joins these departments to create the Industry and Commerce Ministry. Ciro Zaa has been virtually forced to leave the Urban Development Ministry to take over the Transport and Communication Ministry vacated by Cesar Quintini, who was accused of maintaining an attitude of tolerance in multiple cases of corruption in his ministry. Zaa will be replaced by Francisco Gonzalez, former president of the Urban Development fund. The responsibilities of Special Affairs Minister Pompeyo Marquez have been expanded and from now on he will also be responsible for the government's social programs.
