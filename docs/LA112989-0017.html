November 29, 1989, Wednesday, Home Edition

CONSUMER VIEWS: THE SO-CALLED 'ONE-ARMED BANDIT' CAN HOLD A MIGHTY HAUL

Question: My husband and I have just come back from one of our frequent trips to Las Vegas. Both of us are impossible slot machine addicts. How many coins (dollars or quarters) does the average slot machine hold? They must have a fantastic capacity the way people like us pour money into them.

Do they ever run out of coins?

How come we never see anybody from the casino emptying them? How profitable are the slots to the casino, and is there any control over how much they have to pay out?

Finally, if I wanted to buy one for my own use, how much would it cost? -- L. McA.

Answer: I'm sure you're not alone in your curiosity. The slots have had an almost hypnotic fascination for people since a Bavarian immigrant named Charles Fey -- a mechanic employed by an electric supply company in San Francisco -- tinkered around in his spare time and introduced the Liberty Bell (a nickel machine) in 1905.

How much the average slot machine "holds" is an interesting question, because it's a little more complicated than it sounds. The payouts, according to Judy Sullivan, a public relations representative in Chicago for Bally Manufacturing Corp., one of the big three makers of slots, all come from a "hopper," or large tray, in the body of the machine -- naturally.

And, while the capacity of the hoppers will vary, Sullivan said, the average $1 machine will hold about 600 to 800 coins, and the average quarter machine will hold about 2,000. But, sure enough, the machines take in a whale of a lot more coins than that in the course of a day or night, so why aren't the coins squirting out the seams of the machines?

Well, you probably thought that those heavy wooden stands on which every slot machine sits were there to elevate the machine to a comfortable playing level. Actually, the stands underneath the machines are more like small safes; they contain buckets into which the bulk of the coins go as you feed them in. And they do, indeed, have an impressive capacity.

Does a slot ever go dry -- run completely out of coins?

"Oh, sure, it happens all the time," says Jack Deremer, a Bally sales representative in Las Vegas. "The hoppers all have a weight-activated sensor in them so that when the machine starts getting low, the incoming coins will be diverted directly into the hopper to build it back up instead of going into the stand. But, things happen, and the slots suddenly go dry. The floor man fixes it, pays the player what's owed him and that's all there is to it."

And yes, obviously, the buckets under the machine have to be emptied periodically in spite of their capacity.

"Normally," Deremer said, "the casino will do this during the off-hours, sometime between 3 and 6 a.m. Some are emptied every day, others every other day, depending on how much play they get."

How profitable are the slots? Oh boy! The casinos make more from slots than all other games combined.

"It'll vary a lot, of course," Deremer said, "but a dollar machine in a good location -- where it gets pretty steady play all the time -- will net the house about $300 to $400 a day."

That's profit, of course, and it is related to how many dollars are actually passing through the slot.

How much does a slot cost? Well, they're pretty sophisticated and complex. If you walk in off the street to buy one, figure on spending "$4,200 to about $4,500," Deremer added.

Getting back to profits, consider that aforementioned heavily played $1 machine that nets the house, on average, $350 a day. Even at full retail price ($4,500), it will pay for itself in a hair under 13 days; in a year it has grossed $1.2 million and has returned a profit to the house of $127,750.

And how seriously the casinos now regard the slots -- once dismissed as something appealing to little old ladies in tennis shoes with rolls of nickels in their purses -- can be seen in the recent upswing in slots appealing to the traditional "high rollers."

Both $5 and $25 slots have become commonplace, "and a few of the casinos here in Las Vegas," Deremer said, "now have $100 and $500 slots."

There is an edge of awe in his voice: "Can you imagine it -- playing a three-token, $500 machine? That's a $1,500 bet on one pull of the handle."
