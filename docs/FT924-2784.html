921210 FT 10 DEC 92 / Survey of Software at Work - Winter 1992 (18): Importance of clear objectives - Engineering application CAD software was initially bought as an engineering and drafting tool at Hotpoint, the UK manufacturer of white goods. But now, according to David Ellis, IT co-ordinator, 'it is an enabling-tool to improve the design to manufacture process.' Eight years ago the company chose Intergraph's EMS engineering modelling system. When a review looked in 1990 at alternative offerings from Computervision, IBM and HP, Hotpoint decided that it still had the right tool, and stayed with EMS. It now runs EMS, MicroStation, project layout and associated desktop publishing products on a network of Intergraph UNIX workstations and servers. 'There were many excellent solutions available, but CAD must be seen as an integral part of the IT strategy, not an isolated tool, to get the maximum benefit,' says Mr Ellis. Hotpoint was looking for several different levels of integration - not just in-house, but in dealing with third parties without having to translate. It also put a high rating on useability and friendliness. The system has now percolated most areas of Hotpoint, and plays a part not only in the design, but in the manufacture, and even the user manuals provided with Hotpoint's fridges, washing machines and dishwashers. There are now links with the MRP manufacturing software, and with the CNC numerical control systems in Hotpoint's Welsh factories in Rhyl and Gwynnedd. Mr Ellis stresses that the benefits have not been solely in the design improvements attributable to CAD software, but in the new approaches that its use has brought. His advice to others is that 'companies should look at the processes, the people, and then at the technology as an enabler.' CAD should help to develop and improve the effectiveness of individuals, and also benefit and improve the process itself. Too often, he warns, the implementation of CAD software fails because the objectives have not been fully understood - 'the goals have to be defined properly,' he concludes. PRODUCT PROFILE EMS Suppliers: Intergraph UK, owned by US Intergraph Corporation, founded in the US in 1969, and in the UK in 1980. Product: EMS, first launched in 1986, and updated by Version 2 in 1992, is an integrated solid-surface modelling and drafting system. Microstation version 4, first launched 1986 and updated to version 4 by 1991, is a general purpose 3D design-2d drafting package for PCs, Apple Macs and UNIX workstations. GUI-driven, the product provides true associative dimensioning, hot links to database and powerful workgroup facilities. Turnover: Dollars 1.2bn worldwide, with Dollars 71.1m profit in 1991 (UK: Pounds 42m with Pounds 2.2m profit). Employees: 300+ in the UK Number of users: 5,000 in UK, 160,000 worldwide Price: Pounds 3,650 to Pounds 18,250 for EMS (modular), and Pounds 2,950 for Microstation. Hardware and operating systems: Intergraph InterPro UNIX workstations, Sun Sparcstations, Silicon Graphics UNIX workstations. Sales and support/maintenance: UK network of independent Intergraph Solutions Centres. Maintenance is at 8-10 per cent of systems price, per annum. Other services available: customer training, project management, consultancy and design, network design and installation and power supply surveys. Custom software development, hardware maintenance, installation and relocation is also available. User views: Pros: 'Integration, user friendliness.'
