BFN [Article by Yiftah Shafir, a nonconventional weapons proliferation expert at Tel Aviv University's Jaffee Center for Strategic Studies: "A Chinese Slap in the Face of the Clinton Administration"] [Text] Ten days ago the PRC conducted a nuclear test at the Lop Nor testing facility in northwest China. This was the second test conducted by the PRC in less than a year. The Chinese test was a slap in the face of President Clinton, who only recently retracted his resolute demands from the Chinese leaders with regard to arms exports and human rights. The slap in the face is particularly stinging at this point in time when the United States is worried about North Korea developing a bomb, seeing that without the PRC's agreement and assistance it will be impossible to enforce any sanctions against North Korea and that nobody knows how to pressure North Korea to terminate its nuclear program except through sanctions. The Chinese test is particularly hurtful because preventing the proliferation of nuclear weapons is a cornerstone of the Clinton Administration's foreign policy. An integral part of this policy is the moratorium on nuclear tests and the efforts to bring about the signing of an international pact banning such tests in 1996. The moratorium began back in November 1992, when the U.S. Congress forced a nine-month ban on nuclear tests on President Bush's administration. After those nine months, Congress permitted only 15 such tests to be conducted until 1996 and banned nuclear tests altogether afterward. It was President Bush who signed the ban, known as the "Hatfield Amendment." In January 1993, the ball was passed to Clinton, who initially extended it until September 1994. The moratorium on nuclear testing was accompanied by a poignant argument between Congress and the U.S. military establishment. The argument used by advocates of nuclear testing was the need to maintain the high level of expertise for laboratory workers and arms developers. As for the tests themselves, it was decided to conduct three tests to check the reliability of the weapons and nine tests to increase the safety of existing weapons. Another proposal was a compromise to satisfy all sides and limited the tests to "small bombs" not exceeding one kiloton. However, the opponents ultimately had the upper hand. They saw the ban as a trump card in the struggle to prevent the proliferation of weapons of mass destruction. What the opponents saw was "deranged" countries, which at present include Iran and North Korea, with Pakistan, India, and Israel as potential candidates. Another reason for the ban was the ratification of the Nuclear Nonproliferation Treaty (NPT) scheduled to expire in 1995, at which point the countries will meet to discuss its extension and perhaps even to change some of its clauses. Tests would put the "nuclear" countries in an awkward position when urging other countries to evince restraint. However, for the ban to have the desired effect all the nuclear countries have to maintain a united front. Such a front existed until the Chinese test last October. With Russia the problem is negligible: what could not be achieved in the days of the Soviet Union, can easily be attained from Yeltsin, who desperately needs all the U.S. support he can get. With Britain the problem is even easier. Although the British nuclear establishment wants to go on with the tests and the Tory government supports it, the British have a "tiny" technical problem: they test their bombs in Nevada, and without U.S. permission they can hold no tests. Hence, a problem remains with the two "enfants terribles": France and China. France has for some time adopted an independent policy, inconsiderate of its allies' opinion (not to mention public opinion). In spite of all the threats, the Chinese test last October did not upset the moratorium and Russia and France did not hold any further tests. However, there is no certainty that what happened eight months ago will recur today. The global moratorium remains a house of cards built on the principle of "not-the-first-to-test." Back when the moratorium was extended, Clinton ordered the labs "to maintain the ability to resume the testing." Yeltsin also ordered his labs to prepare two to four nuclear tests, and in recent months Russia has been leaning toward an independent policy unconnected to the United States. In France, Mitterrand will have a harder time resisting the pressure of the Army and the right-wing government to resume the tests. The ball is now in Clinton's court. Adherence to the moratorium will enable him to come to the NPT deliberations next year with a clean record. Pressure on Russia may make Yeltsin rein in his nuclear establishment despite his indebtedness to the Army and his desperate need to keep employed those nuclear scientists who have not yet emigrated, and may also help keep France in line. Permission to resume the tests will undoubtedly enable the British to test their bombs. France will waste no time joining the party, and the price will be paid by the whole world in 1995.
