BFN [Article by Jaroslav Kmenta: "There Are Disputes Going On Regarding Exports of the Tamara System"] [Excerpts] Prague--Despite the various disputes regarding the details of the operational results of the Tamara radar that are going on, the world is interested in this system that is capable of monitoring all types of aircraft. The Defense Ministries interested in buying the system include those of India, China, Iran, South Korea, Taiwan, and Thailand. Nevertheless, some military officials are skeptical about the export of this top-ranking product. It is rumored in military circles that the United States is interested in the nonproliferation of this equipment because, as one of a very few systems, it has the capability of detecting the "invisible" U.S. Stealth reconnaissance aircraft. If Iraq, for instance, owned Tamara, the billions spent on the development of Stealth would be wasted. [passage omitted] Tamara is an original Czech discovery that was 35 years in the works. It is a system that, as opposed to other radars, which emit radio waves and evaluate their reflection, only receives radio impulses. Regarding the usage, it neither competes with nor replaces radar but, rather, adds to the radar information such important data as recognition of the type of aircraft or ship, and facilitates the verification of the flight or navigation route by an alternative physical method. [passage omitted]

Its Predecessor Was Ramona
At the end of the 1950's, a new, original method was discovered at the research institute of the then Czechoslovak Army that facilitated an immediate, unequivocal, and extremely precise determination of the location of electrical signal transmitters. The method is based on the so-called time-hyperbolic [casove hyperbolicky] principle. The discovery did not remain just theory but was very rapidly applied. Already in the early 1960's, the Czechoslovak Army had at its disposal the precise PRP-1 Kopac [digger] radiotechnical finder; in the 1970's, the second generation of this technology under the name Ramona; and, in the 1980's, the third generation, called "Tamara." Tamara is a passive radiotechnical system that provides an immediate survey of the activity of ground, water surface, and aerial sources of radiotechnical signals to a distance of 100 km, determines their precise location, and is able to concurrently follow the routes and recognize dozens of types of aircraft or ships of practically all known categories. Experts claim that, if the Tamara system were used, such tragedies as the downing of the KAL-007 airliner by a Soviet Su-15 fighter jet over Sakhalin in the night hours in September 1983, or the downing of the Iranian transport Airbus-300 aircraft by a U.S. Navy missile (from the Vincennes cruiser) in July 1987 over the Strait of Hormuz would not have taken place. Both instances were allegedly caused by errors. In the former case, the aircraft was confused with the U.S. F-16 reconnaissance aircraft. One can safely assume that, in either case, there was no technical system available with the capability to recognize the types of aircraft with the determination precision needed. More than 50 Ramona and Tamara systems have been produced in Czechoslovakia. The vast majority were exported to the then USSR, and one to the former GDR.
