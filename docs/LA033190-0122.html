March 31, 1990, Saturday, Home Edition

CHURCHES BECOME MORE AWARE OF SEXUAL MISCONDUCT BY CLERGY;

ETHICS: A MAIL SURVEY OF PASTORS INDICATES THAT ABOUT 10% HAVE HAD AN INAPPROPRIATE RELATIONSHIP WITH A MEMBER OF THE FLOCK.

Sexual ethics are coming out of the closet and into the hearing rooms and seminaries of mainline Protestant churches.

An increasing number of complaints by laymen and research indicating that up to a quarter of the clergy may have committed some form of sexual misconduct have led churches to address the issue, according to some church leaders and ethicists.

"I think it's coming to the forefront because there are increasing complaints against the clergy," said the Rev. Ronald Barton of the Community Church of the Monterey Peninsula in Carmel. "The cultural environment at this time is one in which victims of all kinds of abuse are ready to name the abuse and the abuser."

And the church is more willing to listen, according to Episcopal Bishop Harold H. Hopkins Jr.

"The church has often looked the other way when inappropriate behavior occurs on the part of clergy," Hopkins said at a recent conference in Minneapolis on sexual misconduct. "Our commitment now is to not only recognize that the problem exists, but to begin immediately to take preventive and educational steps."

In a mail survey of 80 pastors conducted by Barton and the Rev. Karen Lebacqz for the Center for Ethics and Social Policy of the Graduate Theological Union in Berkeley, approximately 10% of the respondents said they had a sexual relationship with a church member, Lebacqz said.

She and Barton said the survey results were consistent with other research, which showed up to a quarter of clergy engaged in misconduct that included such acts as kissing and hugs that were more than friendly embraces.

The results did not surprise some others studying the issue.

"Those figures are not out of line," said the Rev. Emilie M. Townes of the St. Paul School of Theology in Kansas City, Mo., which this week held a conference on ministerial ethics co-sponsored by the South Central College of Bishops and the Division of Ordained Ministry of the United Methodist Church.

The victims are particularly vulnerable because the abuse takes place in a church setting, Townes said.

"By authority of their position, ministers have a lot of power and a lot of sway over people coming to them seeking help," she said. "If the minister's always right, anything he or she asks of them must not be out of line."

But while psychiatrists have been trained to recognize problems of patients being vulnerable to and even initiating sexual advances because of their psychological needs, many clergy have not had such training, Barton said.

When pastors were asked in the survey how they recognized danger signs of sexual misconduct, the top answer was by feeling, intuition and instinct, Lebacqz said.

She said the survey results raise the question: "Are pastors being trained adequately to learn how to deal with sexual-ethical issues?"

In the Northern California Conference of the United Church of Christ, where Barton once served as associate conference minister, church officials worked for five years to develop guidelines for handling complaints, Barton said. In the last four years, he said there have been three or four complaints, compared to one in the four-year period before the guidelines were developed.

Following the Minnesota conference, the Episcopal Church is considering organizing a national task force on the issue.

"Sexual involvement by clergy is tantamount to family incest," said the Rev. Susan Moss, a member of the Interfaith Task Force on Sexual Exploitation by Clergy in Minnesota. "The dynamics are the same: Parishioners come to church expecting that clergy will help provide a nurturing, caring environment, a safe place to grow in the faith. When that trust is betrayed, the whole congregation becomes the victim."
