July 11, 1990, Wednesday, Orange County Edition

ALL FANS ON DECK FOR TRIBUTE TO CITY'S NAMESAKE;

STAGE: A LANDLUBBER ACTOR TRODS THE BOARDS OF A BRIG TO BRING 'TWO YEARS BEFORE THE MAST' ALIVE IN DANA POINT.

When actor Daniel Trent set out to adapt Richard Henry Dana's "Two Years Before the Mast" for the stage, he thought that crewing a yacht up the Northern California coast would give him a feel for Dana's life at sea.

But when the ship left San Francisco Bay, it hit "20-foot waves every six seconds for the next four days." And the actor-writer became a confirmed landlubber.

Lack of sea legs has not stopped Trent from taking California audiences on a dramatized sailing adventure with his one-man show of Dana's classic.

"Two Years Before the Mast," which premiered in 1981 on Pilgrim -- the ship moored in Dana Point Harbor that is a replica of the original brig on which Dana worked -- opened again on the ship Saturday and will continue with two performances each Friday and Saturday night through July. The show is a presentation of the Orange County Marine Institute, also at Dana Point Harbor.

Ocean breezes set the mood, and the Pilgrim's deck becomes a stage as audiences sit below the ship's 96-foot mainmast to hear Trent spin his tale.

"Sometimes you get lucky and the fog rolls in and you get to transport the whole audience," Trent said. "Then it becomes magic."

Trent re-creates seven characters from Dana's true-life story, excerpting directly from the book to recount the young sailor's two years in the service of a tyrannical captain.

The story introduces Dana at 42 as he reminisces about his two-year journey aboard a trading ship. Then the script travels back to 1834, when the 18-year-old Dana began his adventure from Boston Harbor around Cape Horn and up the unsettled California coast, where Pilgrim traded fireworks and saddles for animal hides.

The crew encountered pirate attacks and dangerous waters as well as the cruel hand of their merciless captain. The young Dana, one of Boston's sheltered elite, came of age during the trip that introduced him to injustice and death, as well as the unfamiliar Spanish-Catholic culture of California.

Dana's account of the trip was first published in 1840 and is now in its 91st edition. His frank depiction of the hardships faced by sailors helped to reform maritime law, Trent said. And because the book provided rare information about life on the California coast, the book became a bible for Gold Rush hopefuls.

During nine years performing the show for historical societies, student groups and community organizations, Trent has approximated Dana's path up the California coast with his portrayal.

But the play seems to belong most in the city that Dana called "the only romantic spot in California." The town returned this compliment by officially adopting Dana's name in 1884; a statue of the author now welcomes visitors to Harbor Island, near Pilgrim's mooring at Dana Cove Park and overlooking the harbor entrance.

Trent, who adapted the "Two Years Before the Mast" with partner Victor Pinheiro, said he never tires of retelling Dana's tale of adventure.

"I think I will do Dana as long as someone wants me to do it," he said. "I just love the show. It's a beautiful piece, and I'm real proud that I had a hand in creating it. Heck, I'm the best one who's ever done the show -- I'm the only one who's ever done it!"

And after nine years of re-creating Dana's history, Trent said: "I think I know his inner life pretty well. I think he was a wonderful, noble being. But I feel sad for him sometimes."

Dana returned to Boston and his father, also named Richard Henry Dana, and became a lawyer, leaving behind his life of risk and romance. Trent believes that Dana turned his back on his true destiny:

"At the end of the show Dana says, 'My thoughts wander back to my old sea life. . . . I believe I was made for the sea and all my life on shore was a mistake.' "

"Two Years Before the Mast" continues Fridays and Saturdays at 6:30 and 9 p.m. through July 28 on the Pilgrim at Dana Point Harbor, 24200 Dana Point Harbor Drive, Dana Point. Tickets: $26. Information: (714) 496-2274.
