November 29, 1989, Wednesday, Home Edition

JAMES FLANIGAN: COLD WAR'S END MAY OPEN DOORS FOR DEFENSE WORKERS

The U.S. Armed Services employ 2.1 million people in uniform and an additional 1 million civilians. In addition, more than 2 million people work for aerospace-defense companies and thus stand to be affected by cuts in the defense budget.

And there is a multiplier effect among the millions more who work for the non-defense firms that supply those aerospace companies with everything from paper clips to gasoline. Add them all up, and it's understandable that communities in practically every state are apprehensive about cuts in the defense budget, even as they hail the end of the Cold War and tell pollster Louis Harris that they favor reduced military spending. Fear of unemployment clouds even a hope as bright as peace.

The record is not reassuring: The end of World War I in November, 1918, led to a severe economic downturn in 1920. The economy did better in the transition to peace after World War II, but it had help from the buildup of peacetime defense industry -- the advanced aircraft and high-tech weaponry programs that are now faced with winding down.

So what's likely to happen to the economy this time? Things will be better than communities fear today -- maybe far better. Two things to keep in mind are that simple inertia will ease the economy's transition and that people don't stop thinking just because defense money stops pumping -- hosts of skilled workers in defense may find great opportunities in commercial projects.

First inertia. Cutbacks won't happen as swiftly as feared. "Politics won't allow dramatic cuts to happen," says analyst Michael Lauer of Kidder, Peabody & Co., who notes that the New York congressional delegation's lobbying has kept production of the Navy F-14D fighter plane going at Long Island's Grumman Aircraft. Similar politicking will at least extend the transition from growing to shrinking defense budgets.

Then, too, the amount of the $286-billion budget's ultimate shrinkage is a good question. Reports are that $180 billion will be cut from defense budgets during fiscal years 1992 to 1994 and that defense spending will be reduced from 6% of the U.S. gross national product -- now $5.13 trillion -- to 4% by 1994. But by that time the GNP will have grown to nearly $6 trillion -- which indicates an annual defense budget roughly $40 billion less than today's.

Translation: There will be cuts in defense spending, but not enough to cause an economic depression. In a nutshell, the defense industry is entering a long period of no-growth. "It will just be a tougher business for aerospace-defense companies," says analyst Wolfgang Demisch of UBS Securities. "They'll have to keep their eye on the ball."

Some programs will be canceled: Analysts suggest the B-2 bomber will be a sacrificial pawn in negotiations to get the Soviets to give up mobile missiles. Some programs will be expanded: Surveillance satellites will continue to be built, perhaps even more so in an era when disarmament agreements need verification.

Meanwhile, expect some troops to come home from Europe and expect the armed forces to be reduced by 500,000 or so -- but not to swell the ranks of the unemployed. "Most military people are confident they'll find jobs," says a military analyst. "They're trained people, who have proved themselves in the military, and we have a labor-short economy."

Even more positive are the possibilities that await the thousands of engineers working in defense firms. If their projects get canceled or their departments are cut back, they could find a welcome in commercial industry, even the chance to found their own business, with the aid of venture capital. "We're eager to listen and talk to people in aerospace about ideas, or even better about setting up a business," says Michael Moritz, a partner in Sequoia Capital, a venture capital firm in Menlo Park.

Other venture capital firms, such as Sevin-Rosen Management in Dallas and New York and Kleiner, Perkins, Caufield & Byers of San Francisco and Palo Alto, hope to see a new breed of entrepreneurs come out of defense work -- although they admit that the idea is new and unfamiliar. The conventional thinking is that there is no connection between commercial industry and what one does in defense work -- or as one analyst puts it, "You can't easily sell knowledge of wing-based gun systems."

Perhaps not, concedes Sequoia Capital's Moritz, "but there's certainly a call for advanced semiconductor skills and skills in telecommunications and imaging."

"A lot of good optics work is done in the military -- space lasers, pop-up computer displays -- while the commercial field is crying out for optical engineers," says Floyd Kwamme, a partner in the Kleiner, Perkins firm. "I see engineering talent from the defense field taking jobs in commercial start-up firms and then perhaps founding their own company when the next product comes along."

A heady vision. "It won't take many guys coming out of Rockwell or McDonnell or the other dinosaurs to give their colleagues ideas," says a venture capital type. The message: The economy's need for weapons may diminish but not its need for brains.
