BFN [Text] Guatemala City, 3 Apr (NOTIMEX) -- Today, Guatemalan President Ramiro De Leon Carpio announced he might declare a state of emergency as result of the murder of Constitutionality Court President Epaminondas Gonzalez in the next few days. De Leon made the announcement during the funeral services of one of the country's most distinguished legal experts. The murder, which was carried out early yesterday by unknown gunmen, has been condemned by all sectors of the country. The president acknowledged that he has considered the possibility of declaring a state of emergency but that the measure will have to be approved this week by the State Council of Ministers. On Friday, Government Minister Danilo Parinello announced that a possible state of emergency declaration is being reviewed as result of numerous crimes, attacks, and disturbances taking place throughout the country and threatening to become a national catastrophe. Last week, journalist Marco Tulio de la Roca was shot and, as result, lost his left eye. In addition, Francisco Elias, the cousin of former President Jorge Elias Serrano, was also shot and remains in serious conditions at a private hospital. There was also the recent murder of a so-called "street child" and a mob attacked an American citizen in the northern community of San Cristobal Verapaz, after being accused of attempting to abduct a small boy. Another American tourist was fired on for no apparent reason by a group of unknown individuals on a Guatemala City street. Gonzalez's murder has disturbed the country's judicial, union, government, church, and diplomatic communities, which believe the murder will become just another of the countless crimes that go unpunished in this Central American nation. Yesterday, De Leon announced he had ordered an in-depth investigation to locate the murderers of the Constitutionality Court magistrate, whom he described as an "outstanding citizen, prestigious jurist, and an authentic advocate of democracy and peace in Guatemala." This past July, however, when the president's cousin Jorge Carpio Nicolle, editor of EL GRAFICO newspaper and secretary general of the Union of the National Center party [UCN], was murdered, De Leon appointed a special commission to investigate the crime, which to date has failed to accomplish anything. The Catholic Church and Carpio Nicolle's relatives, who were present when the journalist was murdered along with three other people, insist that the murder was carried out by the paramilitary Civilian Sel-Defense Patrols (PAC), but the authorities have dismissed these claims. The archbishop of Guatemala and the human rights ombudsman have said it is De Leon's moral duty to solve the Gonzalez murder, which occurred just 10 days before Gonzalez was to conclude his term as Constitutional Court president. He was killed four days after the court had ruled that Congress-approved reforms to the Electoral and Political Parties Law were unconstitutional. Gonzalez had argued that the deputies not only modified the law but, in fact, created a new law. He ruled that this was illegal because the Electoral and Political Parties Law can only be repealed by a national constituent assembly. The reforms are aimed at increasing the number of congressional deputies as well as establishing that any party that does not field candidates in an election for deputies should be taken off the official political parties list. Moreover, the congressional deputies affected by the new law rejected the independence of the Supreme Electoral Tribunal and opened the way for civic committees to participate in the election process. The new election law also required the resignation of all political party members holding government positions and whose leader occupied the Presidency [alta magistratura], and also changed name of the new law to the "Election and People's Organization Law [Ley Electoral y de Organizationces Populares]." Gonzalez presided over the Constitutionality Court when it ruled against the 25 May 1993 "self-inflicted coup" carried out by then-President Jorge Serrano. The court's decision proved decisive in reestablishing constitutional order in Guatemala.
