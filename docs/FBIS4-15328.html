BFN [Report by Anatol Awcharenka] [Excerpts] My visit to Minsk was extremely positive. This is how UN Secretary General Butrus Butrus-Ghali assessed his meetings, negotiations, and talks [in Belarus], speaking at a conference with journalists at the National Press Center on 7 April. In the course of a three-day visit, seven concrete issues were discussed at different levels with a Foreign Ministry delegation, at the Council of Ministers, and the Supreme Soviet. All of these issues are very important and urgent for Belarus, but the primary one is the problem stemming from the Chernobyl catastrophe. Mr. Butrus-Ghali arrived in our country to see what Chernobyl is, in reality. It is no secret that some countries are displaying weariness and losing interest in Chernobyl's problems. I am sure that the Belarusian side managed to provide the secretary general with convincing data contained in both the national report on Chernobyl and a special memorandum. These documents provide the most recent figures, the most current information. [passage omitted] In the course of talks, both sides discussed measures to be taken to prevent the world community from reducing its involvement in alleviating the consequences of the Chernobyl accident. However, as was underlined at a news conference held by Belarusian Foreign Minister Pyotr Krawchanka, it will be impossible to fully eliminate the aftermath of the accident in this century. The Belarusian side can also consider the general secretary's visit a success because it was attended by the director of the Economic Commission for European Affairs. His participation in the negotiations was not coincidental. In my opinion, his arrival in Belarus demonstrated to our government that the United Nations is ready to help Belarus survive its transitional phase and integrate into the world, or at least the European economy. Mr. Butrus-Ghali suggested that our Republic take part in peacekeeping operations in various regions of the world. The general secretary said: We would be very grateful to you if Belarus could provide at least a small contingent of blue helmets. He was told that under current legislation, only the Supreme Soviet can decree Belarusian servicemen's participation in operations outside the Republic. If the situation were to change, we could assist the United Nations in keeping peace around the world. The UN and Belarusian delegations also discussed the situation of Belarusian ethnic minorities in Latvia and Estonia. Mr. Butrus-Ghali treats this problem very seriously, and that is why the two sides agreed that the Laotian envoy, who is the UN Supreme Commissary, will come to Minsk and receive data on this complex problem, which is persistently plaguing us. The envoy will then visit the Baltic states to contribute to resolving the problem of national minorities, including Belarusians, in Latvia and Estonia. [passage omitted] UN Secretary General Butrus Butrus-Ghali has left for Geneva.
