Language: Russian Article Type:CSO [Article by Major Vadim Kinshin: "Advanced Maneuverability Fighter"] [Text] Interest in raising the maneuvering characteristics of warplanes persists abroad. The military departments of the USA and Germany are conducting joint research on a number of aviation programs for the future. A program to design a tactical fighter with advanced maneuverability (EFM) occupies a special place. Series X airplanes are the first experimental models. A joint international simulation exercise was carried out in 1985 to evaluate the influence of maneuvering qualities on a fighter's combat effectiveness, especially in close combat. Research was conducted within the program with regard for present trends in combat use of fighter aviation and air defense resources. This is associated with the fact that significant changes occurred in the last 10 years in the homing heads of all types of missiles. The sensitivity of homing heads and their scanning angles were subjected to modernization primarily. Requirements on the tactics of aerial combat, especially close combat, have risen: The modern fighter must have "tactical freedom"--that is, it must maintain its advantages until the absolute end of combat. However, for the time being we can observe a different picture--more than half of the "one on one" contests end in mutually observed missile launchings, or in other words, the potential destruction of one another. In the opinion of foreign military specialists high-speed flight remains the best means of defense, because the commonly accepted trends in development and use of fighters have "wallowed at low speeds." Experts feel that it is only theoretically that a decrease in speed makes it possible to change a fighter's trajectory or maneuvering mode. In fact, an airplane's inertia does not change when it carries out maneuvers at high angles of attack, while its aerodynamic control becomes weak. As a result the possibilities for completing a maneuver quickly are limited. Foreign specialists see passive inertial flight at a speed of around Mach 0.9 in particular sections of the trajectory to be one of the solutions to this problem. This approach makes it possible for a fighter to reduce flying distance while maneuvering, and to complete maneuvers faster. Forestalling the opponent in this fashion, the airplane can assume a more advantageous position for effective use of onboard weapons. The airplane's configuration recalls that of the F-18 fighter. The X-31A has a swept-back fuselage with flat side walls, low wings and a vertical tail fin welded to the fuselage. The engine's air intake has a movable lower edge, and it is located beneath the fuselage. According to design calculations this is the variant that permits the fighter to operate in optimum fashion at large angles of attack. Forward horizontal control surfaces--for pitch control and balancing of the craft's airframe--may be said to be yet another unique feature of the airplane's design. The flight control devices of the X-31A are of the conventional variety. Presence of a group of three large vanes mounted near the jet engine nozzle is an identifying structural feature. They are intended for fast and precise deflection of the main direction of the jet stream by changing part of its flow in the needed direction. In this case the vanes keep the engine's thrust factor at its maximum. They are capable of increasing the total deflection of the main exhaust flow by more than 10 degrees, which makes it possible to obtain up to 17 percent additional thrust energy in any selected direction. Consequently the pilot may quickly change flight direction, and maneuver without resorting to the assistance of conventional control devices. Good aerodynamic qualities of the wings, especially at large angles of attack both at sub- and supersonic speeds, have been noted on several occasions in tests. The flat side walls of the fuselage made it possible to achieve reasonable characteristics during slip. On the basis of maneuverability requirements the X-31A has a "load ratio" (the ratio of a loaded engine to thrust) of not less than 1:1. In addition the aircraft may be flown on the basis of flight control system data, while the layout of the fighter makes it possible to utilize full engine power even at angles of attack of 70 degrees. The high maneuvering qualities of the airplane are associated with presence of forward control surfaces. Tests showed that at large angles of attack in subsonic flight, a critical moment arises at which the center of gravity shifts toward the tail or nose of the fighter--it becomes unstable, and can spontaneously stall. It is to preclude this that the forward control surfaces are used. Research conducted under the program revealed that owing to its advanced maneuverability, the X-31A can quickly assume a stable position while performing any dangerous maneuver, upon entering into a protracted stall, when the engine fails, or when the thrust control system breaks down. This is how higher flight safety of the X-31A in comparison with other fighters is accomplished. There is one unique feature of the fighter's control: Flight control is linked to engine thrust control. Consequently the principal means of flight control is automatic, using the flight control system. Variant of a Maneuver Certain changes were made in the controls and displays in connection with the fighter's new possibilities. There is an additional switch on the joystick allowing the pilot to cause the aircraft to stall or return to ordinary flight. An additional piloting mark is projected onto the heads-up display. Its purpose is to indicate and warn the pilot of the real angular position of the airplane in space and its subsequent oblique displacements in the course of stall maneuvers. Tests showed that the peculiarities of control of the X-31A favorably affect the results of aerial combat, because it is hard for the opponent to predict the airplane's trajectory. In addition, stalling during combat makes it possible to more than double the number of missiles launched, and thus quadruple the fighter's firepower. In turn, the combat exchange factor rises by 160 percent to a ratio of 1:3 in favor of the X-31A. A 15 percent increase in the airplane's mobility reduces losses to air defense fire by 75 percent. New structural concepts and results of tests on the advanced maneuverability fighter encouraged further development of this concept. Thus America's Grumman developed a similar fighter, the X-29A, with forward-swept wings. Flight tests demonstrated the airplane's high maneuvering qualities: At angles of attack of 25-40 degrees its speed of descent was minimal, and it made a complete turn in a radius of 350-400 meters. Control of the direction of engine thrust has also been reflected in a fighter of a totally different type--the STOVL short take-off and landing aircraft. According to reports in the foreign press it will have an improved aerodynamic shape and built-in thrust control. The concept of advanced maneuverability of tactical fighters is now being used abroad as the basis of active efforts to design an airplane capable of changing its length while in flight.
