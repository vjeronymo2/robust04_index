May 16, 1990, Wednesday, Home Edition

GREAT AMERICAN BANK NARROWS LOSS IN QUARTER

Great American Bank on Tuesday reported a $1.2-million first-quarter loss, an encouraging sign after the troubled San Diego-based thrift nearly drowned in a tide of red ink in 1989.

But Great American also reported a continued deterioration of the overall quality of its loans and said it is enmeshed in a dispute with the federal Office of Thrift Supervision over the value of certain loans.

If OTS auditors prevail in the dispute, to be settled during the current quarter ending June 30, a revaluation of the loans could ensue, resulting in writedowns of up to $65 million to be charged against Great American's earnings.

Great American's loss for the quarter ended March 30 contrasted with an $11-million profit for the same period last year. But Great American executives took heart in the fact that the loss was an improvement from the $198.9 million and $263.4 million losses for the 1989 fourth quarter and full year, respectively.

Last year's loss, caused largely by loss provisions set aside to cover non-performing commercial and apartment loans in Arizona, put Great American on the brink of insolvency. As of Dec. 31, Great American was deficient in all three standards applied by the OTS to test a thrift's capital, or its financial cushion against loss. The S&L was given a Dec. 31, 1990, deadline to sell itself or raise $350 million in outside capital to avoid a possible regulatory seizure.

However, there are signs that Great American still has not turned the corner on its bad loans. Its non-performing assets as of March 31 rose to $556.7 million, or 3.61% of assets, from $539.4 million, or 3.39% of assets, as of Dec. 31. The percentage increase also reflects the shrinkage in Great American's assets, to $15.4 billion as of March 31 from $15.9 billion at the end of last year.

Another bad sign for the S&L was that its classified assets, a broader category of problem loans, increased over the three-month period to $1.389 billion, up from $1.297 billion as of Dec. 31.

That OTS inspectors have not fully completed their field reviews of Great American loans could be another ominous sign for the S&L. The OTS has reviewed all of the S&L's Arizona loans and those of its real estate development subsidiary, but not Great American's loans made by its California and Washington branches.

The strict standards imposed by OTS audits of Great American's Arizona loans were factors in the S&L taking huge loan losses in 1989. Great American expects to receive written results of the examination during the current quarter ending June 30.

Thanks to an accounting technique, Great American reported that its capital position improved somewhat over the first quarter. The technique involved a reallocation of $34.6 million in retained earnings from Great American's real estate development operation to the S&L. Despite this, Great American's so-called tangible capital is still $99.1 million short of minimum regulatory requirements.

The S&L also declined to comment on reports that it has offered its chief executive post to James M. Cirona, president of the Federal Home Loan Bank of San Francisco.
