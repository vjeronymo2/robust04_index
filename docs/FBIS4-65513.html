BFN [Unattributed article: "Bosnia: Previti, Martino, on Italian Peacekeepers"] [Text] (ANSA) Rome, May 16 -- There was a chance that Italian peacekeeping forces might one day take part in peace operations in Bosnia but only if there were a unanimous request to this effect from the United Nations, and always within the framework of NATO, and in the presence of a bona fide peace plan to be safeguarded and brought to a successful conclusion, Italian Defense Minister Cesare Previti told journalists today coming out of a meeting with Reginald Bartholomew, the United States ambassador to Rome. In response to a question, Previti said that in his talk with the American ambassador, Bosnia had come up only marginally and was barely touched upon. The U.S. ambassador, however, took the occasion to voice appreciation for Italy's "essential cooperation" in NATO's initiatives in and for the former Yugoslavia. Turning to Italo-American ties, Previti stressed that the long-time alliance between the two countries would continue unfailingly in the future. Meanwhile, out of Brussels, where the new Italian foreign minister Antonio Martino was attending an EU [European Union] ministerial meeting, the first signals were given that the Berlusconi government would like to play a more active role in the Balkan crisis. Stressing that "the problem is very close to our interests", Martino announced to his European colleagues that an Italian diplomatic representation was imminently about to open in Sarajevo and would be placed in the hands of ambassador Vittorio Pennarola. The minister did not rule out the sending in of Italian peacekeepers "under certain conditions" to oversee, within a NATO context, the implementation of an overall peace plan agreed to by all sides. However, Italy's controversial recent experience in Somalia led the government to want to be certain that it had a say in all the relevant decisions before committing peacekeeping troops to Bosnia, Martino said.
