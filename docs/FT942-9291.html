940517 FT 17 MAY 94 / Labour MPs hold advantage on leadership The vote of each Labour MP in the election to find a successor to Mr John Smith will be equivalent to those of 14,500 trades unionists paying a political levy. Furthermore, in spite of the one-member-one-vote label attached to the complicated voting system agreed by the party conference last autumn, the 250,000 party members will each have a vote worth 18 times that of their 4.5m trades union comrades. As officials yesterday wrestled with the arithmetic, it also emerged that most MPs will have several different votes. One senior backbench MP calculated that he would have five separate opportunities to put a cross against his preferred candidate: as an MP, as a member of his local constituency branch, as a levy-paying trade unionist, as a member of the Fabian Society and as a member of the Co-operative Society. The MP assured reporters he intended to cast all his votes for the same candidate, rather than share them between rival claimants. Labour has never claimed that one-member-one-vote was more than a rough shorthand for a more democratic system. The electoral college which chooses the leader is split into three: the 310 MPs and MEPs, local constituency members and members of socialist societies, and trades union levy payers. Each carries equal weight and must ballot its members before casting its aggregated votes. Officials are only now beginning to realise the huge disparity between the influence of MPs, party members and trade unionist members, which is reinforced by the fact that the union leadership no longer has any direct control over how its members vote. Mr Smith was criticised last autumn for not doing more to curb the influence of the unions in choosing the leader. But as one MP put it yesterday: 'He was smarter than we thought.'
