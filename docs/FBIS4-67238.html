BFN [Report by Rodolfo Rojas-Zea] [Text] The Attorney General Office's [PGR] Planning Center for Drug Control [Cendro] has said that the area devoted in Mexico to narcotics crops "increases continually due to, among other things, the poverty created for years by low agriculture yields, the lack of infrastructure, and the low prices of agricultural products." Cendro added that, after narcotics crops on thousands of hectares in northern Mexico were razed, drug traffickers moved to the southern part of the country, devastating forest areas in Veracruz, Puebla, and the tropical jungle in Oaxaca, Chiapas, Tabasco, Campeche, and Quintana Roo. The PGR has admitted that production of narcotics, mainly opium, has increased in nearly every southern state in the country as a consequence of the reactivation of the opium market in the United States and Europe. Oaxaca is one of the four states where opium fields are most abundant. These fields are located in San Vicente Coatlan, Coicoyan del las Flores, Miahuatlan de Porfirio Diaz, Oaxaca de Juarez, Pinotepa de Don Luis, San Baltasar Chichicapam, and San Carlos Yautepec, all of them in La Mixteca and the Southern Sierra Madre, where it borders with Guerrero state and the Tehuantepec isthmus. Cendro reports that as a result of unemployment in Oaxaca, more than 2,000 Zapotec, Mixtec, and Chinantec Indians have been hired by drug traffickers to grow marijuana and poppy on their plots of land and store marijuana in their storage areas. This happens in Ejutla de Crespo, Ixtepec, and San Miguel del Puerto municipalities. In Chiapas, Indian communities and Guatemalans, working together near the border with Guatemala, participate in the production of drugs. The Papaloapan River basin, one of the largest production areas, located on the border between Oaxaca and Veracruz, has begun to be used as a distribution center by drug traffickers who force peasants to grow narcotics crops. According to the Cendro report, the Pacific region was Mexico's largest production area from 1988 to 1992, and it elicited the strongest action by the authorities. Sinaloa was the top marijuana producer, with 20.90 percent of the total that was destroyed throughout the country. Michoacan was second. In Jalisco, the area where marijuana was destroyed in 1992 and 1993 was three times larger than in 1991, particularly in the municipalities of San Sebastian, Tequila, and Villa Corona. Other municipalities have been more active, such as Hostotipanquillo, Magdalena, Colotlan, Pihuamo, Tecalitlan, and Ciudad Guzman, where vigilance was improved with 1,500 soldiers, who have intensified the fight against the growing, harvesting, and transporting of drugs. This fight has been supported by Mexican Air Force and PGR helicopters. In Sinaloa, which ranks as the largest grower of poppy, the area where this crop was destroyed increased by 74 percent in 1992 and 1993, as compared to 1991. The destruction of crops took place mostly in Badiraguato, Mazatlan, and Choix. Last year, 3,552 hectares planted with poppy were razed in the state. The municipalities most affected were Choix, Cosala, Badariguato, Ahome, and San Ignacio, where peasants continue to be hired on a large-scale basis to grow and harvest the narcotic. Irrigation systems and tools were observed in some of the fields that were destroyed. Cendro says that the production of marijuana has been steady in most Nayarit municipalities. Nevertheless, the usage of land increased by as much as 80 percent in 1993, mostly in the municipalities of Ixtlan, Huajicori, Compostela, Nayar, and San Pedro. In Durango, eight times more marijuana was destroyed in 1993 than in 1992. The increase in the identification and destruction of marijuana fields has been marked. The municipalities where this problem is greater are Armeria, Tecoman, Minatitlan, and Coquimatlan. This situation is caused by the weather conditions both along the coast and in the highlands. In states where crops were less abundant, the strategy changed. This is what happened in Atolinga, Zacatecas State, where the Federal Judicial Police discovered marijuana fields interspersed with corn fields. In the Pacific zone, regarded as one of the largest producers, 24.7 percent more narcotics fields were destroyed than last year. In 1992, the eradication of poppy plantations in the area increased by 24.69 percent compared with 1991, mostly in Guerrero State. There, 4,071.4 hectares, which represented 35.1 percent of the total nationwide, were destroyed in 1992. Poppy plantations were located in the municipalities of Ajuchitlan, Copanatoyac, Coyuca de Catalan, Chilapa de Alvarez, Chilpancingo, General Heliodoro Castillo, Iguala, Jose Azueta, Leonardo Bravo, and Metlatonoc. The Cendro contends that physical and socioeconomic conditions in Michoacan favor a high level of drug trafficking. As a result, Michoacan is one of the major producers of marijuana and, according to statistics, 3,231 hectares were eradicated during the 1992-93 period, which is a 60-percent increase over the previous year. The mountains of Acuitzio, Apatzingan, and Tacambaro continue to be areas for growing illegal crops while Nuevo Urecho, Penjamillo, Los Reyes, and San Lucas are municipalities that have recently joined the program to eradicate illegal crops. Heroin is produced in Michoacan, a state where there is virtually no eradication of poppy, which leads to the assumption that the opium comes from Guerrero State. As for central Mexico, the Cendro reveals that drug crops on plots equipped with an irrigation infrastructure on ranches owned by Juan Jose Quintero Payan were detected in San Miguel de Allende. In addition, marijuana plantations have been detected in Dolores Hidalgo and Juventino Rosas. A field of marijuana ready to be harvested was found in Tejupilco, Mexico State. Mexico State is considered a broad area for sowing and growing marijuana, particularly places such as the hills of San Vicente, San Miguel Grande, the highlands of San Pedro Limon, and along some of the road to Los Bejucos.

They Even Have Irrigated Agricultural Areas
There is a generalized increase in the lands used for growing marijuana, expanding its scope of influence in the central area and incorporating new plots equipped with irrigation systems. This practice promotes the relocation of this crop in areas that, because of their characteristics, yield a larger amount and higher-quality drug. These practices have been detected in areas bordering San Luis Potosi-Hidalgo-Veracruz and Guerrero-Michoacan. As for northern Mexico, the problem of drug crops in Chihuahua is increasing, especially in the region inhabited by the Tepehuan and Tarahumara Indians. In those regions, the Cendro notes, given the poverty levels among native groups, illegal crops are easily incorporated. In 1992-1993, there was a determined antidrug effort by the authorities in Baborigame.
Chihuahua, Durango, and Sinaloa: the "Golden Triangle"
Drug-sowing in the Tepehuan region include the States of Chihuahua, Durango, and Sinaloa. This area--also known as "the Golden Triangle"--possesses the best conditions for the production of marijuana and poppy among all areas in northern Mexico. The Tepehuan region garners elements such as geographical isolation, poverty and social backwardness, which make it particularly attractive to drug traffickers. The emergence of drug production in southern Baja California State is troubling. In 1992-1993, marijuana plantations, equipped with a modern and working irrigation system, were eradicated for the first time since 1984 in the municipality of Comondu. Marijuana plantations have also been found near Guerrero Negro. "Mobile" crops represent an option for drug traffickers because they can be removed easily and rapidly if the authorities detect them. During 1992, there was an increase in the eradication of plantations of this type in the States of Sinaloa and Sonora. In the months of July, August, and September 1992, 1,559 hectares of marijuana were destroyed in Sinaloa and Sonora, thus increasing to 33 percent the average of eradicated marijuana registered during the same period in the three previous years. However, reports indicate a reversal for illegal crops in the municipalities of Alamos and Ures, Sonora State. The eradication of mobile crops in El Venadito's ejido in the municipality of Alamos, where marijuana was grown in plastic bags is worth mentioning. Regarding poppy production, 2,713.6 hectares of poppy were destroyed in the states near the northern border. These 2,713.6 hectares represent 23.6-percent of the total destroyed nationwide, and represents a 136.4-percent, 44.2 percent, and 77.2 percent increase compared with the three previous years, respectively, the Cendro ends.
