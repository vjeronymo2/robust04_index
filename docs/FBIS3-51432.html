Language: Spanish Article Type:BFN [Article by Alberto Pepe Leira] [Text] Colonia del Sacramento, Uruguay, 17 Jan (EFE) -- The presidents of Argentina, Brazil, Paraguay, and Uruguay today ratified that Mercosur [Common Market of the South] will go into operation on 1 January 1995. They also confirmed the new work program to overcome the problem related to the common external tariff which is preventing the creation of a customs union. The presidents were not able to fulfill the objective of announcing an agreement on the external tariff which is the basis for the customs union before Mercosur goes into operation. This was the intention of the fifth Mercosur summit after the emergence of a number of problems related to percentages accepted by each country for the protection of heavy industry, electronics, informatics, and petrochemistry. Brazil, which is the main Mercosur partner, wants tariffs of up to 35 percent on those items while Argentina accepts up to 12 percent or a maximum of 14 percent on the exports of third countries. Paraguay and Uruguay, the two smaller partners, are in favor of a minimum tariff to prevent being subject to Brazilian technology and to the lack of efficiency of those industrial sectors. President Carlos Menem of Argentina, Itamar Franco of Brazil, Juan Carlos Wasmosy of Paraguay, and Luis Alberto Lacalle of Uruguay agreed today, upon opening the fifth summit, on the difficulties facing Mercosur in this decisive stage of its operation. At the same time Bolivian President Gonzalo Sanchez de Lozada, who attended the meeting as an observer, warned in his speech that the lack of political will has been the main cause of Latin American failure in the search for economic integration. All the presidents who carried out intensive direct diplomatic activity to overcome the obstacles asserted their desire to continue despite the obstacles. Paraguayan President Wasmosy was the one who clearly defined the current situation by indirectly criticizing those like Brazil which seek to maintain their traditional protectionism. Wasmosy said that Mercosur will only represent "the opening of a market comparable to other world regions if there is a modern framework based on free trade and competition, and where traditional protectionism must adjust to the new scenario." He also said that Mercosur must be "a system for new investments and technology, even the `advanced' ones, as well as real full employment." On the other side, Brazilian President Franco, who admitted the future difficulties, expressed satisfaction with the progress of Mercosur, and with the growth of the economies of the four countries. He supports the extension of the free trade agreements to Chile and the signing of other subregional agreements. He added, however, that the complete opening of the Brazilian markets will have "benefits proportional to the size of the openings in each of the other member countries." In order to overcome the obstacles and interpretations, the presidents decided to approve a new program to define the external tariff. That program must conclude before the sixth summit scheduled for next July, six months before the date established by the Asuncion Treaty of 26 March 1991 to put Mercosur into operation. That work program establishes concrete dates to define each item of the tariff and to move toward a common percentage for imports from third countries. The experts believe that the less complex negotiations will conclude before May and that by July there will be a clear picture on the four most controversial items which Brazil hopes to protect from the imports from third countries: informatics, petrochemistry, capital goods, and electronics. A timetable for reducing tariffs has been established, therefore, it is expected that by the year 2000 there can be a tariff of 12 percent for capital goods. Regarding informatics and electronics, for which Argentina wants a tariff of 4 percent and Brazil 35 percent, the lowest possible tariff will be established by the year 2006 as a way to favor industrial reconversion.
