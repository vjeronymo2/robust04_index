BFN [Article signed "SM/AZ": "Disarmament Conference in Geneva Heads Toward Stopping All Nuclear Tests"] [Text] Geneva 30 Mar (ATS) -- Nuclear tests have been partially prohibited since 1963. The Disarmament Conference devoted most of its winter session, which ended in Geneva on Wednesday [30 March], to drawing up a treaty that would prohibit all nuclear tests. The stage of declaring good intentions is now past, and a treaty could be ready within the next 12 to 18 months, affirmed Mexican Ambassador Miguel Marin-Bosch. Mr. Marin-Bosch, who is the chairman of the committee responsible for negotiating such an agreement reiterated that since the signature of the Treaty of Moscow in 1963 -- banning nuclear tests within the atmosphere, in extra-atmospheric space, and underwater -- more than 2,000 nuclear tests had been carried out by the United States and the former USSR. Both these countries, along with Great Britain, are signatories to the agreement on a partial stop to tests. Mr. Marin-Bosch underlined that a treaty prohibiting all nuclear tests constituted a "very important" part of a universal regime aimed at nuclear non-proliferation. In particular, this regime involves negotiations on certain fissile materials, and an agreement protecting those countries which have no nuclear weapons.

Ratified by 160 Countries
Some 160 countries have now ratified the treaty partially banning nuclear tests. Moreover, the United States and Russia have decided on a moratorium of their underground tests, prolonged until 1995. Great Britain is de facto applying the same rule. For France, President Francois Mitterrand stated that his country would also observe a moratorium for as long as he remained president. China may shortly go ahead with a test in the atmosphere. However, when deciding on its moratorium, the United States probably estimated that fresh Chinese tests would not undermine their technological progress, asserted Mr. Marin-Bosch.
