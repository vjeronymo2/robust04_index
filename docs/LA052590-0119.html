May 25, 1990, Friday, Home Edition

BOESKY TELLS OF ILLEGAL TIPS FROM MILKEN;

WALL STREET: THE FORMER INSIDE TRADER ALSO SAYS HE BROKE FEDERAL LAW BY PAYING FELLOW PRISON INMATES TO DO HIS LAUNDRY.

Ivan F. Boesky gave the first public evidence Thursday that former Drexel Burnham Lambert junk bond chief Michael Milken had illegally fed him inside information about stocks.

Asked by a defense lawyer in the trial of John A. Mulheren Jr. to name all of the people who had given him illegal inside tips, Boesky said "Mr. Milken comes to mind."

But Boesky, formerly one of Wall Street's leading speculators in takeover stocks, said he didn't know how much money he had made from the information Milken had passed to him. "I haven't made a compilation, sir," Boesky said.

Milken pleaded guilty to six felony counts last month and is awaiting sentencing. Several of the counts related to transactions with Boesky. None of them, however, had to do with inside trading, a point which Milken's lawyers and press spokesman strongly emphasized at the time. Milken's spokesman has long denied that Milken leaked inside information or traded on it himself, although he was accused of it in an indictment that has since been dropped.

Ken Lerer, Milken's spokesman, said Thursday that he wouldn't comment on Boesky's testimony.

Boesky, 53, was excused Thursday after testifying for two and a half days as the main prosecution witness in Mulheren's trial. Like Boesky, Mulheren was a stock arbitrager. Mulheren faces federal criminal charges of securities fraud and conspiracy related to his business dealings with Boesky.

Testimony about Milken was elicited by Mulheren's lawyer, Thomas Puccio, who attempted to demolish Boesky's credibility by showing that he had lied repeatedly under oath and engaged in extensive illegal activities. Boesky said Milken was the only individual he remembered leaking him inside information besides the two he had already named, former investment bankers Dennis Levine and Martin A. Siegel.

Throughout the grueling cross-examination, however, Boesky claimed that he couldn't remember names, details or specific amounts of money. He said he couldn't recall which companies Milken had leaked information about.

Boesky's nearly total inability to recall amounts of money at times appeared to stymie Puccio. The defense lawyer is attempting to show that Boesky has considerable personal wealth left despite the $100 million in penalties Boesky paid when he pleaded guilty to a single felony count. The defense theory is that Boesky's agreement with the government to turn in others enabled him to keep a big part of his wealth.

Boesky claimed that the huge volume of stock trading he did -- up to 300 million shares a year -- made it difficult for him to recall how much money he made on any deal. Asked if he had made $57 million in profit after receiving an inside tip about Getty Oil in the early 1980s, Boesky said he didn't remember.

"Isn't it true that if you made in the neighborhood of $57 million on a certain stock it would stick out in your mind?" Puccio asked.

"I don't think it would necessarily stick out in my memory," Boesky said.

Boesky also said he couldn't recall whether he had transferred assets to his wife and children when the government began investigating him in 1986 to reduce the amount of money the government could seize from him. "I do not recall my financial activities in 1986," he said.

But under close questioning, Boesky did recall that he was able to deduct from his 1986 taxes half of the $100-million penalty he paid.

Puccio said outside the courtroom that one possible reason for Boesky's apparent severe memory problems is his concern about the many civil lawsuits he faces. Boesky has testified that he faces more than $1 billion in civil claims filed by people and companies that contend they were defrauded by his illegal activities. Anything Boesky said on the witness stand in the Mulheren trial could be used against him in the civil lawsuits. A lawyer for Boesky failed to return a telephone call asking for comment Thursday.

Boesky also said he had broken federal law during the 19 months he had spent as an inmate at the minimum-security federal prison camp in Lompoc by paying fellow inmates to do his laundry. A spokesman for the federal Bureau of Prisons said in a phone interview that hiring fellow inmates would have violated prison rules, although the spokesman said he wasn't certain what law it might have violated.

Puccio also asked Boesky if he had bribed officials at the prison, including one of the wardens. Boesky strongly denied it.

While answering questions from reporters later, Puccio refused to say if he had any evidence that Boesky had committed bribery. The prisons spokesman said: "We have no knowledge of any bribes occurring in this facility with any staff member."

In court, Puccio had intimated that when Boesky was sentenced in 1987, he had given false information to the sentencing judge that the $100-million penalty would leave him nearly bankrupt. Puccio refused to say afterwards if he plans to introduce evidence later in the trial showing that Boesky had lied on that point.
