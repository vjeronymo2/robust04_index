BFN [Report signed "tom/APA": "Klestil: `Not a Moment of Lone Action'"] [Text] Vienna -- Regarding Austria's membership in the European Union [EU], the Austrian people will be exposed to a serious test of their identity. It will also show their contradictory character. On the one hand, the people are "European champions regarding patriotism." As many as 79 percent see themselves "solely as Austrians," no more than 17 percent "also as Europeans." On the other hand, only one-third of the citizens (two-thirds in Switzerland) believe that "their own political system is very good." One-third of the Austrians even find "their system" bad. This information was given by university professors Fritz Plasser and Ernst Bruckmueller at the beginning of a symposium of the Industrialists' Association (IV) in Vienna on "Austria's Identity in a Larger Europe." The consequences are obvious for political scientists and historians. They say that there is remarkable "inner resistance" against the EU and against what is new.

Excessive Burden
The European integration process calls the image Austrians have of themselves (culture, great scenery, but not quite the managerial skills of the Germans) into question and will lead to "an excessive burden on decisionmaking." Against this background, outstanding individuals made statements in the discussion -- Cardinal Franz Koenig, Theo Sommer, publisher of DIE ZEIT and a former planning chief in the Bonn Defense Ministry under chancellor Willy Brandt, the historian Brigitte Hamann, director Helmut Felderer of the Institute for Higher Studies, and Polish Ambassador Bartoszewski. President Thomas Klestil made his first comprehensive statement prior to the referendum and called upon Austrians to demonstrate solidarity. He said that this was "not the time of pursuing party politics or acquiring personal status at the expense of European policy." Nor was it "a test run" for parliamentary elections or a "reminder regarding problems that are not at issue." The president said he wished to see "all those" gather "who are convinced of the urgency of a larger, common Europe" and form a "group of responsible and farsighted Austrians." In the postwar era, the Austrians in particular had proved to themselves and to the world that unity is strength. This had been demonstrated most recently in Brussels when the agreement was concluded.
Developing Tolerance
Klestil said the Austrians did not have to be afraid of Europe, because "no other membership candidate has ever been better prepared than we." It should also be stressed that "we will not lose our identity." The "really great task" in the EU would be that Austria "will bring in the values of tolerance and the many colors of a multicultural society." Industry Association secretary general Franz Ceska warned about "a strange mixture" of "fearfulness and pompousness." People should not let themselves be influenced by a feeling of uncertainty. However, EU membership was "indispensable" for the country's industry, he said. Johannes Voggenhuber, spokesman for the Greens on EU affairs, on 30 May criticized the government once again for "making propaganda instead of providing real information." He said that copies of the membership treaties for the citizens were out of print. Freedom Party secretary general Karl-Heinz Grasser spoke about attempts at blackmailing the citizens. "Regarding democracy," EU membership would be "a relapse into the 19th century."
