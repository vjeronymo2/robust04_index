BFN [Text] Budapest, 3 May (MTI) -- The first freely elected Hungarian parliament in forty years, convened on May 2, 1990, ended its work on April 7, 1994, after 378 days of sessions. In this time, deputies passed 432 laws that created and expanded the framework of a democratic law-governed state, continued the creation of a social market economy, and accelerated the emergence of capitalist property relations. One of the most important laws passed, regulated on compensation, as a result of which 670,000 people acquired about one-third of the arable land, and two-fifths of the forests. The minority law ensures extensive personal, cultural, and administrative autonomy to the ethnic and national minorities that account for about one-tenth of the Hungarian population. Under the 1989 electoral law approved in the former system, 176 of the 386 deputies in the single-chamber Parliament were elected from individual constituencies, 152 from regional lists, and 58 from national lists. An electoral majority made it possible for the conservative, Christian parties to form a government. The ruling center-right coalition, first led by Prime Minister Jozsef Antall, then, following his death in December 1993, by Prime Minister Peter Boross, initially consisted of 165 deputies of the Hungarian Democratic Forum (MDF), 44 deputies of the Independent Smallholders+ Party (FKGP), and 21 deputies of the Christian Democratic People"s Party (KDNP). The opposition parties were the liberal Alliance of Free Democrats (SZDSZ) with 94 deputies, the liberal Federation of Young Democrats (FIDESZ) with 22 deputies, and the Hungarian Socialist Party with 33 deputies. Apart from these six parties, seven independent deputies also won seats. (The proportions changed significantly over the four years, the government majority was reduced to a minimum, the number of independent deputies rose fourfold, and new party groups were set up.) There were very few professional politicians among the 358 men and 28 women deputies, but very many with higher qualifications: 244 deputies had had professional careers, 80 scientific careers, 20 were entrepreneurs or farmers, and only 10 were from the former political elite. As regards their former occupations, 33 deputies were researchers, 27 lawyers, 22 hospital doctors, 21 company managers, 20 journalists, 17 university professors or secondary school teachers. In order to increase government stability, the two strongest parties agreed with each other at the end of April, 1990, on the principles for a partial amendment of the Constitution, which was approved by deputies. The parts of the amendemnt which affected Parliament were the election of the President by deputies (Arpad Goncz has been President of the Republic since 1990), and that the scope of authority of the president extends, under certain conditions, to the dissolution of Parliament. Parliament elects the prime minister, the president appoints the ministers on the proposal of the prime minister. An absolute majority (50 percent of deputies plus one vote) is needed to elect a prime minister, or for a no- confidence vote against him. A constructive no-confidence motion (supported by one-fifth of the deputies, also having to include the name of the new prime ministerial candidate) can be submitted only against the prime minister. The legal measure in force lists 20 basic institutions and basic rights for which qualified, two-thirds majority is needed (that is votes from two-thirds of the deputies) to amend them. These include changes to the Constitution, making peace agreements, and declaring war or states of emergency. These created the unique features of Hungarian parliamentarism, which shifted the focus of legislation from the English model to the German "chancellorship" model. With the two-thirds majority laws, the Constitution also ensured legislative rights to the opposition. Apart from the general principle of government accountability, parliamentary control also includes a reporting obligation for the government and certain of its ministers. However, with the introduction of the conservative no-confidenc vote, the government can be overthrowin only through the person of the prime minister. A characteristic element in preparation of laws is that the role of parliamentary groups is greater than that of parliamentary committees. However, the House rules provide extensive opportunities for control by institutionalizing questions to the prime minister and ministers, political debates, contributions before the session, and submitting amendment proposals.
