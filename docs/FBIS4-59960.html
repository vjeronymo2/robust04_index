BFN [Commentary by All India Radio special correspondent Bhupal Inder Saini] [Text] The prime minister is today launching upon two days of intensive interaction with decision makers in the United States -- both in the political and economic spheres. Mr. Narasimha Rao will first of all address the joint meeting of both houses of the U.S. Congress today. He will be the first prime minister of any country to get the opportunity since Mr. Clinton took over as U.S. president. Mr. Narasimha Rao is expected to utilize it to spell out India's views on the emerging world scenario -- both in the political and economic fields as well as regional and bilateral issues. Significantly, the prime minister's address to the Congress became possible after several American senators as well members of the House of Representatives expressed their wish to hear in person the prime minister of the largest democracy in the world. It was not on the original schedule earlier. Mr. Narasimha Rao will have his first meeting with President Clinton tomorrow, when they have talks at the White House. After having lunch together, the two leaders will address a joint news conference. An atmosphere conducive to an open and frank Indo-U.S. dialogue has been created with both sides making clear their views on major world and regional issues. There is a large area of agreement though there are differences in perspective on some issues. Both sides recognized that there is need to make the world a secure place free from a threat of major conflagration. There is need for disarmament, especially in the nuclear field. India has made it clear that it is prepared to discuss the question of disarmament, including nuclear nonproliferation, but real world security can be established in a comprehensive nondiscriminatory framework only. The Nuclear Nonproliferation Treaty -- NPT -- is unacceptable to India in its present form as it is discriminatory and seeks to divide the world permanently into two categories -- the nuclear haves and havenots. The Clinton Administration, which was earlier pressing for a regional NPT for South Asia has now shown understanding of India's viewpoint that such an arrangement cannot work. Nuclear weapons cannot be banished from the region just by two countries signing the NPT or a regional variation. Another major focus of Mr. Narasimha Rao's visit is on strengthening Indo-American business ties. A large number of top Indian industrialists and business people are now in Washington having talks with their American counterparts on joint ventures and tie-ups. The finance minister, Dr. Manmohan Singh, is also in the American capital to clear any doubts about the far-reaching character of India's economic reforms. The United States is already India's largest trading partner. But a full potential for greater economic cooperation is yet to be tapped. Hopefully, the interaction during Mr. Narasimha Rao's visit will lead to increased trade and economic cooperation.
