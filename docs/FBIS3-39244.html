Language: Spanish Article Type:BFN [Luis R. Aizpeolea report: "Autonomy Front Reopens"] [Text] The autonomy front is reopening. On the eve of the meeting Felipe Gonzalez and Generalitat [Basque autonomous government] Premier Jordi Pujol will be holding in order to examine the turnabout in autonomy demanded by Catalonia, other "fast track" autonomous communities have reacted by demanding a new boost to autonomy. This is the case with the governments of Navarre, Andalusia, and Galicia, which are demanding new responsibilities, in some cases going beyond the autonomy statutes themselves. The finishing touch to this scene will be provided by Galician Xunta [autonomous government] Premier Manuel Fraga, who will meet with Gonzalez Thursday [3 February] in order to present to him his single administration plan. The situation worries the government, which lacks a clear stance on the new autonomy avalanche. The announced meeting between Gonzalez and Pujol to discuss the turnabout in autonomy in Catalonia and the central government's decision to transfer a package of 20 areas of responsibility to the Basque government has encouraged other "fast track" autonomous communities to demand another boost. Last week, the government of Navarre, whose premier is Juan Cruz Alli of the Navarrese People's Union (UPN), sent the government a document in which it proposed the reopening of bilateral negotiations to give its community new responsibilities. Navarre's list of demands recalls the most substantial part of the package of responsibilities demanded by the Basque government. The transfers demanded by Alli include official credit, the Social Security funding system, the National Employment Institute, and the means of the administration of justice. A spokesman for the Navarre government told this newspaper that the responsibilities demanded "are contained in the improvement of the fuero [regional law code]." The letter sent by the Navarrese government coincided with the visit to Felipe Gonzalez by Socialist Manuel Chaves, the premier of the Andalusian junta. The autonomy responsibilities demanded by Chaves, who is pressured by imminent elections, recall some of the proposals that Pujol announced he will submit to Gonzalez at their next meeting. They include public health funding, the coastline, and the reform of the state's peripheral administration. But the finishing touch is provided this week by Galician Xunta Premier Manuel Fraga, with his meeting with Gonzalez next Thursday. Although the meeting's purpose is the European surcharge on the milk quota, Fraga will explain to Gonzalez his single administration proposal. According to a Xunta spokesman, the single administration plan -- a drastic reduction of the state's peripheral administration, with the abolition of civil governors and the concentration of power in the autonomous governments -- "is virtually complete." To this move that has emerged among the "fast track" autonomous communities must be added the move by a "slow track" community such as Aragon, whose premier, Socialist Jose Marco, has announced the completion of the reform of the autonomy statute for April. The new situation that has been created worries the government, which, two years after the signing of the autonomy pacts -- which place the "slow track" communities almost on a par with the "fast track" communities and whose process of transfers is taking place now -- is seeing the autonomy process being reopened by the "fast track" autonomous communities without there being a clear stance laid down by the government. Over the past few years, the government has expressed its concern at the "fast track" communities' persistence in making a normal procedure out of an exceptional procedure, such as delegating exclusive state responsibilities to the autonomous communities contained in Article 150.2 of the Constitution. The current demands of the Basque, Navarrese, and Galician governments have recourse to this procedure, which in the opinion of government sources goes beyond the autonomy statutes. Last week, Socialist Manuel Chaves made some points after meeting with Gonzalez. He condemned effecting the autonomy process by boosting one or several communities, and demanded a "harmonious and balanced process" involving all of them. He advocates setting up a conference of the 17 autonomous government premiers as a way to organize the autonomy process, a proposal which has been repeatedly delayed by the premiers of Catalonia and the Basque Country, who are in favor of a bilateral relationship with the central government. The government has not yet commented on the new boost for autonomy. A government spokesman transferred the responsibility for the debate to the Spanish Socialist Workers Party Congress in mid-March. On that occasion, a report from the Basque Socialists suggesting an individual autonomy strategy with respect to the nationalist parties and proposing making progress in the complicated problem being created between each autonomous community's singularity and the balance between all of them will be debated.
