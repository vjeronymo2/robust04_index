May 14, 1990, Monday, P.M. Final

PEOPLE'S PHARMACY: FLUORIDE HELD UNLIKELY TO INCREASE BREAST CANCER RISK

Question: Seven years ago at menopause, I was told by my doctor to start taking Florical (calcium carbonate and sodium fluoride). It is supposed to keep my bones from getting brittle and to keep me from getting stooped over.

Now I have read that fluoride might cause cancer. Two of my sisters have had breast cancer -- my twin died at 35, and my younger sister had her breast removed 11 years ago.

I am worried about doing anything that would increase my risk of cancer. Is the Florical a problem?

Answer: Fluoride has been in the news recently because a study for the National Toxicology Program turned up a rare form of bone cancer in some of the rats getting high doses of fluoride in their water. Scientists are uncertain how this may apply to humans, so further studies are in order.

It is extremely unlikely that the fluoride you are taking would increase your risk of breast cancer, but there is little likelihood it is protecting your bones from osteoporosis. New research indicates that fluoride treatments do not prevent fractures in post menopausal women.

There is, however, an experimental treatment for osteoporosis that looks very promising. A Danish study recently found that women taking Didronel (etidronate) had stronger bones and fewer fractures than those on a placebo. Perhaps it is time to discuss your osteoporosis prevention program with your physician.

Q. My grandson, age 10, has frequent urinary infections. My daughter would like to able to check his urine when she suspects there is a problem.

I remember reading in your column about a test that can be done at home. My pharmacist didn't know anything about it. What is the name of the product, and will she need a prescription?

A. Microstix-Nitrite Reagent Strips or Biotel u.t.i. home screening tests for urinary tract infections are available without prescription. These little strips change color when dipped in a urine sample. Such home tests can be helpful in alerting a parent to the need for treatment.

Q. I've been taking Mevacor to lower cholesterol for two years. Not long ago I read that after 15 months on Mevacor, a person should have a liver biopsy. When I asked my doctor about this, he said there was no need for it. He does a blood test on me twice a year.

Now I am afraid of this drug. Do I need to have my liver tested or is my doctor right?

A. The risk of liver damage from Mevacor (lovastatin) is low. Nevertheless, blood tests (not a biopsy) should be performed every four to six weeks during the first year and a half of treatment.
