Language: Spanish Article Type:BFN [Report by Javier Ignacio Mayorca] [Text] During a news conference in the presence of the 100 members of the Inter-American Defense College's [CID] Class 33 currently visiting Venezuela, Brigadier General Alvaro Rafael Barboza Rodriguez, Joint Staff operations director, reported that between 50 and 60 tons of various drugs circulate through Venezuela annually. The CID director and U.S. Army Major General James Harding said that the students come from 14 countries on the continent. CID courses last 10 months, beginning in July. "This is an opportunity to exchange views and get to know these countries a little better. It is a very simple project," he said. In halting Spanish, Maj. Harding said that according to data supplied by CID and the Inter-American Defense Board, which he leads also, terrorism and drug trafficking are the major threats to the continent's security. "We should strive jointly to find a solution to the drug trafficking problem, which goes beyond borders and helps the entire world. The cooperation of all governments is required," he said. In the morning Harding paid a courtesy call to Defense Minister General Rafael Montero Revette. The CID members will fly to Colombia on Thursday. Gen. Barboza, former commander of the Venezuela unit which operated in Honduras and Nicaragua on pacification duties, was selected by the Defense Ministry to make a general assessment of the "military situation" in Venezuela. He described as a threat the use of Venezuela as "a springboard for the traffic of drugs to Central America and their final destination, the United States." An estimate of the quantity of drugs circulating through Venezuela was made by the National Guard. "Only 10 percent of this quantity is seized. This gives an idea of the seriousness of the problem," he said. He also reported on the discovery of marijuana and coca plantations in the Perija Mountains and areas close to the Colombian border. Money laundering "has been detected essentially in four areas: Tachira; Zulia, near Maicao; Caracas, through real estate operations; and the country's eastern region," he said. He recalled that the most successful years in the counternarcotics struggle were 1991 and 1992, when 11.981 tons and 12.681 tons, respectively, were confiscated. In 1993 the drug seizure level again was close to 3 tons, a figure similar to that of 1990. Cocaine is the most often confiscated drug. Next comes marijuana, 400 kg of which are seized annually. "The fact that heroin is entering the local market again is worrisome," he said. The UN believes this is a worldwide problem. The number of involved countries has risen from 64 in 1990 to 138 in the first half of 1993. Gen. Barboza announced that this year the Armed Forces will put two flight control radar sites into operation. One of them will be located in Falcon State and cover the country's central and western region. The other, located in Nueva Esparta, will serve to monitor flights between the country's central and eastern regions. These sites are being installed through agreements with U.S. counternarcotics agencies, such as DEA, CIA, and others. "These sites will be operated jointly with our Armed Forces," he said. This illegal activity can be monitored more effectively by observing the flight patterns of the drug traffickers' planes, that is to say, speed, altitude, time, and routes, as well as the types of planes used. A special overflight program, originating in Panama and reaching Peru and Bolivia with observers aboard, is being set up. Special surveillance operations can be conducted at the request of the Venezuelan Government or the U.S. Southern Command.
