May 7, 1989, Sunday, Home Edition

TRAVELER'S DIARY: INTRIGUING RUINS AT COBA AND TULUM

Today we breached the jungle at the "Queen City of the Yucatan," hub of a civilization that lasted for centuries.

First explorations of the area were made in the late 1920s and early 1930s, but this once-vast city, most of whose estimated 6,500 structures remain hidden beneath a tangle of rain forest, did not really begin to be uncovered until 1972.

Maryknoll sisters arranged medicine and food drops until a landing strip was peeled away. Now, there is a road, and travelers who want a break from Cancun, roughly 100 miles away, can rumble in by bus.

Among the other discoveries they will make at the end of a longish tramp along jungle trails is the Nohoch Mul pyramid, more than 120 feet tall.

It takes 120 aching steps to reach the top. But from that lofty perch one can survey pyramid mounds that rise above the plain and are especially visible when sunrise or sunset casts shadows over the endless carpet of green.

Just to the left of Nohoch Mul is a 400-foot square leveled terrace bearing evidence of houses that once stood on what the archeologists call "The Great Platform."

Near the plaza in front of the pyramid is a stele, or tablet, depicting a Mayan ruler standing atop two kneeling slaves, with two other slaves kneeling nearby.

The Mayan culture endures, and candlesticks, money and flowers are often placed at the foot of monuments. Some of the stelae, covered now by straw canopies, are 10 to 15 feet tall.

Conveniently -- albeit almost incongruously -- a Club Med has risen at Coba.

A two-story inn encircles a swimming pool and overlooks a thatched-roof pavilion. It offers air-conditioned rooms at $42 a night double occupancy, with breakfast an additional $5.

Coba lies at the hub of several dozen ancient roads, one of them stretching almost 60 miles. These highways, both ceremonial and utilitarian, once formed the largest network in North America.

A new 30-mile stretch from the inland jungle leads to the walled seaside city of Tulum.

Our guide, Raul, insisted that Tulum does not mean "wall" as some believe, but rather "evil-smelling ground," a sobriquet it earned because of its location above and around mangrove swamps.

He said the city was also called Zama, meaning dawn, and that, too, makes sense because from Tulum's clifftop perch one can see the sun rising out of the Caribbean.

Tulum, open and visible, with no hint of jungle to impair the view of its ruins, belongs to a period, perhaps AD 700 to 1000, when the Mayan civilization was already in decline. The earliest-known stela here is said to date from AD 564, but there is some argument on this point; some insist that the tablet was carved elsewhere and transported here.

There is little argument that the city-fort was constructed three centuries before the Spaniards first came ashore in 1518. The Spanish, viewing the city, called its most imposing structure El Castillo, the castle.

Buses and rental cars are available daily in Cancun for the visit to Tulum with its myriad souvenir shacks.

Now, with the 30-mile spur road to Coba and the promise of its little inn, Tulum and Coba make for an unforgettable if somewhat exhausting trip.
