February 5, 1989, Sunday, Home Edition

WALL STREET / JOHN CRUDELE: TIPS TO AVOID LURE OF CLEVER PONZI SCAMS

Suppose a stranger called you on the telephone and suggested that you invest in a company that refined sand from a Costa Rican beach into gold.

Would you give him money? Probably not.

Well, what if an executive from an oil and natural gas exploration company that you've never heard of asked you to hand over some of your hard-earned money so his company could drill for oil in the Appalachian Mountains?

This executive proves to you that he's found oil once already -- and he really has -- and that his first group of investors made out pretty well.

This one you might go for.

Both were scams -- the first a pretty transparent one; the second ended up as a cleverly disguised Ponzi scheme in which a couple of groups of investors got what looked like a return but really all they were getting was some of the money being invested by subsequent generations of investors.

Investors lost a lot of money before the government shut the scams down.

In fact, regulators have been shutting down lots of so-called penny stock and commodity scams lately. And just last week the Justice Department and other government regulators stepped up their vigilance by establishing a task force in six major U.S. cities to combat fraud.

The only problem, experts say, is that unsavory characters, including organized crime figures (see below), seem to be creating new investment scams faster than the old ones can be eradicated.

Since regulators seem to be making only a little headway on the supply side of the fraud equation, experts say that controlling the demand for these shady investments seems to be the best way to reverse the trend.

This won't be easy, of course, since a lot of investors get caught up in scams because they're greedy. "There's very little a consumer can do except stay away," says Philip Feigin, Colorado's securities commissioner.

Investors can also watch for certain clues that a scam is in the works. Here are a dozen tips from Joseph Goldstein, associate director of the Securities and Exchange Commission's enforcement division (who has even received calls from probable scam artists while at work), and Sarah Ackerson, assistant director of market regulations, that might help even the greediest investor avoid getting scammed.

* Rule 1: Be wary of any investment promise that seems too good. And if the "broker" guarantees that an investment will yield a certain amount, ask him to put it in writing. Chances are, says Goldstein, that a crooked broker will balk.

* Rule 2: Don't be lured into buying penny stocks just because you think it would be cool to own 10,000 shares of anything.

* Rule 3: Don't trust any broker you don't know. Chances are he got your name off a list of several hundred potential investors that he's calling that day. There's a second thing to be wary of here, says Ackerson. Some unscrupulous brokers will try to develop a rapport with an investor by calling him several times over a short period of time. But he won't try to sell you anything until the third phone call or so.

* Rule 4: If you don't understand an investment, don't invest in it.

* Rule 5: Be wary of out-of-town calls. Why would a broker in Florida be calling your home in Kansas City? Some regulators say that if you can't walk across the street and shake the broker's hand, why would you trust him with your money? (A lot of scam artists work out of Florida, according to the SEC. Since they don't want to meet their prospective customers anyway, the con men figure they might as well live someplace warm.)

* Rule 6: Before writing that check, investors should investigate the brokerage firm's history and get a copy of its most recent financial statement. Another warning: Some scam perpetrators will acquire a legitimate brokerage firm and assume its identify, so even this precaution isn't foolproof.

* Rule 7: If a broker tries to sell you stock in a company whose price you can't follow in any of the usual places, be careful. Some legitimate over-the-counter stocks are listed on what's called the "pink sheets." But if you can't find price quotes on a stock (except through the broker who sold you the security), it could mean that the stock and the company don't really exist.

* Rule 8: Ask a regular brokerage like Merrill Lynch whether it would be able to sell the stock you're thinking of buying. If a regular brokerage firm can't execute a sell order, you might be stuck with the stock for a long time.

* Rule 9: If a broker you don't know says you have to buy a stock quickly because something good is going to happen soon, hang up. The typical trick, says the SEC, is to tell an investor that another customer just canceled a buy order for some stock that has already gone up in price. The broker then tells an investor that he can have part of the trade that the other investor canceled, but only if he moves quickly. "The urgency of the situation is another red flag," Goldstein says.

* Rule 10: If a broker you are not familiar with tells you he has some hot information about a company -- for instance, that it is about to be acquired -- chances are you're being set up, Ackerson says.

* Rule 11: Check out the brokerage representative that calls you to see if he has a record of securities violations. Most states keep tabs on this sort of thing, as does the SEC. The North American Securities Administrators Assn. in Washington acts as a liaison with state regulators. Another warning: Most scam artists are now hiring salespeople right out of college, so the person who called you last night might not have a record yet.

* Rule 12: Remember that brokers from these scam firms use scripts. So just because the guy can answer all your questions without stumbling doesn't mean that he's on the up and up. "They will put you at ease," Goldstein says.

Nobody knows for sure how many stock scams are going on at this time, and even the experts won't venture a guess as to how much money is taken from defrauded investors around the country each year. But experts sense that scams are growing in both number and complexity.

"The criminal element has become more sophisticated," says John Arterberry, who works on stock frauds at the Justice Department. "They take some of the old schemes and dress them up."

Consider this: Last summer, regulators in Florida alone inspected 17 brokerages and found serious violations at 15.

Charles Harper, associate regional administrator at the SEC's Miami office, says the successful penny stock salesperson can earn between $20,000 and $30,000 a month. An office manager for an unscrupulous brokerage can rake in over $2 million a year.

The salespeople work hard, probably each making hundreds of calls a day. One questionable brokerage operation working out of a motel in Jacksonville, Fla., was generating 900 phone calls a day. "They sell nationally," Harper says, "but a lot of it is sold in Florida because there is a lot of wealth in this state." To be a success, a salesperson probably only has to get five or so new customers a day.

Not all crooked stock dealers live in Florida. Feigin, the securities commissioner in Colorado, says his office's case load has increased 575% since 1982.
