October 29, 1989, Sunday, Home Edition

THE GODFATHER'S SON

Having opposed Brooklier in a loan-sharking case some years ago while I was a special attorney with the Organized Crime Strike Force, I have no quarrel with those who are impressed with the trial skills of the son of L.A.'s former don. It is the aura of glamour (or is it a halo?) that Murphy constructs around Brooklier that does a disservice to those who accept the burden of immigrant status without resorting to illegal activities to make their way. That Tony Brooklier's father may have been faced with the difficult and soul-searching choices of immigrants the world over does not excuse, or sanction, an end run around those barriers by the use of extortion and racketeering. I object to the rendering of criminal figures in sympathetic terms by the sleight of hand of humanizing the experience of someone like Tony Brooklier. I recall, with as equal and untainted an affection as Tony Brooklier has for his father, my grandfather pressing pants for 50 years in the poor but proud sections of Brooklyn for the munificent sum of $65 per week, 25 cents of which was usually shared with the grandchildren. A tattered picture of my grandfather with a sign reading "Please to tell your friends" in his tailor shop as he sought to scratch out a living on the Lower East Side trumpets more courage than an army of racketeers and hoodlums.

GEORGE KARL ROSENSTOCK

Los Angeles
