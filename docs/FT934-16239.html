931007 FT 07 OCT 93 / SIB faults North of England over selling of bonds THE Securities and Investments Board, the City's chief regulatory watchdog, has publicly reprimanded the North of England Building Society for failing to ensure that its independent financial advisers sold suitable products to clients. The rebuke, delivered yesterday, was the first such reprimand by the SIB against one of the 77 firms it regulates directly. The North of England said that, as a result, it may have to pay compensation to up to half the 2,000 customers it has advised since 1987, at a cost of roughly Pounds 500,000. The SIB's charges against North of England, the UK's 20th largest building society, deal mainly with its sale of so-called single-premium bonds and its advice to people to transfer from their existing pension arrangements. Single-premium bond investors pay a lump sum for a fixed term and earn higher returns. But cashing in single-premium bonds before their maturity date leads to a penalty. Mr David Hodgson, the society's secretary and solicitor, said that although there were inspection visits in earlier years, they had lasted no longer than one day. But last September, inspectors announced they intended to conduct a much more intensive review of records. That review, which focused on 600 client files, turned up enough instances of mis-selling to cause the review to be broadened to the society's entire 2,000 clients. 'We could be looking at remedial action on 1,000 cases,' he said. North of England ceased giving independent advice last Friday and is now acting as a 'tied agent' selling the products of General Accident exclusively. The SIB does not have the authority to set fines for firms which break its rules, unlike the self-regulatory organisations (SROs) which oversee the bulk of the financial services industry. Firms which do not wish to join any existing SRO have had the option of seeking direct supervision by the SIB. The SIB said that while it has privately disciplined firms before, this is the first case where it believed public action was warranted. Firms in the retail financial services industry have complained that direct regulation by SIB, chosen mostly by banks and building societies, is a 'soft option' because it is less rigorous than action by SROs. Mr Andrew Large, SIB's chairman, has said he wishes to withdraw from direct regulation completely and has urged the creation of a new self-regulatory body, the Personal Investment Authority, which will include many firms now supervised by SIB.
