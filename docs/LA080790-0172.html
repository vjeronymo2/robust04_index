August 7, 1990, Tuesday, P.M. Final

LIZ SMITH: WHITNEY HOUSTON ON THE ROAD TO HOLLYWOOD

WHITNEY HOUSTON, that young, gorgeous pop diva, has finally found a movie script that she is eager to do. Whitney turned down dozens in the past few years. She has now said yes to Tri-Star's screen version of the Terry McMillan novel "Disappearing Acts," from Pocket Books, a contemporary love story between a singer and a construction worker. It's certainly about time we had another major black female movie star.

FLASH! Tom Hanks and Rita Wilson welcomed a 6-pound boy last Saturday in Los Angeles. Tom has two children from a previous marriage.

FEAST or famine: That's the actor's life most of the time. Well, Kevin Conway, who made such an impression in the off-Broadway hit "Other People's Money," is truly feasting. Conway opens this week in the Ted Whitehouse play, "The Man Who Fell In Love With His Wife," at the Quaigh Theater in New York. His real-life wife, Mila Burnette, co-stars.

The actor will also make his debut as a movie director when his film "The Sun and the Moon" opens at Joe Papp's Festival Latino in New York later this month.

Conway has also just been signed by Disney for a two-picture deal, the first of which, "One Good Cop," starring Michael Keaton, starts shooting in New York on Aug. 27.

SEVERAL MONTHS ago, when that "Designing Woman" Dixie Carter was setting New York on its ear with her cabaret act, she and I spoke of various things, among them, her hit CBS series and her wonderful co-stars, including the then-beleaguered Delta Burke. Dixie was affectionate and enthusiastic about everyone and everything on "Designing Women," including Delta, whose talent and beauty she praised. She was particularly incensed about the tabloid coverage of Delta's much-publicized battle with her weight.

Now Delta has been nominated for an Emmy for the episode written especially for her that deals with society's attitude toward the overweight -- particularly if that overweight person happens to be a beautiful woman.

Unfortunately, on the heels of this triumph, Delta Burke has become embroiled in a war of words with producer Linda Bloodworth-Thomason. And from the bitter comments being flung about, it may be that Delta won't return to "Designing Women" as the femme fatale Suzanne Sugarbaker.

As a fan of all the "DW's," I certainly hope this does NOT happen! "Designing Women" could survive without Delta, but her presence would be sorely missed, and, realistically, could Delta ever find such a marvelous role in any other series -- a character written expressly for her, so accommodating to both her talents and her real-life personality and problems?

Let's hope cooler heads prevail.
