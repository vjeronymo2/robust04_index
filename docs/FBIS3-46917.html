Cabinet Support Rate Remains High
The Hosokawa cabinet still maintains a high level of public support four months after its inauguration. Under the LDP ruling, the cabinet support rate moved in parallel with the LDP support rate. However, under the Hosokawa administration, the popularity of the JNP and the coalition parties is in decline while the administration itself receives broad support. In other words, high public support for the cabinet is due to Hosokawa's personal popularity. The LDP support rate remains at 22 percent, or the lowest level since the beginning of the survey. Only farmers, fishermen, and those over 60 years in age offered support of over 30 percent. Support for LDP President Yohei Kono is also dropping. ------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |Table 5. If things are left as they are, the Hosokawa administration may lose its popularity. This reminds me of former U.S. President George Bush's failure to win reelection. Political Reform and the Hosokawa Cabinet's Responsibility
During a news conference immediately after his inauguration, Prime Minister Hosokawa said: "I will take responsibility if I fail to enact the political reform bills during the ongoing Diet session." But the voters are not strict about Hosokawa's responsibility for the political reform issue. In our survey, only 21 percent said "the cabinet should resign en masse to take responsibility." On the other hand, 26 percent said "the cabinet should dissolve the lower house, and 38 percent said "neither the cabinet's resignation nor the lower house's dissolution is needed." ------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |Table 7. Voters' Favorite Parties
Following last month, the JNP again defended its number two position, beating the SDPJ. Until January 1992, more than 50 percent of the voters said they would vote for the LDP, but the rate declined by half. NOTE: The survey, conducted by Chuo Research Inc. on 11-14 November, was carried on as individual interviews of 2,000 eligible voters picked at random. Responses were obtained from 70.5 percent of, or 1,410, pollees. Data belong to the Multipurpose Statistic Databank of Tsukuba University.
