September 1, 1990, Saturday, Home Edition

CONTROVERSIAL BEHAVIORIST B.F. SKINNER

The Times' obituary of Skinner (Aug. 20) was remarkably well-done. It provided a good sense of the life of this dramatically influential psychologist.

There was neither confusion of the baby tender with the Skinner box nor of Palov's classical conditioning with operant conditioning, a distinction that eluded a national news magazine and a scientific journal.

It tells us something about ourselves that even such fine journalism is no protection against a major distortion of one of Skinner's fundamental beliefs: that behavior uncaused by heredity and environment, sometimes called free will, is a fuzzy mirage. The article informs us that Skinner "argued to the end . . . (that) there is little or no free will." "Argued to the end" is a phrase you'd expect to find applied to an earlier century to an atheist who died without the expected death-bed recantation. In fact, Skinner explained, as have many before him, that so-called free will is a misconception. Therefore, he could not believe that people might have just a little of it, except in the sense that they might have just a little of the misconception.

Skinner did not propose "forsaking individual freedom . . . in favor of controlling human behavior to achieve a world free of war," etc. There is nothing to forsake but a muddled concept, which is held to because it is confused with a meaningful freedom: the freedom to do what one wishes uncoerced by others. A clearer understanding will reduce resistance to needed social engineering and also sensitize us to the dangers of being subtly manipulated to our disadvantage by those using rewards rather than threats.

RICHARD O'CONNELL

Professor of Psychology

Cal State Northridge
