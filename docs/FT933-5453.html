930903 FT 03 SEP 93 / News Corp versus The Rest Media organisations' reaction to Rupert Murdoch's announcement on Wednesday of BSkyB's expansion, which involves subscribing to a package of up to 18 satellite TV channels: The Sun proclaimed on its front page, 'SKY ON A HIGH'. An inside report, headlined 'Skies of Freedom', quoted without additional comment from Mr Murdoch's speech. Its choice quote was: 'Today is the first day of a new age in satellite television.' Today had a small piece on page nine with the headline 'New Age for TV viewing'. It reported: 'He threw down the gauntlet to TV chiefs claiming that 10m UK homes will have satellite TV by the year 2000 . . . insiders believe advertisers could transfer up to Pounds 50m of business.' The Times on its front page referred to Mr Murdoch's 'vision of a world in which national and class barriers are broken down by technological advances . . .' A second report said: 'At the heart of both the BT agreement and a batch of others revealed in Mr Murdoch's Banqueting Hall speech last night is the development of what President Clinton has called the 'digital super highway of the future'. On Sky News, a Murdoch TV channel, reporter Alex Crawford said: 'This is what the industry's been holding its breath for. Rupert Murdoch plans to shake up the industry like never before . . . The audience was taken through the whole gamut of his dreams.' In the ninth item on BBC's Nine O Clock News reporter Nick Higham spoke of 'Rupert Murdoch's ambitious plans to develop his global media empire that now spans four continents . . . In Britain Mr Murdoch means to stay at the forefront of satellite television even though he says that media moguls like himself have very little real power.' The Daily Telegraph said: 'Yesterday's floorshow - with cowgirls, celebrity lookalikes and a worrying live boa constrictor - was for the benefit of journalists and advertisers.'
