February 23, 1989, Thursday, Home Edition

THE BUTCHER: SHOULDER CUT TRIMS BILL AT MEAT COUNTER

If you want to save money at the meat counter, you must get to know "chuck." Chuck is not the name of any particular friendly and generous butcher. It is rather the name of a large part of a beef carcass. For some reason unknown to me, the front shoulder of the beef animal is called the chuck. Every other animal in the world has shoulders -- lambs have shoulders, veal have shoulders, pigs have shoulders. Beef have, instead, chucks.

The chuck makes up about 26% of the total carcass weight and, for the most part, has been considered a less-tender part of the animal that generally requires long-slow-moist-heat cooking. That there is a lot of it and that it is generally considered tough and less desirable have made the chuck a problem for the beef industry for years.

Butchers have traditionally had to discount the chuck in order to move it. That has made it a good value for consumers throughout the years. If you have, as grandmother seemed to, plenty of time to make stews and pot roasts using long-slow-moist-heat cooking, the chuck is a cut that can save plenty of money on meals.

Well-Rounded Chuck

Nowadays, according to a recent study conducted at South Dakota State University, you might not even need much time to save a lot of money with chuck. Some chuck muscles from today's leaner younger beef cattle, it was found, are tender enough to do all kinds of things with besides pot roast.

The research was funded by the Beef Industry Council, the South Dakota Beef Council and the California Beef Council for the purpose of discovering if the chuck might not have potentially greater value if sold as individual cuts rather than as one cut with several muscles. The result of the study was reported in the Journal of Food Science.

Scientists studied the eating characteristics of 34 muscles/muscle groups within the forequarters of 16 Angus steers of similar genetic and nutritional background. They determined the fat and lean content of each, as well as the chemical characteristics of the connective tissue, the component largely responsible for the chuck being less tender.

What's in It for You?

What the research indicated was that if the beef industry could develop fabrication and processing techniques to allow easy and cost-effective separation of the muscles of the chuck, its value could be significantly increased.

So what does this mean to you? Two things. First, you can be sure that the beef industry is going to be working overtime to find ways of taking the chuck apart so the parts can be sold to you for more money. And second, if you're willing to do a little cutting-up in the kitchen, you can take a chuck apart yourself and do all kinds of wonderful things with the meat. You don't have to be a scientist -- or even a butcher -- to take a chuck apart, and the results can be surprisingly rewarding both in terms of good food and money saved.

After reading about the South Dakota study, I decided to do my own. I went to the market and picked up a three-pound piece of boneless chuck roast. I would like to have found one with the bone in, they are always a better buy, but those are getting harder and harder to find. They must have found some way to cross those critters with jellyfish; they don't have any bones anymore.

The Lean Is Left

At home, I took it apart. It was more a matter of simply pulling it apart rather than any skilled knife work. The individual muscles are separated by connective tissue and they come apart rather easily. Then, by carefully trimming away all fat and silvery connective tissue, you are left with several pieces, some large and some small, of solid lean meat.

I don't know the names of the muscles that I cut from my chuck. I don't know which, if any, was the Brachiocephalicus or the Longissimus dorsi or the Triceps brachi. But it was not difficult to tell, even without cooking, which were the more tender. Those with a finer texture, some almost like velvet, are apt to be more tender than the muscles with a coarse stringy look to them.

Try this quick beef stew Italiano. It takes less than 30 minutes and tastes like you cooked it all day.

STIR-FRY STEW ITALIANO

1/2 pound well-trimmed boneless chuck meat

2 cloves garlic, minced

5 teaspoons olive oil

1 medium onion, sliced

1 medium zucchini, sliced

2 teaspoons flour

1/2 teaspoon dried basil leaves

1/2 teaspoon salt

1/8 teaspoon crushed red pepper pods

2 medium tomatoes, seeded and chopped

1/3 cup white wine

Polenta wedges, optional

1 tablespoon grated Parmesan cheese

Cut boneless chuck into 1/4-inch thick strips. Cut each strip into about 1 inch pieces. Using side of cook's knife, mash garlic cloves to form paste. Combine garlic with 2 teaspoons olive oil and add to beef strips in small bowl, stirring to combine. Set aside.

Saute sliced onion in 2 teaspoons olive oil in large skillet 3 minutes. (Good-sized black iron skillet with lid is preferred.)

Add beef and garlic and cook, stirring, 2 minutes. Add sliced zucchini and continue cooking 1 to 2 minutes. Sprinkle flour, basil, salt and red pepper over meat and vegetables. Stir in and cook another minute.

Add tomatoes and wine. Cover and simmer 4 or 5 minutes, just until sauce thickens slightly. Serve over wedge of polenta. Sprinkle stew with grated Parmesan cheese. Makes 4 servings.a

Note: Neva's Quick Polenta, slices of fried commercial cornmeal mush, may be substituted for regular polenta.
