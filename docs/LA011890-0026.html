January 18, 1990, Thursday, Home Edition

HINTS FROM HELOISE: JELLIES, SPREADS AND JAMS CAUSE CONFUSION

DEAR HELOISE: I don't understand the differences between jam, jelly and fruit spreads. Can you define them?

-- BETTY, Minneapolis

DEAR HELOISE: Jam is fruit that has been cooked and pureed into a soft and thick consistency. It can also be made from vegetables -- zucchini chutney or jam, for example.

Jelly is made from fruit juice or clear liquid and syrup or sugar. It is called jelly because when cooked the mixture gels.

Fruit spreads are usually smooth and thick, and are made from fruit pulp and spices.

Since all these different terms can be confusing, the most important thing is to read the jar label carefully.

-- HELOISE

DEAR HELOISE: I am seriously watching my calories as I fight the ever-present enemy, my bulging waistline.

One of the hardest tasks for me was dining out with friends or family. I had a habit of splurging because I would follow their lead when ordering. To finally get this situation under control I started ordering first, after explaining to everyone why I needed to do so.

I've almost reached my goal now and I still enjoy dinners out.

-- GLORIA, Colorado Springs, Colo.

DEAR HELOISE: Often I need to take an entire label off of a jar for a refund or a special offer and I can't get it off in one piece. It usually ends up in pieces and in the trash. Do you know of an easy way to remove one?

-- P. B., Lititz, Pa.

DEAR P. B.: One of the easiest ways to remove a label is to moisten a couple of paper towels or a cloth with hot water and wrap it around the label on the jar. Leave it on for a few minutes and remove. The label should peel off. If not, remoisten the cloth and try again.

When the label is damp, peel gently because it will tear quite easily.

-- HELOISE

DEAR HELOISE: I have a favorite recipe that calls for finely minced candied fruits. The problem was the fruit was so sticky and difficult to cut.

I cut the fruit into small pieces and put them in the food processor to mince and now I enjoy baking even more since my favorite dessert is so easy to make.

-- JAMIE, San Antonio

DEAR HELOISE: I just know that my hint will save a lot of moms and dads the trouble of baby-bottle nipples and caps getting ruined or lost in the dishwasher. I put them in a small hosiery bag (made to wash hosiery in a washing machine) and secure them by tying the bag to the top rack of my dishwasher.

-- DENISE, Fredericksburg, Va.
