940223 FT 23 FEB 94 / Tory warns PM of worries on Rover sale The government faced further criticism yesterday over the sale of Rover to BMW as a ministerial aide joined backbenchers in voicing his concern over the deal. Mr Tim Devlin, parliamentary private secretary to Sir Nicholas Lyell, the attorney-general, yesterday took the unusual step of asking the prime minister about the possible risk, sparked by the BMW takeover, to further Japanese investment in Britain. At question time in the Commons, he asked Mr John Major: 'With regard to the comments of the chairman of Honda, reported in today's newspapers - that the British are no longer serious about manufacturing - will you take steps to reassure the heads of Japanese businesses that Britain's future does lie with manufacturing, and we are serious about producing cars, fridges and other solid goods?' Mr Major said that Honda had confirmed its intention to develop Swindon as a centre of manufacturing excellence and to continue helping improve the performance of UK component suppliers. There was no doubt about the government's position in believing that manufacturing industry was important and required support. Mr Devlin said later: 'I remain deeply worried about letting a strategic industry pass into foreign hands and fear that the sale could be seen by other Japanese companies as a message that you cannot rely on Britain.' A significant number of Tory backbenchers share Mr Devlin's concern. Sir Teddy Taylor has already asked for the government to make a statement to reassure other Japanese companies investing in the UK. Mr Michael Heseltine, trade and industry secretary, dismissed suggestions that Honda's decision to sever links with Rover could signal that future Japanese investment in Britain was at risk. Mr Heseltine said the Honda move to end its financial relationship with the British company in the wake of the Pounds 800m sale to BMW was a decision 'to disengage in terms of shared ownership and not to remove the commitment of investment through Britain in the European Community'. 'The case for Japanese investment in the UK is a very powerful one,' he added. 'Something like 40 per cent of all investment into the EC from Japan comes to the UK.'
