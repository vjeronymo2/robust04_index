CSO [Article by Hidenaka Kato, staff writer] [Text] Four foreign aerospace firms will join the government-backed project to produce the YSX passenger jet, Ministry of Trade and Industry officials said last week. The plane would be the first commercial airliner produced by Japan's aircraft industry since 1972. Three Western companies -- Boeing Co. of the U.S., Aviation de Transport Regional, a French-Italian joint venture and Saab-Scania AB of Sweden -- plus the state-run Aviation Industries of China will this year join feasibility studies for the mid-range YSX aircraft, say the officials. Since halting production of the short-range YS11 passenger aircraft in 1972, the Japanese aerospace industry has long talked of producing another commercial plane. The YSX, aimed to begin service early next century, has a capacity for 70-80 passengers and a cruising range of between 1,000-1,300 nautical miles (1,800-2,400 km). The foreign participation is seen as vital -- particularly that of Chinese. The Japan Aircraft Development Corp. (JADC), a private consortium backed by MITI, always aimed to attract partners with big markets. Industry estimates suggest that China will need up to 800 airplanes in the next 15 years and will become the world's third-largest aviation market next to the U.S. and Japan. That demand would make ready-made sales for mid-range regional jets like the YSX. Foreign partnerships should also help reduce development costs, estimated to total ¥80 billion ($708 million). MITI, together with JADC, launched the first feasibility study in 1989. Since then almost ¥400 million has been spent on the project. For fiscal 1994, starting next April, MITI plans to allocate ¥1.6 billion for the YSX regional jetliner with another ¥800 million coming from the private sector members of JADC. The foreign firms are expected to become full members of the development team in fiscal 1995, although no concrete agreements have yet been made on the partners' status when the feasibility study finishes, said a MITI official. JADC hopes to start engineering the YSX in 1997. The non-Japanese companies hope to take advantage of the growing demand for small passenger aircraft, industry observers explained. Boeing, the world's largest aircraft builder, for example, currently has no airplanes with less than 100 seats. JADC estimates a total of 650 airplanes with less than 100 seats will be needed on the world market during the first decade of the next century. At least 300-400 orders would be required for the project to break even. There are considerable risks. Many European aircraft builders are also trying to enter the small-to-mid-range jet market and have projects for planes with less than 100 seats already up-and-running. Both Saab and ATR have independently-launched projects to develop aircraft similar in size to the YSX. That could, fear Japanese officials, marginalize their involvement in the YSX project. Those with long memories will also point to the previous Japanese commercial airliner, the YS11, a 75-seat plane, which was a commercial flop. European manufacturers are already touting their own commuter aircraft on the Japanese market. Nichimen Corp., Japan's second-tier trading house, will carry out demonstration flights of German Dolnier 328 in Tokyo and Okinawa in March, hoping to win orders. Bombardier Inc. of Canada has also proposed a 50 seater small aircraft.
