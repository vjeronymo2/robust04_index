930421 FT 21 APR 93 / UN races against time to disarm Moslems: UK to appeal for caution in any new military initiatives during talks in Washington UNITED NATIONS forces in Bosnia-Hercegovina yesterday formally asked for more time to disarm Bosnian Moslem troops in the besieged town of Srebrenica, but the request was rejected in advance by the Bosnian Serb army. Failure to disarm the Moslem troops by today's noon deadline could wreck the ceasefire negotiated by Bosnian Serb and Moslem military commanders last weekend. The UN Protection Force (Unprofor), which has asked for a 72-hour extension of the limit, has admitted that only a few of the town's Moslem defenders have so far surrendered their weapons. However, General Milan Gvero, deputy commander of Bosnian Serb forces, has insisted the disarmament pact be carried out to the letter. An Unprofor official said the peacekeepers, who were due to meet representatives of the warring parties at Sarajevo airport yesterday, would also renew efforts to persuade the Serbs to withdraw from the perimeter of Srebrenica to beyond gunfire range. The UN said it had not yet determined the parameters of the 'safe zone' to be established around the town, in the Drina River valley. It has also proposed the creation of similar safe havens around Gorazde and Zepa, the last two remaining Moslem strongholds in eastern Bosnia. The Bosnian Serb army protested yesterday to UN peacekeepers against the flights of two Nato F-16 jets, which 'buzzed' the Bosnian town of Banja Luka for the second consecutive day, the Tanjug news agency reported. The jets, taking part in the UN mission to enforce a no-fly zone over Bosnia, streaked across the north-western Bosnian Serb stronghold at a height of less than 200 metres. The Bosnian Serbs said such low flights frightened the inhabitants and violated air traffic regulations in the area. Meanwhile, Britain is expected to press the case for caution over any new military initiatives in Bosnia during two days of talks in the US between Mr Malcolm Rifkind, the defence secretary, and his US counterpart, Mr Les Aspin. Mounting pressure for more forceful western action in Bosnia is expected to dominate the discussions. The talks follow a brief meeting in London on Monday night between Mr Rifkind and Mr Francois Leotard, French defence minister, who agreed that military action to try to stop the fighting in Bosnia would jeopardise UN humanitarian efforts and endanger the lives of British and French troops protecting aid convoys. 'We also have very strong reservations about the idea of military action from the air for the moment, as long as the UN soldiers have not been regrouped and better protected,' Mr Leotard said. UK defence officials said they saw no sign of a change in the French position following the appointment of a new centre-right government in Paris. In Washington, President Bill Clinton yesterday summoned his top foreign and military policy advisers for briefings on what the White House called the 'very serious' situation in Bosnia. The intensified talks between western allies coincided with preparations by Lord Owen, the European Community's peace mediator, to fly to Belgrade. He is expected to have a frosty reception today when he meets Serbia's President Slobodan Milosevic. With only five days to go before a UN deadline imposing tough, new sanctions on the rump Yugoslavia unless Bosnian Serbs agree to sign the Vance-Owen peace plan, Lord Owen will warn Mr Milosevic that the Serbian people face extended isolation from the outside world unless they help end the Bosnian conflict. But the mood in Belgrade is reported to be unconciliatory. 'The peace process is not dead, but it is not very healthy, and what undermines it is the continued fighting,' Mr Fred Eckhard, the mediators' spokesman, said in Geneva yesterday.
