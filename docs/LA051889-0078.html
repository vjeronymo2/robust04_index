May 18, 1989, Thursday, Home Edition

THE MEDIA;

MURDOCH JOINS SPAIN INVESTORS IN TV VENTURE

Media magnate Rupert Murdoch has teamed up with Spanish investors to launch a private television channel that may eventually beam programs to Spanish-speaking audiences in the United States and South America.

Univision Canal Uno will be owned 25% by Grupo Zeta, the leading newspaper and magazine publisher, and 25% by Murdoch's News International PLC, Zeta Chairman Antonio Asensio told reporters Wednesday.

Asensio said Univision will have initial capital of $120 million and will finance an array of films, sports, documentaries and news programs from advertising revenue.

"In the future we hope to broadcast to Spanish-speaking audiences in the Americas using Murdoch's satellites," he said.

The deal is Murdoch's second joint television venture in as many days. On Tuesday, Murdoch's Sky Television satellite network joined with Robert Maxwell's Cable TV network to take its four-channel Sky programs into 50,000 more British homes.

Murdoch made a last-minute entry into the Spanish broadcasting fray against Italian media tycoon Silvio Berlusconi and French pay television channel Canal-Plus. Both have separately joined Spanish investors to bid for three private TV franchises.

Five consortiums have bid for the three national television franchises that the Spanish government has put on the block. Madrid has until mid-August to choose the winners.

Television broadcasting is limited to two nationwide state channels and five regional stations, controlled by local governments.

Barcelona-based Grupo Zeta, publishing dailies and sensationalist magazines such as best-selling weeklies Interviu and Tiempo, will be Murdoch's main Spanish partner. Asensio said Zeta chose Murdoch over Tele-France 1, a leading French private television channel that offered to present a joint bid with the Spanish group.

Other leading investors include Madrid-based financier Jacques Hachuel, with 10%, and two leading Spanish banks, Banco Central and Banco Espanol de Credito. Under controversial rules drawn up by Madrid, no single investor, Spanish or foreign, can own more than 25% of a private channel.
