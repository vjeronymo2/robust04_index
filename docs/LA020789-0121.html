February 7, 1989, Tuesday, Home Edition

GORBACHEV URGED TO DRASTICALLY ALTER ECONOMY

One of President Mikhail S. Gorbachev's principal economic advisers proposed radical changes Monday, including a 30% cut in the Soviet defense budget, in an effort to restore popular faith in Gorbachev's reform program.

Abel G. Aganbegyan, secretary of the economic section of the Soviet Academy of Sciences, called on the government to spend a quarter of its foreign earnings to import Western consumer products and to cancel dozens of major industrial projects and use the money for new housing.

Aganbegyan, writing in the Communist Party newspaper Pravda, called for an intensive three- to four-year effort to satisfy widespread expectations, so far unfulfilled, that perestroika -- as Gorbachev's political and economic reforms are known -- would bring a better life.

"Most Soviet families are not feeling changes for the better," he said, acknowledging that the resulting disappointment is building into a serious political problem for the Soviet leadership. "On paper, we have been taking one step forward, but in practice we have been going back two steps."

Going boldly beyond most other reform economists, Aganbegyan urged a massive effort to satisfy consumer demand, which greatly outstrips supplies for everyday necessities and for luxury goods, in order to build confidence in the reforms and to spur faster economic growth.

Video Recorders, Computers

Aganbegyan sees in the pent-up demand for consumer goods -- particularly luxury items such as videotape recorders, personal computers, high-quality furniture and passenger cars -- a way of accelerating economic growth -- 4.4% last year compared to the planned 6.6% -- and building the political as well as economic momentum needed for reform.

The whole Soviet economy is changing too slowly, Aganbegyan said. The technical level of production and the quality of Soviet products have improved only a little despite the reforms of the past four years, he said.

"It is necessary to go two or three times faster," he said of the effort to restructure the Soviet economy. For example, passenger car production, now about 1.8 million vehicles a year, should be increased by 2 million to 3 million annually, he said.

The urgency with which Aganbegyan views the need to bolster popular confidence in the reforms, as well as his desire to foster an economic takeoff, takes him well beyond the proposals offered by other reform economists and the positions taken by Gorbachev himself in the debate on the country's economic strategy.

Where Gorbachev has announced a 14% reduction in military spending, Aganbegyan calls for a 30% cut so that the savings can be used in the civilian sector.

Where other reform economists are suggesting that some foreign loans might be used to purchase as much as $7 billion in Western consumer goods over two or three years, Aganbegyan proposes doubling such purchases so that they constitute at least 25% of all Soviet imports. He calls for cutting back on the purchase of machinery to make this possible.

And where others have questioned the immediate need for some of the major development projects, Aganbegyan demands the immediate cancellation of two giant hydroelectric plants and several massive new irrigation canals, along with any other project that cannot quickly pay for itself. He also urges the closure of money-losing state enterprises.

"The principal problem is to meet people's demands," Aganbegyan wrote in Pravda, "but the measures should not be administrative -- quite a new approach is needed to expand independence (of enterprises) and abolish directive-type planning."

But Aganbegyan, once the leading proponent of major price reform as the key to fundamental economic reform, urged the government to postpone for three or four years any effort to restructure the Soviet price system. He had earlier insisted that these changes, essential if market forces are to play the larger role advocated by most reform economists, be completed by 1991.

Major Implications

If Aganbegyan's proposal is accepted by Gorbachev, it would stretch out the overall economic reform effort and have major political and social implications.

"The main thing now is to take measures to make the ailing economic mechanism healthy," he said, arguing that the country is not prepared for the fundamental shifts he believes are needed.

Market-driven growth could be "a powerful engine for the renewal and improvement of the whole economy," he continued, but it would require a number of support measures, including the large-scale shift of resources now controlled by government ministries and state enterprises.

Another major economic goal of his proposal is to reduce inflation, widely estimated at 6% to 7% but potentially much more than that, by meeting the demand for high-cost items. At the same time, he hopes to cut the government's budget deficit through the profits brought in by the sale of the new products.

In his proposal, one of several put forward by leading Soviet economists in recent weeks, Aganbegyan dodges responsibility for the failure of Gorbachev's initial reforms -- of which he was the principal architect -- to fulfill the implicit promise of a better life.

The country's economic stagnation is even deeper than he and other specialists had realized, Aganbegyan said, and resistance to the reforms is greater than anticipated.

As they have been implemented, he said, the reforms are largely half-measures and not radical enough to transform the economy.
