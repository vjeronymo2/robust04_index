November 22, 1989, Wednesday, Home Edition

CONSUMER VIEWS: CONSUMERS;

GOING FOR GOLD AS AN INVESTMENT

Question: In 1982, I bought gold South African Krugerrands for $444 each. In 1988 I started selling them, piece by piece, and received $397 to $415 each, even though the gold Maple Leaf and other gold coins were going for more than $470. I was told by Deak International that the low price of the Krugerrands was because of our boycott against South Africa.

Since gold is gold, regardless of what country the gold comes from, please tell me if I'm getting ripped off. -- M.D.

Answer: Gold is gold, sure enough, but price is still the measure of the equation between demand and supply. And the demand for Krugerrands "has just not been there for several years," according to Karen Jennings, manager here for Deak International Ltd.

Despite constant arguments that gold is "just another metal," the metal has had a hypnotic appeal since the Egyptians discovered it about 4000 BC, and for centuries it was the world's "ultimate store of value" and was the backing for almost all currencies. Even now, gold is generally regarded as one of the more reliable hedges against inflation -- which is why the price of the metal soars during periods of high inflation and drops precipitously during periods of stable prices.

But holding gold, either as an investment or as an inflation hedge, has always presented problems. To hold it in "raw" form, as ingots or dust, is unwieldy because it has to be assayed every time you try to sell it. A much better solution: Hold it in the form of established coinage, where the gold content is clearly a matter of record.

For years, then, investors (as distinguished from numismatists, who collect coins for their historic value and their visual condition) concentrated principally on two widely circulated international coins -- the Mexican 50 Peso Centenario and the Austrian 100 Corona. A third coin, the Hungarian 100 Korona, had a limited circulation for reasons that are comparable to the Krugerrand's woes -- investors disliked benefiting Hungary's Communist government.

All three coins, though, had one cumbersome feature -- the offbeat weight of the coins, which made it difficult to determine their current market value. Both the Corona and the Korona are .98 Troy ounce and the Centenario is 1.2 Troy ounces.

When South Africa, which sits on most of the world's known supply of unmined gold, decided in the 1960s to create the Krugerrand, leave it undenominated, but peg it at exactly 1.00 Troy ounce, it was an instant hit because of its convenience. If you had 100 Krugerrands you had exactly 100 ounces of gold, and all you had to do was look up the "spot" price of gold on any given day and you'd know exactly what your holdings were worth.

"But, because of their convenience," Jennings adds, "coins traditionally sell at a slight premium."

On the day we discussed this, the "spot" price for gold was $367 an ounce and Deak was buying the Canadian Maple Leaf (also 1.00 Troy ounce) for $372 -- a $5-an-ounce premium -- but was buying the Krugerrand for $357, a $10-an-ounce discount.

"But the 'spot' price," she says, "isn't actually the price of an ounce of gold that's been melted down, as you might think, but is simply the market determination of gold's value on that day."

Because of the Krugerrand's additional convenience of being exactly 1.00 Troy ounce, "it was tremendously popular for several years."

But then apartheid and its ugliness flared up, the violence accelerated, and the world's growing disillusionment with South Africa's unyielding position on racial relations began sharply eroding the Krugerrand's appeal.

"When the Canadians came out with the gold Maple Leaf in the mid, or late, '70s," Jennings adds, "it was beautiful timing. Investors really wanted a one-ounce gold coin, and for several years Canada practically had a corner on the market."

Then Uncle Sam took the hint and came out with the one-ounce Eagle in 1986. "A lot of the bigger dealers today," she says, "and we're one of them, won't even sell the Krugerrand, and we buy them back only because so many of our customers originally bought them from us. We feel obligated."
