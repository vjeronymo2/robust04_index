BFN [Text] [Announcer] Montenegrin Prime Minister Milo Djukanovic is paying an official visit to Great Britain. We have established a direct link with London where Mr. Prime Minister [Djukanovic] is. Good day! [Djukanovic] Good day! [Announcer] To get right to the point for the listeners of Argument Vise [An Argument More] program -- what is the objective of your visit to Great Britain, have you had any concrete meetings and talks? [Djukanovic] Together with the deputy prime minister and Montenegrin foreign minister I am visiting London at the invitation of one of the members of the foreign affairs' committee of the lower chamber of the British Parliament. This afternoon I am supposed to present my vision of the current situation in Yugoslavia in this lower chamber committee. [Announcer] I presume that we will obtain some concrete information about your talks there later in the day. [Djukanovic] Hello? [Announcer] Yes, we can hear you. Rather optimistic assessments have been coming from Albania, to which you paid an official visit beforehand -- that is, mutual satisfaction with this visit. Could you say anything more about it for our listeners? [Djukanovic] I took the opportunity of the fact that I was travelling to London via Albania to hold talks with the Albanian Government. We held talks with Prime Minister Meksi, naturally, in the presence of the foreign minister and other government officials. We discussed the need to intensify mutual relations. We took the opportunity to tell Mr. Meksi that the overall foreign affairs' goal of the FRY [Federal Republic of Yugoslavia] is to establish good relations with its neighbors and that, within this context, Montenegro has an explicit need to establish good, that is to say, better relations with Albania, with whom it borders directly, than we have had in the past. On that occasion, we discussed some essential issues that represent the most important segments of the improvement of mutual relations -- the rights of minorities in both countries and some vital issues that we perceive as a possibility to promote economic and other cooperation. I received assurances that, in accordance with the democratic changes that are underway in that country, the rights of the Serbs and the Montenegrins who live in Albania will receive appropriate treatment parallel to a demand that such a policy be pursued for the Albanians in the FRY. Naturally, as always, special interest was shown in the problem of Kosovo. It seems that there have been some ambiguous statements by some diplomatic representatives of some superpowers -- I am primarily referring to the statements that have been coming from London recently -- which have contributed a lot that Albania abandoned the aggressive attitude toward the FRY regarding Kosovo, acknowledged that Kosovo is an integral part of Serbia and Yugoslavia, and that this issue can only be discussed at the level of rights that must be guaranteed to the citizens of Albanian ethnicity in Kosovo. I would say that for me all together this was a sign that a new leaf has been turned in the attitudes toward this issue and in the general FRY-Albanian relations. [Announcer] Thank you very much. We wish you all success in you visit and activities in London.
