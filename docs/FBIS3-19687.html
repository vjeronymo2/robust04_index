Language: German Article Type:BFN ["deu" report: "Scharping Calls Criticism `Wretched Propaganda'"] [Excerpt] Bonn -- The Social Democratic Party [SPD] leadership's tax plans if the SPD wins the Bundestag elections in October gave rise to a heated debate at the weekend [19-20 March]. Chancellor Helmut Kohl and other leading representatives of the coalition categorically rejected the plan to introduce a supplementary tax of 10 percent of the income tax on annual salaries of up to 50,000 German marks [DM] for singles and DM100,000 for married couples. In contrast, SPD Chairman Rudolf Scharping defended the concept. His deputy, Oskar Lafontaine, for his part, accused the government of planning further tax increases. The government denied this. Scharping rejected criticism of the planned supplementary tax as "wretched propaganda." Anyone who talks about "destroying the readiness to work hard in Germany," is "not quite normal," Scharping told SUEDDEUTSCHE ZEITUNG. The difference between the solidarity surcharge of 7.5 percent of their income tax, which is planned by the coalition, and the supplementary tax, is small. For a citizen with a monthly gross income of DM8,000 it is only DM50. "I think that this is bearable; I do not expect enthusiasm but understanding," the SPD chairman said. He said that the coalition's claim that higher taxes make people less willing to work hard a "completely trite and stupid theory." Over the past years the upper income echelons have experienced a considerable easing of burdens. If this had significantly raised people's willingness to work hard, one should not be in the most profound economic crisis since the end of the war now. [passage omitted]
