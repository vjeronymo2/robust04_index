From its ten categories of expense (which include the four subdivided categories above), Runzheimer selected a marketbasket of items on which to base its goods and services analysis. A ``marketbasket'' is a selected group of products and services that represent hundreds or even thousands of other items. Pricing every item available to consumers in a given locale would be unnecessary, inefficient, and probably impossible. Runzheimer selected typically purchased items and weighted these according to their relative importance in terms of consumer expenditure patterns. Each marketbasket item represented a specific group of related expense items. Using CES data, we determined the relative importance (weight) of each item. We compared the average price of each marketbasket item in each allowance area with the average price in the Washington, D.C., area. The price differences (expressed as indexes) were aggregated based on the item, subcategory and category weightings, resulting in a total Goods & Services component index at each income level.
