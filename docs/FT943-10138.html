940806 FT 06 AUG 94 / Finance and the Family: Building society snapshot In Kingsley Amis' novel Lucky Jim, Jim Dixon expounds a theory that nice things are nicer than nasty ones. On a similar theme, one could say that well-managed building societies are better managed than poorly-run ones. The publication this week of the annual analysis by UBS of how the 20 largest societies performed, in terms of 12 financial ratios, should be some guide to how well societies are run. Its authors, however, emphasise that it is a snapshot, based just on 1993 results. Top of the table, for the first time, was Halifax, the UK's largest society. In joint second place were Cheltenham & Gloucester - which has had a Pounds 1.8bn cash offer from Lloyds Bank - and Yorkshire. Fourth and fifth places are taken by Northern Rock and Leeds Permanent. At the other end of the scale, Skipton comes in last, behind Bristol & West (19th) and Leeds & Holbeck (18th). A quick glance, right, at the 'Highest rates' table shows that there is no direct linkage between it and positions in the UBS league. Halifax and Yorkshire each appear only once in the best rates table - but Skipton and B & W also feature. On that basis, what use is the UBS analysis to potential customers? It is probably least useful for those looking at fixed-rate investments than it is for customers considering borrowing or saving at rates that can vary over time. Over the long term the stronger societies should be able to offer keener rates. At the very least, they should not need to keep existing customers at uncompetitive rates to make up for mistakes in or beyond the core business. Building Societies Research: The Major Players.
