921125 FT 25 NOV 92 / Passengers say new trains create strain BRITISH RAIL'S new Networker trains, intended to bring relief to the 130,000 commuters using Network SouthEast's poorly performing Kent Link suburban services, look in danger of running into ingratitude. Many who have sampled the 'state-of-the-art' trains on trial runs say they are cramped and some say they are worse than the 40-year-old 'slam-door' trains they are due to replace. The Networker, regarded by BR as the flagship of a new generation of commuter trains, is to be launched today by Mr John MacGregor, transport secretary, and Sir Bob Reid, BR chairman. A Pounds 600m fleet of the trains will gradually replace the slam-door rolling stock on routes from Dartford and Sevenoaks to London Bridge, Cannon Street and Charing Cross. The trains, which have automatic doors, are much cheaper to run and maintain than existing ones, but some passengers say the seats are too close together and dislike the interior layout. Mrs Maureen Collins and her daughter Joanna, passengers on a trial run from Charing Cross to Gravesend, said luggage racks were out of reach and there was nowhere to put briefcases or shopping. 'I'd hate to be on one of these trains in the rush hour,' said Mrs Collins. 'There's not enough standing room and nothing for people to hang on to. The old trains were better.' Ms Jane McGrath, a bank clerk, disliked the airline-style seating arrangement which left many passengers facing the back of the seat in front. 'And I'm worried about the automatic doors,' she said. 'What happens if someone gets stuck in them? It'll happen all the time in the rush hour, with people rushing to catch trains.' Other passengers thought the seats were too hard or too close to each other. Passengers also criticised the lack of legroom and disliked the fact that the new trains have many fewer doors than the old ones. But there was praise for the smoothness of the ride, and many liked the brightness and airiness of the interior design. 'Let's face it, anything has to be better than the old trains,' said Mr Alan Weir, a retired teacher. 'And I think we can look the French in the face with these.'
