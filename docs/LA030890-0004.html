March 8, 1990, Thursday, Home Edition

COOK IT LIGHT: REDRESSING THE CLASSIC CAESAR SALAD

Though the Ides of March proved to be Caesar's downfall on March 15, 44 B.C., the salad bearing his name has been a year-round favorite ever since it was created by Caesar Cardini, an Italian restaurateur, in Tijuana in 1924.

This version has far less cholesterol, since I use only the white instead of the whole egg called for in a classic Caesar salad.

HAPPY HEART CAESAR SALAD

2 heads Romaine lettuce

2 cups garlic croutons

2 tablespoons grated Parmesan cheese

1/2 cup Happy Heart Caesar Dressing

Several hours before serving, wash and dry lettuce thoroughly. Tear into bite-size pieces (there should be 16 cups) and place in large bowl lined with cloth to absorb any remaining moisture. Store in refrigerator.

When ready to serve, remove cloth from under lettuce and add dressing. Toss salad until every leaf glistens. Add croutons and again toss thoroughly. Makes 16 cups, 8 (2-cup) servings.

Each serving contains approximately:

66 calories

3 mg cholesterol

2 gm fat

140 mg sodium

Happy Heart Caesar Dressing

1 clove garlic, minced

2 tablespoons extra-virgin olive oil

1 1/2 teaspoons anchovy paste

1 egg

1/4 teaspoon ground black pepper

2 tablespoons lemon juice

1 tablespoon Worcestershire sauce

2 teaspoons red wine vinegar

1/4 cup freshly grated Parmesan cheese

Using garlic press, squeeze garlic into oil in skillet over low heat. Cook just until garlic sizzles. Do not brown. Remove from heat and allow to cool.

Add anchovy paste to garlic oil and mix well.

Dip egg into boiling water for 30 seconds. Separate egg, discarding yolk. Add egg white to oil mixture. Blend well.

Add pepper, lemon juice, Worcestershire, vinegar and Parmesan, mixing well. Store tightly covered in refrigerator. Makes 1/2 cup, 8 (1 tablespoon) servings.

Note: Although many recipes call for uncooked eggs, the U.S. Department of Agriculture has found them to be a potential carrier of food-borne illness and recommends that diners avoid eating raw eggs. Commercial egg substitutes may be used in place of raw eggs in certain circumstances. Check egg substitute package for applications.

Each serving contains approximately:

52 calories

4 mg cholesterol

5 gm fat

97 mg sodium
