May 18, 1989, Thursday, Home Edition

STAGE REVIEW;

A BREATHLESS VERSION OF BRECHT'S 'BAAL'

A tumultuous production of Bertolt Brecht's "Baal" has been transported to West Hollywood by the Project III Ensemble Theatre from New York.

The 20-member SoHo troupe is flying its expressionistic colors at the Callboard Theatre. The tumult doesn't always make sense, but it frequently freezes your attention. The production careens and slurps through the lustful adventures of a wandering poet nicknamed Baal (the fertility god) whose degeneracy swallows up those around him and, finally, himself as well.

The setting is Germany around 1911. "Baal" was Brecht's first play, written when he was barely 21 (in 1918), and its roguish elements anticipate "The Three Penny Opera" and, later still, Brecht's formalized theater of alienation.

Unlike Brecht's better-known plays, "Baal" is pure, rampaging hedonism brushed with an impish, sprite-like innocence orchestrating its voracious central character.

Director Charles Otte's sense of gesture and style, with cast members occasionally addressing the audience directly, seems as true to the early, experimental Brecht as it's possible to get. This company knows what it is doing even when the material taxes your patience.

Playing the title role demands a reckless hairy ape who is a child at heart. And Will Kepper's shirtless (at one point totally naked) bulk and life-gorging spirit fill the stage with an appetite that is vulgar and astonishing.

Kepper's part-monster, part mirthful satyr is an insatiable drinker and fornicator, irresistible to women, having and discarding them like grapes. Finally, he's even a murderer, killing his loyal follower and fellow artist (well played bv Richard Schiff).

The dreamlike ending materializes with a nice symbolic image conceived by director Otte. A little boy whom we've seen sitting under barroom tables now stands bathed in light as Kepper's Baal, spent, ruined and, worse, with nobody to play with, slips into innocence/death and fades into the glade. All Baal wanted was to gulp down the sky and the moon.

Kepper is especially fine at suggesting the man/child who grabbed all the toys and was puzzled when they broke apart in his hands.

The play is rarely staged. It didn't show up in this country until 1965. Some theatergoers may recall Ron Sossi in the title role at the Odyssey (1981), which was also a rough, vibrant affair, but this SoHo ensemble is notable for its visual and musical panache.

The show's opening is as deceptively pristine as a church recital. The cast, with bell-like tones, is lined up a capella style singing the "Chorale of the Great Baal" from Brecht's prologue (including the telltale "Baal eats all the grass and smacks his lips"). The 21 scenes that follow are filtered through fractured, primitive, expressionist art reflecting Baal's inner pursuit and painted by Jan. Gillen.

Gillen's brother, Theodore Gillen, composed the original music. And the producer is yet another Gillen, sister Anne Marie.

The ensemble work is carefully crafted and three of the women etch this Brechtian canvas with lingering color: the softly pale Hilary Rappaport, the starkly sinuous blond Christine Sang, and the avaricious, auburn Lucile Ford.

At 8451 Melrose Place, Thursdays through Saturdays, 8 p.m., Sundays, 2 p.m. and 7 p.m., through May 28. Tickets: $12.50-$15. (213) 466-1767.
