January 7, 1990, Sunday, Home Edition

UNTITLED ON GANG WARFARE;

BY DINO

My people are dying in an un-natural act

In a war on the streets and it's black against black

They're dying for the color of a useless old rag

The rags are different colors and they call them flags

They fight these battles on our city streets

The fighting is very strong, but the reason is much too weak

Our children and babies get caught up in their fire

And before they can live, for no reason they must die

The police will kill one brother, they took his life for free

Gangs take two for colors, so brother now that's three

Before the week is over, at three a day, that's twenty-one

Twenty-one young black brothers that died just for fun

Wake up, young black brother, our race is dying, can't you see?

The war you should be fighting is with them, not with me

Fight that war on drugs, and you know there is a war on crime

That's what's killing us, brother, and destroying all our young minds

You kill a black brother, then I kill one and flee

And give them another reason to put their killer on our streets

But we're killing the most, and letting them kill the rest

In this war you are killing my people, so there's no winner and no best.

From "The Word" (Homeless Writers Coalition, 6340 Lankershim Blvd., 311, North Hollywood, CA 91606; 818 505-8802). 1989, The Homeless Writers coalition. Reprinted by permission. Wednesday Elizabeth Venant writes about Dino and other homeless writers in a feature story in View.
