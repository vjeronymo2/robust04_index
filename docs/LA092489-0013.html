September 24, 1989, Sunday, Home Edition

CLASSICS ON CASSETTE: THE SOUNDS OF THE JAZZ AGE

All those lovely images in "The Great Gatsby" came flowing out of my cassette player, but what moved me, what made me hit the rewind button, was something more ordinary.

A schedule.

"Rise from bed, 6 a.m. Dumbbell exercise and wall scaling, 6:15-6:30 a.m. Study electricity, etc: 7:15-8:15 a.m. Work, 8:30-4:30 p.m. . . . Practice elocution, poise and how to attain it, 5:00-6:00 p.m. . . . Read one improving book or magazine per week."

As you may recall, that was the regimen followed by the modestly born Jimmy Gatz of North Dakota, who made himself into one of the great inventions of the Jazz Age, Jay Gatsby.

Nothing in this, F. Scott Fitzgerald's greatest work, is more poignant than the moment when Gatsby's father reveals the self-improvement schedule written on the last fly-leaf of a paperback copy of "Hopalong Cassidy."

"The truth was," wrote Fitzgerald, "that Jay Gatsby of West Egg, Long Island, sprang from his Platonic conception of himself. He was a son of God -- a phrase which, if it means anything, means just that -- and he must be about His Father's business, the service of a vast, vulgar and meretricious beauty. So he invented just the sort of Jay Gatsby that a 17-year-old boy would be likely to invent, and to this conception he was faithful to the end."

And when did Gatsby abandon any chance that he might pull it off? When he kissed Daisy Fay on a magical, moonlit night in Louisville, Ky. Here's Fitzgerald: "He knew that when he kissed this girl, and forever wed his unutterable visions to her perishable breath, his mind would never romp again like the mind of God. So he waited, listening for a moment longer to the tuning fork that had been struck upon a star. Then he kissed her. At his lips' touch she blossomed for him like a flower and the incarnation was complete."

Fitzgerald biographer Andrew Turnbull writes that the author was counting on big sales -- at least 75,000 copies -- for "The Great Gatsby" when it was published in April, 1925.

By then, Scott and Zelda Fitzgerald were well into their lavish life-style and were living on the French Riviera partly on the proceeds from the author's first two books.

(It was also at this time that Fitzgerald wrote Maxwell Perkins, his editor at Scribner's, and urged him to take a look at a young writer named Ernest Hemingway. "He's the real thing," wrote Fitzgerald.)

Inexplicably, "The Great Gatsby" sold only 23,000 copies when it came out, and Fitzgerald was so discouraged he began laying plans to go to Hollywood and write screenplays.

For the critics, though, "Gatsby" represented major growth for Fitzgerald. T. S. Eliot wrote the author after reading the new book that ". . . it seems to me to be the first step that American fiction has taken since Henry James."

Why there are not more recordings of "The Great Gatsby" is a mystery to me.

The only rental I found is offered by Recorded Books. Luckily, it is read by the incomparable Frank Muller, who adopts a tone for the book's narrator, Nick Carraway, that perfectly fits Nick's amazement at the outrageous events he witnessed.

The monthly rental for this unabridged 4 1/2-hour recording is $9.50. For those building a shelf of recorded classics, the Muller reading can also be purchased for $21.95.

If you have wanted to own a reading of a modern classic by the legendary Alexander Scourby, there is also that chance here. Scourby, who died in 1985, has the rich, cultured voice that Nick Carraway surely would have had in his later years.

Listening Library sells the Scourby reading to individuals and libraries for $44.95. G. K. Hall sells it to libraries only. In addition to "The Great Gatsby," you get five Fitzgerald stories, including "Babylon Revisited" and "The Lost Decade."
