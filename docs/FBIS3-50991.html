Article Type:BFN [Editorial Report] The following is a compilation of reports on Guatemalan economic developments monitored by Panama Bureau through 11 January; source follows in parentheses after each item. Social Investment Fund (FIS) sources have reported the FIS will receive over 270 million quetzals this year through contributions from government and international agencies. The sources have explained the FIS will receive 50 million quetzals from the government, 116 million quetzals from a World Bank loan, and 104 million quetzals from the German Development Agency. Foreign financing was made possible thanks to the executive passing of the regulation on FIS activities. (Guatemala City PRENSA LIBRE in Spanish 5 Jan 94 p 65) The National Statistics Institute (INE) has reported that inflation grew by 11.64 percent in 1993, the level expected by government authorities. Inflation grew by 1.31 percent and 1.22 percent in November and December of 1993, respectively, representing a sudden increase in monthly inflation as compared to previous months. The government could not meet the objective of reducing inflation, which totaled 14.22 percent, by 8 points, in 1992. The INE has concluded that the increase in the cost of vegetables and fruits in December caused the hike in inflation. (Mexico City NOTIMEX in Spanish 1758 GMT 5 Jan 94) The Central Bank of Guatemala has reported that as of 30 September the public foreign debt totaled $2.1 billion. Of this amount, $1.5 billion corresponded to the public sector and $619.8 million to the Bank of Guatemala. This report cited a debt reduction of $124.6 million with respect to 1992, of which $85.7 million came from the Bank of Guatemala and $38.9 from the public sector. The foreign debt-gross domestic product relationship increased from 21.6 percent by the end of 1992 to 18.7 percent in September of 1993. (Guatemala City EL GRAFICO in Spanish 6 Jan 94 p 62) The National Cotton Council director has reported that cotton production in the country generated $13.9 million in revenues during 1993, a 1.7-percent increase with respect to 1992. According to cotton producers' estimates, national production will disappear in Guatemala within two years. The source concluded the decline in international cotton prices has continued, forcing several countries out of the market. (Mexico City NOTIMEX in Spanish 2357 GMT 6 Jan 94) A study conducted by UNICEF's Social Planning Secretariat has revealed that 25 percent of the Guatemalan population is suffering from poverty. The document has reported that poverty is growing 5 percent annually. The study also highlighted the low social security coverage in urban areas, which is even worse in rural areas. UNICEF has concluded that some 77.6 percent of Guatemalan children suffer from some degree of malnutrition. (Mexico City NOTIMEX in Spanish 1904 GMT 8 Jan 94)
