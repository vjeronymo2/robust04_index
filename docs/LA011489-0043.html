January 14, 1989, Saturday, San Diego County Edition

OCEANSIDE TASK FORCE MAPS ATTACK ON GROWING PROBLEM OF HOMELESS

The visible increase in the homeless people who drift aimlessly along Oceanside's streets and find shelter in doorways has spurred city and community leaders to establish a task force to find the best ways to help the city's needy.

The Homeless Task Force, established earlier this week, will try to coordinate the efforts of social agencies that serve the city's homeless, said Councilwoman Lucy Chavez, who presented the group's objectives on Friday.

The 15-member task force, which includes representatives from social agencies and religious and business leaders, hopes to identify the specific needs of the different groups of people that constitute the city's homeless population, said Chavez, the group's chairwoman.

"There are various types of homeless people, various degrees of homelessness," Chavez said. "There are the migrant workers, the street people, homeless families, homeless teen-agers, the mentally ill and those who have drug problems.

"There are various reasons why people become homeless, and they have to be treated separately," she said. "There is no one solution."

Won't Replace Agencies

The city's task force has no plans to take over existing agencies or finance homeless services, Chavez said. But it will assist such agencies in finding funds to expand and help establish a list of short- and long-term priorities.

"If we can figure out a way to immediately help, say the homeless street people, especially with the recent cold weather, we will," Chavez said. "But the task force was not established to be a Band-Aid. This will be a long, analytical study to find a long-term solution, not a quick fix."

For example, task force members are likely to consider establishing "transitional housing" in the city, Chavez said.

Such housing plans provide homeless people with temporary residences and often special assistance to enable them to regain autonomy. Transitional housing can include medical care, counseling, child care and job-training programs, Chavez explained.

"We're not interested in the quick fix where we house them overnight and kick them out the next day," Chavez said. "For those people that can be rehabilitated, we want to find a way to get them back into the mainstream and be productive."

The group's immediate goal is to assess the size and composition of the city's homeless population, which is believed to have increased in recent years. The task force's inaugural meeting is scheduled for Jan. 30.

"We don't have any statistics" about the size of the homeless population, she said, "but you can see the homeless street people sleeping under door fronts and under bridges. We're getting more phone calls from people concerned or complaining about the homeless."

Obtaining such statistical information is necessary to receive state or federal assistance, said Councilwoman Melba Bishop, who is also on the task force.

"We intend to find as many funding sources as possible," Bishop said. "But, when we apply, the first thing we'll be asked is, 'How did you document your need?' If we can't tell them, we won't be getting any money."
