October 9, 1989, Monday, Home Edition

COLLEGE

We just crammed the new linens and Mac along with the shoe boxes of early life mementoes into the Cadillac and drove our third -- and last -- child to college. We can set up a dorm room like professionals by now, and have learned how to dash off frequent, neutral, comforting letters.

Our current 18 year old leaves home even better prepared and more mature than her brother and sister four and two years ago.

But her parents fixated at the stage of having teen-agers around. Despite our anticipated pleasurable ignorance of the hours she will keep and the diet she'll choose, we dreaded the goodby. Although we will be free to have fish and eggplant, foods she hates, for dinner every night, we will miss her.

The laundry bin will stand empty; the phone won't ring. Our excitement about the professors and peers who will make her think and grow cannot match our selfish need for her to need us.

We take the kids to college and they're fine. We only keep the children who they were and meet the people they will be when they visit for Thanksgiving.

BARBARA C. MARSHALL

Irvine
