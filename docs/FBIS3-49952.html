Language: English Article Type:BFN [Text] Brussels, Jan 20 (TANJUG) -- Bosnian Moslem leader Alija Izetbegovic was told on Thursday at NATO headquarters in Brussels that the Western alliance would intervene in the Bosnian civil war only at the request of the United Nations. Izetbegovic, who was meeting NATO Deputy Secretary-General Amadeo de Franchihis, reiterated the Moslem position for resolving the conflict in the former Yugoslav republic of Bosnia-Herzegovina as presented at the peace talks in Geneva, a NATO spokesman told TANJUG. The peace talks ended on Wednesday without any concrete results because Bosnian Moslems again rejected an internationally drawn peace plan although the representatives of the other two Bosnian nations -- the Serbs and the Croats -- had met European demands for ensuring to the Moslems one-third of the territory. The NATO spokesman said de Franchihis once again confirmed that NATO was ready to contribute to implementing any Bosnia peace agreement and help plan any action demanded by the United Nations. The spokesman refused to comment on the details of Izetbegovic's meeting with de Franchihis save for saying that it was informative in nature and that Izetbegovic's visit to Brussels was private in character and organized at his request. U.N. General-Secretary Butrus Butrus-Ghali said on Thursday he had not received any requests for a military intervention in Bosnia and that he could not decide alone on that issue, but only in consultations with the Security Council. Butrus-Ghali was officially reminded of this by Russia, one of the five Security Council members with veto powers, which opposes the use of force in Bosnia. Diplomats say that special U.N. envoy in ex-Yugoslavia Jasushi Akashi said in a report to the secretary-general he was explicitely against the use of NATO troops for a military intervention in Bosnia. Well-informed sources say Akashi has established that the Serbs are willing to allow the replacement of Canadian U.N. peace-keepers in the Moslem enclave of Srebrenica in eastern Bosnia, and that the reopening of the airport in Moslem-controlled Tuzla is not so urgent because humanitarian convoys can reach the area by land. At its summit in Brussels ten days ago, NATO threatened to mount air strikes against Bosnian Serbs who the Western military alliance alleged would not allow the replacement of the U.N. troops in Srebrenica and reopening of Tuzla airport.
