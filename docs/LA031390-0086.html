March 13, 1990, Tuesday, Home Edition

SIOUX CITY CRASH PROMPTS FAA ORDER TO ALTER DC-10S

The Federal Aviation Administration on Monday ordered air carriers to modify all DC-10 jetliners' hydraulic systems, hoping to make them less vulnerable to the kind of crippling accident that last year caused the fiery crash of a commercial aircraft.

The FAA's order came in direct response to the July 19 destruction of a United DC-10 in Sioux City, Iowa. In that accident, the plane's tail engine exploded, severing the three independent hydraulic lines that powered all of its controls and sending the aircraft toward a cartwheeling crash landing that killed 112 of the 296 people aboard.

In a statement, the FAA said placing electronic shut-off valves and check valves in the hydraulic system "would keep the hydraulic fluid from leaking out in the event hydraulic lines in the tail are cut by flying debris, as was the case in last July's accident."

Such a change, the statement said, would "preserve enough hydraulic power following damage to the system to enable the flight crew to safely fly and land the airplane," even if all three hydraulic lines were severed in the tail section.

The FAA's order follows design changes recommended for the airliner by its manufacturer, McDonnell Douglas Corp., of St. Louis. It is likely to be given final approval after a period of public comment ending May 1, after which airlines would have six months to make the modifications in DC-10-10s and a year for all other DC-10 models.

The DC-10-10 is the DC-10 model most widely in use for domestic flights. It is similar to McDonnell Douglas' five other design variants but has a comparatively shorter range, a company spokesman said.

The 130 DC-10-10s in service are first in line to be modified because "they are, unfortunately, the aircraft identified with the Sioux City accident," said Steve Swinney, a spokesman for Douglas Aircraft Co., McDonnell Douglas' Long Beach-based component that will coordinate the modification with the airlines.

McDonnell Douglas and the FAA estimate that the cost to modify each of the 243 U.S.-registered DC-10s, including 59 KC-10s in use by the military, will be $25,000 to $29,000 per plane. The FAA expects foreign aircraft authorities to follow its example in ordering the modification of the 185 foreign-registered DC-10s in service.

United Capt. Alfred C. Haynes, the pilot during the Sioux City disaster who is widely credited for having saved the lives of most passengers aboard, has told investigators that the only way he had been able to turn the plane was by increasing the thrust of one of the wing-mounted engines and decreasing it in the other. However, he recalled, the plane had been sapped of hydraulic fluid and had no way to get its nose up.

With the FAA's proposal for less vulnerable hydraulics, the pilot of a DC-10 suffering comparable damage still would lose control of most of the tail-mounted controls -- the rudder, which turns the airliner, and the elevators, which help control climb and descent.

But other controls, such as the wing-mounted ailerons that allow the pilot to keep the plane level, would remain operable, the FAA said. So would the wing flaps, which primarily slow the aircraft when landing.

Also, if the system controlling the horizontal stabilizer trims in the plane's tail was left intact, the FAA said, the crew could control the angle and rate of descent.

The FAA already is under way with an ultrasonic inspection for possible microscopic defects in DC-10 engine fan disks.
