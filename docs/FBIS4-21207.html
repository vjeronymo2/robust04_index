Detailed PY1504190694 Lima EXPRESO in Spanish 4 Apr 94 p A8 PY1504190694 Lima EXPRESO Spanish BFN [Article by Liz Mineo] [Text] Since February 1978, when the government promulgated the first national antinarcotics law, the antinarcotics struggle in the country has been characterized by its isolated efforts and meager results. At the end of the 1970's, farmed coca production did not surpass 30,000 hectares. Last year it surpassed 150,000 hectares according to the Ofecod [Executive Office for Drug Control], which is under the jurisdiction of the Interior Ministry. Some less conservative statistics suggest the total is about 300,000 hectares. U.S. sources have stated that during the period 1978 to 1993, drug trafficking activities which were previously concentrated in the Upper Huallaga Valley expanded at the national level. Some 200,000 families now farm coca and manufacture cocaine paste. Drug-related activities generate an income of about $2 billion per year within the Peruvian economy. Many experts believe the failure of the antidrug struggle follows the lack of an antidrug strategy and the absence of an organization to plan, coordinate and implement it. The national drug prevention and drug control plan seeks to resolve one of the most vulnerable points of the antidrug struggle in the country by establishing an organization to centralize all government efforts in this field. The General Law on Drugs foresaw in 1978 the need to establish an organization to draft a drug control policy, to implement it and to coordinate all the actions to be developed. Law No. 22095 established the Multi-Sectorial Drug Control Committee (Comucod) [Comite Multisectorial de Control de Drogas], an organization to approve the government's antidrug policy; and the Ofecod, an executive and coordinating organization. Under the law, Comucod was to comprise six cabinet members and an alternate from the Supreme Court. The truth is that Comucod did not fulfill the duties with which it was entrusted by law and never really worked. Ofecod came under the jurisdiction of the Interior Ministry. It was to have controlled drugs, and implemented and coordinated government actions. As it turned out, Ofecod handled administrative functions of disposing of the goods seized by the police during antidrug operations and ignored the other duties with which it was entrusted by law. Experts believe neither of these organizations completely fulfilled its duties. This, undoubtedly, directly influenced the expansion of coca farming and trafficking at the national level. The lack of leadership, and the dilution and ineffectiveness of government actions in the antidrug struggle, became obvious in the early 1980's. The main institutions involved in the drug control program at the time were: the National Coca Enterprise, (Enaco); the Upper Huallaga Coca Control and Reduction Agency (Corah); and the Upper Huallaga Development Program (Peah). Enaco was the government organization responsible for the monopoly, legal marketing, and industrialization of coca. In fact, it only captured an insignificant part of Peru's coca harvest which was mainly being grown for drug traffickers. Corah, which was entrusted with coca eradication programs in the area, eliminated several hundred thousand hectares of coca in the Upper Huallaga region but paradoxically the valley became the main coca producing region in the world. Peah, which was responsible for promoting alternative farming programs in coca producing regions, yielded insufficient results and enjoyed little support among coca farmers. Some experts said the failure of these government organizations to control coca farming and trafficking lies in the fact that trafficking is merely considered a criminal activity that must be disciplined with police repression and severe legal punishments. For a long time the antinarcotics struggle was only based on repressive actions. The police were the main organization entrusted with conducting these repressive actions through its diverse specialized units--among them the Mobile Rural Patrol Unit (Umopar), which was involved in the compulsory eradication of thousands of hectares of coca; the former Drug Police Division (Dipog); the Illicit Drug Traffic Investigative Division (Dintid); the Antidrug Division (Dirandro), which later became the Antidrug Division (Dinandro). Even today repressive actions are still prevalent. The number of actions has risen since July 1992 when the government established the Internal Operations Front Command (COFI) which falls under the jurisdiction of the Armed Forces. This is the top organization, responsible for the planning, implementation, and control of the struggle against drugs and terrorism. In the last few years, in addition to repression, the authorities have introduced the strategy of alternative development. This program seeks to replace economies based on coca farming with the alternative farming of legal crops and an improvement in the standard of life in coca-producing regions. The Peruvian Government has acknowledged the prevalence of the economic and social problems that encourage trafficking. It paid particular attention to alternative farming but its results in practice have been almost nil. Neither the Institute for Alternative Development (Idea), nor the Autonomous Authority for Alternative Development (Aada) were ever implemented. Considering the antidrug draft bill grants ``the highest priority'' to the alternative development program, there are great expectations that this time around, this program will finally be implemented.
