January 22, 1990, Monday, Home Edition

MARKET BEAT: WESTWOOD ONE IS A CLASSIC WALL ST. TUG OF WAR

Back in 1985, Westwood One had a great story to sell, and Wall Street bought it. Instead of merely syndicating radio programs, it built its own radio network by acquiring existing networks that were in trouble. First, it bought the Mutual Broadcasting System, followed by the NBC Radio Network two years later, making Westwood the country's biggest radio network after ABC. Its stock, which sold for as low as $4.375 in 1984, shot to as high as $32 in 1987.

More recently, Westwood has added radio stations in New York, Los Angeles and San Diego, among other properties. But fast growth may have taken its toll on the $100-million-plus Culver City-based company. Quips one short-seller: "It's a Cinderella story gone sour."

Indeed, its stock is resting near its 52-week lows, in part because of a slower-than-expected turnaround in NBC, and the bears believe that it will go much lower. It closed Friday in over-the-counter trading at $7.625 a share, up 12.50 cents.

(After the markets closed Friday, the company announced that it expects to report a record loss for the latest fiscal year of about $24 million, or $1.57 to $1.63 a share.)

One short-seller who declined to be identified -- which is often the case -- believes that in the best case, assuming the company's assets are sold, the stock is worth $2.50 a share after paying off about $190 million in debt. Not only is Westwood's debt double its equity, but it lost $18.6 million for the first nine months of its last fiscal year and interest payments more than doubled.

What's more, Financial Statement Alert, which is produced by Los Angeles-based Kellogg Associates, has questioned some of Westwood's accounting. Kellogg has been early in spotting controversial activities at numerous companies whose stocks have subsequently declined, such as Prime Motor Inns, Cineplex Odeon and First Executive Corp. In the case of Westwood, Kellogg questions whether the company fiddled with its income statement in the third quarter "to avoid showing a larger loss."

But bulls are also sounding off on Westwood, which is quickly evolving into a classic Wall Street tug of war. Most analysts are as bullish as ever. The lower the stock goes, they say, the more attractive it becomes. On Thursday, in fact, Donaldson, Lufkin & Jenrette analyst Dennis Leibowitz issued an "aggressive buy" on the stock, dismissing bear talk that has mauled the stock in recent days as "the same old issues."

He's impressed by what he sees as the company's strong cash flow and the underlying value of its assets, which he thinks are worth about $20 a share.

As for the company's position on all of this, President William Battison takes issue with concerns raised by Kellogg & Associates. He insists that Westwood generates enough internal cash flow to service its debt, and he believes that the company will be profitable sometime in 1991.

Meanwhile . . . there's another less obvious cloud hanging over Westwood. A former Westwood executive has lodged a complaint with the Federal Election Commission against Chairman Norman Pattiz.

A commission spokesman confirmed receiving the complaint but would say only that the case hasn't yet been closed. The letter states that Pattiz "secretly financed campaign contributions in the name of his employees and their relatives, each of whom was subsequently reimbursed by Mr. Pattiz through his corporation."

According to the letter, the money, $13,000, went to the presidential campaign of Sen. Joseph Biden (D-Del.) as a way to enable Pattiz to testify before a broadcasting-related congressional subcommittee run by Sen. Daniel Inouye (D-Hawaii), who happened to co-chair Biden's short-lived presidential effort in 1988. The company said it is cooperating with the FEC to resolve the matter and believes there was no intentional violation of the law. It declined further comment, citing a confidentiality requirement imposed by the commission.
