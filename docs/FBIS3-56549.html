Language: Ukrainian Article Type:BFN [News conference by President Leonid Kravchuk with unidentified reporters at Borispil Airport in Kiev on 14 January -- recorded] [Text] [Reporter] Can you say if the document that was signed by the three presidents in the Kremlin today will have to be ratified by the Ukrainian parliament? Will it help resolve the issue of disarmament in general, both in Europe and on CIS territory? Is this primarily a new step or not? Third, to what extent will it help Ukraine's economy? [Kravchuk] The document is a continuation of the Ukrainian Supreme Council resolutions. If you recall, the reservations that it put forth were about compensation for tactical and strategic nuclear weapons, their technical maintenance, financial and technical aid, and security guarantees. These, approximately, were the main reservations of the Supreme Council. When you read that document, and it will be published, you will find that all of those demands were met. In other words, we can positively state that the executive branch has not been acting contrary to the Supreme Council resolutions but acting toward fulfilling the reservations put forth by the Supreme Council. I would like to emphasize this fact so that no one thinks, as some try to imply, that the president and the parliament were following their own separate courses. We did satisfy the reservations. I said in the past -- and I am confirming it now -- that we must join the Nuclear Nonproliferation Treaty. This is my deep conviction, because everyone has already joined it -- Russia, Belarus, Kazakhstan, and 131 countries. This is a normal step for Ukraine. This is why I hope that when we inform our parliament about our actions since December... [pauses] a very difficult time for Ukraine, which had to withstand pressure from all over the world. Not everyone understood our steps, not everyone understood our actions and there were grounds for this. This, I think, is a matter of principle. Now about your question as to who this document will serve. Both the talks with the U.S. President in Kiev and the trilateral talks have confirmed one irrefutable truth -- problems can be resolved only on the path of compromises and agreements. No reservations, no guarantees expressed in documents or agreements or declarations or anything else will be realized if one or another state follows the policy of confrontation instead of compromise. If, for instance, Ukraine... [pauses] now they say that Ukraine has been brought to its knees. This is absurd. First, Ukraine has come out of the situation with credit. We will be compensated and given assistance; we will be respected politically and in other ways. If we had opposed the adopted course and stood in the way of nuclear disarmament -- and it was anticipated -- if Ukraine had failed to fulfill its obligations, it would have blocked the further development of nuclear disarmament. It would have blocked it. START II depends on the resolution of the START I issue. I hope that there will be START III as well, and the final goal will be achieved in the total dismantling of nuclear weapons on our planet. Thus, Ukraine, having resolved this issue, unblocked nuclear disarmament for the whole world, for all of humanity. By taking this step Ukraine testified to the fact that there are no separate paths for Ukraine -- it follows the path of civilization and all human interest. This is the main issue for all the European and world community. By taking this step we have raised Ukraine's political prestige and image, we have confirmed that Ukraine's policies are predictable. Finally, there is one more point. Whatever they might say, there is an arrangement of forces in the world, an arrangement along political lines. And today, great powers are standing as the cornerstones of these forces and lines, including the United States of America. Relations among states develop depending on the influence made by these great powers. Whether one likes it or not, that's the way it is. Ambitions are out of place here. This is not a policy but just the basic way of thinking. If we -- contrary to the world's civilized development -- failed to accept compromises...[pauses] it does not mean that we have been humiliated; we have compromised, but the other party has also made compromises. Thus, we established normal relations. Let us imagine that Ukraine opposes the good will of other states, the good will of the world-wide development of nuclear disarmament, and the interests of the world states. However, not just their interests, but the common interests of mankind -- nuclear disarmament. This is important, because we can take into account the interests of the state. However, it will not coincide with the interests of mankind. What will be the international opinion of Ukraine? We, three presidents, of the United States, Ukraine, and Russia -- have reached an agreement and solved the issue in a civilized manner, in line with the reservations and the interests of the people of Ukraine. There are also economic perspectives opening for us; this was mentioned by President Clinton in Kiev. In other words, Ukraine is seen as a state that took those brakes off, enabling cooperation with the United States, with international financial organizations, and with Russia. Our acting prime minister, Yukhym Leonidovych Zvyahilskyy, and Defense Minister Radetskyy met today with their Russian counterparts and resolved a wide range of specific issues. Because, this [as heard] is the main barrier. Why is it the main barrier? Take for example, the military and technical side of the whole matter. We have nuclear weapons that we inherited, but we do not produce. Everyone, who understands at least something about this is aware that we are not capable of servicing them, providing maintenance. We just cannot keep them as a heavy burden on our shoulders. We cannot maintain them with due responsibility for our future and the future of our neighbors and all mankind. This has been proven by specialists and by everyone. Those who do not understand this are the ones who do not want to. Who is servicing nuclear weapons today? Russia. Well, when the president of Russia says: If you want to have nuclear weapons, why should we service them? He is right, is he not? This is a military and technical aspect. Further, look at the aspect of the use of nuclear weapons. Everybody knows it, and our minister said this, that some time in 1999 the service life of the nuclear weapons would expire. Excuse me, I am telling the entire nation that no reservations, no resolutions or edicts will prevent [as heard] the president, the defense minister, or the government when the service life of these horrible weapons expires. We are obliged to remove them because they are more dreadful than all the Chernobyl-type accidents taken together. And those people who accuse the executive authority today -- they either do not understand this or take advantage so as to confirm their particular policy, their particular course. There is yet another aspect. Take, for example, most superficial things. They say that recoding is possible, that something can be done to retain the nuclear arsenal as a deterrent. First, when it comes to the issue of recoding, it cannot be an abstract code. A question arises: Who is to be given the new code? Well, you ask those extreme forces -- they will tell you who -- I do not want to repeat the words. But the one who is to be given the new code -- will he wait until we finish recoding? What if someone would like it to be done, suddenly? Second, nuclear weapons require a nuclear industry. Without it these are not nuclear weapons. If all this is summed up...[pauses] and also it is necessary to have testing grounds to carry out tests. We do not have these. There is a whole complex of problems facing Ukraine. The situation can be assessed as follows: We have nuclear weapons. You do not touch us because if you come and do something suddenly, they can explode spontaneously, therefore you risk dying, together with us. In other words, this is an inhuman act. We have a dangerous thing in our hands which may explode. If it explodes and an opponent dies, it is his business, he himself makes his way to such an end. But he wants millions of people to die, our neighbors included. Who can put up with this? Who can regard this as normal? I cannot stop wondering if this is a political game, if this is an election game -- one can understand this somehow. But it is necessary to sit down at a table, place facts here and facts there. If these are facts they are facts. But if these are mere words and appeals, and deception of the people who experienced the Chernobyl catastrophe -- who knows what it is -- if these are attempts to put our people in jeopardy, presenting the people of the young state -- which is just in the making -- as a monster to the whole world, so that they would not recognize it in Europe and the world. This is not serious. This is why I think that this is our political, economic, humanistic victory. This is our victory. Not mine, not Ukraine's but our common victory. The victory of Ukraine, Russia, the United States of America, the victory of the entire world. If we all maintain friendship and normal relations with Russia and the United States and others, we will have our guarantees. But to oppose this, and exhibiting our high ambitions and proceeding from postulates having nothing to do with the understanding of the situation may be dangerous. I am happy with one matter: Those particularly vocal about this are a minority and they demonstrate their total ignorance, and second, presenting themselves as patriots they, as a matter of fact, do not help Ukraine with their actions, to put it mildly.
