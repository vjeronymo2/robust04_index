June 16, 1990, Saturday, San Diego County Edition

JAZZ REVIEW;

JAMES MOODY AT ELARIO'S: MORE THAN JUST SAX

Jazzman James Moody says that a true master can handle many instruments with equal ease. Wednesday, the first of his five nights at Elario's, he proved that he practices what he preaches. Exotic streams of bebop-based invention came from tenor and alto saxes and flute, and even his quirky singing voice.

Backed by locals Bob Magnusson on bass, Jim Plank on drums and Harry Pickens on piano, Moody started off on tenor for Tadd Dameron's "Good Faith," with extended solos by Moody and Pickens.

Next came "Yesterdays" (by Jerome Kern, not Paul McCartney), with Moody switching to flute. Pickens settled in as the rhythm section's glue, building rhythmic undercurrents with his subtle chord combinations.

Pickens' gave his right hand the full run of the keyboard on an extended solo. He's a thoughtful, percussive player reminiscent of McCoy Tyner, but with a more cerebral, calculating approach.

On saxes, Moody shows the same kind of intensity that characterized his earliest work with Dizzy Gillespie in the late 1940s. Long strings of notes tumbled out with a precision that forced both an intellectual and emotional response. How does a guy play so fast without the squeals and squeaks that sneak from the horns of mere mortals?

"Moody's Mood for Love" featured Moody's facile voice handling both male and female parts in a love dialogue. He also sang "Happy Birthday" to a friend in the audience, improvising a series of exotic yodels. There's an album worth of singing in this guy.

"I Can't Get Started With You," a tender ballad, provided the first set's highlight, as Moody hoisted his alto to put on an amazing display of virtuosity, sliding from bright, clear highs to buttery smooth lows.

If there was an overall disappointment, it was that he often yielded solo space when his own playing was just hitting the hot zone, especially on "Cherokee" and "Hey Jim."
