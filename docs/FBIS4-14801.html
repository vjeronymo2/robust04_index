BFN ["Exclusive" interview with UN Secretary General Butrus Butrus-Ghali by unidentified RIA-NOVOSTI correspondent, commissioned by ROSSIYSKAYA GAZETA; date, place not given: "Butrus Butrus-Ghali: I Am Inspired by the Active Efforts of Russian Diplomacy"] [Text] UN Secretary General Butrus Butrus-Ghali arrived on an official visit in Moscow yesterday. He plans to meet with President Boris Yeltsin, the leadership of the Russian Federation Federal Assembly State Duma and Federation Council, the foreign and defense ministers, and also with Aleksiy II, patriarch of Moscow and All Russia. Butrus Butrus-Ghali pointed out that he visited Moscow many times while he was Egyptian foreign minister. This is the second time that he has been to the Russian capital in his capacity as UN secretary general. He said that one of the main aims of his visit to the Russian Federation is to "strengthen direct ties with the country's leadership" and also to help "boost Russia's contribution to UN activity." Before his arrival he gave an exclusive interview for ROSSIYSKAYA GAZETA. [ROSSIYSKAYA GAZETA] Do you support the idea that Russia's efforts to avert and resolve conflict situations on the territory of the former USSR should receive international status? To what extent and in what way is the United Nations Organization prepared to take part in peacekeeping efforts in places like Nagorno-Karabakh, Abkhazia, Tajikistan, and the Dniester Region? [Butrus-Ghali] As for the conflicts that have been unleashed in certain independent states on the territory of the former Soviet Union, the United Nations and the CSCE have agreed on a division of labor with a view to preventing duplication of effort. In accordance with this formula the CSCE has assumed a peacekeeping role in the Nagorno-Karabakh conflict, in Moldova, and in Georgia -- South Ossetia. While the United Nations, on the other hand, has taken the lead with regard to the conflicts in Tajikistan and between Georgia and Abkhazia. You may be aware that the United Nations opened a small mission in Tajikistan in late 1992. My new special envoy to Tajikistan, Ambassador Piriz-Ballon [name as transliterated], recently got the parties to the conflict to agree to meet in Moscow. A UN observers' mission in Georgia was opened by the Security Council last August. This is the first operation of its kind on the territory of one of the former Soviet republics. My special envoy in Georgia, Ambassador Brunner [name as transliterated], has held several rounds of political talks with representatives of the Georgian Government and the Abkhazians. The Russian Federation is acting as mediator in the Georgian-Abkhazian peace process and my special envoy has received vigorous support from [Russian] Deputy Foreign Minister Pastukhov, President Yeltsin's special envoy in Georgia. We are continuing our efforts to seek an all-embracing solution to this conflict. [ROSSIYSKAYA GAZETA] Whenever questions of the former USSR's Baltic republics are examined, the United Nations only places emphasis on the Russian troop withdrawal, while Russia underlines its concern over the violation of ethnic minorities' political and civil rights in the Baltics. Do you think that this problem exists and merits the attention of the United Nations? [Butrus-Ghali] The General Assembly has extended my mediator's mandate with regard to the troop withdrawal from the Baltic republics. Last year my special envoy, Ambassador Tommy Cox, visited Moscow and the three Baltic countries and I reported back to the General Assembly on his information and findings. I am inspired by the recent Latvian-Russian agreement and I hope that the talks between Estonia and Russia, which are expected to resume early in April, will also be completed shortly. I know that 25 million Russians have ended up outside Russia as a result of the breakup of the former Soviet Union. What is more, I know about the difficulties which Russians living outside their homeland, including in Estonia and Latvia, are experiencing. [ROSSIYSKAYA GAZETA] The moves to form a Muslim-Croat federation in Bosnia-Herzegovina to some extent contravene the peace plan that was agreed with the participation of the UN special envoy, a plan which presupposed creating a confederation of three ethnic states in Bosnia, precisely indicating their territories and borders. Does the United Nations intend to formulate a new initiative for an all-embracing settlement in Bosnia-Herzegovina and in the former Yugoslavia as a whole? How do you see Russia's role in resolving the Yugoslav conflict? [Butrus-Ghali] I welcome the recent agreement between the Bosnian Muslims and the Bosnian Croats regarding the formation of a federation in Bosnia-Herzegovina. The cochairmen of the Steering Committee of the International Conference on the Former Yugoslavia also welcomed this accord. At the same time I very much hope that representatives of the Bosnian Serbs will take part in the talks to find a settlement to the conflict in Bosnia-Herzegovina in the near future. The Conference on the Former Yugoslavia will continue its work since it is the Security Council's approved means of securing an all-embracing settlement to all the conflicts in the former Yugoslavia. I am inspired by Russian diplomacy's vigorous creative efforts with regard to the former Yugoslavia. In particular, I welcome the recent talks between representatives of Serbian Krajina and the Croatian Government that took place in Russia's embassy in Zagreb under the auspices of Deputy Foreign Minister Churkin. I am grateful to Russia for its help for the UN Protection Forces [UNPROFOR] in unblocking Tuzla Airport and for Russia's willingness to send an extra 300 servicemen to the UNPROFOR.
