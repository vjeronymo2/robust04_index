June 14, 1990, Thursday, Orange County Edition

STATE WON'T SUE ACCOUNTANTS WHO AUDITED LINCOLN

The state agency that regulates accountants decided Wednesday that it would not sue any of the three accounting firms that performed audits or worked on the financial statements of the failed Lincoln Savings & Loan and its bankrupt parent company, American Continental Corp.

Instead, the 12-member State Board of Accountancy voted unanimously in a closed-door session to continue with its ongoing investigation to determine if any disciplinary action should be taken against California-licensed accountants in the scandal, according to Jack Kazanjian, a Fresno accountant and board president.

The board, during a special meeting on the Lincoln matter, decided not to pursue civil litigation now because there is "far too much information" yet to be learned, Kazanjian said.

The board has authority to discipline licensed accountants and to sue accountants and accounting firms for violations of state business laws that prohibit false and misleading statements. About 23,000 large and small investors in American Continental debt securities -- as well as a host of state and federal agencies and politicians -- relied, in part, on financial audits and other statements by the accounting firms about the health of American Continental.

The primary accounting firm targeted by investors in pending litigation is the former Arthur Young & Co., now called Ernst & Young. Other defendants are Arthur Andersen & Co. and the former Touche, Ross & Co., now Deloitte & Touche.

The investors now hold about $250 million in worthless securities after the company filed for bankruptcy on April 13, 1989. The following day, regulators seized Lincoln. The S&L is expected to become one of the nation's biggest thrift-repair jobs, costing taxpayers at least $2 billion.
