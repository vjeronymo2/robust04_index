February 18, 1990, Sunday, Home Edition

ERMA BOMBECK: SIGNS SAY GOD IS NOT A VEGETARIAN

Food shoppers are being faced with a major decision these days. Should they buy a zucchini or make the house payment? It's happened before. Every time the South experiences a deep freeze, the price of fruits and vegetables soars out of reach.

To the veggie freaks whose idea of pigging out is to graze on the front lawn, these are indeed dark days. For me, it's an omen.

Two weeks ago I said, "God, if you want me to get off this crummy diet, give me a sign." The very next day in the supermarket I picked up a single tomato. It was marked $3. At that very moment, I heard organ music. The tomato was returned and I hit the Dove bars.

It doesn't take much to dissuade me from buying and eating greens. Not only am I not a vegetable disciple, I have never been convinced that vegetables make you thin. The first time my mom took me to the zoo, I watched as the keeper threw a bucket of greens to an orangutan. I wasn't the brightest kid on the block, but I figured here was an animal that exercised regularly, ate healthy and got a lot of sleep, and look how he turned out. He had a waistline the size of a Disney parking lot.

That sorta set the pace for me. I'm possibly the only person who can visit a salad bar and run up a calorie tab of 1,200 on one plate. I start with a bed of croutons and drown them in a sea of blue cheese with bacon bits, then garnish it with two cherry tomatoes.

As is generally the case, carbohydrate eaters are attracted to mates who thrive on proteins. I came from a family where gravy was a beverage and ketchup was a vegetable. My husband's family bought lettuce by the field.

After we married, it wasn't easy trying to blend our eating habits. I thought a long time before I made a decision to bring a child into a world polluted by Brussels sprouts.

When my husband looked into the refrigerator last week, he said, "Where's the vegetables?"

I handed him an artichoke with the price on it and he gasped. "Let me guess. You bought a designer artichoke? I thought I told you to get eggplant."

"If you want me to buy eggplant," I said, "I'll arrange financing, but the way I look at it, we are being given a sign that the world is coming to an end as we know it."

"What kind of nonsense is that?" he asked.

"All the foods that experts say are good for you are dwindling -- fresh fish, vegetables, fruits."

"The shortage can be explained," he said. "The weather is an act of God."

"That's what I've been telling you," I said. "Hear the organ?"
