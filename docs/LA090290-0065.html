September 2, 1990, Sunday, Home Edition

ERMA BOMBECK: FIRST DAY OF SCHOOL IS A BUMMER

The first day of school is rough on mothers -- especially the young ones who are saying goodby to an era. I dropped in on Jean Carp to hold her hand and reassure her that everyone would survive the day.

"Did you get Kenny off to school?" I asked.

"Yes, finally," she groaned, pouring herself a cup of coffee.

"What happened?"

"He didn't want to go."

"You're kidding."

"No, he said the kids wouldn't like him."

"Didn't you tell him how ridiculous that was?"

"Yes, I told him how during the summer he had made a lot of friends. Then he said his teachers had it in for him."

"Why would he say a thing like that when he doesn't even know them yet?"

"I don't know. I almost got him out of the door when he came back and changed his clothes for the third time. Said his belt was too wide and his shoes too clean."

"Oh, good grief."

"That's when he slumped down into a chair and said, 'I'll never find the restroom or the cafeteria. I'm surely not going to ask someone.' "

"What did you tell him?"

"I told him I'd put a note in his pocket with B-O-Y-S printed on it and pack his lunch."

"Did that do it?"

"Nope. He wanted to know how many days he had to put in until Christmas vacation."

"What an attitude," I said, shaking my head.

"He got out to the driveway. Then he came back in again and said, 'Why do I have to go to school anyway? Why can't I just stay home and help you? I could run errands and take out the garbage and watch the baby for you when you go to the dentist.' Can you believe he actually offered to get a regular job?"

"Incredible."

"I really felt sorry for him, though," said Jean. "When I watched him walk down the street clutching his lunch bag and hanging onto those pitiful few books and a new pencil, I wanted to call him back. But when a man is 44 years old and the new principal, he has no choice."

I shook my head. "Don't be surprised if he bolts at lunch."
