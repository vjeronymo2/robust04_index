BFN [By Daniel Kwan, recently in Taipei] [Text] The separatist tendency within Taiwan's leadership is far more dangerous to cross-strait relations than the Qiandao Lake murders, according to former premier Hau Pei-tsun. Mr Hau, vice-chairman of the Kuomintang (KMT), lamented the decline of the ruling party and expressed worry that the KMT might lose to the opposition Democratic Progressive Party in the 1996 presidential election if its leadership failed to "wake up and face up to" its problems. In an interview with Hong Kong journalists, the former premier said the separatist arguments expressed by individual political leaders in Taiwan hurt cross-strait relations more than the Qiandao Lake murders. The murders, when three mainlanders killed 24 Taiwanese tourists on board a pleasure boat in the Qiandao Lake in Zhejiang Province in March, at one point almost brought Taiwan- mainland exchanges to a standstill. Although Mr Hau did not identify the political leaders, political analysts said he was referring to President Li Teng-hui who caused an uproar in the island last month when he described the KMT as a "foreign regime" and compared his government in Taiwan with those of Moses leading the Israelites out of Egypt in an interview with Japanese writer Ryotaro Shiba. When asked to comment on Mr Li's remarks, Mr Hau said: "Oh! All I can say is that I can hardly understand. I can't agree with this kind of talk. But I am not in power and I take no responsibility. These (arguments) are the most important reason behind the present poor relationship between the mainland and Taiwan," he said. "They are even far more harmful than the Qiandao Lake incident," he added. The former premier, who stepped down last year, also greatly disapproved of the rise of the so-called "strongman-like politics" in the KMT and did not hide his frustration in the interview that the KMT was no longer the same political party founded by the national father Dr Sun Yat-sen a century ago. Instead, it was the New Party -- a spin-off from the KMT last year -- that could truly claim to be the real KMT, he said. "In reality, it is the New Party which represents the traditional value of the KMT... But I can't join the New Party, as a real KMT, (I believe) one must stay in the party and try to do something to reform the party," he said. "The problem we (KMT) face today is that we can't find a consensus within the party. This is very unhealthy and this struggle for (political) power got mixed with the lust for (personal) gains. This is a fundamental problem facing the KMT today and we need to look into it seriously," he said. And Mr Li, who is the KMT chairman, could not deny responsibility for the present poor shape of the party, Mr Hau said. Although the former premier said he was optimistic Taiwan and the mainland would eventually be reunited, he made it clear Taipei could not accept the "one-country-two-system" formula suggested by Beijing. Instead of bullying Taiwan, Beijing should resume political reform and democratisation before both sides could engage in meaningful discussions on reunification. But Mr Hau said both Taiwan and the mainland must first accept the principles of "one China, (reunification) through peaceful and democratic means" as the "basis for negotiation." "In my view, cross-strait relations can improve gradually if we (can accept) these principles."
