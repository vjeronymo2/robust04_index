Language: Japanese Article Type:CSO [Article by Toshifumi Hirai, general manager of the Aircraft & Ordnance Division of MITI] [Text] The aircraft industry is in a predicament worldwide as a result of cutbacks in defense spending and poor business conditions among the airlines. Although the Japanese aircraft industry has been growing steadily up until now -- first, in connection with overcoming the handicap of being left behind by the rest of the world as a result of Japan's defeat in World War II and, second, in connection with surviving in the narrow niche in the market -- it has yet to face many problems that are now known to other high-tech industries. Historically, it is a universal fate of the aircraft industry of any nation that it can not live without the support of the government, because of its close ties to the defense industry and because of the huge risks involved in development projects. In addition to that, the Japanese aircraft industry is unique in that the degree of its dependency on defense needs is much higher than that in the United States and Europe; there are only a few manufacturers that are dedicated to aircraft production; defense needs consist of mostly license productions; and the bulk of commercial aircraft and engine productions are also limited to subassembly-level productions. Although we cannot deny that such an industrial structure has made sense up until now, it is obvious that, in light of the situation the world aircraft industry will be at the threshold of the twenty-first century, an increase in the ratio of commercial needs is urgently needed and supporting factors such as R&D capabilities and human resources need to improve.

Pursuit of Autonomy
If the first task consists in maintaining an optimum balance between risk-free defense needs and the risky commercial needs, the second task consists in tackling various projects that maintain the optimum balance. The projects that are currently under way can be divided into those that require developments under Japan's own leadership, such as YSX (commercial jet, seating 75-90 passengers) and the ``small engine'' project, and those futuristic large-scale international projects such as the ``supersonic airplane'' and the ``ultralarge airplane.'' In addition to the above, there are projects that are already in the production or marketing phases, such as the ``B 777'' and ``V 2500 engine'' projects. Development of new technologies such as helicopters and new materials should not be overlooked, either. While all of those projects need to be promoted in a concerted manner to order to keep an optimum balance, it is important for us to realize that the premises for it have to be aggressive challenges in technological innovations and the pursuit of Japan's autonomy under the framework of international cooperation.
Maiden Flight in 21st Century
YS-11 was developed in Japan about 30 years ago (182 airplanes sold since); more than 100 of them are still in service around the world today. Many people wonder why that turboprop airplane seating 60 passengers is, for whatever reason, the only commercial airplane ever produced in Japan, a country of such technological capability. But times are changing, and many promising buds are about to unfold. The YSX project has entered the feasibility study phase already; if an economically and politically favorable conclusion comes out of it, development will start within two years, and the maiden flight will probably take place at the beginning of the twenty-first century.
Enhancing International Cooperation
Despite its current stagnation, the number of airline passengers worldwide is predicted to increase by 6 percent annually on a long-term basis. Amidst the worldwide trend of expectations of long-term stable growth of the aircraft industry, which is certain to bring about innovations and have synergistic effects on related industries, Japan must try to further advance and expand its horizons while enhancing international cooperation at the same time.
