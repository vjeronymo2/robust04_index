September 14, 1989, Thursday, San Diego County Edition

REGULATIONS SOUR SWEET SMELL OF FRESH BREAD

Baker Frank Fornaca Jr. is one of many people who think of the aroma of baking bread as one of life's simple pleasures. But, when government air-quality control officials in December branded the smell a pollutant, Fornaca knew that tough times were ahead for the baking industry.

Indeed, pending regulations would force Fornaca and other bakers to retrofit their ovens so as to curb baking emissions. That's a costly requirement for all bakers, but especially for smaller independent operations, said Fornaca, vice president and general manager of Fornaca Family Bakery -- San Diego county's largest independent, family-owned and operated bakery.

"Our retrofitting costs could be as much as $2 million," said Fornaca, citing another reason why being an independent baker is not easy these days.

The emission-control ruling is just one of many problems confronting the independent baker. Others include the high cost of equipment, fierce competition, consolidation and a younger generation's aversion to long baker's hours, problems that have led to a steady shrinking of the independent bakers' ranks.

The Number Fell

From 1935 to 1980, for example, the number of independent U. S. bakers plummeted from 10,000 to 1,000, industry officials say. Now only about 600 remain.

But, at a time when bread baking doesn't appear to be a good way to make dough, the Fornaca Family Bakery is thriving.

Established in 1912 by Italian immigrant Mario Fornaca, the bakery is now under the reign of a third generation of Fornacas. Still based at the National City bakery where Mario, Frank Jr.'s grandfather, delivered bread by horse and buggy, Fornaca now has about 100 distributors that take its products to customers as far north as Santa Barbara and east to El Centro.

The bakery has endured a shortage of workers during World War II, as well as during the Depression, and adapted to a changing environment to preserve a family business. Instead of competing head-to-head with plentiful and powerful white-bread bakers, such as Wonder Bread, Fornaca succeeded by carving a niche in a crowded marketplace. It won loyal customers by baking specialty and mixed-grain breads.

"We've been successful because we haven't tried to force-feed white bread," Fornaca said.

But not all bakers recognized the changing marketplace, industry experts say. Nor were they willing to experiment with new products.

"There are a lot of people who do it (bake white bread) and they're all competing for a fairly fixed market," said Bill Lockhart, a spokesman for the American Institute of Baking. The Manhattan, Kan.-based organization runs seminars about industry issues, operates a baking school, conducts nutrition research and inspects bakeries.

The combination of rising equipment and ingredient costs plus thin profit margins has persuaded many independent bakers to sell their businesses.

"To compete against Wonder Bread, you've got to buy the speedier, more rapid baking equipment," said Robert Pyle, president of the Independent Bakers Assn., a national trade association based in Washington. "Such production lines are very expensive, which means you have to put money back in to the bakery. Not everyone is willing to do that.

Baking Is Hard Work

But, more often than not, economic factors aren't the principal ones behind the death of a family bakery.

"Baking bread is hard work," Pyle said. "The younger generation doesn't want to get up at 3 a.m. to run the bakery. They would rather be at the beach."

But the Fornacas say they were destined to bake bread and are striving to maintain a family tradition. In fact, Fornaca has rejected several suitors who have offered to buy the bakery.

"We're good at what we do," Fornaca said. "Besides, this is all we know how to do."

Mario Fornaca immigrated to Los Angeles from Italy via Ellis Island in 1907. After learning the baker's trade, he moved to San Diego to start his own shop five years later. Mario turned over the company to his two sons, Frank and Felice, in 1952. Back then, the bakery had 25 employees and turned out about 540 loaves each day.

Today, the family bakery has 235 workers and bakes about 100,000 loaves daily, including such well known brands as Bohemian Hearth, Holsum Grain, Deli Breads and others.

The Fornaca bakery produces more than 1 million pounds of bread each week. Fornaca revenues are expected to reach $30 million in 1989, a 10% increase from last year.

Although the Fornaca Bakery has undergone impressive growth in the past, new obstacles such as oven-emission controls, threaten its success.

On Sept. 20, the Bay Area Air Quality Management District in San Francisco is expected to pass a regulation that will require bakeries that produce more than 100,000 pounds of bread daily to install "after-burners" in existing ovens to reduce the ethanol emitted into the air. The regulation will also require new ovens to be equipped with such devices.

"Ethanol (a baking by-product) is a volatile organic compound that is a precursor to the formation of ozone," a pollutant that the local air quality boards are charged with lowering, said Lucia Libretti, a spokeswoman for the San Francisco region's air-pollution-control district.

Targeting Bakeries

The Bay Area is the first, and so far, only district in the nation targeting bakeries in efforts to lower pollution. But the baking industry is watching events there closely because it fears that other districts will follow suit.

Indeed, the San Diego County Air Pollution Control District and the South Coast Air Quality Management District -- which oversees Los Angeles, Orange, Riverside and San Bernardino counties -- have listed the installation of oven-emission controls as objectives in their pollution control plans.

But Fornaca maintains that the amount of ethanol put out by bakers is so small that it seems almost ridiculous to go after it.

"Installing after-burners on any new ovens that we construct is one thing, but asking us to go back and retrofit all the old ones is asking a little too much," Fornaca said. According to air quality officials, it would cost $400,000 to install after-burners in existing ovens. Building new ovens equipped with such controls would cost $800,000.

Another problem concerns bakers' delivery trucks. The Los Angeles City Council is considering a law restricting delivery trucks on freeways during rush hours to reduce congestion. The South Coast Air Quality Management District is considering a similar curb on a regional basis in the Los Angeles area.

Such restrictions could disrupt and complicate delivery of baked goods to customers, Fornaca said. Nevertheless, he is confident the family bakery will find ways to overcome these problems. After running a successful business for 77 years, Fornaca has reason to be optimistic.

No Rocket Science

"We're not talking about rocket science here," Fornaca said. "Bread baking is a basic business. As long as we do the basics right, provide a good product and good service, we're going to do all right."

At first, the Fornaca bakery only produced French and Italian breads. But, when Mario Fornaca's two sons inherited the business in the 1950s, they diversified the product line. They introduced Hillbilly bread, a light wheat bread considered unique at a time when grocers' shelves were stocked almost completely with white bread. The introduction of other brands followed, including Bohemian Hearth, Holsum Grain, Deli Breads, Italiano and Poulsbo, which is a bread made from sunflower seeds.

Although the bakery does most of its business with major restaurants and supermarkets, Fornaca also owns seven thrift stores (small bakery shops). Five of the outlets are in San Diego County, one in El Centro and the other in Riverside County. The Fornaca bakery also sells to chains such as Price Club and supplies Camp Pendleton.
