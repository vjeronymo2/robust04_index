January 29, 1990, Monday, Home Edition

DR. JOYCE BROTHERS: BANKROLL, SEX STYLE RELATED?

DEAR DR. BROTHERS: Both my husband and I are very generous with money so we're surprised that our 17-year-old son is so cautious with his spending. His father says that he saves almost everything and has built up quite a bank account. For the past year, he's had only one girlfriend and he seems as cautious about women as he is about his money. What does this mean? -- V.R.

DEAR V.R.: What you're describing isn't necessarily bad. There does seem to be some relationship between fidelity and closeness with money. Psychotherapist Rosemarie Schultz, who wrote the book "Sex and Money: Strange Bedfellows," says that the way a person handles his bankroll is frequently the way he handles his sex life.

Generally, careful spenders are equally cautious about the selection of their sex partners, while big spenders may use less discretion and are more likely to be unfaithful.

There are just two points, however, that I'd like to make. One, it's generally a good idea for young people to date a number of different people before going steady, because this is one of the ways in which they learn about themselves. And two, it would be a good idea to teach your son to be compassionate and generous toward those who have less than he. Empathy and generosity toward the less fortunate needs to be taught. Too much thrift can soon become greed.

DEAR DR. BROTHERS: I guess because my wife was left alone a lot as a child, she won't leave our baby's side for a moment. But I would like my wife to attend social functions with me related to my work. Is it necessary to be with an infant every moment? -- G.B.

DEAR G.B.: No. It's healthier to have some separation. Of course, babies need round-the-clock care and nurturing, but this doesn't mean that the mother or father have no time to themselves. The child's legitimate needs should be filled as soon as possible, but there are also times when the baby should be left alone to sleep in her own crib or to play by herself.

During the first three or four months, it's a good idea for the baby to sleep in the parents' room, but after that it's fine for him to sleep in a room nearby.

Little children need to get used to the idea that when mother leaves the room or the house she'll return later. If the baby cries, that's fine. This is a part of growing up and the fact that mother does return is one of the ways the child builds a sense of faith and trust.

Parents need time off for their health, as well as their child's. Encourage your wife to go with you. Find a responsible substitute to care for your baby.
