September 30, 1989, Saturday, San Diego County Edition

IMPRESSIVE LIST OF PLAYS PLANNED ON SAN DIEGO'S COLLEGE CIRCUIT

A world premiere musical that had a reading at the Eugene O'Neill Theatre Center in Waterford, Conn., two world premiere dramas, a West Coast premiere of a Eugene Ionesco play and a San Diego premiere by Canadian playwright George Walker.

It sounds like an impressive season for any professional company, but these shows actually are a composite of what is scheduled on the seasons of the theater departments at UC San Diego, San Diego State University and United States International University.

All these shows will be performed, and many will be directed, by graduate students. Still, the quality can be high. "Strindberg Sonata," a production last year at UCSD, picked up three San Diego Critics Circle nominations this year -- best production, best direction (Anne Bogart) and best new play (by Bogart, Jeff Halpern et. al).

More commonly, the season can be used as an opportunity to preview talent before it goes professional.

Prominent San Diego State graduates in San Diego theater include Craig Noel, executive director of the Old Globe Theatre, Kit Goldman, managing director of the Gaslamp Quarter Theatre, Dianne Holly, a faculty member at San Diego State, who is resident costume designer at the Gaslamp, and Kathy Najimy, co-star of the San Diego-spawned Off-Broadway hit, "The Kathy and Mo Show."

UC San Diego, which works exclusively with the La Jolla Playhouse, most recently gave us Jim Lewis, co-writer of "Dangerous Games," the Playhouse production that is soon to open on Broadway. And USIU's latest undergraduate success story is John Barrowman, who took a leave of absence to star in "Anything Goes" in London's West End.

The world premiere musical "First Star" promises to be the star of the upcoming SDSU season. Written by Steve Lutvak and Mitchell Ivers, it tells the true story of America's first acting troupe, the Hallam Company, which came to America from Great Britain in the late 18th Century. Theater department chairwoman Alicia Annas has high hopes for the production, which was commissioned by theater professor Robert Chapel. The contract stipulates that San Diego State will be credited in

any commercial productions.

"First Star" will have a workshop production in November followed by a full production May 4-12 at the Don Powell Theatre. It will finish a season at SDSU that is already in progress with the the world premiere of a new translation by Jeffrey Mason of the Alexandre Dumas fils story "Lady of the Camellias," through Oct. 7 at the Don Powell.

The rest of the SDSU season includes:

* "The Boys From Syracuse," the musical by Rodgers and Hart based on Shakespeare's "The Comedy of Errors," Oct. 20-28 at the Experimental Theatre.

* The West Coast premiere of Ionesco's "Man With Bags," a story of cultural estrangement in which a man returns to his nameless native country and conducts a nightmarishly frustrating search for his family, friends and youth, Nov. 10-18 at the Don Powell.

* John Guare's "Landscape of the Body," about the struggles of a mother and son to survive in Greenwich Village, Feb. 17-24 at the Experimental Theatre.

* William Shakespeare's "The Winter's Tale," the story of a jealous king, (which may be changed for another Shakespeare play).

* "Zastrozzi: the Master of Discipline," a San Diego premiere about a cunning European criminal by Canadian playwright George Walker, March 30-April 7 at the Experimental Theatre.

One of the highlights of the UC San Diego season should be the work of acclaimed theater artist Andrei Serban (known for his electric stagings at Lincoln Center, La Mama Experimental Theatre, the New York Public Theatre and the Los Angeles Music Center), who will direct a play of his choosing April 11-15 at the Mandell Weiss Theatre.

The other is "The Trials of Don Edwardo," a new play by graduate student playwright Daniel Camacho, which will be the premiere production by the new Hispanic-American Theatre program, May 9-13 at the Warren Theatre. The play, which will be directed by Jorge Huerta, theater department faculty member and head of the Hispanic-American Theatre program, will be presented in West Germany as part of the "International Conference on Hispanics in the United States" in July and continue to tour Europe.

These two shows will conclude a season that begins with the participation of seven actors, three designers, two directors and one stage manager from the UCSD theater department in the La Jolla Playhouse production of "Macbeth," directed by Playhouse artistic director Des McAnuff Oct. 15-Nov. 19 at the Mandell Weiss Theatre. It continues with:

* "Fefu and Her Friends," by six-time Obie-award winner Maria Irene Fornes about a group of women in the 1970s who look back at their idealism in the 1930s, Nov. 1-12 at the Warren Theatre.

* "Tartuffe," Moliere's classic about a scoundrel who worms his way into the heart of a gullible aristocrat susceptible to his flattery, Nov. 29-Dec. 3, at the Warren Theatre.

* "The Bacchae," Euripides' tragedy about how a king's rejection of Bacchus, the god of wine and revelry, results in his own destruction, Jan. 31-Feb. 4 at the Mandell Weiss Theatre.

* "Sweet Patria," a new play by graduate student William Reyes, tells the story of a general in charge of the secret police in a small Latin American town who is forced to confront a past that has come back to haunt him, Feb. 14-18 at the Warren Theatre. It will be presented next summer as a workshop production by Teatro de la Esperanza in San Francisco.

* "From Morn to Midnight," by Georg Kaiser, an Expressionist tale of an Everyman who searches for an existence that affirms the significance of the human spirit, Feb. 28-March 4 at the Mandell Weiss Theatre.

At USIU, musical theater usually dominates the season, and this year is no exception, with four popular musicals and two non-musicals scheduled. All will be at the Theatre in Old Town with the exception of "Hello Dolly!," the Michael Stewart & Jerry Herman musical based on Thornton Wilder's play about busybody matchmaker Dolly Levi. "Hello Dolly!" will be presented at the Lyceum Theatre in Horton Plaza Feb. 14-March 11 as part of a USIU festival at the Lyceum, including presentations from the USIU ballet, orchestra and visual arts department.

The highlight of the USIU season, though, may well be the USIU debut of film producer and director Jack Ofield, winner of a Peabody, an Emmy and an American Film Festival Blue Ribbon. Ofield, who also is a professor at San Diego State, will be taking a departure from his recent productions for PBS to direct the show of his choice -- "The Fantasticks." The Tom Jones and Harvey Schmidt musical, still running Off Broadway after 29 years, tells the sweet and sour Romeo and Juliet story of two young lovers who meet despite a high garden wall that separates their houses, Jan. 31-March 18.

Surrounding these selections are:

* "The Mikado, or The Town of Titipu," the Gilbert and Sullivan classic about Nanki Poo's courtship of Yum-Yum who is sadly affianced to the Lord High Executioner, Koko, set in ancient Japan, Oct. 4-29.

* "The Front Page," the Ben Hecht and Charles MacArthur comedy about a tough reporter who tries to leave the business just before the story that may prove the biggest of his career begins to break, Nov. 15-Dec. 3.

* "Cyrano de Bergerac," Edmond Rostand's story of the poet whose great nose stands between him and the woman he loves, April 11-29.

* "Carnival," Michael Stewart and Robert Merrill's musical about an embittered carnival owner and his wandering troupe, May 16-June 10.
