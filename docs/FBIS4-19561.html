BFN [Luis Ayllon report: "Government Makes New Credit for Moscow Conditional on Gradual Repayment of Current Debt"] [Excerpt] Madrid -- The Spanish Government is prepared to grant the Russian Government new credit lines provided that Moscow begins to repay the debt of over 60 billion pesetas that it owes Spain, according to government sources. The government envisages converting into new credits 425 million hitherto unpaid dollars, as they are paid, and adding another $200 million on certain terms. Although the government was initially reluctant to grant Russia new credit facilities during President Boris Yeltsin's visit to Spain, Prime Minister Felipe Gonzalez has finally acceded to the wishes of the Russian authorities, who do not wish to return to their country empty-handed and without something to show Spain's political support for the current leaders. Late on Friday [15 April], ABC has learned, the Spanish negotiators reached an agreement with the Russian delegation to mobilize over the next four years, through 1997, around $925 million (around 130 billion pesetas), aimed at boosting economic relations between the two countries and Russia's development. The accord will take shape today, when an agreement on economic and trading cooperation between the two countries is signed, but with a clause stating that all this will be done "while safeguarding the two sides' economic interests." Translated into everyday language, this means that Spain will not begin to put a single peseta more into Russian hands unless Russia has previously started to repay its debt, which now exceeds 60 billion pesetas. In fact, the government has agreed with Yeltsin to convert into a new trade credit the unpaid $425 million, which belonged to the line of finance granted by Gonzalez to Gorbachev in 1991 and inherited by the Russian Federation. So if the Russians repay $50 million this year, for instance, Spain will grant a credit to that amount. But, in addition, as soon as repayments begin to be made, the government will start to supply another $100 million ($50 million short-term and $50 million medium- and long-term credits), and if the Russians secure financing on international markets for Spanish enterprises' projects in Russia, they could obtain up to another $100 million from Spain. It is expected that the remainder, up to the total of $925 million -- that is $300 million -- will be contributed by private enterprises. Gonzalez, who visited Yeltsin for an hour and a half at the El Pardo Palace yesterday and who will sign the Treaty of Friendship and Cooperation between Spain and Russia with him today, wished in this way to give a token of confidence in and support for the Russian president at a time when he is experiencing a difficult internal situation because of the economic crisis and the pressure which Communists and ultranationalists are exerting on him.
