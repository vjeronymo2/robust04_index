931028 FT 28 OCT 93 / Cook attacks Tory pit policy FEARS that British Coal is about to shut down 12 pits as part of its restructuring programme triggered a furious debate in the Commons yesterday, with Labour accusing the government of issuing false assurances about the industry's future. Mr Robin Cook, shadow trade and industry secretary, said ministers had fraudulently suggested in a white paper in the spring that they would reprieve 12 pits that had been marked for closure. 'The white paper has not saved a single miner's job, and it was not intended to,' he said. Addressing himself to Mr Michael Heseltine, trade and industry secretary, Mr Cook said: 'The only job it was intended to save was yours and that of your junior minister for energy (Mr Tim Eggar).' Mr Cooke was opening a debate on the industry that was called by the opposition. There was some criticism of the government from its own backbenchers. But, although a few Tories looked likely to abstain on Labour's motion criticising the pit closure programme, the government seemed assured of a majority. Last March the government completed a coal review that raised the industry's hopes with promises to save a dozen pits from closure. But British Coal said recently that it was on the point of shutting down these pits, and that others might close soon afterwards. Before the debate more than 300 miners and their families had lobbied parliament. Addressing the lobby, Mr Arthur Scargill, president of the NUM mineworkers' union, said the aim of the action was to maintain public pressure on the issue. But he warned: 'Tory rebels voting for a common-sense policy are as rare as snowflakes in . . . July.'
