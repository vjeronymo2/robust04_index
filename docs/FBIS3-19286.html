Language: English Article Type:BFN [Unattributed article: "G-7: Nuclear Safety, Key to Russian Membership"] [Text] Florence, 13 Mar (ANSA) -- The safety of nuclear power stations in Central and East Europe may prove a key for allowing Russia to join the elite club of the world's most industrial nations after the Group of Seven summit in Naples this July, according to Italian Minister for the Environment Valdo Spini. Specifying that he was speaking on a personal basis, Spini, who chaired a summit of G-7 environment ministers here this weekend, said an enlargement of the group to eight nations would be "positive" for environmental issues. Spini said the summit decided to add nuclear safety in East and Central Europe to the six-point agenda on environmental issues already sketched in for Naples. "We wanted to underscore a sense of alarm, without going into detail about possible interventions," explained Spini. One consideration was that the region in question has scarce primary sources of energy whence an option for nuclear power is more probable. The latest figures indicate that, even Russia, which is a major oil and natural gas producer, already has 27 working reactors for an installed power of 5,800 megawatt and plans to increase nuclear power's contribution to its total energy needs from 14 to 34 percent by 2000, bringing three new reactors on stream by 1995 and developing a further eight for 2005. Spini told reporters that a major demand from the summit here is that the G-7 nations should supply greater technical and financial assistance to the Central and East European nations in this field. He added that a major obstacle for making Russia the eighth nation in a Group of Eight was its economic problems, but he said that direct Russian involvement in the debate on the environment planned at the end of the Naples summit could be a first step to the group's enlargement.
