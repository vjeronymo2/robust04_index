BFN [Editorial Report] The following is a compilation of reports on economic activities monitored through 12 May. In 1993, Brazilian industrial productivity grew by a record 18.78 percent with respect to 1992, when it grew 4.86 percent with respect to 1991. This information was released on 5 May by Yolanda Ramalho, chief of the Economic Department of the National Bank for Economic and Social Development. Ramalho explained that the productivity rise caused unemployment to grow 1.7 percent among industry workers, while the dollar value of salaries paid in 1993 grew 10.88 percent. (Sao Paulo O ESTADO DE SAO PAULO in Portuguese 6 May 94 p B4) During the first quarter of 1994, Brazilian electric power consumption grew 2.8 percent with respect to the same period in 1993. This growth rate did not reach the expected 4 percent rate that had been estimated by Eletrobras' [Brazilian Electric Power Company, Inc.] market department. (Sao Paulo GAZETA MERCANTIL in Portuguese 5 May p 14) The April inflation rate measured by Getulio Vargas Foundation totaled 42.46 percent. It was 2.3 points lower than the March rate. The Agricultural Wholesale Price Index in April totalled 36.88 percent, almost 12 percent less than in March. Industrial prices remained stable, decreasing from 42.06 percent in March to 41.90 percent in April. Compared to March, retail sales prices, however, increased 2.1 points in April, ending at 45.57 percent. (Sao Paulo GAZETA MERCANTIL in Portuguese 10 May 94 p 3) The level of employment in Sao Paulo industries fell 0.26 percent in April, with 5,792 industrial workers being dismissed. (Sao Paulo GAZETA MERCANTIL in Portuguese 10 May 94 p 10) The profile of the Brazilian trade balance in the first quarter of 1994 shows that imports in general increased. Capital goods imports increased 41.5 percent and totalled $2.35 billion, while consumption goods imports increased 29.8 percent and totalled $645 million. Brazilian exports from January to March reached their second best level in the country's history, totalling $8.8 billion. Brazilian exports to the PRC, however, decreased 67 percent during the quarter, totalling only $98.2 million. Brazilian sales to Latin American Integration Association member countries, except Argentina, dropped 6.74 percent, showing that the developing countries, which always supported Brazilian sales abroad, are responsible for a 91 percent drop in overall Brazilian exports. (Sao Paulo GAZETA MERCANTIL in Portuguese 10 May 94 pp 1, 3)
