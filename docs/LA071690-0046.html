July 16, 1990, Monday, Home Edition

MORE 'KILLING FIELDS'? U.S. MAY HAVE TO BACK LESSER OF EVILS IN CAMBODIA;

SOUTHEAST ASIA: GAINS BY KHMER ROUGE MAY FORCE THE ADMINISTRATION TO DEAL WITH HUN SEN. MEANTIME, U.N. CEASE-FIRE TALKS OPEN TODAY.

The Bush Administration may soon find itself facing a foreign policy problem that it once considered unthinkable: a Khmer Rouge return to power in Cambodia.

Administration officials acknowledge that Khmer Rouge guerrilla forces have in the past several months made significant gains in the Cambodian countryside, militarily and politically. At the same time, the Vietnamese-installed government of Cambodia has seemed increasingly weak, in part because of financial problems resulting from reduced Soviet aid to Vietnam and Cambodia.

Secretary of State James A. Baker III has said repeatedly that the United States opposes a return to power by the Khmer Rouge. When the Khmer Rouge last ruled Cambodia, from 1975 to 1978, between 1 million and 2 million Cambodians are believed to have been murdered or to have died of starvation or illness.

But the United States is also opposed to allowing the Cambodian government to fight the Khmer Rouge, mainly because of Cambodia's close ties to Vietnam, an old American adversary.

As a result, since the early 1980s, the United States has been supporting a coalition of non-Communist groups joined in an uneasy alliance with the Khmer Rouge. The triumvirate, headed by Prince Norodom Sihanouk, a former Cambodian ruler, holds Cambodia's seat at the United Nations.

There is growing sentiment in Congress to cut off aid to these non-Communist groups, at least until they separate themselves from the Khmer Rouge.

"The best way to stop the Khmer Rouge is to not support their allies," said Rep. Jim Leach (R-Iowa), the senior Republican on the House Foreign Affairs subcommittee on Asia.

Baker himself suggested last week that the Administration might be willing to reduce or end its longstanding support for Sihanouk's forces, support that now totals about $17 million a year.

"I think it's fair to say that we are rethinking the question of assistance to the non-Communist resistance," Baker said recently on ABC's "This Week With David Brinkley."

Some lawmakers are also urging the Administration to change a second element of the longstanding U.S. policy toward Cambodia -- Washington's refusal to recognize or deal with the Cambodian regime of Prime Minister Hun Sen.

U.S. officials have opposed Hun Sen and his Communist government for more than a decade, arguing that it is illegitimate because it was installed by force when Vietnam invaded Cambodia in late 1978. Vietnam withdrew most of its troops last year.

Some congressmen, among them California Rep. Mel Levine (D-Santa Monica), believe the United States should throw its full support behind Hun Sen's government.

"I think we have to cut our aid to the non-Communist resistance," Levine said. "The sad fact is that there's no alternative to Hun Sen or the Khmer Rouge. None of us have any reason to believe Hun Sen is a Jeffersonian Democrat. But Hun Sen is the lesser of two evils, and we have to recognize that."

Others favor prodding the two sides to cooperate in some sort of coalition. Sen. Alan Cranston (D-Calif.), chairman of the Senate Foreign Relations subcommittee on Asia, said, "The only way to defeat the Khmer Rouge is to achieve an agreement between the non-Communists and the Hun Sen government."

But Administration officials contend that such a coalition would not work, because Sihanouk and his allies are incapable of working with the Vietnamese-backed government, which they have been fighting for nearly a decade.

Another official argued that even if the two sides could form a coalition, it probably could not defend itself against the Khmer Rouge.

"The Vietnamese army couldn't eradicate the Khmer Rouge in 10 years with 110,000 troops," he said, "and we know the Vietnamese army isn't bad. What makes anyone think Hun Sen and Sihanouk could do it on their own?"

But the Administration's critics argue that even if this new approach doesn't succeed, it is still worth a try -- and is far better than the current policy.

"Our present policy in Cambodia is bankrupt," said Democratic Rep. Chester G. Atkins of Massachusetts. "Right now, we've got a fight between Hun Sen and the Khmer Rouge, and we're supporting the Khmer Rouge's battlefield allies. The Khmer Rouge is using Sihanouk as their cloak of respectability to go in and organize the countryside."

How far should the United States go in trying to stop the Khmer Rouge? Some congressmen contend that the United States should begin to provide at least some military and intelligence support for Hun Sen's government and others fighting the Khmer Rouge.

But Administration strategists warn that such action might lead to renewed U.S. military involvement in Southeast Asia.

The Bush Administration has been emphasizing the need for a political settlement. For the last year, Washington's strategy has been to try to persuade all the parties in Cambodia -- Hun Sen and his supporters, the non-Communist parties and the Khmer Rouge -- to join in a coalition government. The aim is to contain the Khmer Rouge by making it a political minority within the government.

The Administration has also been working with the United Nations to arrange an international peacekeeping force and a transitional government to serve until after democratic elections.

Richard H. Solomon, assistant secretary of state for East Asian and Pacific Affairs, has met several times with officials of the four other permanent members of the U.N. Security Council -- the Soviet Union, China, Britain and France -- and he is to attend another round of talks in Paris today.

Initially, it was hoped that the Security Council members could use their influence to bring about a political settlement in Cambodia. For years, China has been supplying arms to the Khmer Rouge, while the Soviet Union has been the leading financial supporter and arms supplier to both Vietnam and the Hun Sen government.

But now, Bush Administration strategists acknowledge that the prospects for a negotiated settlement are not good.

"The Khmer Rouge says no to everything," a State Department official said. "And Hun Sen and the Vietnamese seem to be hardening their positions, too."

Until recently, the Administration had little trouble persuading Congress to go along with its Cambodia policy. Leading Democrats, such as Rep. Stephen J. Solarz (D-N.Y.), chairman of the House Foreign Affairs subcommittee on Asia, and Sen. Charles S. Robb (D-Va.), supported the President.

But now that support seems to be eroding. Last month, Senate Majority Leader George J. Mitchell (D-Me.) broke with the Administration, charging that its policy on Cambodia is "incredible" and "insupportable." Mitchell urged the Administration to talk with the Hun Sun government and provide it with development aid.

In late June, a week after the House of Representatives voted to continue aid to the non-Communist resistance groups, the Senate Intelligence Committee voted to cut off such aid. Congressional sources say the issue will be in doubt at least until September, when conflicts between the House and Senate can be worked out in a conference committee.

On Friday, a bipartisan group of 10 senators -- including Robb and moderate Republicans John C. Danforth of Missouri, Nancy Landon Kassebaum of Kansas and William S. Cohen of Maine -- joined Mitchell in a letter urging the Bush Administration to alter course on Cambodia.

The Administration itself is deeply divided over Cambodia policy. A number of officials in the State and Defense departments say privately that they favor ending the U.S. effort to isolate the Hun Sun government. Indeed, although they are not willing to be quoted for the record, some Administration officials have taken the position that Washington should begin to normalize relations with Vietnam.

"The problem is the White House," one official said.

The strongest opponent of change is said to be Brent Scowcroft, the national security adviser, who in the early 1970s took part in the Nixon Administration's frustrated effort to negotiate an end to the Vietnam War.

Meanwhile, Administration officials still cling to the hope that they can persuade the Cambodian government and the Khmer Rouge to agree to some form of settlement supervised by the United Nations. But they acknowledge that the Khmer Rouge is making progress in rural Cambodia and that amid a series of financial and political problems, the Hun Sen government could collapse.

NEXT STEP

Diplomats from the five permanent members of the U.N. Security Council -- the United States, the Soviet Union, China, Britain and France -- will meet in Paris today for the fifth time to discuss Cambodia. The talks center on a plan for a U.N.-supervised cease-fire there, followed by a caretaker administration and elections. The U.S. hopes to achieve a political settlement that will prevent the Khmer Rouge from returning to dominance and will lead to stability to Southeast Asia.
