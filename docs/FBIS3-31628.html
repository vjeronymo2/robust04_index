Language: English Article Type:BFN [Text] Tokyo, Feb 21 (TANJUG) -- U.N. Secretary General special envoy for former Yugoslavia Yasushi Akashi stated on Monday that the solution for the partial demilitarization of Sarajevo will be a model for other cities in the former Yugoslav Republic of Bosnia-Herzegovina, for example Mostar. Fighting has been going on for one year already in Mostar, southern Bosnia-Herzegovina, between Bosnian Moslems and Croats for domination over the city. If there are no grave breeches of the ceasefire in the region of Sarajevo, we could say that the possibility that NATO air strikes be launched in Bosnia is longer existant, Akashi said in an interview to Japanese television. Serbs and Moslems have complied with NATO's demand that by midnight February 20 heavy weapons around Sarajevo be withdrawn or placed under U.N. control, therefore according to UNPROFOR [UN Protection Forces] headquarters, NATO air strikes are not necessary. The ceasefire, which was signed last Wednesday by Serbian and Moslem forces in Sarajevo, is also respected. Akashi said he hoped that new agreements between the warring sides will be reached soon. The economic and social life in Sarajevo could be renewed and citizens could leave and enter the city freely, he said. Akashi explained that NATO's threats of air strike have had a specific role in achieving the partial demilitarization of Sarajevo, but that other factors have also helped. The complete calming of the situation in Bosnia-Herzegovina cannot be achieved for several months yet, Akashi assessed and added that the U.N. will continue to use the carrot and stick method, to bring the warring sides to an agreement. Akashi considered that the three warring sides in Bosnia- Herzegovina, Moslem, Serbian and Croatian, will form three republics, gathered in a federal Bosnia-Herzegovina. Akashi said he hoped that the earlier communal life would be possible once again. The plan to divide Bosnia-Herzegovina into three ethnic republics, supported by the European Union and on the basis of which peace negotiations on Bosnia-Herzegovina are being led, is accepted by Bosnian Serbs and Croats, and rejected by Moslems.
