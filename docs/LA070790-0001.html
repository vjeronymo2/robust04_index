July 7, 1990, Saturday, Ventura County Edition

SIMI VALLEY SCHOOL CHIEF TO RESIGN;

EDUCATION: JOHN DUNCAN SAYS HE WILL TAKE OVER THE TOP SPOT AT A NORTHERN CALIFORNIA DISTRICT IN THE FALL.

Supt. John Duncan of the financially troubled Simi Valley Unified School District said Friday that he will resign this fall to take over as chief administrator of a Northern California district.

Duncan, who has headed Ventura County's largest school district for 16 years, said his resignation will become effective Sept. 16. He said he has accepted the position of superintendent of the San Ramon Valley Unified School District in Contra Costa County, near Oakland. The job will pay $98,900, a year, about $2,000 more than what he makes in Simi Valley.

"I've been in Simi Valley for 16 years," Duncan said. "Simi Valley has been awfully good to me. But I'm ready for a new challenge."

Duncan, 57, said the district's struggle to offset an estimated $8-million budget shortfall next year did not enter into his decision to leave the 18,000-student district. The budget crunch has forced district officials to lay off 32 temporary teachers, four librarians and four nurses at some of its 26 campuses. Dozens of administrators were also reassigned to lower-level positions with reduced salaries.

"Over the last five months, it's been very difficult . . . but it's very much like any other school district," said Duncan, who has been the subject of much criticism from teachers and union officials because of the cost-cutting measures. "They all have budget problems."

Duncan said one reason he decided to leave Simi Valley is that several of his children live in the Bay Area. Another, he said, was the pay increase.

He said he informed members of the Board of Education about his decision during a closed session Tuesday night.

"We'll miss him and his outstanding leadership," board President Helen Beebe said. "He has left us a real heritage."

Beebe also dismissed any notions that the district's budget problems had anything to do with Duncan's decision to leave.

School officials have attributed a large part of the district's financial problems to a lack of state money, due to an expected $3.6-billion shortfall in the state budget next year. Officials also blame a declining student enrollment and an 8% salary increase for teachers this year for contributing to the district's financial problems.

Some of the district's 800 teachers and their union representatives, however, have accused Duncan and other district administrators of failing to adequately plan ahead to avoid major cuts. They have said it is unfair to blame the teachers' salary increase for the budget problems when Duncan and other district administrators are among the highest paid school officials in Ventura and Los Angeles counties.

Bill Davenport, president of the Simi Valley Educators Assn., and several other top-level union officials are attending an out-of-state educators conference this week and could not be reached for comment Friday.

Beebe said district officials will talk to several consulting groups to assist them in their search for a new superintendent. She said if a new administrator is not in place by September, an acting superintendent would be appointed to serve in the interim.

Supt. William Streshly of the San Ramon Valley Unified School District said his district is slightly smaller than the Simi Valley system, with 16,000 students and 23 campuses, but that it is growing fast. Streshly said he is leaving the district to take a teaching position at San Diego State.

San Ramon Unified has had its share of problems this year. Streshly said the district's 800 teachers participated in three wildcat strikes over a two-week period in April and May during union contract negotiations. He said the teachers had been demanding a 7% salary increase for next year but finally had to settle for a 3.5% raise.
