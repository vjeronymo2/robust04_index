CSO [By Marielba Nunez] [Text] Recognition of about 240,000 hectares of land located south of Caicara del Orinoco as Piaroa territory is currently being requested by the representatives of that indigenous community, as they fear that concessions covering that area will be granted. This would threaten the ecology and the development plans they have been undertaking in recent years. This is according to statements by Jose Gregorio Rodriguez, Miguel Gonzalez, and Guillermo Tovar, representatives of the Piaroa community and members of the civic association called Original Peoples of the Chivapure Forest Reserve, Porfochi. "We want the different governmental agencies to recognize the Piaroa people of the Suapure, Parguaza, and Guaniamo River basins as the legitimate inhabitants of these lands. Our future is uncertain in this region because there are plans to exploit the lumber on these lands." They add that they cannot agree with this possibility because it would mean the end of the organization that the Piaroas have achieved there. So far, to prevent the exploitation of lumber, they have approached the National Agrarian Institute, the Ministry of Agriculture and Livestock, and the agrarian court, which are the agencies that oversee matters concerning landholding among the indigenous people. "We need an opportunity to continue our self-managed development project, which includes the rational use of natural resources, such as extracting seje [a kind of palm tree] oil, a medicinal product that can be used to cure different types of illnesses; producing honey; and raising buffalo. We have done all this through cooperatives." They add that they know of the plans to grant concessions in these areas, and they have been denouncing this situation for the last four years. On several occasions they have submitted documents requesting that their territory in the Suapure area be recognized. "Legally we are basing ourselves on Article 77 of the National Constitution, on the rights of the indigenous peoples; Article 2 of the Agrarian Reform, which establishes the recognition of the indigenous peoples' right to land; and Convention 169 of the International Labor Organization. This not only establishes our right to the land, but also our cultural right to conserve our language and customs."
