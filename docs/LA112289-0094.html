November 22, 1989, Wednesday, Home Edition

GOBBLING UP PROFITS;

WITH NEW PRODUCTS BOOSTING SALES, TURKEY FARMERS HAVE MUCH TO BE THANKFUL FOR.

They have been tossed from low-flying airplanes in an odd Arkansas ritual, bowled frozen down the aisles of an Orange County supermarket, adopted as pets and hijacked as political pawns.

They have been ridiculed as too dumb to live and found to be too fat to procreate unassisted. They are a strutting symbol of suffering stupidity, and yet we invite them to dine with us in increasing numbers.

The turkey's time has come, and we're not just talking Thanksgiving.

When processors started turning out various turkey products in the 1970s -- turkey bologna, turkey pastrami, turkey kielbasa, ham and hot dogs -- the bird's fate was sealed as one of America's fastest-growing foodstuffs.

"Things are really happening out there," said Mervin Amerine, manager of the California Turkey Industry Board. "We're moving in on the other animal protein items -- beef, pork, you name it. We keep getting their business all the time."

When consumers want beef or pork these days, they're often gobbling up turkey instead -- and at a rate that's turned it from holiday fare into everyday eating. In fact, the Thanksgiving-through-New Year's Day turkey glut now accounts for only about 35% of the total turkey market, said Laurie Wilson, National Turkey Federation spokeswoman.

"We haven't lost the holiday image," Wilson said. "We've just added on."

While the turkey has such a secure position in American mythology and cuisine, there is relatively little known about the bird for certain. One solid fact is that the plastic-packaged fowl has its roots in the native American wild turkey, Meleagris gallopavo, which roamed the forests from southern Canada to Mexico.

The bird was brought from Mexico to Europe in the 1500s by the Spaniards. Merchants from the eastern Mediterranean, often Turks, usually touched down in Seville on their way west and are thought to have picked the bird up there and introduced it to England. The English, it is said, named it the "turkie cock," though it was called everything from the "cock of India" to the "elephant bird" throughout the Old World.

Then there's the question of whether the Pilgrims actually ate the bird at the first Thanksgiving back in 1621.

William Bradford, governor of the Plymouth Colony, allegedly described a turkeyless feast in his personal notes, listing venison, duck and goose among the menu items. Some historians contend, however, that the Indians brought the birds to the potluck dinner or that Pilgrim hunters went out and shot a few before bowing their heads over grace.

Either way, turkey eating had a slow start in the United States, a situation that showed no improvement for more than three centuries. By 1930, Americans were eating a mere 1 1/2 pounds of turkey each year -- about enough for a drumstick or two on Thanksgiving Day, followed by a sandwich and a plate of Turkey a la king during the post-holiday weekend.

According to the National Turkey Federation, consumption grew slowly but steadily for about 50 years.

Then 1980 hit. That year, Americans ate 10.4 pounds apiece; in 1988, it was up to 16.1 pounds.

The turkey federation predicts per-capita consumption to reach nearly 18 pounds by 1990. Consumers in California are already eating 23 pounds each year -- more than any other area of the country.

Health is a major reason for the switch. The decade that brought us aerobics and lycra tights, that saw alcohol consumption take a plunge and gym memberships rise, also taught us about the dangers of cholesterol.

"Today, people are perceiving turkey as a low-fat, low-calorie alternative for red meat products," Wilson said.

Consider: A three-ounce portion of roasted white turkey meat contains 119 calories, one gram of fat, 55 milligrams of cholesterol and 26 grams of protein. The same-sized serving of a beef rib roast has 217 calories, 12.9 grams of fat, 68 milligrams of cholesterol and 24 grams of protein.

Turkey processors were quick to catch on that the way into Americans' stomachs is through unclogged arteries. Tom Herrick is generally credited as being the father of turkey ham, a product he seasoned into being in 1974 when he was general manager of a Turlock, Calif., turkey processing company.

Five years later, the Louis Rich Co. introduced turkey bologna, salami and pastrami to the sandwich-eating public. Last June, turkey bacon made its debut in American frying pans, thanks to Con-Agra Turkey Co., producers of the Turkey Selects by Armour label.

Why did it take 15 years for turkey to metamorphose from drumstick to breakfast strip? Robert Wangerien, director of marketing for ConAgra's Turlock Division, figures it's a combination of technology and taste.

"It took a little bit of more technology on the part of the manufacturers to make a turkey breakfast strip," Wangerien said. "And now there's greater and greater consumer demand for products that are healthful, lower in fat and more nutritious in general. And there's a greater awareness of turkey."

The 1980s also taught turkey processors that small is beautiful. Instead of just offering a frozen 20-pound Tom, they began to cut turkeys up like chickens and offer turkey cutlets and tenderloin, legs, thighs, breasts.

In addition to growing more turkeys, farmers are also growing different turkeys, birds selectively bred to carry more meat.

"Ours are broad-breasted turkey, and it's a genetic thing, a development through the years," said Joyce Wesolowski, general coordinator at Willie Bird Turkeys in Petaluma. "They have developed the turkey to where we get very, very large hens. When they get too big, they'll topple over, because they're too heavy on the breast."

Increased weight also makes breeding the birds more difficult, industry experts say, so turkey breeders rely on artificial insemination and trickery to increase mating.

Left to their own devices, turkeys would breed earlier in the year than optimal for mass production and holiday sales. So some turkey producers keep the birds in darkened sheds. When light is suddenly let in, the birds think it's spring and nature takes its course.

But then, turkeys were never known for their native intelligence. Their major activities consist of eating and sleeping; young turkeys have been known to stand in the rain with their heads up and mouths open until they drown.

But some turkey owners would beg to differ when it comes to talking turkey intelligence.

Wilbur, a Wisconsin Tom, made national news when his owner decided not to eat him for Thanksgiving in 1982.

The reason? After eight months in the home of Bob Fecteau, Wilbur had taken up jogging, his owner insisted. It was proof, Fecteau said, of turkeys' kinship with humans. Fecteau ate ham for his holiday dinner.

He's not the only American who has spent time with a turkey in activities outside of raising, cooking and eating.

Derrick Johnson, for one, has created the Poultry Bowling Assn. to promote a sport he dreamed up while stocking shelves. The Santa Ana grocery clerk was watching workers in the meat department slide a Butterball across the linoleum one Thanksgiving a few years back when he realized how fast and accurate the bird was in its frozen state.

Within minutes, turkey bowling was born -- a new, late-night indoor sport. To play, stack 2-liter plastic soda pop bottles at the end of the grocery aisle. Grip the Butterball by its yellow handle, wind up and slide. But Swift-Eckerich, the Chicago-based maker of the Butterball, has said it is not amused.

Three years ago, members of Farm Sanctuary, a Delaware-based animal rights group, took six turkeys on the road for a kind of protest of fowl treatment and delivered them to families in Cincinnati, Boulder, Colo., and Camarillo -- families that promised not to braise the beasts.

"People have to be aware that animals have feelings, at least feelings of pain," said Linda Callahan, a Camarillo resident whose back yard became the home of Trotter and Strutter at the end of their trip West.

Farm Sanctuary would almost certainly question the motives of the Yellville city fathers. In Yellville, Ark., wild turkeys, which can fly better than their domestic cousins, are thrown from zooming airplanes during the annual Turkey Trot Festival in mid-October.

"The plane flies pretty low over the park, and they throw out the turkeys and they fly and people try to catch them," said Joyce Newton, Yellville city clerk. "There's a farm nearby, and ones they don't catch usually end up there. The farmer has quite a herd of them by now."
