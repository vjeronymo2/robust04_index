September 12, 1990, Wednesday, Home Edition

FOR MOST OF US, THE NO. 1 ISSUE IS NO BIG DEAL;

CRIME: IT'S DOWN IN CALIFORNIA, BUT THE POLITICIANS STILL FIND IT AN EASY ATTENTION-GETTER.

A monumental paradox has surfaced in this election season: While polls and other measures of public sentiment indicate ever-rising levels of concern about crime -- and coincidental levels of support for more laws and more cops and prisons -- Californians' actual chances of being victims of crime have been declining rather markedly.

A month ago, the Department of Justice issued, with little official fanfare and equally scant media interest, a thick report on crime in California during the 1980s. During the decade, the California Crime Index -- the number of serious crimes per 100,000 population -- decreased sharply from nearly 4,000 in 1980 to less than 3,500 in 1989, even counting an upward bump in the last two years of the period.

While the actual number of crimes may have increased, that has been offset by the state's huge population growth during the period, roughly 6 million. In other words, crime grew more slowly than population; or, to say it still another way, one's statistical chances of becoming a crime victim decreased.

During that same period, moreover, the "clearance rate" for crimes increased by 14%, although only about 22% of all major crimes were being cleared by arrest or other actions. And, as everyone knows, the number of criminals being locked up has escalated sharply.

If relatively fewer crimes are being committed, more are being solved and more felons are going to jail, that should be cause for great celebration. We should be congratulating ourselves that all of those additional laws and billions of tax dollars are having a major impact.

Why, then, are Californians more, not less, concerned about crime?

For one thing, the decrease in crime is a big secret, one that the crime industry wants to keep locked away. Think of the hundreds of thousands of cops, lawyers, judges, prison guards, prison builders, gun and lock merchants, insurance agents, burglar alarm installers and others whose incomes depend on crime. Even the military wants to get in on the crime-fighting trade these days as its main reason for being, the Soviet Union, becomes a pussycat.

Chief among those who engage in the conspiracy of silence about crime are politicians. Contemporary political campaigns consist of identifying supposed threats to the welfare of voters and then promising to oppose them effectively.

Bombarded by real and fictional crimes daily through the media, we become more acutely sensitive to incidents from our own lives and thus become utterly convinced that waves of crime are threatening to inundate us.

Crime is, in fact, a very serious matter in some inner-city neighborhoods. But for most Californians, it is a minor annoyance at most, certainly not something that should preoccupy their lives.

The danger is that politicians will continue to dwell on crime for tactical reasons, not only contributing to the irrational climate of fear, but also neglecting policy issues that are fundamentally more important to the state's future.
