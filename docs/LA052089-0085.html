May 20, 1989, Saturday, Home Edition

U.S. SUIT ALLEGES REP. ROSE FAILED TO DISCLOSE LOANS

The Justice Department on Friday accused Rep. Charlie Rose (D-N.C.) in a civil suit of failing to report on his federal financial disclosure statements $138,694 in loans he obtained over six years.

The department sought $30,000 in civil penalties for the six alleged violations of the Ethics in Government Act in the lawsuit filed in U.S. District Court here. It also sought an injunction to bar future violations of the reporting requirements of the law.

The suit charged that Rose, who chairs the House Agriculture tobacco and peanuts subcommittee, failed to report the loans from his campaign and financial institutions on the annual disclosure forms filed from 1980 through 1986, with the exception of 1983.

Rose's personal loans were obtained from financial institutions and his campaign fund, from which he borrowed $52,995, according to the complaint.

The suit charged that Rose failed to report as gifts interest that was forgiven on loans he obtained from his campaign.

In a statement, Rose said that the lawsuit was intended to "force me to file amendments which the House Ethics Committee has not asked me to file and to pay a fine for amendments (to disclosure forms) I have already filed."

The suit was filed after efforts by government attorneys to negotiate an out-of-court settlement of the Justice Department's claims against Rose.
