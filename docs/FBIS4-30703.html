BFN [From the "International Report" program] [Text] Australian Prime Minister Paul Keating is on his way to Jakarta this afternoon with his eyes firmly set on what he calls one of Australia's most important international relationships. The focus on the interests the two countries share in the Asia-Pacific means that the Indonesian media crackdown is well down on the Keating agenda. Mr. Keating is under pressure from some Australian lobby groups to take Indonesia to task after Jakarta last week closed the country's three leading weekly news magazines -- TEMPO, EDITOR, and DETIK. But the prime minister says he'll be using his informal talk with President Suharto tomorrow to discuss the APEC [Asia Pacific Economic Cooperation] Asia-Pacific leaders' summit in Jakarta in November. Mr. Keating told Graeme Dobell that the Suharto government's closure of its three leading news magazines was a backward step during a period of rapid change: [Begin recording] [Keating] Indonesia is like many countries in the region; it's in a state of constant change where there are many imperatives starting to impact on the country: the growth imperative and all that brings with it for change, and diversity and plurality of opinion. I don't doubt that these are pressures which haven't been as evident in bygone days as they are now. For our part, Gareth Evans as foreign minister has made clear Australia's disappointment about the fact that these [word indistinct] have been changed. But again, from our point of view, I mean there are the same, similar things -- there is press control in China and we're still doing trade with China, there is a fairly tight control of the press in Malaysia and we are still doing trade with Malaysia. I think, you know, this relationship now has many links to it and it does give us the opportunity, I think, to, you know, maintain a working relationship despite some of these things which are disappointments to us. [Dobell] Does it symbolize [words indistinct] to put on the brakes? Is it a step back from the policy of openness that President Suharto has been talking about? [Keating] Well, I think it may be that we will witness in many countries similar to Indonesia -- such as Indonesia, similar to Indonesia -- that there'll be, you know, (?full) four steps forward and one back and four forward and one back and then I think we'll see a bit of this. But, there's also strong public opinion -- reactions -- about this, where there is any crimp on information, wherever it occurs in most places, and I understand this to be the case in Indonesia as well. [Dobell] One of the comments from Jakarta has been from the head of Indonesia's largest Muslim organization, Abdulraham Wahid, who said that he thought you, as a friend, should speak to the president on this.
