April 23, 1989, Sunday, Home Edition

WALL STREET / JOHN CRUDELE: ON THE STREET, BEING HOT IS BETTER THAN BEING GOOD

Wall Street has always liked winning streaks. Even though it spends a fortune to hire the brainiest people it can find, the financial community is still very partial to a hot hand. "I'd rather be hot than good," you could say, has always been its motto.

Here are some of the people, concepts and things that are hot right now. Read them quickly, because sometimes the flame doesn't burn for very long.

* Hottest Raider: Carl C. Icahn, the head of Trans World Airlines, is still the man to follow if you are looking to make money from corporate restructurings. Michael Lamb, president of Wealth Monitors in Kansas City, estimates that Icahn has made more than $700 million from his investment in Texaco (which he tried to take over); $300 million from his USX Corp. holdings (which he tried to take over), and he could reap a $400-million profit if he decides to sell his stake in TWA (which he took over). If you're looking for a hot hand to shake, Icahn's the one.

* Hottest Mutual Fund Manager: David Alger, who runs the Alger Small Capitalization Portfolio, has seen the value of his fund's holdings appreciate 20.21% so far this year. According to Lipper Analytical Services, which tracks these things, that's the best performance of any stock mutual fund so far this year. The Alger Small Cap fund was up 17.5% in 1988, which was slightly better than the market as a whole.

* Economist on a Roll: David Bostian, head of Bostian Research Associates, New York, was the most accurate forecaster of U.S. economic growth and inflation in 1988, says Robert J. Eggert of the Blue Chip Economic Index, Sedona, Ariz. Eggert surveys over 50 economists, and Bostian came the closest to predicting both the GNP and consumer price index. Bostian now says the economy is weakening, and the gross national product should grow at only 2.2% this year. Inflation isn't a big problem, and interest rates should come down, he says.

* Matchmaker on Fire: While there aren't any official statistics, Wasserstein Perella Group, the 1-year-old private investment banking firm, seems to have been involved in the most new corporate mergers and restructuring transactions in the first three months of 1989. Wasserstein Perella counts 14 deals it began -- but hasn't necessarily completed yet -- in 1989, including involvement in the proposed merger of Time Inc. and Warner Communications.

* Analysts With the Midas Touch: Lawrence Harris of Bateman Eichler, Hill Richards in Los Angeles was right on the money in a report last month when he predicted that Lockheed Corp. would sell some assets and real estate and repurchase stock. His report came out weeks before Lockheed did just that. And Roger Redman, an analyst with Piper, Jaffray & Hopwood in Minneapolis, was on target when he predicted a restructuring at Control Data Corp.

* Hottest Investment Concept: Yuppies apparently want to make sure they can afford to put their kids through college, so state bonds that help guarantee the little yups a higher education are very popular. More than a dozen states have already offered prepaid tuition plans or educational bonds, and another dozen are thinking of doing so. The federal government countered recently by making Series E savings bonds tax free, starting in 1989, for most people, if the money is used for education.

* Explosive New Product: Of all the products put out by American corporations within the past year, one of the most popular has been RJR Nabisco's Teddygrahams, bite-sized cookies. Gorman's New Product News estimates that Teddygrahams are now the third-best-selling cookie in the country, selling about $120 million worth last year.

* Most Popular Scam: Telemarketing scams are the hottest thing on the dark side of investing, according to Scott Stapf of the North American Securities Administrators Assn. And, he says, this season there are three new phone scams. In one, investors are sold precious metals that the scamster says will be stored in a well-known bank. But the gold never quite makes it to the bank, and the crook takes a hike. In the second, investors are sold interests in restaurants that don't exist. And in the third scam, the crook puts his victims into a family of scams. Stapf says this is being billed as a "hard-asset management program."

* Most Surprising Company: Based on the last quarterly earnings it reported, Jacobson Stores Inc. gave Wall Street one of the biggest favorable surprises. According to the Institutional Brokers Estimate System, Jacobson reported profits of $1.31 a share for its fourth quarter that ended in January. Wall Street only expected $1.04.

* Hottest Stock: Shares of Silicon Systems, a California electronic equipment manufacturer, have doubled to $20 from around $10 in the past few months. Knight-Ridder Tradecenter, which keeps tabs on unusual stock activity, says it's one of the hottest issues around.

Let's say a guy goes to the doctor, worried that he has cancer and only has three months to live. "No," the doctor says, "you're all right. You've got heart disease and you'll be alive for at least another six months."

"Thanks, doc," the relieved patient says, "that makes me feel a whole lot better."

The stock market is the patient. And the U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics is the doctor, who, by disclosing last week that inflation in consumer goods and services was rising at just 6% a year, gave the patient a new lease on life.

The only trouble is that the financial markets, in looking at the silver lining, failed to see the cloud.

The official explanation of why stocks rallied brilliantly last week was that the government, when it reported that the nation's consumer price index rose 0.5% in March, made Wall Street feel a little better than it had been feeling, although it didn't cure the disease. That, the stock market concluded, was worth a nearly 42-point jump in the Dow Jones industrial average on Tuesday.

There are a lot experts who don't feel the same way about the latest inflation figures. Rising prices, they say, are still very troublesome, even if March's rate wasn't a blockbuster. And eventually, these experts predict, the financial markets will pay dearly.

"The number released last week shows inflation rising at a rate of 6% in the first quarter," says Rudolf Thumberg, economist with Ried Thumberg, a Westport, Conn., financial research firm that tracks the Federal Reserve Board. "I would say (the Fed) is not thrilled and almost certainly worried" about the latest figure.

Fed Chairman Alan Greenspan has expressed a dedication toward fighting inflation and has called 4.5% annual inflation unacceptable.

What makes the inflation situation even more troubling to some experts is the fact that March's number did not include a steep rise in the price of gasoline -- about 10 cents a gallon since late March. When gasoline prices start seeping into the consumer price index, the experts say inflation will certainly be aggravated. The higher inflation numbers that are expected in the months ahead could lead, the experts believe, to another boost in interest rates.

If you didn't have your ear to the ground last week, you never would have figured out why Vista Chemical Co. was one of the hottest stocks on Wall Street.

On the surface, Vista looked like any other company that trotted out its executives before a group of analysts to make some optimistic predictions. The only difference was that Vista's stock soared before and after the Tuesday meeting in New York, and the cause of the activity wasn't the record per-share profits that the company was predicting.

Instead, analysts were chattering over the grapevine about how Vista, a Houston-based specialty chemical company that one follower said is "intensely worried about a takeover," was getting set to announce a special dividend. The rumor even had a price: $30 a share, or about half what the company's shares were selling for on the open market.

Vista would only say that it wants to enhance shareholder values -- it calls it a mission -- but wouldn't be specific.

What do analysts think about the talk of a windfall dividend? They wouldn't be surprised. Vista, they said, is intent on remaining independent and isn't afraid to borrow money toward that goal.

The only thing in dispute, the analysts say, is when the company will take aggressive action such as a special dividend. Most say there isn't any hurry.

A usually reliable Wall Street source says Pennzoil's stock portfolio these days includes big -- but not big enough to disclose -- stakes in Lubrizol Corp., Santa Fe Southern, Kerr-McGee Corp. and USX Corp. as well as Burlington Resources. Will any of these become the target of a takeover by Pennzoil? Who knows, the source says . . .

Not all of America's foreign trading partners are in favor of higher interest rates. While the West Germans raised interest rates late last week, the Japanese are said to be afraid that higher rates in the U.S. will boost the value of the dollar. This, in turn, will increase America's trade deficit and cause a revival of protectionist trade legislation in Washington .
