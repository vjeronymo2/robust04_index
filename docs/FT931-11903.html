930201 FT 01 FEB 93 / Devaluation of the Punt: Five-month battle for the punt September 4, 1992: EC finance ministers meet in Bath to discuss pressures in exchange rate mechanism. September 13: Italy devalues by 7 per cent in ERM. September 16: Sterling abandons ERM and Spain devalues by 5 per cent. September 17: Punt falls to ERM floor against D-Mark. Finance Minister Bertie Ahern says he will 'do what is necessary' to keep punt within ERM narrow band. Punt trades at 1.00 to sterling. Italy suspends lira from ERM. September 28: Irish central bank raises base rates 3 points to 13.75 per cent after wholesale money market rates surge to 25 per cent. September 30: Government announces it is borrowing IPounds 500m abroad to rebuild reserves. October 1: Sterling falls to 1.05 to the punt. Irish Exporters' Association warns that more than a third of members 'are now facing serious problems'. October 6: Irish government announces IPounds 50m market development fund to subsidise wage costs of exporting companies adversely affected by sterling's devaluation. October 16: Birmingham EC summit. November 4: Irish coalition collapses over beef tribunal allegations. Fianna Fail continues as minority government. November 21: Spanish and Portuguese devalue by 6 per cent within ERM. Irish government dismisses as 'speculative loose talk' rumours that it was offered the chance of joining in the realignment. November 25: Labour party holds balance of power following general election. Negotiations begin on formation of new coalition. November 30: Central bank increases overnight lending rate to 100 per cent. Meeting of 30 top industrialists concludes exchange rate is 'unsustainable'. Market analysts say that a devaluation 'of at least 10 per cent' is necessary. December 13: Edinburgh summit. Ahern makes impassioned appeal for support for the punt to EC colleagues. January 1, 1993: All Irish exchange controls lifted under obligations of single European market. January 4: On first post-holiday trading day, punt comes under renewed selling pressure. Inter-bank rates surge to 40 per cent. January 5: Government exchequer returns for 1992 reveal that overseas holders of Irish gilts had sold IPounds 1.8bn of IPounds 4bn total since crisis began. Ahern says the economy cannot 'stagger on indefinitely' and criticises 'sweetheart deal' between Bundesbank and France to defend franc. January 6: A new run on the punt. Drops to floor of ERM, overnight lending rate increased from 14 per cent to 50 per cent. January 8: Central bank raises overnight rates to 100 per cent as selling pressure continues. Banks and building societies warn they will have to increase commercial rates. January 12: New Fianna Fail-Labour coalition formed, committed to defending punt, but with priorities set on job creation and new infrastructure investment. January 18: EC finance ministers make strong statement in support of punt, but no concrete measures proposed. January 22: Government announces IPounds 1.1bn exchange rate guarantee fund, to avert threatened commercial and mortgage interest rate rises. January 25: Opposition leader John Bruton calls for a devaluation 'to save jobs'. January 26: British base rates cut 1 point to 6 per cent. Irish government says 'the disadvantages of devaluation far outweigh the short-term advantages'. January 27-28: Renewed heavy selling of punt. Sterling falls to 1.09 to the punt. Central bank puts overnight rates back to 100 per cent. January 30: Government requests a meeting of the EC monetary committee in Brussels. Punt devalued by 10 per cent within the ERM.
