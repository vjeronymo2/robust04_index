August 10, 1989, Thursday, Valley Edition

ANTONOVICH CALLS FOR COMPROMISE ON WETLANDS;

ENVIRONMENTALISTS, LANDOWNERS AT ODDS ON DEVELOPMENT PLAN FOR DESERT OASIS

Los Angeles County Supervisor Mike Antonovich said Wednesday that he hopes a wetlands area near Palmdale that has been the subject of a debate over proposed development can be protected because of its archeological and biological value.

Antonovich toured the Barrel Springs wetlands in the Antelope Valley with residents who oppose a plan to build 8 homes on the 63-acre county parcel on the edge of Palmdale between Avenue T-8 and the California Aqueduct.

"It's a very valid issue," Antonovich said. "Because of the archeological significance, this area could be a learning center for young people from kindergarten all the way up through community college."

Antonovich and his staff members said the supervisor is not taking sides in the dispute. Antonovich said that he hopes local activists, government agencies and the landowners, Stanley and Lois Sevilla, can fashion a compromise, and said that state parkland or education funds might be available for preservation of the area.

Biological Study

Last month, the county Regional Planning Commission instructed Sevilla to commission a biological study of wildlife in the wetlands, delaying until October the appeal of a hearing officer's approval of the construction proposal.

State and federal environmental officials have urged stringent restrictions on development of the Barrel Springs area because it is a rare oasis in the desert. It has been a hub of human activity for thousands of years, yielding the earliest archeological evidence of human presence in the Antelope Valley and a wealth of Indian artifacts.

Antonovich's comments were praised by Jim McAvoy, president of the North Los Angeles County Coalition for Planned Growth and Responsive Government, who arranged the 5th District supervisor's tour. "I think he seemed genuinely interested," McAvoy said. "I believe he recognized the uniqueness of the area and the fact that it's worth saving."

Also touring the area was Antelope Valley Community College President Allan Kurki, who said he is interested in preserving the area because of its potential educational value.

Low Density

While the landowner has said his low-density development would not harm the natural resources of the area, McAvoy said even minimal development would be ecologically devastating. He and others want the county to acquire the property and designate it an environmentally protected area.

The dispute could come to the Board of Supervisors if either side appeals the eventual decision by the Regional Planning Commission. But before the commission reviews the case Oct. 11, Antonovich suggested compromises, such as clustering the proposed homes away from wetland areas, or obtaining county approval to transfer the proposed housing to a 118-acre site south of the California Aqueduct, the southern boundary of the Sevillas' property.

But Lois Sevilla said Wednesday that moving the project is impossible because she and her husband have already sold the land they owned south of the aqueduct.

"It has been our feeling all along that the development could be done consistently with ecological values," she said. "That's why it's low density. We are surprised that we're getting this kind of response."

In another development, the Palmdale City Council and Planning Commission will hold a joint meeting Tuesday to discuss possible methods of preserving the wetlands.
