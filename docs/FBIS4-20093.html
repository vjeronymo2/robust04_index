BFN [Interview with UN Secretary General Butrus Butrus-Ghali by Sir David Frost; from the "Breakfast with Frost" program on 24 April; Frost in studio, Butrus-Ghali in New York -- recorded] [Text] [Frost] Earlier I spoke with the United Nations Secretary General Butrus Butrus-Ghali, and I began by asking him whether the UN would authorize air strikes if the Serbs met some but not all of NATO's conditions. [Butrus-Ghali] This is a very technical question, I perceive that this had to be negotiated by the people who are on the ground. If they see that the violation is a real violation then we will use the air strikes without hesitation. [Frost] If there is no response to the ultimatum, how does the coordination work. Can NATO then on the basis of your letter, and so on, can they just go ahead with air strikes, or do they have to get your approval? [Butrus-Ghali] They have to get the approval of my special representative who is on the ground, because we have troops in a United Nations forces on the ground, and we must protect them. So the special representative in coordination or collaboration with the command of United Nations on the ground will decide where and when they have to use the air support. [Frost] They will decide that? [Butrus-Ghali] Yes. In other words you need the agreement of the two parties -- the NATO and the United Nations. [Frost] Then what about when it comes, Mr. Secretary General, to the actual targets, do they have to be specifically agreed. I mean if one side wants to attack only encampments.... [Butrus-Ghali] ...do you see, there is a perfect coordination between the military people of the NATO and the military people of the United Nations, they will coordinate and they will chose together what are the targets which ought to be bombed by the NATO air power. [Frost] And what about when it comes time to stop. If either one, if either the UN or NATO want to stop, then the bombing stops? [Butrus-Ghali] Yes, supposing that the United Nations say for the time being it will represent a danger for our soldiers or we are negotiating and let us postpone this air strike. They will coordinate with the NATO, and the NATO will postpone. [Frost] Do you think secretary general, that in fact these air strikes could -- we've seen indications of this -- could these air strikes endanger in fact, either UN personnel or UN peacekeeping forces? [Butrus-Ghali] This is why we are coordinating and this is why we are insisting to obtain the agreement of the UN. This is the reason that we want to offer the maximum of protection to our United Nations staff, to our personnel working in the humanitarian assistance, and to the peacekeeping forces. [Frost] There hasn't been progress in Bosnia, and of course the blame has been attached to the UN. Has it been a major blow to the UN the fact that there's been no progress? [Butrus-Ghali] Certainly it is a blow to the image of the UN, but the expert who knows the reality knows quite well that the responsibility is shared between the UN and the major member states. The UN have no army, no money, so unless the member states will be ready to sustain the decision which had been adopted by the UN, i.e. by the member states, we will not be able to execute the different resolutions that have been adopted. So there is a very special relation between the UN and the member states. [Frost] And in fact you're disappointed really at the number of troops made available? [Butrus-Ghali] This is another aspect -- we have, since June last year, been trying to obtain additional troops, and we have obtained a resolution from the Security Council, but we have not been able to obtain those additional troops. Because the air strikes have a value of dissuasion to reinforce the bargaining position of my mediators, but practically unless we have ground forces, our forces on the ground, it will be quite difficult to obtain a military result with air power only. [Frost] So you were disappointed I presume, that when Friday [22 April], President Clinton reaffirmed that his troops would only be available after a peace. You need troops before a peace. [Butrus-Ghali] Yes I need troops before peace, but I was not disappointed, because I know quite well what are the positions of the United States, and the position of many other major members of the international community, who are ready to help the United Nations in different other activities, but not in participating in peacekeeping operations. [Frost] In terms of the situation though, this action and the fact that you asked for this action at the beginning of the week, I suppose means that you've decided that as of now the Bosnian Serbs -- the target of these air strikes -- are the aggressors? [Butrus-Ghali] Yes. The Bosnians are the aggressors, but you see you may have aggressors from the other side too. So the air strike will punish all those who are not respecting the resolutions adopted by the United Nations, or the last resolution which was adopted the NATO. [Frost] And Mr. Akashi made a reference a few days ago to there being certain circumstances in which the UN might pull out of Bosnia. Are there any circumstances in which you might pull out of Bosnia? [Butrus-Ghali] Certainly I will not advocate this. But this decision, if it will have to be taken, has to be taken by the Security Council. Because the troops are there according to a decision which was taken by the Security Council. If we want to pull out the troops, to diminish the number of the troops, this is a decision that will have to be taken by the Security Council. I'm in favor to maintain our presence, to continue to work on the ground until we will find a solution to this problem. [Frost] Tell me something secretary general, looking back over the last two years or so, can you perceive from your unique position, was there somewhere where the international community, the nations of the world, the tactics went wrong? [Butrus-Ghali] Yes I believe that what is important [is] that during the last two years, that the member states still have not decided if they want to reinforce the United Nations or not. The member states have not decided if they want to give a role to the United Nations in peacekeeping operations, in solving international disputes, in reinforcing international cooperation. There is still [the fact that] the United Nations as such is not a priority for the majority of the member states. They have their bilateral diplomacy, they have their own defense, and the United Nations is an important instrument but it is still a marginal instrument. And this is one of the explanations why, during the last two years, we have not been able to cope with all the problems, because we have received demands every day from the 1,600 observers who are working now in South Africa, to control the elections, to the negotiations that are going on in Salvador, or in Guatemala, to the situation in Kigali, or in Bujumbura or in Mozambique, or in Angola.... [Frost] So are you saying you think that while it's clear that the UN can succeed at peacekeeping, that in fact peacemaking is getting too difficult a task? [Butrus-Ghali] I will say that UN can succeed in peacekeeping and UN can even succeed in peacemaking or even in peace enforcement, on condition that we receive the necessary support from the member states. There are certain elements where certainly we failed, like what happened in Goradze is a failure for the United Nations, I will even say more, it is a humiliation for the United Nations, but this is one setback. You must see the other element of the other situation where we have been successful -- in Cambodia, in Salvador. [Frost] Do you think there's any hope that the safe havens, so-called, will become safe in the future or will the phrase safe haven seem like a bitter joke? [Butrus-Ghali] No the safe haven is just a step toward the comprehensive solution of the problem of former Yugoslavia. The safe haven must be considered as just a step. We need to continue to negotiate, we need a global approach so that we will reach a comprehensive peace and a comprehensive just and lasting peace in former Yugoslavia. We must continue to negotiate, we must continue the dialogue until we find a peaceful solution. And until we move to a second step -- we have already appointed a coordinator in Sarajevo for the reconstruction, because this is the first step, once we agree then we have to build the peace, we have to do the reconstruction, the rehabilitation, the return of millions of refugees.
