September 13, 1990, Thursday, Home Edition

FOOD AND COMMON SENSE: DO WE HAVE A NATURAL HUNGER FOR NUTRIENTS?

In Woody Allen's movie "Sleeper," set far in the future, he explains to a surprised traveler from the past that hot-fudge sundaes and steaks have been found to be good for you. We'd all like to live that fantasy: having foods that look, smell and taste good turn out to be the most nutritious. Even better would be if the reverse were true: if finding a food distasteful signaled that it was not nutritious, or even plain bad for us.

Alas, that implies an inborn ability to select a good diet and avoid a poor one -- equipment we lack. It's true that we do have some general genetic information handed down from remote ancestors, as well as more recent scientific and cultural guidelines. Otherwise, we're on our own in dealing with a huge, varied food supply perhaps as challenging as that faced by the hunter-gatherers of long ago. The human race has been so successful as a species partly because we are omnivores, able to adapt to new sources of nourishment -- provided we experiment with caution.

By this time in our evolution, the ways we choose foods are complex. Dr. Paul Rozin, professor of psychology at the University of Pennsylvania, recently published an update in Nutrition Reviews on how long-lasting food preferences develop.

There are a few genetically programmed biases for or against certain food substances. Thousands of years ago, two bits of enduring information were acquired by our nomadic progenitors: Sweetness provides energy and bitterness can signal harm.

Today, when drops of salty, sour, bitter or sweet solutions are placed on the tongues of newborn babies, their expressions range from emphatic rejection of the bitter solution to beaming acceptance of the sweet one. The age-old lesson is still fresh.

Humans also instinctively display ambivalence about new foods -- suspicion coupled with curiosity. Historically, man has had an inborn ability to make the link between a given food and unpleasant consequences. This information was handed down through the generations, promoting survival of the species.

There are innate hungers for a few essential nutrients. Earlier in this century, Dr. Curt Richter conducted classic studies with rats (another successful omnivorous species). Sodium-deficient for the first time, the rats selected high-sodium foods. Made thirsty, they chose foods high in water. It's also possible, but unproven, that there are innate hungers for protein, carbohydrate and calcium.

Be that as it may, over time humans (and rats) learn to identify foods that are deficient in specific nutrients and foods that supply the lack. Long before a deficiency of Vitamin C was pinpointed as a cause of scurvy, British ships were carrying preventive medicine in the form of limes (citrus fruits are high in Vitamin C) on long voyages. Centuries before, the diet of Chinese sailors included the fresh, Vitamin C-rich leaves of ginger plants.

Predominantly, however, our food choices are influenced by culture and ethnicity, and by individual experiences, pleasant and unpleasant. Sometimes cultural knowledge is a plus. The ancient Mexican practice of soaking corn kernels in lime water to soften them for tortillas also releases niacin in the corn for absorption by the body and helps prevent pellagra in corn-eating populations. Fermenting milk to produce yogurt makes it possible for lactose-intolerant people to enjoy its nutritional benefits, among them protein, riboflavin and calcium.

On the other hand, if you belong to the people of northern Japan, who habitually eat a high-salt diet, you have a better chance of developing hypertension than inhabitants of the southern Japanese islands, where salt consumption is low. An innate recognition of the need for enough sodium is not paralleled by an inner gauge signaling when you've had too much. Similarly, the universal inborn preference for sweet flavors doesn't extend to an instinctive knowledge that sugary foods, particularly sticky ones, are harmful to teeth.

Within cultures, there are many variations in individual food preferences. Strangely enough, though, family relationships have little impact on individual choices. Heredity plays a role in obesity, but bows out when it comes to food selection. Even a mother's or father's likes and dislikes have no great influence on those of their children.

Why the family has so little imprint on individual choices is a mystery. Another public-health puzzle is why people like or dislike foods because of their intrinsic sensory properties. Obviously, it's easier to persuade people to eat healthy foods if they also taste good. Worldwide, the food industry is devoting some $2.5 billion annually to finding ways to give flavor to low-salt products, add sweetness to foods low in sugar, or introduce a rich and creamy "fat" sensation without using fat.

Both as nutritionists and as food lovers, our taste is for fresh and lightly processed foods. But with all this research going on, maybe Allen's hot-fudge-sundae future isn't so far away after all.

Question: While listening to a TV talk show that took viewers' calls, I heard a mother call in and ask if it was safe to let her two daughters, age 10 and 12, become vegetarians. The answer given was that it was fine, especially if the diet contained dairy products and eggs. However, the person on the show was a health reporter, not a nutritionist. Was the advice sound?

Answer: Indeed it was, as far as it went. A lacto-ovo vegetarian diet can provide enough of the essential nutrients young people need for growth and development. Contrary to popular belief, however, the nutrient that deserves special attention is not protein. Almost certainly, it will be present in sufficient amounts -- not only from dairy products and eggs but from the vegetables and grains the youngsters eat to get the calories they need.

Iron is a different story. In vegetarian diets, the amount of iron is more limited. Iron from vegetable sources also is absorbed less efficiently than iron from animal flesh. For these reasons, it's important to pay particular attention both to getting enough iron into the diet and to making sure that it's well absorbed.

Iron-rich foods include enriched and fortified breads and cereals, dark-green leafy vegetables and dried beans. The key to enhancing absorption from these foods is Vitamin C. That's found in generous amounts in many fruits and vegetables, among them citrus fruits, tomatoes, cantaloupe, honeydew, broccoli, green pepper and cabbage. Serving a C-source along with an iron source makes good nutritional sense.

Q: I bought a tube of concentrated tomato paste in an Italian grocery. The packaging had no instructions about how much to use in place of regular tomato paste in a can, and nobody in the store could tell me. Do you know?

A: Whether it comes in a tube or a can, we're talking about the same product. Either way, that means about 15 calories per tablespoon, virtually all from carbohydrate. Use the same amount as you would if the paste came in a can. The advantage of the tube is when a recipe only calls for a tablespoon or two. Of course, it can be more expensive to buy it in this form. You could open a can of paste when you need a small amount and divide the rest into small portions to store in the freezer until needed.

In this country, tubes of tomato paste and other seasoning are only beginning to become popular. In Europe, this form of packaging has been in use for a long time.

Q: Are there any differences in the Vitamin-C content of frozen orange juice prepared from concentrate versus the juice available in the dairy case?

A: The differences are insignificant. In six ounces of juice, the average amount of Vitamin C ranges from 74 to 76 milligrams, considerably more than the Recommended Daily Allowance of 60 mg. It's true that Vitamin-C losses occur as the juice sits in the refrigerator. To minimize losses, keep the juice tightly covered. Refrigerate it promptly after use, and buy amounts that you can use up within a few days.
