BFN ["Today" -- BTA lead] [Excerpts] Sofia, June 22 (BTA) -- During the second day of consultations on the Judiciary Act, which passed late last week, President Zhelyu Zhelev received Prosecutor General Ivan Tatarchev and the 25-member Supreme Judicial Council headed by its chairman, Justice Minister Petur Kornazhev. Supreme Court Chairman Ivan Grigorov set forth the council's opinion that 20 texts of the law contravene the Constitution. In contrast to other council members, Ani Kruleva, director of the National Investigative Service (NIS), believes the president should not veto the law, which meets with approval from investigators. According to her, the Judiciary Act adequately regulates the structure and functions of the NIS, as well as the status of investigators. President Zhelev, who is also Supreme Commander-in-Chief of the Armed Forces, today received Vice-Admiral Ventseslav Velkov, Commander of the Navy. The sides discussed the state of the Navy, ways to resolve its problems and the progress of reform, says a press release of the president's office. They exchanged views on the Breeze-94 Naval Exercise, the first in this country within the framework of the Partnership for Peace programme. Bulgaria, Russia, Ukraine, Romania, Turkey, Greece and the United States will be taking part in it and Georgia will send observers. Filip Dimitrov, leader of the Union of Democratic Forces (SDS), met with top-level representatives of the United Romany Alliance headed by Vasil Chaprazov, the SDS press office said. The Gypsy leaders expressed concern over the SDS's failure to commit itself sufficiently to the problems of the Gypsy community, which could make it easy prey to manipulations. Also on the agenda were national economic problems and prospects for cooperation between the two organizations for their resolution. The Press Office of the Bulgarian Socialist Party (BSP) today denied stories in DEMOKRATSIYA, EXPRESS and OTECHESTVEN VESTNIK, misquoting a statement by socialist leader Zhan Videnov who spoke at a news conference yesterday [21 June]. The Press Office said questioners had asked Mr. Videnov to comment on the BSP's silence and lack of official stand on the census in Macedonia [The Former Yugoslav Republic of Macedonia -- FYROM] and Skopje's policy on ethnic minority issues, to which he responded, "On the contrary, the Bulgarian Socialist Party was the only political force two and a half years ago to refuse to applaud the hasty unconditional recognition of the Republic of Macedonia, arguing that, predictably, such problems would arise between the two countries." According to Mr. Videnov, the language problem, the problem over the "Bulgarian" option in the census forms, and the "Macedonian" option in similar censuses in Bulgaria were bound to arise. He went on to say, "Disputes over the historical legacy were also bound to arise, and territorial, maybe even border disputes, are probably taking shape. No doubt, national security is another such area of which the two states have profoundly different concepts. For Skopje, it amounts to the presence of foreign troops on Macedonian territory, while Bulgaria sees it as balanced and peaceful relations with all of its neighbours, integrated into the European and world security systems. Thus the BSP cannot in the least be accused of taking a conformist, vague and inconsistent stand on these issues. They cannot mar Bulgarian-Macedonian relations, though many opportunities for their preventive settlement have been lost in the last two and a half years. Now, undoubtedly, the responsibility will have to be shouldered by the respective state institutions, and not by those who warned that the complicated configuration in the Balkans was fraught with grave difficulties." National Bank Governor Todor Vulchev met today with members of the BSP leadership, national radio reported. Speaking to a radio reporter, BSP floor leader Nora Ananieva said the meeting had taken place at Mr. Vulchev's request. Doncho Konakchiev of the Socialists' top leadership said the BSP had asked for such a meeting much earlier and it had been occasioned, among other things, by vacancies on the central bank's Governing Board and forecasts about the exchange rate of the lev. President Zhelev uses the political centre to fulfill his ambitions, Velislava Dureva of the Social Democracy Alliance within the BSP told a news conference today. [passage omitted] Speaking at a news conference in Razgrad (northern Bulgaria), Nedim Gendzhev, former chief mufti, now leader of the Democratic Party of Justice, said he expected the World Bank to extend a $1 million loan for the opening of food enterprises in northeastern Bulgaria (a region with a large ethnic Turk population), as well as for other projects in the region. Gendzhev believes the Organization of the Islamic Conference may rescind its decisions against Bulgaria. [passage omitted] Experts of the Ministry of Labour and Social Affairs and of the Confederation of Independent Trade Unions (KNSB) hold similar views on the government ordinance on permissible levels of pollutants in industrial units, the press learned from KNSB Deputy Chairman Zhelyazko Khristov today. The entry into force of the ordinance, scheduled for January 1, was postponed at the trade union's request. If it is enforced in its present shape, some 220,000 people working in a harmful environment will lose their social benefits, Dr. Khristov said. [passage omitted] In an open letter to Prime Minister Lyuben Berov, the two influential trade unions -- KNSB and Podkrepa -- today called for the resignation of Culture Minister Ivaylo Znepolski. Their demand is motivated by what they see as a "disgraceful action aimed to eliminate the institutions which direct, create and popularize Bulgarian culture". The customs office in Kyustendil has collected over 1.5 million leva in fines for customs irregularities this year, BTA's correspondent reported. Cigarettes, batteries, water pumps and welders have been confiscated and 5 kilos of heroin has been seized.
