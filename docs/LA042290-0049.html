April 22, 1990, Sunday, Home Edition

LOS ANGELES ART: IS IT A (WORLD) CLASS ACT?

George Santayana once said, "Most people think that genius is as common as cheese, whereas it is as rare as love." Christopher Knight's "Is L.A. a World-Class Art City?" (April 8) was well intentioned and made some good points but didn't bite the real bullet.

The trouble is that he confuses art success with institutional and commercial success. Nothing could be farther apart. Art is simply much harder to do than you think.

You can't (and never will) have a real art city, center, movement, whatever, if you persist in calling second-class art first-class art. It's as simple as that.

What passes as first-class art is mainly halfhearted, half-understood, half-achieved art efforts that have been puffed up by culturally illiterate taste makers; these pass off the second-rate goods largely to perpetuate their authority and to ingratiate themselves with a possibly more ignorant collector class whose interest it is to call it all art so that they can make power plays with their money and social networks (including museums).

The thing that has made New York an art town is not the money, the hype, the hoke, the museums, but the continuing existence of a relatively few artists who aspire to serious achievement. The rest is a media success.

Marcel Duchamp (influential but himself somewhere between a second- and a third-rate artist) held that when times were bad for art, the real artists would go underground. That's where most of them are now -- but the promoters and the puffers-up will never discover it.

LOUIS FINKELSTEIN

(A visiting New Yorker)

Temecula, Calif.
