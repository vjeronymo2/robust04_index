June 21, 1990, Thursday, Home Edition

FOOD AND COMMON SENSE: TIME FOR A 'HEALTHY SUMMER' PLAN

Vacation time spells freedom from work, and this casual, carefree attitude may stretch into our diets as well. In today's "What, me worry?" atmosphere, caution and concern about health are sacrificed on the altar of the great god of leisure.

"I work so hard the rest of the year," goes the thinking, "that I deserve to gratify my every wish for these few weeks. Besides, what harm can it do?"

No harm at all -- if you don't mind letting your health slide and your waistline spread. But don't expect us to take the killjoy role and warn you against breaking the rules. Au contraire: If you're determined to enjoy absolute freedom from sound nutrition during a vacation, here are some suggestions to help you get the job done right.

Forget regular meals. Who needs the hassle of planning and cooking? Encourage your family to eat on the run. Stock the refrigerator with cold cuts, snack foods and soft drinks. You'll never have to sit down together at a regular meal; you can all gobble at different times. Try to eat out a lot, too, grabbing frankfurters, colas and ice cream at the ballpark or beach.

If you must cook, make it a thick, juicy steak or a grilled hamburger layered in cheese, rather than chicken, which requires somewhat more time to prepare.

Fresh vegetables abound in summer, which is way too much work on vacation. Even frozen vegetables take time to heat up, so don't bother. If the family screams for a side dish, pass a bowl of potato chips. And why serve fresh fruit for dessert when store-bought cookies are so much easier?

If you're taking a trip with loved ones, stop as often as possible at fast-food eateries. You'll be able to eat your fill of fat, sugar and sodium-laden foods such as fried chicken and fried clams, hamburgers slathered in catsup and pickles and thick shakes. There's nothing like a load of calories consumed at high speed to bring a family together.

And speaking of traveling with kids, one way to keep them quiet in the car is to fill their little mouths with sticky sweets and candies. This practice is a great money-saver too, because if you stuff them enough they'll be too bloated to eat anything when you sit down at a restaurant.

Restaurants, by the way, are a great place for throwing nutritional rules out the window. Don't forget to eat all the bread and butter they provide, and order twice as much food as usual because, hey, it's your vacation and you're supposed to be having fun, not worrying about little things like your heart or your weight.

But let's not confine ourselves to eating. Those hot summer days are the perfect time to drink up. It's true that low-fat milk and icy fruit or vegetable drinks are just as refreshing and more nutritious than soda, but who wants to play safe? Any old stick-in-the-mud can have low-calorie iced tea, coffee with skim milk, or plain or sparkling water with a twist. But a real bon vivant can guzzle down two or three gin and tonics or four or more cans of soda in the course of an afternoon.

At that high-calorie rate, you must be having fun, right? And you can double the thrill by eating nuts or chips hand-over-fist along with drinks.

Now let's not forget one of the most important guidelines of all: relaxing. Vacation means rest, so don't even think about exercising. If any misguided friends suggest taking a bicycle trip, playing tennis or swimming laps, tell them to take a hike -- without you. Make yourself comfy, lie back on the couch or hammock and drift into never-never land. On your sacred vacation, walking to the refrigerator is all the physical labor you need.

On the other hand, maybe relaxing really isn't your style. If you're cut from more energetic cloth, our advice is just the opposite: Don't relax. Ever. And don't let your family relax, either. Drag them on a sightseeing trip and make sure you see everything, even if it means hustling. Night flights are nice, and they put everyone in a good mood. Long car trips are great too, especially if children are small.

Whatever you do, make sure the pace never slackens, because why did you come all this way if you weren't going to get the most for your time and money? And no whining, please. This is your vacation.

What? None of the above sounds appealing? Then forget it all and follow a more sensible path. Because rest assured, you can have a happy holiday without putting health on hold and paying a price later.

Question: A friend insists that her chronic fatigue results from a yeast infection. In response, she follows a somewhat odd diet and avoids refined sugar and alcohol. Can you tell me about this?

Answer: Called by at least three names -- "yeast connection," "candidiasis hypersensitivity" and "the missing diagnosis" -- this concept gained popularity as an explanation for chronic fatigue in two popular books of the early 1980s.

Besides chronic fatigue, the "syndrome" includes a laundry list of symptoms from headache and lethargy to skin rashes, muscle aches, tearing eyes and bloating. It's also used to account for a variety of genito-urinary symptoms, among them vaginal burning and itching, prostatitis and even impotence. The long-term use of antibiotics is often singled out as the root cause.

Many scientifically indefensible "treatments" have been offered at clinics specializing in treatment of the alleged problem. These include various dietary manipulations and, predictably, dietary supplements.

In a recent study of 100 individuals with chronic fatigue, conducted by investigators at the University of Connecticut School of Medicine, eight believed their problem to be related to a "yeast connection." Their physical symptoms and laboratory profiles did not differ from the 92 others who came with a complaint of "chronic fatigue." Three of the eight thought they had an active candida infection. But in all cases, tests for the presence of the fungus were negative.

The upshot? Yeast infections do occur, but evidence that chronic fatigue is related to Candida albicans is lacking. Alleviation of the problem, along with uncomfortable symptoms popularly ascribed to a yeast connection, is best left in the hands of a competent physician.

Q: Can you straighten me out about which fruits and vegetables need to be blanched before they are frozen?

A: In general, vegetables are blanched and fruits are not. Peppers and tomatoes are exceptions to the vegetable rule and can go into the freezer as is.

The purpose of blanching is to inactivate enzymes and prevent other chemical reactions that impair flavor, texture and color even at very low temperatures. Blanching also helps remove air from tissues, making it possible to pack a greater amount of vegetable into a small space. But with fruits, the adverse effect on flavor and texture make blanching impractical. However, using sugar syrup with ascorbic acid added to it helps prevent browning.

Blanching is usually accomplished by pouring boiling water over the vegetables and letting them sit for a short period, and then quickly reducing the temperature by immersing vegetables in cold water to stop the cooking process. Blanching time varies from one vegetable to another. To ensure top quality, follow directions carefully. For information about this and other aspects of home preservation, try "Putting Food By" by Janet Greene, Ruth Hertzberg and Beatrice Vaughan (Stephen Greene Press).

Q: Several years ago I recall reading about a link between increasing intake of Vitamin A and reducing the risk of lung cancer. Did that idea ever pan out?

A: The idea hasn't been disproved, but it has been modified. It's now 15 years since Norwegian researchers first reported that after taking cigarette smoking into account, the rate of lung cancer was one-third less among men whose Vitamin A intake was estimated to be above average compared with those with below-average intake. In that study, effects of preformed Vitamin A were not considered separately from those of carotenoids found in dark green vegetables and in yellow fruits and vegetables, which the body can convert to Vitamin A.

Carotenes do more than function as precursors of Vitamin A. They can also act to prevent undesirable reactions in the body. Several years later, the first follow-up study among the Norwegians confirmed that the protective effect against lung cancer was linked to carrots and other vegetables, with an added contribution from milk, which contains both carotenes and preformed Vitamin A. Since then, other research has shown similar links.

A different type of investigation, so-called case-controlled studies, have compared dietary intakes of individuals with and without the disease. Again, the generally consistent observation is that carotenoids -- but not preformed Vitamin A -- exert a protective influence.

Using yet another approach, levels of beta carotene in blood samples that had been collected for other purposes were estimated. Levels in individuals who later developed lung cancer were lower than in those who didn't develop it. But whether the beneficial link is with carotenoids themselves or with some other factor associated with carotenoids is uncertain. Some controlled studies in which some subjects are given beta carotene and others are given a placebo are currently under way. Results of these studies should shed light on the relationship.

It appears that the link is to something in fruits and vegetables, especially those rich in carotenoids. A good case can be made for eating them in generous amounts. But remember that any protective effect pales compared to what you get by avoiding smoking.
