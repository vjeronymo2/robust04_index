BFN [Report by Mariano Guindal: "Government Says That It Will Be Possible To Create 1,000 Net Jobs Per Day Over Next Two Years"] [Text] Madrid -- Minister of the Presidency Alfredo Perez Rubalcaba stated following the cabinet meeting yesterday that 2,000 net jobs per day were created in May, according to the initial figures supplied by the National Employment Institute (INEM). According to him, Social Security has had 105,638 more contributors in the first five months of the year than at the end of 1993. The government believes that this good performance by the labor market will continue in June. This could offset the adverse impact on employment that the entry of young people into the labor market on completing their education traditionally has. Last June, unemployment grew by 35,000, and the estimates at the Labor Ministry's disposal forecast that this figure will be offset. Rubalcaba stated the the government anticipates that job creation will be consolidated as economic growth improves. Over the next few weeks, the government will study the new plan for convergence with Europe, which forecasts economic growth of 1.3 percent for 1995 and twice as much for 1996, reaching 3.5 percent in 1996 [year as published]. This growth of GDP suggests that 1,000 net jobs per day could be created over the next two years. "That is why this assertion by the labor minister is something more than an election slogan," Rubalcaba stated. The minister said that the cabinet received with great satisfaction the Community's assistance for the development of the most backward Spanish regions, totaling 4 trillion pesetas. "If the cohesion funds are added to the structural funds, it can be said that Spain will receive 6 trillion pesetas from Europe between 1994 and 1999." These resources will have a direct impact on job creation, "and this has been included in the convergence program, whose revision will be presented in Brussels in July." Rubalcaba stated that the structural funds have three aims -- to improve the production system, train human resources, and create water and power infrastructures -- although he said that the construction of transport infrastructures, which was the previous program's aim, will slacken. Some 77 percent of Spanish territory and 66 percent of the population, which falls within the so-called first objective, will benefit from this assistance. The minister explained that "the aim is to promote public investment in the most disadvantaged regions and their gradual alignment with the Community average." These investments total 7.58 trillion pesetas, half of which will be contributed by Europe and the remainder by the Spanish Government. Andalusia is the autonomous community that benefits most, with 28.8 percent of the funds; Galicia has 13.7 percent; Castilla and Leon, 12 percent; Valencia, 11.2 percent; Castilla-La Mancha, 8 percent; the Canary Islands, 6.8 percent; and the remainder is shared out by Asturias, Cantabria, Murcia, and Ceuta and Melilla. Most of these funds -- 34.3 percent -- will be earmarked for developing human resources and improving the quality of life through vocational training programs and environmental projects. Some 33 percent will be earmarked for improving the production system, with programs to assist industry, tourism, fisheries, and research. Some 24.8 percent will be earmarked for transport infrastructures, while 7.9 percent will be invested in water and energy. With respect to Seat [automobile enterprise], the minister stated that, although this is not scheduled, it is not ruled out that Prime Minister Felipe Gonzalez and German Chancellor Helmut Kohl will discuss this problem during the summit that they are to hold next Monday [6 June]. He emphasized that "Seat is not in danger," and stressed that "Volkswagen has never proposed winding up the make." He stated that "the Spanish Government will help to seek a solution and to save as many jobs as possible."
