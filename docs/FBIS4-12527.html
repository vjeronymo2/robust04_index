BFN [Text] India has cautioned the United States against triggering of an arms race in the subcontinent by supplying sophisticated aircraft to Pakistan. The Indian side told the U.S. deputy secretary of State, Mr. Strobe Talbott, that any supply of F-16 aircraft to Islamabad will compel New Delhi to take measures in response. This will diversify the much-needed resources for the welfare of the people and affect the ongoing economic reforms. It was emphatically stated at the talks that the U.S. move is of great concern to New Delhi. The Indian side said in the public mind there is a U.S. tilt toward Pakistan seen as the replay of the cold war attitude. It said that Jammu and Kashmir is an integral part of India and territorial integrity will not be sacrificed at any cost. On nuclear nonproliferation and disarmament, India backs the 1988 action plan announced by Rajiv Gandhi which offers several initiatives for achieving the goal. On the Washington-backed Pakistan proposal for a multilateral fora for nonproliferation, India and the U.S. agreed to continue the dialogue on the concept. Mr. Talbott had an hour-long meeting with the prime minister today. Mr. Narasimha Rao has accepted in principle President Clinton's invitation to visit the United States. The dates of the visit will be worked out later by the two sides. Addressing a press conference, Mr. Talbott ruled out supply of F-16 aircraft to Pakistan, if Islamabad does not agree to a verifiable cap on its nuclear weapons program. He said nuclear proliferation is a major issue which needs to be addressed immediately. He said the U.S. favors expansion of the UN Security Council and India being the largest democracy is a clear leader to (?claim) a position in the council. Mr. Talbott has since reached Islamabad on a three-day visit to Pakistan.
