Cabinet Support Rate Remains Unchanged
The support rate for the Hosokawa cabinet remained almost unchanged from the previous survey. However, we should mention that two big political events took place during the last month. First, the enactment of the political reform bills was determined at the 28 January talks between Prime Minister Hosokawa and LDP President Kono even though that was thought impossible after rejection by the upper house. Most of the public welcomed the enactment while it did not believe the bills can completely clean up Japan's politics. Second, Prime Minister Hosokawa suddenly announced his plan to introduce the public welfare tax at a news conference held early in the morning of 3 February. The SDPJ opposed this idea at the risk of its own survival in the coalition, and the mass media also blamed the Hosokawa decision made "without adequate explanation to the public." In some opinion polls conducted immediately after the announcement, the support rate for Prime Minister Hosokawa dropped by nearly 20 percent. In our survey, however, the support rate remained unchanged since Prime Minister Hosokawa immediately apologized to the public dropping his tax increase proposal. Support for SDPJ Also Unchanged
It is said that the SDPJ lost public support when a lot of rebels contributed to the upper house's rejection of the political reform bills, and that it then won back popularity by saying no to the public welfare tax. Such moves were not observed in our monthly survey on the SDPJ support rate. In the February survey, the support rate for the coalition dropped by 4 percentage points from the previous month, and this was as low as that for the LDP. Public Perceptions of Living Conditions
While it is believed Japan is still experiencing recession, voters' perceptions of their own living conditions were not very bad in the last 12 months. Over 60 percent answered their living conditions remain unchanged, and 35.5 percent said they are facing difficulties in making their living. These figures are a little better than those in December 1993. While the public is very concerned about the prolonged recession, it probably does not seriously impact on the living of individuals. ------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |Table 5. Pollees' Voting Intentions
The top three popular parties were the LDP, the JNP, and the SDPJ. Compared with the January survey, support for Shinseito dropped by 2 percent. NOTE: The survey, conducted by Chuo Research Inc. on 10-13 February, was carried on as individual interviews of 2,000 eligible voters picked at random. Responses were obtained from 68.1 percent of, or 1,361, pollees. Data belong to the Multipurpose Statistic Databank of Tsukuba University.
