September 15, 1990, Saturday, Home Edition

ENTERTAINERS SWEETEN WALK IN GARDENS;

MUSIC: ABOUT 15 GROUPS REPRESENTING DIFFERENT CULTURES WILL PERFORM IN DESCANSO GARDEN NOOKS FOR VISITORS WHO STROLL PAST TODAY AND SUNDAY.

Strolling entertainers are nothing new, but it is the audience that will do the strolling at Descanso Gardens during the Los Angeles Festival's closing weekend today and Sunday.

Performers will set up in nooks and crannies throughout the lush Los Angeles County park in La Canada Flintridge while the public circulates like diners sampling food from an exotic smorgasbord.

"The sound will lead you through the gardens," said Patty Mannatt, special events coordinator for the county Department of Arboreta and Botanic Gardens. "Descanso Gardens has a lot of camellia trees that have grown into a camellia forest, and there are lots of little pockets that will hold 25 or 50 people who can stop and listen to a performer."

Organizers opted for the unusual setup in part to give people a chance to see performers who have appeared at locations throughout the Los Angeles area.

Among the groups scheduled: the Chinese Classical Music Ensemble; Garba-Ras, Indian folk dancers who invite audience participation; the Nigerian Talking Drum Ensemble; the World Kulintang Institute, performers of gong music of the Muslim Philippines; Bibak, 130 performers from the Philippines; Los Pregoneros del Puerto featuring harp virtuoso Jose Pepe Gutierrez and Hawaiian dancer Randy Chang.

In all, about 15 groups are scheduled, including last minute additions like the traditional Bolivian music group, Miriam Mita and Ayllu Sankayo.

Schedules of final times and locations of performances will be handed out at the park entrance.

"There will always be something going on," said Peggy Riley, producer of the Descanso Gardens weekend. "It's very free-form. People can sit and listen, then move to a new place and hear something new."

The park will open at 9 a.m.; entertainment begins at noon each day and ends at 6 p.m., although Sunday's program is expected to run until dusk to mark the festival's closing. There are some benches, but most seating will be on the ground.

Music is presented every Sunday during the summer on the main lawn, but this is the first time that artists will play at various spots. Simultaneous performances raise the prospect of cacophony, but organizers said they expect no problem. The park, a popular wedding site with a waterfall, is large enough and the vegetation thick enough to keep the music from clashing, they said.

Traffic and parking are not expected to present problems, either. Mannatt said that an annual garden festival attracts about as many people as are expected this weekend and that "people get in and out just fine."

Festival organizers hope to draw 4,000 people each day. Descanso Gardens holds 2,000.

"If everyone came at the same time, we'd be in trouble," said Mannatt, "but that's not going to happen. People will be coming and going all day."

Both Mannatt and Gail Boatwright, head of the Descanso Gardens Guild, a nonprofit support group, said no community opposition has surfaced to the Los Angeles Festival weekend. The guild sponsors all public events at the gardens.

"We approve of what they're doing and we're cooperating," Boatwright said, adding that she does not fear damage by crowds to the garden's plants and lawns.

"That's quite a lot of people for the area, but we're hoping they'll be spread out throughout the day," she said. "There will be monitors to make sure that groups of people don't get too big."

Ethnic food will be sold near the parking area and there are picnic tables nearby. Eating is permitted on the main lawn area but not in the rest of the park because it is a garden. Alcoholic beverages are prohibited.

Admission is $3 admission for adults, $1.50 for senior citizens and students, and 75 cents for children 5 to 12. Children under 5 are free.
