910507 FT 07 MAY 91 / Economics: German data will be scrutinised THERE are no economic statistics scheduled for release in the UK or France but plenty this week in Germany and the US. With the D-Mark so weak on foreign exchanges, and anxiety growing about the shape of the post-unification economy, the releases will be scrutinised for signs that the German decline has been exaggerated. This might prompt sentiment that the D-Mark is oversold. Improved weather in March could boost the production data scheduled for release this week. For eastern Germany, data is thin on the ground but anecdotal evidence suggests further output falls this year. Analysts expect foreign manufacturing orders to have revived after the dollar's recovery. Orders offer good clues to future growth in gross national product, which is expected to slow to 1.9 per cent this year from 4.5 per cent in 1990. Unemployment data should continue to show that joblessness is falling in the west of the country, but not as fast in April as in March. The current account, meanwhile, was in deficit for the second month running in February, partly as a result of Germany's contributions to the allied effort in the Gulf War. With domestic demand strengthening, Germany's merchandise trade position has been deteriorating, with export volumes lower than a year ago and imports markedly higher. In the US the focus will be on the Treasury's quarterly refunding. Though the outlook for US bonds is favourable - with last week's cut in the discount rates and evidence of subdued inflation likely in producer prices figures on Friday - there are fears that the Treasury will eventually have to raise more funds because of the recession. This is making the market anxious about supply and the likely absence of Japanese buyers.
