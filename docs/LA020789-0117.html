February 7, 1989, Tuesday, Home Edition

BARBARA TUCHMAN DIES; AWARD-WINNING HISTORIAN

Historian Barbara Tuchman, who for nearly 50 years wrote steadfastly of her belief that the world is the creation of men and women "invested with power to affect their destiny," died Monday.

The two-time Pulitzer Prize-winning author of a dozen books, most recent among them "The First Salute," a current best-seller, died of complications following a stroke.

She was 77 and had lived for years in Cos Cob, Conn. She was taken from there to a Greenwich Hospital, where she died.

Ironically, Mrs. Tuchman, who became one of the most popular historians of her time, was not trained in history.

"It's what saved me," the 1933 Radcliffe College graduate once admitted in an interview. "I think if I had taken a doctoral degree, it would have stifled any writing capacity."

Since her first book in 1938, her narrative accounts of history were praised for their literary merit. She answered that she loved to "do" history, rather than merely write about it.

For "The Guns of August" in 1962, she walked the battlefields of World War I; for "A Distant Mirror: The Calamitous Fourteenth Century" in 1978, she trod the same path as the Crusaders of that distant time and traced their tumult by following the career of a feudal lord, Enguerrand de Coucy VII.

She won the Pulitzer Prize for history for "The Guns of August" and for "Stilwell and the American Experience in China" in 1971.

She was always more popular with her public than with academicians, many of whom disdained her lack of credentials. But as the London Times Literary Supplement once reported, "Tuchman may not be a historian's historian, but she is a pleasure for the layman to read."

It was one of many accolades the privileged daughter of Maurice and Alma Wertheim struggled hard to achieve.

She was born to a family of diplomats and bankers: Her grandfather had been U.S. ambassador to Turkey and Mexico under President Woodrow Wilson and an uncle was secretary of the Treasury under President Franklin D. Roosevelt. In times of limited opportunities for women, she had come to look on herself as "just a Park Avenue matron."

Out of school, she became an editorial assistant for the Nation magazine and American correspondent for the London-based New Statesman. Then in 1940, she married Dr. Lester R. Tuchman.

With the children that followed, she told Newsweek magazine a few years ago, it became difficult to achieve a long-dreamed ambition: to begin creating word pictures of the paths of history.

She was, she stated without complaint, the wife of a physician and "had three small children and no status whatsoever."

But she gradually carved out the time and had written "The Lost British Policy: Britain and Spain Since 1700" (1938) and "Bible and Sword: England and Palestine from the Bronze Age to Balfour" (1956) before coming up with a captivating account of intrigue that became her first best-seller.

It was "The Zimmermann Telegram," published in 1958, in which she traced the World War I German telegram to Mexico that promised the Mexicans part of the American Southwest if Mexico would enter the war on Germany's side. The discovery of the document was credited with helping bring the United States into the war in 1917.

"The Guns of August" was an even bigger best-seller. Although it focused on the tactical and logistical aspects of World War I, it was widely praised for illuminating aspects of the conflict as would a storyteller by a campfire.

In "The Proud Tower: A Portrait of the World Before the War, 1890-1914," published in 1966, she examined society at the end of the last century and the beginning of this one. The years before World War I, she wrote, "lie like a band of scorched earth dividing that time from ours."

Her second Pulitzer came for "Stilwell and and the American Experience in China." It traced the career of Joseph Stilwell, the feisty commander of U.S. troops in China, Burma and India before and during World War II, who watched as China moved from a feudal republic to communism.

In 1979, she became the first woman elected to the Jefferson lectureship of the National Endowment for the Humanities. A year earlier, she had won the gold medal for history from the American Academy of Arts and Sciences.

She also lectured in such disparate spots as Harvard University and the Naval War College, but she continued to write. For her next work, she retreated six centuries from wartime China for "A Distant Mirror."

In 1981, she put out what might be described as a Tuchman primer -- a volume of her selected essays called "Practicing History." It explored her views of how history should be written while also promulgating thoughts on subjects as diverse as Watergate and Vietnam.

In 1984, nearly all of recorded history became her palette in "The March of Folly: From Troy to Vietnam."

Los Angeles Times book critic Richard Eder called it a collection of "historical folly: a kind of prideful, or as she puts it, 'wooden-headed' error committed by powerful societies, despite the presence of voices arguing against it and the existence of visible alternatives."

Her subjects ranged from Britain's ill-fated struggle with its American Colonies to the Trojan War to the Renaissance Papacy to Vietnam.

Mrs. Tuchman's latest book, "The First Salute," a look at the American Revolution and the Dutch revolution that preceded it by 200 years, has been on the New York Times best-seller list for more than nine weeks.

In one of her last public appearances, an interview at an October book signing, Mrs. Tuchman said she had not given any thought to her next book.

"I don't really need a new project now," Tuchman said. "I need a good, long rest, really."

At her death, the woman who chose to ignore a doctoral degree while in school could count 22 honorary ones from a like number of institutions.

The New Republic wrote once that "Tuchman's books provide (her readers) with examples that lift the level of their lives. Before the academic deluge, this was what history was supposed to do -- to teach philosophy by example -- and few writers have performed this office more inspiringly than Barbara Tuchman."
