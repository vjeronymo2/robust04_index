December 28, 1989, Thursday, Home Edition

MISS MANNERS: CASH GIFTS TO BOSS ARE IMPROPER

DEAR MISS MANNERS -- The boss was given $100 each by two married men. She is married, and she took it for a Christmas present.

I say no married man with any morals would consider doing this, especially if he thought anything of home and family. And no married woman with morals would accept a gift of that amount.

So that means the two men were without morals to offer it, and she had no morals in taking it.

I think it was tasteless and rude. One of the wives is thinking of divorcing her husband over it. It's full of trouble for all three parties.

GENTLE READER -- Miss Manners hopes that you are not the wife thinking of divorcing her husband, and that you will discourage any such action in cases of which you know. The situation you describe is trouble, all right, but it's not that much trouble.

Presents to one's boss are inappropriate from any employee. Money given in a working situation at the end of the year is called a Christmas bonus, and it is properly given by a boss to employees.

To violate this practice may be foolish or subservient, but it is not immoral. And who is married and what anybody thinks of his family has nothing whatever to do with it.

DEAR MISS MANNERS -- My husband and I were invited to a Christmas party, with an "open house" invitation of the kind that you buy at a stationery store. It did not indicate the proper form of dress. We are all professional people in our early 30s.

We arrived at this party in casual dress (my husband was without a jacket or tie and in deck shoes, and I was in slacks and a sweater) and were surprised to find ourselves at a dressy and catered affair. Many of the male guests were in tuxedos, and the rest were in jacket and tie. The women were all in holiday dresses.

It was obvious when we arrived that the hostess was not pleased with our dress. We spent the first half-hour wondering when we could leave. Finally we retreated to the kitchen until we felt it appropriate to excuse ourselves.

When I saw the hostess at work afterward, I thanked her and said that we thought it would be a more casual affair, otherwise we would have dressed more appropriately. She responded, "Oh, well, we had a little bit of everything." I thought this response was flippant.

This experience leaves us wondering what went wrong and hoping that it will never happen again. We feel that hosts have a responsibility to inform their guests, either directly or through the tone of the invitation, if the dress is to be anything but casual.

GENTLE READER -- While it is true that hosts are supposed to indicate what dress will be appropriate to their parties, Miss Manners is not going to exonerate you as thoroughly as you are prepared to exonerate yourself.

Among adults, standard dress is business attire, unless you are informed -- or know, from intimacy with the hosts' habits -- otherwise. That is what you should have worn. The men in tuxedos were every bit as wrong in their dress as you were.

But why do you object to your hostess being cheerfully flippant after the fact? Her comment, generalizing the error and trivializing it, seems to Miss Manners to have been in the right tone. Would you have preferred "Next time you'll know better"?
