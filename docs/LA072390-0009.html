July 23, 1990, Monday, Home Edition

NON-SENATOR FOR A NON-STATE

On Nov. 6, Jesse Jackson will be elected by voters here as a "shadow United States senator" for the District of Columbia.

This does not mean that Jesse Jackson is on the verge of becoming a real U.S. senator. For that, he would need to be elected by a state.

In fact, the "shadow senator" seat Jackson seeks here is designed not for lawmaking but lobbying. The only reason District of Columbia voters are being asked to choose two "shadow senators" and a "shadow congressman" this November is to give the district three high-level advocates for statehood.

Jackson holds a much grander notion of the job, of course. He's made it clear that from the day he is elected he expects to be treated like a member of the United States Senate -- admission to the floor, membership in the Senate Democratic Caucus and the right to participate in public hearings. He has no intention of limiting his new job description to that of lobbyist for D.C. statehood. Just like any senator, he will want to speak out on any issue, domestic or foreign, that engages his or the nation's attention.

Watching Jackson pull this off will tell us a great deal about the relative strengths of the old, coalition-built Democratic Party and the new media-built politics of racial polarization.

Let's be clear on this. If the Democrats were a self-confident party in the 1990's, if they knew who they were and were willing to act on it, the whole notion of the Senate Democratic leadership extending the trappings of membership to a lobbyist for the District of Columbia would be ludicrous. If Jesse Jackson were not both confident and fluent in the race-sensitive politics of the same 1990's he would not have the chutzpah to demand these things, not having earned them.

Should Jackson succeed even partially with this exasperating "shadow senator" gambit, the spoils of victory will be great. Just seconds after we see or hear Senate Majority Leader George Mitchell treat him like a senator, we will probably hear Dan Rather start calling him by that title. The term "Sen. Jesse Jackson" will be part of the nation's boilerplate.

Yet Jackson will not really have proved himself in public office. To win the credential he still needs for the Democratic presidential nomination, all Jackson has to do is to create the pretense of having won such a high-prestige position as U.S. Senator. In reality, he will be nothing more than a non-senator from a non-state. He will have the best of both worlds: a title without a job and a constituency without a record to defend.
