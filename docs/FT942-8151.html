940521 FT 21 MAY 94 / Finance and the Family: Permanent interest-bearing shares Prices of permanent interest-bearing shares have not differed substantially since the table was published on April 16. But movement has not been uniform, with most prices weakening, some improving and others static, writes Scheherazade Daneshkhu. Pibs are issued by building societies to raise capital; they pay a fixed income indefinitely but, since they have no redemption date, investors can get back their capital only by selling them in the stock market. There was excitement in the Pibs market when the proposed takeover of Cheltenham & Gloucester by Lloyds bank was announced last month and, soon after, by the news that North of England would merge with Northern Rock. Those holding C&G Pibs on or before March 31 1994 will be entitled to Pounds 500 (if they are a voting member) as well as 10 per cent of their holding, to a maximum of Pounds 10,000, when the takeover is completed. North of England has not announced a similar benefit for its clients but there has much speculative buying of Pibs issues from societies considered likely takeover candidates. These include the Newcastle, Cheshire, Bristol & West, and the Leeds Permanent. David Barker, director of Hoare Govett, says: 'Activity levels have picked up substantially in recent weeks and the market generally has strongly outperformed the gilt-edged market, which has been quite weak over the past month. 'The reasons are twofold - investors believe the high real yields offered by Pibs are attractive in a low-inflation environment, and many investors were lucky enough to own Pibs issued by C&G and North of England.
