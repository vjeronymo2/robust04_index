May 28, 1989, Sunday, Home Edition

BAKER'S INAPPROPRIATE ROLE AS AN EVENHANDED BROKER

Secretary of State Jim Baker's speech to the American Israel Political Action Committee last week is being widely interpreted as the first comprehensive, authoritative statement of the Bush Administration on the Arab-Israeli conflict. It is generally believed that its style and substance reflect the thoughts and feelings of the Bush-Baker team about problems of the region.

Examination of the speech establishes that it is less harshly critical of Israel than was generally reported, especially on radio and television. Nonetheless, the speech was not good news for Israel and its friends, and is more likely to heighten tensions than lower them.

The substantive proposals made by Baker were rendered less attractive than they might have been by some gratuitously negative language, which gave the speech its negative tone.

"Stop the challenges to Israel's standing in international organizations," he told the Arabs; "repudiate the odious line that Zionism is racism."

He told the Palestinians to renounce the policy of a phased takeover of Israel and to give up the uprising for diplomacy.

To Israelis he said, "Forswear annexation. Stop settlement activity. Allow schools to reopen. Reach out to the Palestinians as neighbors who deserve political rights."

There are important problems with Baker's speech. Most notable, perhaps, is the curious tone of evenhandedness in Baker's approach, the kind of assumption that fairness lies in a middle ground between Israeli and Palestinian goals, and his request that all parties -- Israelis, Palestinians and other Arabs -- make concessions.

Baker referred in the introductory portions of his speech to the fact that Israel is a "vigorous democracy" with which the United States has a "strategic partnership." But his discussion of the conflict communicated no sense of greater concern for one side than for the other. It communicated no sense that Israel is a beleaguered democracy confronting a violent adversary bent on its destruction, no sense that Israel has fought three wars for survival.

In a phrase seized on by the media, Baker called on Israel to "lay aside, once and for all, the unrealistic vision of a greater Israel." This is very strange language; it suggests that the secretary thinks that Israel is driven by an expansionist vision. But, of course, it was not expansionism that delivered the West Bank and Gaza to Israeli occupation. It was the need to defend against repeated attacks by neighbors.

Baker's curious language becomes curiouser when it is remembered that in the since 1967, no Israeli government has acted to annex the territories, and that, to the contrary, in the one available opportunity to negotiate peace with an Arab neighbor, Israel returned the Sinai to Egypt in exchange for a very formal peace. In fact, no significant political party in Israel is driven by the vision of a "greater Israel." And to suggest otherwise is unfair and inaccurate.

However, there is a state in the region driven by dreams of expansion. It is Assad's Syria, which occupies more than half of Lebanon. Jim Baker has yet to discuss this most-important obstacle to peace in the region.

It is not clear why Baker should be more concerned with the peace and rights of Palestinians than of Lebanese. It is not clear why a U.S. secretary of state has a greater concern with peace on the West Bank than with peace in Lebanon.

Baker's goal for the West Bank and Gaza seems to me reasonable and compatible with the security of Israel, Jordan and West Bank Palestinians. His support of Israel's proposed plan for elections in the territories is consistent with our values and Israel's. His recognition that it will take time and concession by all is realistic. But his desire to be (or to seem to be) an evenhanded broker in a life-and-death struggle involving a faithful ally seems to me inappropriate.

Responsibility for the Arab-Israeli conflict is not evenly distributed. This conflict was born of Arab refusal to accept the existence of a Jewish state, born of three Arab wars and of the refusal of Israel's neighbors even to discuss peace. It is a conflict that has led to the murder of Jews and Palestinians, and also of Anwar Sadat, Bashir Gemayel and other Arabs who have dared to advocate peace. It is a conflict that successive Israeli governments have sought to end -- even at great cost -- whenever a reasonable opportunity appeared.

These are simple facts that need to be faced, even in this age when neutrality is often confused with reason and justice.
