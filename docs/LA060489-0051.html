June 4, 1989, Sunday, Home Edition

ON AGING: FORD CO. DELVES INTO CUSTODIAL CARE

In an area where few have dared to tread, the Ford Motor Co. has begun a pilot program for workers in Louisville that pays for non-medical custodial care at home or in a nursing home. About 6,600 people, including active employees and those who have retired since 1987, and their dependents, are eligible.

The program provides help with activities of daily living -- eating, bathing, dressing, preparing meals and housecleaning -- for the severely impaired. It is intended to support rather than replace care provided by family and friends. Beneficiaries can briefly stay in a custodial nursing home, giving family members respite from care-giving.

The Jefferson County Department of Human Services manages the program under contract with Blue Cross and Blue Shield of Michigan and Kentucky. County nurses and social workers determine eligibility and supervise the care. The program extends longstanding employee health benefits at Ford, which include skilled nursing care under a physician's supervision and hospice care for the terminally ill.

The new health plan, requested by the United Automobile Workers Union, is a pioneer employee-financed program. Similar pilot programs are being planned at General Motors and Chrysler.

Question: I am a 67-year-old widow in excellent health who enjoys her independence and freedom. However, like everyone else, I am beginning to feel the pressures of inflation. While I could use extra cash, I don't want to sell my house or take in boarders. A friend suggested I apply for a reverse mortgage, but couldn't provide details. Can you?

Answer: Reverse mortgages, also called home-equity conversion mortgages, allow owners to convert the equity in a home into cash that does not have to be repaid until the borrower moves or dies.

A new Housing and Urban Development demonstration project will help those who own their homes but can't meet expenses to use their home equity as a source of income. Between now and Sept. 30, 1990, HUD will insure up to 2,500 FHA-insured reverse mortgages to homeowners age 62 or older who live in homes they own free and clear (or nearly so).

According to HUD's Office of Policy Development and Research, the insured mortgages offer three payment options: a tenure option, which provides monthly payments to homeowners as long as they occupy the home as a principal residence; a term option, which provides monthly payments for a fixed period selected by the homeowner; and a line-of-credit option, which permits homeowners to draw money at times and in amounts of their choosing up to a maximum amount.

In addition, the homeowner may receive lump-sum draws, combine a line of credit with monthly payments or change the payment plan if needed.

Regardless of the option selected, homeowners may continue to live in the house until they move, sell or die. They may sell their property at any time, retaining proceeds that exceed the amount needed to pay off the mortgage. Homeowners cannot be forced to sell their home to pay off the mortgage. HUD will insure lenders against the risk that the mortgage may grow to exceed the value of the property.

For more information and a list of participating lenders, contact HUD USER, P.O. Box 6091, Rockville, Md. 20850, (800) 245-2691 or (301) 251-5154.
