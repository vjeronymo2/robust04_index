941209 FT 09 DEC 94 / Post Office faces fresh investigation by MPs A powerful committee of MPs is to reopen its inquiry into the Post Office's future. The decision will intensify pressure on ministers to find ways of enabling the Post Office to secure its commercial future after the forced shelving of privatisation. The Commons trade and industry committee expects to question Mr Michael Heseltine, the trade and industry secretary, early in the new year, probably in January. Mr Bill Cockburn, the Post Office's chief executive, will also be invited to give evidence, along with other senior managers. With privatisation off the agenda many MPs are worried about the Post Office's ability to combat mounting competition unless it is given greater commercial freedom within the public sector. But in a decision that boded ill for the organisation's prospects of securing this increased freedom, Mr Kenneth Clarke, the chancellor, last week rejected pleas by the Post Office board for a Pounds 100m cut in next year's Pounds 213m government levy on post service profits. The Post Office campaigned for the reduction when plans to privatise Royal Mail, its letters division, were shelved last month after opposition from a small number of Tory backbenchers. Mr Cockburn said that a cut in the levy - known as the external finance limit (EFL) - was essential to increase capital investment. However the organisation has been cleared to handle a significantly greater range of transactions than previously permitted through its extensive retail network. Post Office managers are keen to exploit the guidelines to push into new areas, particularly in the financial services field. It recently presaged a possible move into selling general insurance products by announcing a deal with General Accident, the composite insurer, to sell travel insurance at many Post Office branches. The cross-party committee's first Post Office report, published in March, said the EFL and the 'traditional Treasury restraint' on the organisation's activities were having a 'detrimental impact'. It came out in favour of the organisation being given the freedom to raise money on the commercial markets without it being classed as government borrowing. Mr Richard Caborn, the committee's Labour chairman, said at the time: 'The committee strongly feels that the status quo cannot prevail.'
