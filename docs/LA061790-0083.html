June 17, 1990, Sunday, Ventura County Edition

ENVIRONMENTALISTS LOOK TOWARD CITY RACES;

POLITICS: TWO UNEXPECTED VICTORIES HAVE GIVEN SLOW-GROWTH BACKERS HOPE THAT VOTERS ARE READY TO LIMIT DEVELOPMENT.

Unexpected victories by slow-growth advocates in three Ventura City Council races and in a county supervisor race have convinced environmentalists that the greening of politics has begun in Ventura County.

"I would suggest that anybody running on a strictly environmental platform can get into office if they are credible," said Stan Greene, president of the 700-member group Citizens to Preserve the Ojai. "There is a groundswell now. I think the last two elections bear that out."

Some environmentalists are already looking to elections in November, when they expect numerous candidates to run for City Council seats in attempts to slow Oxnard's rapid growth and to curb what they see as development abuses in Camarillo and Thousand Oaks.

Indeed, with the astounding upset victory by 25-year-old political novice Maria VanderKolk for 2nd District supervisor on June 5, slow-growth advocates say they see unparalleled political possibilities.

"This is our window of opportunity," said Ken Bauer of Westlake Village, a key strategist in VanderKolk's victory over Supervisor Madge L. Schaefer.

VanderKolk's success -- attributed to her opposition to two large housing projects and to Schaefer's refusal to seriously campaign -- also reflects the impact of canvassing by about 75 volunteers recruited primarily from the fledgling environmental group Save Open Space.

That type of shoe-leather support by volunteers also marked the victories last fall of new Ventura City Council members Cathy Bean, Gary Tuttle and Todd Collart, who argued that city limits on growth were too lax.

"VanderKolk's victory underscores the point that you can't get elected in this area without seriously addressing the issue of growth," said Kevin Sweeney, public-affairs director for Ventura-based Patagonia Inc., a clothing company that contributes to environmental groups and local campaigns.

"There has been a victory in the north part of the county and a victory in the south part," he said, "so now you begin to fill in the blanks in between."

How that can be done is uncertain. But it appears that several slow-growth candidates will run in Oxnard this fall, when a majority of the five positions on the City Council will be on the ballot.

Though filing does not begin until mid-July, Paul K. Dolan and Scott Bollinger have already announced their candidacies, citing "unequivocal opposition to future rampant growth" as part of their joint platform.

Accountant Scott Weiss, who co-founded a slow-growth group early this year, said last week that the VanderKolk upset had encouraged him to consider running for the Oxnard council.

Weiss said his small organization is looking for other slow-growth candidates to back and is soliciting help from Patagonia, which provided financial backing and advice to VanderKolk and to the three Ventura candidates.

In Camarillo, representatives from five homeowner groups are meeting to decide how to fight zoning changes that are allowing agricultural land to be developed as shopping centers and industrial parks, homeowner Chris DeSales said last week.

He expects an umbrella organization to be formed soon that will back City Council candidates in the fall campaign, said DeSales, a resident of Woodside Greens at the base of the Conejo Grade.

"It's the same issue with each group," he said. "People are seeing more agricultural land going under. We are all concerned about this kind of development, which seems to be continuing unabated."

In Thousand Oaks, some supporters of VanderKolk say they hope to continue their crusade for stringent growth controls in City Council races there. Thousand Oaks, under Measure A passed in 1980, limits residential construction to 500 units a year -- a sharp cut from the thousands per year proposed before its approval.

But Eloise Zeanah, campaign manager for VanderKolk and twice an unsuccessful council candidate, said she and others are bothered by the City Council's borrowing of housing allocations from future years.

"The residents need to know that our elected officials have skirted the intent of Measure A," she said. "It has not controlled growth, it has only metered it. These problems are beginning to surface now."

Despite the environmentalists' surge of optimism since the recent victories, some slow-growth advocates have begun to discuss how they can push their message more effectively.

Sweeney, Bauer and others said that, if Ventura County environmental groups -- traditionally linked loosely and only marginally active in politics -- are to take advantage of voters' heightened concerns, a countywide environmental organization needs to be established.

"If we are going to prevent Ventura County from becoming another Orange County, we really do need to get better organized," said Bauer, an ARCO executive who moved from Los Angeles in 1976 to escape crime and congestion. "I'd love to get something more permanent set up."

Sweeney said the current race-by-race approach to politics is not the best way to win elections. In addition, it provides little research to support the positions of slow-growth candidates once they are elected and "have to say no to development 400 to 500 times in four years," he said.

"The next stage that has to happen will be a more proactive environmental organization," Sweeney said. Such an office, staffed with a full-time environmental researcher, would mean that "this county begins to develop a backbone around its environment," he said.

A model for that organization can be found 30 miles up the U.S. 101 in Santa Barbara County, where a strong push since a disastrous 1969 oil spill has led to a network of environmental organizations.

The groups include an agency to monitor neighborhood growth issues, a political action committee, a large research institute and a legal center with three attorneys.

Slow-growth advocates, assisted by this network, now hold a majority of the seats on the Santa Barbara City Council and the County Board of Supervisors, said Robert Klausner, head of the political-action committee.

"This issue is a slam dunk," Klausner said. "We haven't had to sell anything. It's tapping on concerns about the quality of life. It's not like someone is laying a philosophy on them."

In Ventura County, individual environmental groups have scored a number of legal and political victories during the last 20 years, but no such network has ever been formed.

Patagonia recently has filled the role of a countywide environmental agency, contributing $35,000 to local organizations last fiscal year and offering political support in three campaigns, beginning with an unsuccessful congressional bid in 1988 by state Sen. Gary K. Hart (D-Santa Barbara).

The outdoor-clothing company, whose influence has grown as its sales have boomed since 1984, contributed $15,000 in slow-growth advertisements to last fall's city races and $9,000 in ads leading up to this month's supervisorial election. Owners Yvon and Malinda Chouinard contributed $2,000 personally to VanderKolk, the maximum allowed by law.

"In a lot of ways, we've been a deep pockets for this county," said Sweeney, who has orchestrated the company's move into political activism since he arrived two years ago.

Patagonia has also hired part time the pre-eminent environmental researcher in the county, Russ Baggerly, a former shoemaker from Meiners Oaks who for years was a spokesman for both Citizens to Preserve the Ojai and the Environmental Coalition.

Baggerly and his wife, Pat, are still directors of the Environmental Coalition, a loose-knit group that takes positions on countywide issues and claims about 400 members.

Several directors of the Environmental Coalition said in interviews that they too would like to see a more coherent countywide approach to environmental problems but see no obvious way to achieve that goal.

"For 10 years now, I've been told that there are two things I have to do -- go out and get volunteers and get people elected to office. I'm absolutely worthless at both," said Cynthia Leake, a coalition director and chairwoman of the 1,800-member Sespe Group of the Los Padres Chapter of the Sierra Club.

To create a political arm "takes money, time and energy. We need a staff and don't have one," she said.

Russ Baggerly said that while local environmentalists want more support for both research and political campaigns, they haven't come to expect it.

"We'll probably take it a race at a time," he said. "We know we have to get environmentally sensitive people elected to office. But it comes down to money." And neither staunch environmentalists nor residents who simply want to slow the pace of growth seem to contribute much to such campaigns, he said.

Neil Moyer, president of the coalition and a new member of the Ventura Planning Commission, said there are other basic problems with the concept of a countywide environmental group.

"Different groups have different objectives, and they don't always overlap," said Moyer, an air-quality expert who works for Texaco.

In addition, the east and west counties, separated by the Conejo Grade, are geographically distinct and have different problems, as do the inland areas near Ojai and Fillmore, he said.

"So things are still very much fragmented on both a geographical basis and on an issues basis," he said. "That's always going to be a problem with environmental groups. Though they all arguably are under this green umbrella, they have different interests and some of them are not very political at all."

Ventura Councilman Gary Tuttle, a former world-class runner elected on a slow-growth platform last fall, said: "I think the environmental movement is here to stay.

"But I'm not going to go out and try to organize the county. I'm staying in Ventura. I've got enough problems right here."

Ventura County's desire to hold back the sprawl of Los Angeles County has been apparent for at least three decades, veterans of the environmental movement say.

Citizens to Preserve the Ojai, formed in the mid-1960s, blocked an effort to widen California 33, which would have allowed a growth boom.

In 1970, Pat Ellison, fresh from cleaning sea birds soiled with oil from the Santa Barbara slick, helped found the Environmental Coalition. And in 1973, she joined two other slow-growth candidates on the Ventura City Council.

With John McWherter, who is still on the council, they approved a series of pioneering reforms that created an agricultural preserve within the city and forced the clustering of new construction closer to the city.

Ventura County also approved a General Plan that requires development to be clustered in or near cities. Now, all 10 cities in the county, except for Oxnard, limit construction in some way.

"We've had a lot of victories over the last 20 years, but we're still struggling hard," Ellison said.
