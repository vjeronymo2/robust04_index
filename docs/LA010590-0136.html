January 5, 1990, Friday, Orange County Edition

Correction Appended

VIETNAM VETERAN KOVIC MAY RUN AGAINST DORNAN

Vietnam veteran and anti-war activist Ron Kovic, whose life is chronicled in the film "Born on the Fourth of July," is considering a race this year for the House seat held by archconservative Rep. Robert K. Dornan (R-Garden Grove), according to Kovic associates and Democratic Party officials.

"We met with Mr. Kovic. We encouraged him to run," said Howard M. Schloss, a spokesman for the Democratic Congressional Campaign Committee, which recruits and backs Democratic candidates in key congressional races.

The DCCC's chief recruiter, Rep. Mike Synar (D-Okla.), traveled to Southern California last fall to talk over a Dornan challenge with Kovic, 43, who is paralyzed from the chest down as the result of a Vietnam combat wound. The meeting with Synar was held because of "mutual" interest in a Kovic candidacy, Schloss said.

Kovic, who lives in Redondo Beach outside of Dornan's Orange County congressional district, said through a spokesman that he would not comment.

Dornan, 56, said he would welcome the fight, should Kovic choose to run.

"Kovic is going to have to defend the Hollywood far left," Dornan said Thursday. "If he thinks he's going to recruit Oliver Stone (who directed Kovic's film biography) and Top-Gun-turned-top-malcontent Tom Cruise (who plays Kovic in the film), and bring the whole Jane Fonda team down here to Orange County, I welcome it. Let's go."

Sid Galanty, a longtime friend of Kovic and a Los Angeles political consultant, said that Kovic was intrigued by the DCCC's interest and that he is considering a race against Dornan.

After Synar's visit, Galanty said, Kovic also was wooed by several House Democratic leaders in another Los Angeles meeting.

House Speaker Thomas S. Foley (D-Wash.), Majority Leader Richard A. Gephardt (D-Mo.) and Democratic National Chairman Ron Brown met with Kovic while they were in Los Angeles for a DCCC fund-raiser in November, a source said.

Foley's and Gephardt's staff had no immediate comment.

"I think he was very taken by the fact that some of the most powerful men in the Democratic leadership in the country would want to meet him and convince him to run for office," Galanty said.

"From that point on, he expressed the fact that he would love to serve his country again," he said. "Having served as a Marine and winning a Medal of Honor, he felt . . . that might be an opportunity he would consider."

Galanty said he suggested Kovic's name to DCCC officials when they called him last fall during a national search for potential candidates. He said Kovic had not considered a campaign bid before he was approached and that he was sensitive about being perceived as an opportunist capitalizing on the celebrity status created by release of the movie.

Ironically, Dornan said he had played a key role in one of the pivotal events in Kovic's life. During the 1972 Republican National Convention in Miami, Kovic and another disabled Vietnam veteran gained access to the convention floor and briefly disrupted the proceedings. Dornan, then a television producer, said he was the man who let Kovic in.

"I personally wheeled him in," Dornan said. "Against my better judgment, but he promised he'd be polite. He broke his word within the hour."

Dornan also said that Kovic appeared three times on "The Robert K. Dornan Show," a KTLA television program that Dornan hosted from 1970 to 1973.

"We kind of get along," Dornan said, "because he knew I respected him for not dodging the draft and for serving. . . . Ron is a fighter, he's a Marine. He wouldn't want me to baby him. He'd want a two-fisted race."

Dornan's 38th Congressional District, which includes Santa Ana, Garden Grove, Buena Park and sections of Anaheim, is the only House seat in Orange County with a Democratic edge in registration. Democrats lead Republicans in the district by about 50% to 41%.

The district also includes the largest concentration of Vietnamese in the United States, according to the National Journal's 1990 political almanac. The almanac said that the Vietnamese community has supported Dornan's hawkish politics and contributed to a 69% turnout in the district for Ronald Reagan in 1984.

If Kovic chooses to enter the race, one of his more formidable tasks will be raising money. Dornan's 1980 congressional campaign was the most expensive in the country. In 1988, he spent $1.75 million when he won reelection over Democratic political novice Jerry Yudelson by a margin of 60% to 36%. Yudelson managed to raise only $250,000.

"Let's face it," said Schloss of the DCCC. "Running against an incumbent, especially one who has been there for a while, it's not only very expensive, but the odds are certainly against winning. Not that it can't be done. But it's a rough road."

The political dynamics of a Kovic-Dornan race would be unusual.

Schloss said the DCCC promised to help finance a Kovic candidacy. And Kovic would be expected to tap the significant pool of Democratic money in Hollywood, which is experiencing a new activism by celebrities in liberal causes and candidates.

On the other hand, Dornan played a major role in President Bush's 1988 campaign and was rumored to have been considered for a high-level presidential appointment. If Dornan's seat is threatened, political experts said, they expect Bush to campaign on Dornan's behalf.

"I still have the biggest star of Hollywood of all time in politics -- Ronald Reagan," Dornan said. "So, what I say to the Hollywood left is: 'Try again. Come and get me.' "

Christopher Townsend, an officer in the Orange County Democratic Party, said a Kovic-Dornan race would pit a candidate "who has actually fought for the flag against one who just wrapped himself in it."

Townsend, a vice president in the Orange County development company of Democratic activist David Stein, said he was aware that Kovic was being encouraged to run for the seat. He added that Kovic "will have tremendous fund-raising potential from a national base."

"Born on the Fourth of July" was released just before Christmas and has been nominated for several Golden Globe awards, including best picture, best actor for Cruise, and best director for Stone, whose earlier Vietnam-era movie, "Platoon," won four Oscars.

As depicted in the movie, Kovic was raised in a strict Catholic family on Long Island and grew up to be a gung-ho Marine who volunteered to serve in the Vietnam War and was paralyzed during a 1968 combat mission.

The movie follows Kovic's personal struggle when he returned from the war as a decorated and disabled veteran who received cruel treatment in a government hospital and was alienated from his former friends and family.

Kovic eventually became active in the anti-war movement. The closing scenes of the movie depict Kovic and Bob Muller, founder of Vietnam Veterans of America, being ejected from the GOP convention after shouting anti-war slogans during President Nixon's acceptance speech.

In the dramatic scene, Cruise is beaten and bloodied by police outside the convention before he is rescued by fellow protesters. In the final scene of the movie, Cruise is wheeled onto the podium of the 1976 Democratic National Convention in a re-creation of Kovic's speech, which was enthusiastically received.

Dornan, who has been in the House for 11 years, is a former Air Force fighter pilot as well as television talk show host. He has acquired a reputation as a flamboyant, theatrical advocate of a strong national defense and a staunch foe of abortion and homosexual rights.

Even so, Dornan has been known to forge unusual coalitions around key issues. For example, last summer, Dornan joined liberal Rep. Barney Frank (D-Mass.), an avowed homosexual, in sponsoring legislation to create a national police corps. The program would attempt to recruit new police officers for the nation's cities by offering to reimburse them for college tuition.

Dornan was first elected in the district in 1984 when he defeated incumbent Democrat Jerry Patterson by a margin of 53% to 45%.

Before that election, Dornan had already served three terms in Congress representing the old 27th Congressional District, centered around Santa Monica in Los Angeles County. When the district was redrawn by Democrats during the 1981 reapportionment, Dornan lost many of his Republican constituents and decided to run instead for the U.S. Senate in 1982. He came in fourth in the Republican primary.
