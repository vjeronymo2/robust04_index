March 19, 1989, Sunday, Home Edition

AT BAHOOKA: TIKI DREAMS AND DEMON RUM

Of course, Polynesian cuisine, as we know it, has nothing to do with laulau, poi or lomi lomi salmon. Think instead of plastic parrots glowing in the dim light of a hundred hurricane lanterns, or the reeling sensation caused by too many Flaming Puku-Puku Bowls bought on a forged ID. Think rumaki and flagstone waterfalls, fried shrimp and bamboo. If you grew up on Virgin Zombies and candy-sweet spareribs, you know what I mean.

Thirty years ago, every neighborhood had at least one tiki-bar, built, I think, to slake the tropical thirsts of men who had served in the far-Pacific theaters of W.W. II. James Michener and Thor Heyerdahl cleaned up on '50s tikimania; so did Trader Vic and Don the Beachcomber. The most elaborate bars featured hula shows or giant volcanoes that erupted every hour or so; the food, when it existed, tended toward the coco shrimp and the pu pu platter. Lengthy drink menus described, in florid pre-AA prose, exactly how a Suffering Bastard or a Head Shrinker would anesthetize your date, and the drinks in question were invariably served in a coconut or something, with two straws, a parasol and a flaming crouton.

But like drive-ins and Art Deco office buildings, most local tiki-bars were demolished or retooled in the '70s and '80s, followed too late by hip nostalgia. (If everybody who collected Martin Denny records or Hawaiian shirts had gone out for a Missionaries' Downfall every so often, the places would've survived.) Countless Kelbos and Don the Beachcombers closed, the Torches was razed for condos, the Islander became the briefly tony 385 North, and the Luau on Rodeo Drive, where three generations of Beverly Hills High School students purchased their first illicit drink, was replaced by the world's most expensive mini-mall. Even Trader Vic's had its funky soul remodeled right out of it a couple of years ago.

Upscale tiki-revival restaurants might be right around the corner -- I've heard of one opening soon in Beverly Hills -- maybe serving kiawe-grilled ono or lychee-macadamia creme brulee. But the most interesting tiki-bars were never all that wholesome, and it's that scintilla of tawdriness, of otherness from home, that makes the experience a bit of an adventure. That's why I take my cravings for demon rum to Bahooka, though the drive from Rosemead seems halfway to Samoa some nights.

Bahooka is the kind of place you'd expect to find near a scruffy tropical seaport, all rusted nautical gear, stolen street signs, and scarred dark wood, lit like a Navy-base bar and with more bobbing tropical fish than you'd find in a Jacques Cousteau special. Lifeboats hang out back -- after the bar closes on a weekend night, you'll always find a giggling kid or two waving from inside of one -- and a mysterious board engraved "Joyce Kilmer" is nailed to the rear wall: "I think that I shall never see/A tiki lovely as a tree," I guess. A sign inside the front door reads "2 IDs Required -- No Swapmeet IDs." There are fish in the foyer, fish tanks surrounding three sides of each booth, and fish swimming inside the glass-topped bar, but not much fish on the menu, unless you count some cod that seems to have swum all the way from Iceland through a sea of old oil.

Fish puffs go with a Monsoon or a Jet Pilot or a Flaming Honey Bowl better than you could possibly imagine, though the leaden deep-fried balls of food aren't anything you'd want to look at by the light of day, and onion rings aren't half bad. (Shy away from the "wingettes," by the way) There is no rumaki. Sorry.

When the steel-guitar lowings on the P.A. start to sound good, it's time for a Shark's Tooth or a Cobra's Strike. Halfway into one of those, a sticky order of Exotic Ribs seems just the thing, because the ribs are moist and kind of tasty and soak up a lot of alcohol, and come with fries, sweet baked yams or cobbettes. The cobbettes, definitely the cobbettes. You can also get teriyaki chicken breast, which is sort of dry, ham with sweet 'n' sour sauce, roast beef or fried golf balls of shrimp, but you won't. What will happen is that your date will suck up the last of his or her Jolly Roger Bowl and carve your initials in the booth. Don't worry, it's happened before.

Bahooka, 4501 N. Rosemead Blvd., Rosemead, (818) 285-1241. Lunch and dinner daily. American Express, MasterCard, Visa accepted. Full bar. Reservations not necessary.

Recommended dishes: onion rings, $3.25; Bahooka's Special Exotic Ribs, $9.95.
