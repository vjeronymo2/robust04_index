940214 FT 14 FEB 94 / Ministers seek to shrug off latest scandal The government yesterday called for an end to disclosures about the private lives of MPs, as ministers attempted to shrug off the fourth sex-related resignation in two years. Senior Conservatives praised Mr Hartley Booth, MP for Finchley, for quitting quickly as a minister's political assistant after newspaper disclosures of his friendship with a female Commons researcher. Mr Booth's resignation minimised the embarrassment caused to the government by detailed allegations about the relationship in the Sunday Mirror. Mr Booth denies sexual impropriety. However, there was widespread concern that no end appeared to be in sight to the catalogue of disclosures which has undermined the government's Back to Basics campaign on traditional moral values since the start of the year. Mr Peter Lilley, social security secretary, said Mr Booth's resignation had no connection with Back to Basics, launched by Mr John Major at the Conservative party conference in October. But, in a sign of the government's growing irritation about the stream of disclosures, he accused the media of pursuing a 'ludicrous' campaign for the government to 'purge itself' of MPs thought guilty of sexual impropriety. 'All the newspapers have got several journalists assigned full-time going through files, going through every rumour they have ever heard, looking for some scandal. I am amazed they have found so few,' he said. 'If they want to find a cabinet composed entirely of people who have never committed any sin it is going to be a bizarre place. It would have to be composed entirely of people like the editor of the Sunday Mirror, who I assume is perfect.' Mr Booth's resignation follows the resignations of Mr David Mellor, Mr Tim Yeo and Lord Caithness from the government after media disclosures about their private lives. The Conservative party has also been rocked by the death of Mr Stephen Milligan, MP for Eastleigh, in bizarre circumstances; and a series of revelations about the extra-marital relationships of other Conservative MPs. The government has virtually given up hope of winning the Eastleigh by-election, expected to take place on May 5, in which the opposition parties are expected to campaign strongly on moral issues. The Liberal Democrat party, which came second in 1992, is favourite to win. However, Labour plans to allocate its top by-election strategists to the campaign, and frontbench spokesmen are soon to visit the constituency. 'This is a genuine three-way split, and we intend to fight hard to win it,' said a senior Labour official. 'We think we have a real chance.'
