January 14, 1990, Sunday, Orange County Edition

COSTA MESA CAN SHOW IT REALLY HAS A HEART

The Costa Mesa City Council has an opportunity Monday to salvage the city's reputation as a community with a heart. It should rescind its decision to evict the Share Our Selves charity from a community center until a new location can be found.

SOS plans to ask for a 2- to 3-month extension on its lease to give it time to negotiate purchase of a new site, an action made possible only last week by the generous outpouring of donations that have come in the wake of publicity over SOS's plight. The council majority, responding to neighborhood complaints about SOS, ordered the charity to move its food, clothing and financial assistance operations. Although the city offered to help SOS find another location, it balked when faced with the cost of helping it move to an industrial building the city had located. SOS's fate hung in the balance.

But pressure has continued to build on the council, with Orange County religious leaders jumping into the fray. Summing up the matter, Father Michael P. Driscoll, the vicar of charities for the Diocese of Orange, said, "It strikes me that if Jesus lived today, the city of Costa Mesa would find him undesirable." Meanwhile, SOS supporters vowed to fast this weekend and Monday to try to get the council to reconsider its order to start eviction proceedings Thursday.

On Friday, Mayor Peter F. Buffa made a new proposal to move SOS to Community Development Council offices in another part of town, well away from residential areas. Whether or not Buffa has the two votes needed to win approval of his plan is unclear at this time; it calls for the city to subsidize the lease by $177,658 over five years, with SOS paying slightly less.

While that would be money well spent, the city should instead give SOS an extension on its current lease to give the charity the time needed to purchase its own building. This would not only relieve the city of financial responsibility but give SOS the autonomy it needs. The latter is important because there are City Council members who want to exclude illegal aliens from receiving SOS services, a restriction that cannot be tolerated.

Two council members who voted against SOS in the past have accused the charity of dragging its feet and playing politics in order to get its way. Frankly, it's good to see SOS employing political pressure to save itself. We only hope it is not too late.
