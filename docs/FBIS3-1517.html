Article Type:BFN [Editorial Report]

THE CITIZEN
ANC To Escalate Mass Action in kwaZulu/Natal--Johannesburg THE CITIZEN in English on 28 March in its page-6 editorial notes that according to pollsters "there is no doubt that the ANC [African National Congress] will win hands down -- and might even get a two-thirds majority. Sadly, the ANC has become arrogant and intolerant." ANC Secretary General Cyril Ramaphosa has announced that the ANC would "escalate its rolling mass action" in kwaZulu/Natal "until Chief Buthelezi guarantees free political activity in the region. The danger that the low-key civil war there could flare into a full-scale conflict does not worry the ANC -- it is not going to give up its plan to topple Chief Buthelezi and it will proceed whatever the consequences."
THE STAR
State May Have To Consider State of Emergency -- Referring to the violence that erupted in the wake of the Zulu rally in Johannesburg on 28 March, Johannesburg THE STAR in English on 29 March in a page-14 editorial says: "Any parties, or leaders playing a political role -- as Zulu King Goodwill Zwelithini is playing in this period -- have to ask themselves two questions among others. First, will our planned action have the effect of being provocative, and placing lives at risk? It is no defence to say this was not the intention. Second, have we ensured that none of our supporters is armed? Again, it is not good enough to say one didn't know. If political leaders -- from whatever party -- are not prepared to honour these basic responsibilities, then they invite inevitable illiberal censure: the temporary prohibition of some or all high-risk demonstrations." "A reversion to state of emergency politics would inevitably open the door to political abuse, and it would tragically mar the birth of the new South Africa. But if politicians are so cynical as to actively explore the Bosnia option, then it is a step that might have to be considered." Only SADF Can Impose Free Political Activity in Natal -- A second editorial on the same page believes that if all else fails "one thing would stand between South Africa and a descent into utter chaos -- the presence of an effective and apolitical Defence Force." The South African Defense Force (SADF) has been called on recently to restore order in Bophuthatswana, Ciskei, and Lebowa. "The SADF could yet face its stiffest challenge in kwaZulu. As matters there deteriorate, pressure is building up for a deployment of troops. Both ANC secretary-general Cyril Ramaphosa and the organisation's kwaZulu/Natal premiership candidate, Jacob Zuma, have called on the TEC [Transitional Executive Council] to send troops into the region. It is no longer a far-fetched prospect, and nor is it one to relish. But if free political activity has to be imposed, only the SADF can do it."
BUSINESS DAY
Attempts To Overthrow Kwazulu To Have `Horrendous Costs' -- Johannesburg BUSINESS DAY in English on 29 March in a page-16 editorial also refers to the rally "called by traditional Zulu leaders in support of their king." "The Johannesburg chaos, it should be noted, was not exceptional compared with the violence that has afflicted black residential communities on the East and West Rand, and in many parts of Natal, with the loss of thousands of lives. Strife of this magnitude rarely comes to the city centres, so for those not used to life in the townships it was particularly alarming." The events in Johannesburg "are evidence that ANC attempts to overthrow kwaZulu Chief Minister Mangosuthu Buthelezi, and Buthelezi's apparent resistance to a political settlement which may cost him his office, will have horrendous costs, possibly more than the economy can afford. The election must go ahead. But the chances of it being universally free and fair are declining. And it is almost too late to halt the spiral."
SOWETAN
Editorial Blames IFP for Johannesburg Rally Violence -- Johannesburg SOWETAN in English on 29 March in a page-8 editorial places the blame for the deaths linked to the protest march in Johannesburg "squarely on the leadership of the Inkatha Freedom Party [IFP]. Nobody is fooled by people changing names at the drop of a hat -- one minute they are IFP leaders and the next they are `Zulus' protesting rather strenuously that they are not speaking for the IFP." SOWETAN also believes the violence "could only have been premeditated. First, there has been war talk from the leadership of the IFP; then there was the turning of a blind eye on the weapons that were displayed during the march; and finally there was the glaring absence of marshals to control the huge crowds."
