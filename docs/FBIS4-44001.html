BFN [Text] Berlin, June 23 (AFP) -- One of the main leaders of the South Korean student opposition is to meet North Korean leader Kim Il-song in Pyongyang and put proposals to him for detente and reunification, he said here Thursday [23 June]. Choi Jong-nam, 26, who is also one of the main instigators of dialogue between North and South Korean students, told AFP he would meet with Kim within one week. Civilian activists like Choi, who lives in exile here, have often served as intermediaries between the two Koreas in the past. He said in an interview that the meeting, arranged two months ago, would go ahead despite recent progress in dialogue between the Koreas and prospects of a summit between the ideologically opposed states. "We felt it was vital to pursue a civilian initiative," Choi said, "If discussions only take place at the level of governmentals, they will again be used by both sides to keep themselves in power." He said he would propose that a federal structure be created so that both systems, (communist in the north and free-market in the south), could coexist after eventual reunification. Choi said he believed "the negative experiences of Germany, Vietnam and Yemen show that reunification can only work in a federation." He also called for an international conference grouping China, Japan, Russia, the United States and the Koreas to be convened to resolve the nuclear crisis in the peninsula. An agriculture student, Choi was expelled from Seoul University for political activism. The South Korean student organization Hanchongnyon [South Korean Feceration of General Student Councils] sent him to Berlin last July to coordinate dialogue with North Korean students within the Pan-Korean Student Alliance for Peace and Reunification. Students from both sides of the Korean divide have maintained contact in Berlin for years. During the communist era, North Korea was particularly well represented in former East Berlin within the framework of agreements with like-minded nations. Choi said he was to travel to Pyongyang via Moscow and spend two weeks in North Korea. Three South Korean students have visited the North in the past, despite a ban on such travel by Seoul and the threat of stiff prison sentences.
