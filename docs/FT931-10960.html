930205 FT 05 FEB 93 / Sales of new cars rise for fourth month in succession UK NEW car sales rose by 7 per cent in January, the fourth consecutive month in which registrations have been higher than in the corresponding period a year ago. Mr Ian McAllister, chairman of Ford of Britain, said the figures indicated that 'a sustained recovery' in the market could well be under way. The growth in sales last month was because of a higher level of purchases by private customers rather than by company fleets, he said. The increase in retail sales was 'particularly encouraging' and indicated that abolition of special car tax last year and the reduction in interest rates was having a positive effect on consumer confidence, said Mr McAllister. Professor Garel Rhys, Professor of Motor Industry Economics at Cardiff Business School, cautioned against optimism based on the January sales figures. 'Last month's car figures were in fact dreadful,' he said. 'The previous January was very poor, being 11.7 per cent down on the 1990 figure. But since then the 10 per cent special car tax has been abolished. That should should have lifted last month's market by 12 per cent on its own. 'The extent to which the market is up shows the recession is still there and that people remain very cautious about buying,' he added. New car registrations in January totalled 164,939, a 7.3 per cent rise on a year before, according to figures from the Society of Motor Manufacturers and Traders. UK new car sales for 1992 at 1.59m were virtually unchanged from the very depressed level of the previous year and were 31 per cent below the peak sales of 2.3m in 1989. There were tentative signs that demand had begun to recover in the final months of last year, however, and new car registrations have been higher than a year earlier in five of the last six months. Registrations of new commercial vehicles in January rose 4.4 per cent to 17,418, which was also the fourth consecutive monthly year-on-year increase. Daf, the beleaguered Anglo-Dutch truck maker that collapsed into receivership this week, was the clear UK truck market leader in January, with a share of 25.4 per cent. In the UK new car market both Ford and Vauxhall, the market leaders, lost ground heavily in January while Citroen, Renault and Peugeot all made significant gains, as did Rover, Toyota, Nissan, Volkswagen and Audi.
