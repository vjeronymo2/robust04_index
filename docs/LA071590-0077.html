July 15, 1990, Sunday, South Bay Edition

GALANTER OFFERS PLAN TO SAVE SAND DUNES

Los Angeles City Councilwoman Ruth Galanter proposed Friday that 200 acres of ecologically sensitive sand dunes at the west end of Los Angeles International Airport's runways be turned into a nature preserve.

Flanked by a handful of supporters at the site and fighting to be heard as jumbo jets screamed overhead, Galanter said the airport should abandon its long-stalled plan to convert about 200 acres of the 302-acre dunes into a golf course.

Rather, Galanter said, the airport should use only 100 acres of the dunes, best known as a habitat for the endangered El Segundo Blue butterfly, for the golf course. The airport could complete the course by using another 50 acres it owns next to the dunes on the north side of LAX, she said.

"We can have the butterfly and we can have the golfers," Galanter said.

Galanter's proposal has not been formally presented to other council members or to the city Airport Commission. Clifton Moore, general manager of the Airport Department, said he and Galanter have discussed her proposal in general terms but not in detail.

"We have indicated we will keep an open mind and talk about it," Moore said.

Galanter's plan to use 50 acres on the airport's north side for a golf course clashes with the airport's plan to use the land as part of a large office and research park development. The parcel is part of 350 acres once dotted with homes that were acquired by the airport because of severe jet noise.

Donald Miller, the airport's deputy executive director, said work has already began on a four-lane highway for the office park project, which is to extend from Sepulveda Eastway to Pershing Drive. The airport recently received final approval from the city for the development to proceed, he said.

Galanter contends that most of the land for the office project will be paved for parking lots. By redesigning the project and building underground parking structures, she said, 50 acres could be found for the golf course.

The dunes that Galanter wants to protect are the last large section of a dune system that once stretched from Point Conception, above Santa Barbara, south to Mexico.

The airport began work to establish a habitat for the El Segundo Blue butterfly on the dunes in the late 1970s, shortly after the insect was classified as endangered by the federal government. About five years ago, the airport proposed using 200 acres of the dunes for a golf course and turning the rest into a nature preserve.

That plan was stalled in 1987 when the California Coastal Commission refused to give the airport permission to move ahead because it was unclear what effect the golf course would have on the butterfly.

Airport officials hired scientist Rudy Mattoni to do a biological study of the dunes to determine the golf course's effect. Mattoni's study identified at least nine insects, including the El Segundo Blue butterfly, and a flower that apparently live only on the dunes and could be classified as endangered.

Mattoni, who supports Galanter's plan, said the butterfly is now flourishing on the dunes. If 200 acres of the dunes are preserved, he said, the entire dune ecosystem would have a better chance of surviving.

"We're trying to preserve the whole ecosystem, this whole community of interactions," Mattoni said. "I'm not worried about the butterfly; I am worried about everything else."

Mattoni's study found that native plant and animal life on the dunes has been seriously affected over the years by development and soil contamination, which in turn have allowed non-native species to invade the area.

Of the 20 original mammals believed to have lived on the dunes, only three remain, Mattoni said. Of the 91 original plant species, only 58 remain and 19 are in danger of being eliminated.
