January 18, 1990, Thursday, Home Edition

FOOD AND COMMON SENSE: 'ALTERNATIVE' AGRICULTURE IS GAINING MOMENTUM IN THE MARKETPLACE

When word got out last year that use of daminozide (trade name Alar) might threaten the safety of apples, consumers were shaken. If apples -- our unofficial national fruit, and famous for healthy eating -- couldn't be trusted, what was going on down on the farm? In our desire to produce larger and more abundant crops, were we putting ourselves and our environment at risk?

Long before Alar, a movement was already at work. For years, momentum has been gathering for "alternative agriculture." This concept recognizes the need to find new ways to grow food economically and safely, and without further damage to the environment. Its strength rests on the assumption that although conflicts do exist, farmers, environmentalists and consumers might all find some common ground.

The movement began by questioning the underpinnings of traditional agriculture. Take nitrates, widely used as fertilizer. For some time it had been known that using excess amounts to maximize crop production was causing a buildup in ground water, the reservoir that lies within and beneath the soil.

It also has been shown that levels of pesticides in ground water are rising. Most pesticides currently in use are herbicides, which are sprayed onto the ground. Previously it had been thought that they were simply absorbed on the surface of soil particles, where they would eventually be broken down. But they too seeped through to the ground water. Not only were levels higher than expected, but the contamination had spread far from where the pesticides were applied.

What makes this worrisome is that, except for oceans, ground water is the final sink. Once chemicals are dissolved or suspended in it, they are there to stay -- unless they are taken out when water is used. No one can measure the risk to consumers, but evidence shows that we'd be better off with fewer pesticides and nitrates.

In rural America, ground water is drinking water. Thus, farmers who live in affected areas responded quite naturally with concern about the safety of the water supply used by their families. The sharp drop in the price of crops, and increased cost of chemicals, provided still more reason for farmers to look for alternative methods, if they prove cheaper.

Farmers are in a key position to affect agricultural practices. But they are by no means the only group concerned about potential dangers. The limitations of chemical pesticides are now understood. Insects quickly develop resistance, rendering a particular pesticide ineffective. And government regulations make it expensive to develop and register new ones. If a pesticide might be useless after a period of, say, five years, and it costs a lot to come up with a new one, the outlook for manufacturers is bleak. There is incentive to diversify. Those factors combine with support from environmental groups to press for finding safer methods of raising our food.

These alternatives will emerge from a blend of modern advances and traditional techniques. One objective is to identify natural methods of controlling agricultural pests, making use of pesticides less necessary. Part of this solution lies in breeding disease-resistant strains of crops.

In most of nature, plants grow without pesticides and fertilizers, so one approach will be to search older varieties of commercial plants and their wild relatives for desirable qualities that can be bred into commercial species while maintaining satisfactory yields. This plan relies on traditional breeding methods. But the objectives are broadened to include disease resistance and reduced need for fertilizer, as well as yield.

A second goal is to provide a favorable habitat for natural organisms that can control pests. A third is to capitalize on natural sources of fertilizers. Crop residues ranging from tomato plants to corn husks can be plowed back into the soil to enrich it. Livestock manure can be treated and used safely. And leguminous plants harboring nitrogen-fixing bacteria in their roots can be used to furnish the nitrogen that crops need.

None of this is a job for amateurs. Researchers at the nation's land-grant universities, sites of much agricultural research, are hard at work. Also, more and more, grass roots groups and other private organizations are exploring economically viable alternatives.

On the farm itself, important changes are expected. In the past, government programs to assist farmers have encouraged greater specialization in crop production. That, in turn, led to more use of pesticides. It is thought that these programs will now be rewritten with an eye toward developing alternative farming methods. It's true that, to date, concern about problems with traditional agriculture has outstripped progress in finding suitable alternatives.

But now, however quietly, the revolution has begun. And there is every reason to believe it will flourish.

Taking More Vitamins Is Not the Answer for Warming Up Cold Feet

Question: My hands and feet suffer in cold weather. I just can't keep them warm enough, even with good boots and mittens. A friend said she'd read that B complex vitamins could help. I know you tend to oppose large doses of vitamins, but do you think that in my case they could provide some relief?

Answer: Sorry, but there is no evidence to support this. We can only guess at the basis for such a claim. Cold temperatures are a form of environmental stress, and the widely available "stress formula" vitamins are usually mixtures of large doses of Vitamin C and members of the B complex vitamins. There is likewise no data to support the use of these supplements in coping with stress, either mental or physical, but perhaps they are somehow entwined with the rumor that B vitamins will protect you from the cold.

A second explanation for the claim relates to the immediate physiologic effect of one form of niacin, called nicotinic acid. When taken in large amounts, nicotinic acid causes dilation of blood vessels and temporary flushing. This brief effect wouldn't do you much good on a trek on a cold winter's day. Besides, while niacin is now used in big doses in the treatment of elevated serum cholesterol, it has the potential for harmful side effects, and should be used only under a doctor's supervision.

Q: My 14-year-old son is considerably shorter than all his friends. I try not to show it, but I'm concerned. I think he eats well, but I wonder if there are dietary steps we could take to help him grow.

A: No, and any claims to the contrary are irresponsible. It is true that some children go through their growth spurts much later than others. If he's among that group, time will solve the problem. Remember, too, that genetics is a powerful predictor of height, so it's important to take into account both your own and your husband's stature. Short parents are less likely to have tall offspring. But if you are worried that your son's growth is not proceeding at a normal rate, and have not already done so, schedule an appointment with his physician to rule out any health problem that could delay growth.

At no time is it more important to ensure that the diet provides enough calories and sufficient amounts of essential nutrients than when children are still growing. That can be achieved by consuming ample amounts of dairy products, meat, fish, poultry and eggs, fresh fruits and vegetables, and whole grain or lightly processed breads and cereals. In addition, a single multivitamin and mineral containing 100 percent of the RDA for each nutrient can provide some nutritional insurance. Supplements sold as growth promoters are a waste of your hard-earned cash.

Q: Are prenatal fluoride supplements helpful to the unborn infant's teeth?

A: No. Although there is no evidence that they are harmful, there is no data to show that they are of benefit, either. To begin with, any benefit that did occur would affect only the deciduous or "baby teeth." It is generally believed that it is a good idea to start fluoride supplements for the infant shortly after birth. But there is no evidence that caries prevention is extended by the use of prenatal fluoride supplements.

Q: Does a bulging lid on a can mean that the food inside is spoiled?

A: Not necessarily. But all the same, we urge you not to use the contents.

There are three reasons why a lid might bulge. It could occur in response to a dent in the can. Or, it could be caused by a chemical reaction between the food and the container, resulting in the release of hydrogen gas. Pressure from the expansion of the gas makes the lid swell. Then there's the other possibility: The bulging lid may be a sign that botulism spores are multiplying and producing a potentially deadly toxin.

Since it's impossible to diagnose the cause of the bulge with certainty in your kitchen, the only reasonable alternatives are either to return the container and its contents to the store where you bought it, or to discard it in such a way that pets and other animals cannot consume the contents. We should point out that the risk of botulism food poisoning from commercially canned foods is minute.
