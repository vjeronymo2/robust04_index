May 16, 1989, Tuesday, Home Edition

THE CANNES FILE;

THE FESTIVAL BUZZ ON THE CROISETTE: 'JESUS,' OUI; 'ANGELS,' NON

Three of the four American films in competition for the Gold Palm were shown during the first four days of this year's Cannes Film Festival, and as far as the critics were concerned, it was rookie director Steve Soderbergh who brought the best film.

Soderbergh's "sex, lies and videotape" was the clear favorite of an international panel of critics polled in the trade paper Screen International.

The panel rated "sex, lies and videotape" well above both director James Jarmusch's "Mystery Train," the Australian entry "A Cry in the Dark," and French director Bertrand Blier's "Too Good for You."

But on Monday, audiences saw what may prove to be the most and least popular films in the festival. Hugh Hudson's "Lost Angels," a Los Angeles-based story about a middle-class juvenile gang member, was hooted at two press screenings while Canadian director Denys Arcand's "Jesus of Montreal" drew the longest ovation yet heard on the Croisette from the demonstrative media crowds. ("Lost Angels" has already opened in many American cities, including Los Angeles, and hasn't done much better with critics there.)

The Gold Palm winner, set to be announced May 23, will be determined by a 10-person jury headed by West German film maker Wim Wenders. The jury's lone American member is actress Sally Field.

Some notes on a few of the early films at Cannes:

"Jesus of Montreal," Canada. Denys Arcand was discovered at Cannes three years ago when his "The Decline of the American Empire" became the hit of the Directors Fortnight.

"Decline," a French-language film about eight upscale Canadians concerned with their sex lives, made some strong statements about the deterioration of values in the West and Arcand's new film drives the point home with a stake.

"Jesus of Montreal" is a daring updating of the death and resurrection of Christ, told through the story of a Montreal actor who puts on a revisionist avant-garde play about Christ and has his own life parallel both the art and the legend.

Segments of the film cast doubts on the biblical version of Christ and comparisons will inevitably be made to Martin Scorsese's "The Last Temptation of Christ." But Arcand, who says he hasn't even seen the Scorsese film, seems less interested in challenging the faith than pointing out that man's inhumanities are alive and ill in Montreal. The film includes unflattering references to lawyers and agents, a bias Arcand admits was promoted by the phony attention he got after "Decline." He said his sudden popularity changed everyone around him, even his own lawyer.

"While negotiating for this film," Arcand said, "my lawyer said to me, 'What about T-shirts? People are going to want to buy them.' I said, 'Listen, I'm doing a film about Jesus Christ. Can you forget about T-shirts?' "

"Mystery Train," United States. James Jarmusch was also discovered at Cannes through the unsanctioned Directors Fortnight. His "Stranger Than Paradise," which was later named best picture of 1984 by the National Society of Film Critics in America, was an off-beat black-and-white comedy about two immigrant cousins wandering through the United States.

Two years ago, he continued the theme with "Down by Law," which was in competition for the Gold Palm, and this year he returns with what he calls -- retrospectively -- the third leg of a trilogy on immigrants' impressions of America.

"Mystery Train" is three separate but simultaneously occurring stories linked by a flea-bag hotel in urban Memphis, Tenn.

The first, and I can sense it's the best segment, is about a Japanese couple making a pilgrimage to the birthplace of rock 'n' roll. The boy reveres Carl Perkins and Roy Orbison; she is strictly an Elvis fan. The second story is about an Italian woman stranded briefly in Memphis after her husband dies on an airplane. The third is about an English Elvis Presley look-alike who, in a drunken rage, shoots a liquor store clerk and goes on the lam with his shocked American buddies.

"Mystery Train" delivered bigger laughs than either of Jarmusch's earlier films, but it roused little enthusiasm among the American critics here.

The $2.7-million film was financed by JVC, the giant Japanese electronics firm, and will be released in the United States this summer.

"sex, lies and videotape," United States. This first film by 26-year-old American Steven Soderbergh has been the talk of the festival so far.

The crisply written film focuses on a Baton Rouge, La., couple whose troubled marriage is finally undone when the husband's old college friend returns to town with a peculiar hobby of interviewing women about their sex lives on videotape.

The film is in equal parts about deceit, sexual repression and aggression, and the inundation of video.

Critics are raving about all the actors in the film, but James Spader, getting a break from all those preppy roles, may be in line for a best actor award for his moving performance as the video voyeur trying to make peace with himself.

"sex, lies and videotape," made on a budget of $1.2 million, will be released this summer in the United States by Miramax Films.

Bertrand Blier's "Too Good for You," which stars Gerard Depardieu as a Paris businessman whose affections are divided between his homely mistress and his beautiful wife, was better appreciated by French critics than the Americans, but is nonetheless one of the early festival hits.
