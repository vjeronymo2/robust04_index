July 13, 1989, Thursday, Home Edition

HINTS FROM HELOISE: TAKING PRECAUTIONS AGAINST FOOD POISONING

DEAR HELOISE: I recently had a severe case of food poisoning. The doctor told me that I had gotten it from some chicken that he thought may not have been cooked thoroughly.

Would you please share some more information on how to avoid food poisoning? I don't ever want to go through that again.

-- A READER, El Paso, Tex.

DEAR READER: Food-borne poisoning can make you feel terrible and can even be life-threatening. There are many types of food-borne poisoning:

One type is botulism and can be found in home-canned or other canned goods. Always check all canned foods to make sure there are no bulges in the can. If any food looks bad, don't taste it. If you think you might have botulism, seek medical attention immediately.

Salmonella is a bacteria that is found in meat, poultry, eggs or fish. Foods that aren't thoroughly cooked and those that are eaten raw are the major culprits.

All foods, utensils and surfaces that have come in contact with raw foods should be washed thoroughly with soap and water. If you use a wooden cutting board to cut up the raw chicken, be sure to wash it thoroughly in soap and water. The knife that you use to cut up the raw chicken shouldn't be used to cut up anything else unless washed first. Don't forget to wash your hands if you have handled the food. When taking raw chicken to the barbecue on a platter, don't use the same platter to put the cooked chicken on unless it has been washed.

Staphylococcus is a bacteria that may be found in foods that are left unrefrigerated for longer than two hours. Some foods prone to this bacteria are cheese, egg products, poultry, meat, pasta, macaroni or potato salads, cream-filled desserts or custards.

Again, wash your hands thoroughly before and after preparation.

-- HELOISE

DEAR HELOISE: I was given a beautiful old glass decanter that belonged to my grandmother. There are some stains in it that I don't know how to remove.

I don't want to use anything that can damage it. Do you know what I should use to clean it?

-- SARAH, Roanoke, Va.

DEAR SARAH: I have been told by some antiques dealers that sometimes stains cannot be removed from old glass. What works best for them is to mix about one-quarter of a cup of dishwasher detergent compound with hot water. When the water cools, fill the decanter to the top. Let it soak for a few hours or overnight. Use a bottle brush to try and remove stubborn stains.

Some stains may remain. Rinse well and let dry. Good luck.

-- HELOISE
