May 1, 1990, Tuesday, Home Edition

BOB HOPE AGREES TO TRANSFER 5,700 ACRES FOR PARK SITES

With all due respect to Bob Hope, he just doesn't get it. Before we proclaim him a saint for having agreed to donate and sell some of his land to the state, we need to examine the downside of his would-be deal ("Hope Agrees to Transfer of Lands for Parks," Part A, April 20).

The new agreement between Hope and the Santa Monica Mountains Conservancy is simply the latest counteroffer in negotiations that would result in a major development inside Santa Monica Mountains National Recreation Area.

This counteroffer is an attempt to 1) heal Hope's tarnished image, 2) bail out the fast-sinking plan of Potomac Investment Associates to develop Hope's Jordan Ranch, 3) restore the good name of the conservancy, which has signed off on the PIA development plan and 4) pressure the National Park Service to give away our public lands.

The bottom line remains the same: the public does not get fair value, and the national park will be degraded. PIA still plans a major golf course and luxury homes on Jordan Ranch. This development would harm the natural resources of not only the ranch, always viewed as a key tract for park service purchase, but adjoining Cheeseboro Canyon, as well.

The Park Service can block this deal because PIA needs a piece of federal land to build a highway to Jordan Ranch. The mandate of the National Park Service is to protect our natural resources -- not to deal them away. Yet, under pressure from PIA, the park service is acquiescing in this plunder. Doing so would set a terrible precedent.

To make matters worse, this deal would constitute a raid on the federal treasury. The law requires that any lands exchanged be equal in value, and Park Service regulations direct that the contributory value of the exchanged land to the Jordan Ranch development be considered in its appraisal. But the value of the lands and money PIA plans to give the federal government do not approach the value of the proposed Jordan Ranch development.

The peculiar postion of the conservancy should not go unnoticed. Last year its representatives told the Wilderness Society that no funds were available to purchase Jordan Ranch. Now, they suddenly have found $20 million to acquire some of Hope's lands that do not even lie within the boundaries of Santa Monica Mountains National Recreation Area. What gives?

The Wilderness Society is working with Congress to stop this assault on our public lands. The more that members of Congress learn about this development plan, the more concerned they become. It is clear that the conservancy, the park service, and Bob Hope just don't understand what is at stake.

What they need to do is so simple: Stop scheming on a way to build those luxury homes and the golf course inside the boundaries of a national recreation area.

GEORGE T. FRAMPTON JR.

President

Wilderness Society

Washington, D.C.
