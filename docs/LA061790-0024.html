June 17, 1990, Sunday, Home Edition

MARKETS: COLLECTORS CASH IN ON OLD FINANCIAL DOCUMENTS

Back in 1947, George Steffes paid $100 for 100 shares of Tucker Corp. common stock. The check barely cleared the bank before Tucker, an upstart auto company with uniquely designed cars, went broke. The 100 shares were worthless.

But now -- 43 years later -- Steffes could get his money back. Thanks to a popular movie about Tucker, as well as a growing interest in historically significant financial documents, the Tucker stock certificate that has been sitting in Steffes' drawer would fetch about $100 at auction.

"Collectors are getting more interested in these kinds of collectibles. They find them colorful and interesting," says Diana Herzog, president of R. M. Smythe & Co., which is holding its annual auction of financial documents this weekend in Memphis. "The hobby is still within the range of people who no longer want to spend a lot of money for paintings, prints or coins."

When the auction is over, Herzog expects about 500 lots of financial documents to have been sold -- for a total of about $1 million. That auction's average of $2,000 per lot isn't going to make Sotheby's quiver. But experts say prices have been rising steadily the past 10 years.

In fact, financial-document hobbyists are delighting over reports that American corporations may do away with paper stock and bond certificates in favor of computer entries. They believe that the change could make their collections skyrocket in price.

Even if financial documents don't become as prized as a Mickey Mantle rookie baseball card, collectors say they get pleasure just owning -- for so small a price -- a piece of history.

"I wouldn't advise anybody to go into this with the idea of making money. It's for your fun, for your pleasure. If the thing goes up in value -- hooray," says Sanford Hock, a stockbroker with Paine Webber & Co. in Beverly Hills. He has more than 250 historical financial documents.

Among his holdings are a large number of Confederate bonds; a lottery ticket signed by George Washington that he estimates is worth about $5,000; a stock certificate for Charles Chaplin Productions, signed by Charlie, with an estimated value of $4,500, and a receipt for work on a church bell signed by Paul Revere. Hock says he paid $6,500 for the Revere piece last year.

The most valuable financial document so far is a stock receipt for the East India Company dated 1657. Because it is believed to be the oldest piece of stock paper in private hands, it sold in 1989 at auction for $42,500.

As in any other hobby, most of the people who dabble in these collectibles know what to look for. Many are employed in the financial industry.

The experts say amateurs should be alert for three types of documents.

First are those bearing important signatures. Many famous industrialists signed early stock certificates for their companies.

Haley Garrison of Williamsburg, Va., an expert on antique stocks and bonds, said the most prized signatures are those of John Jacob Astor and his son William, J. P. Morgan, John D. Rockefeller, Thomas Edison, Andrew Carnegie, Stephen Girard, Marshall Field, Henry Ford, Jay Gould, Meyer Guggenheim, Andrew Mellon, Henry Wells and William Fargo.

The second thing to look for are financial papers of historical significance and, third, papers of exceptional beauty.

Stocks and bonds aren't valuable just because they are old. A 500-ruble Russian bond issued by the czarist government in 1912 was recently found in Los Angeles. But it has no value to collectors because, in the waning days of that regime, such bonds were as common as ditch water.

Most people who find themselves holding financial collectibles are no more collectors than kids who bought baseball cards in the 1950s were investors. What makes financial collectibles exciting is that it is still possible to stumble onto valuable finds in a way that is no longer likely with paintings, coins or even baseball cards.

"A guy came to me recently with a bond of the Erie Railroad Co.," says Garrison. It turned out to be a rough draft signed by financier Jay Gould.

Garrison says it is probably worth between $10,000 and $15,000 -- with 60% of the value attributable to its historical significance and 40% to Gould's signature.

"Valuable stock and bond certificates are very frequently found by individuals in the papers or belongings of relatives," notes Scott Winslow, a dealer in Nashua, N.H. He says an 80-year-old woman recently found a pile of stock certificates for mining companies that dated to the turn of the century. "The companies all went bust, and (the stocks and bonds) had no negotiable value," he says.

"But today they have collectible value because of the quality of the graphics. They had vignettes of mining scenes," says Winslow. He estimates that each certificate is worth about $150.

"This is a fairly common occurence," Winslow adds.

Even more common, however, is an old stock certificate that isn't pretty, wasn't signed by someone famous, doesn't have its history rooted in some important event and isn't worth a nickel.

Novices should also remember that cashing in big on an old stock they might unearth isn't easy. Getting a fix on the true price of an old stock is difficult. One expert estimates that the Tucker certificate is worth $15 and another $250.

Also, finding a buyer may be difficult, since there may be as few as 20,000 collectors right now. "We're a relatively new hobby," says Garrison, who is helping organize the biggest auction ever for financial documents. It is to be held in Dusseldorf, West Germany, in September.

Why Dusseldorf? "Because Europeans are avid collectors of American financial history," says Garrison.

Federal Spenders Don't Have Corner on Deficits

State and local governments are learning from the federal government how to spend more than they make.

According to the U.S. Commerce Department, state and local governments had a record $45.7 billion deficit in the fourth quarter of 1989. That combined deficit was the worst one ever recorded. The deficit dropped to $44.6 billion in the first quarter of 1990 -- the last period for which there are figures.

"The numbers we have at the moment indicate that the deficit has gotten considerably worse," says a Commerce Department source. "This has been going on for a few years."

State and local governments started running deficits in the fourth quarter of 1986 after more than three years of surpluses.

There have been deficits in the recent past, according to Commerce Department stats -- specifically from 1970 to 1972; from late 1973 to 1976 and briefly in 1980 and 1982. But the magnitude of the current aggregate deficit among the states and locals dwarfs any from the past.

In July, the department will put out deficit statistics for the first quarter of 1990. It will also revise and update its statistics from the past three years.

Most of the problem is occuring in the Northeast, but Louisiana and West Virginia are perennially in bad shape and parts of Florida and California are also piling up debt, the department said.

The bottom line is that local and state governments -- like their federal counterpart -- will soon have to come up with ways of either generating more revenue, or they'll have to cut spending dramatically.

Johnson's Departure Leaves Void at the Fed

Manuel Johnson's decision to leave the vice chairmanship of the Federal Reserve Board to become a professor could have some serious effects on the Fed.

On the plus side, Johnson's move could give chairman Alan Greenspan an even stronger voice in policy decisions. On the negative side, Johnson's departure will leave the Fed without a well-versed liaison with foreign central banks.

"Foreign central bankers are all sorry to see him go," says one source with strong contacts in those circles. "With his departure, the international side of the Fed needs some strengthening," says this source. The Fed and foreign central banks, of course, have been coordinating monetary policy.

The International Assn. of Financial Planning in Atlanta says Americans who responded to a recent survey believe that the 1990s will be a decade of decreasing economic opportunities. The most pessimistic respondents, the survey found, were those 35- to 44-year-olds who see their retirement plans hurt by the need to put their children through college and care for aging parents. The survey found that 61% of those answering were less inclined to take financial risks now and 62% are more inclined to save money over the next 10 years . . .

Mara Balsbaugh, entertainment analyst at Smith Barney & Co., recently told clients that a 3-for-1 stock split is a "strong possibility" for Time Warner Inc. in the near to intermediate term. She also expects Time Warner to announce some "fairly significant restructuring moves . . . involving asset and/or equity stake sales, potentially eliminating billions of dollars in debt." . . .

In a recent conference call with Prudential-Bache analysts, Sharon Vuinovich, McDonald's Corp.'s head of financial communications and investor relations, indicated that the burger company was disappointed with its second quarter. "We're all looking for a pickup. Our comparisons in the second quarter will be the easiest comparisons that we'll have all year. But, at the same time, I have to tell you that we aren't seeing any substantial pickup in business right now . . . So I guess we'll just have to see," said Vuinovich in the Pru-Bache transcript of the call. . .
