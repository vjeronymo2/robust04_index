BFN [Editorial: "Iranian Speaker's Address -- Where Do We Stand?"] [Text] Iranian Parliament Speaker 'Ali Akbar Nateq-Nuri addressed the Pakistan National Assembly and has fully and strongly supported Pakistan on its nuclear program and the Kashmir issue. He warned that attempts by any foreign power to influence events in the region will go against the security interests of the neighboring Islamic countries. Iran has established close friendly relations with Pakistan after the Islamic revolution and has always sympathized with Islamabad whenever it faced problems. The manner in which Nateq-Nuri, in his National Assembly speech, has openly assured Iran's support on the serious problems facing Pakistan will further strengthen the spirit of Islamic brotherhood in this region. In fact, the nuclear issue or the Kashmir dispute does not affect Pakistan alone, but they also have a direct impact on the neighboring Islamic states. If Pakistan is made weak, God forbid, in the nuclear field, then it will lead to the weakening of the entire Muslim community. If, under the alleged conspiracies, a third option [preceding two words in English] is adopted to settle the Kashmir dispute, then it is feared the United States and anti-Islamic forces might turn Kashmir into a base for international machination and from there orchestrate their conspiracies to weaken the Muslim countries of the region. Iran is also facing U.S. pressure on the nuclear issue, and, hence, it can fully realize Pakistan's difficulties in this regard. Nateq-Nuri's remarks help us to assess to what extent Iran desires to see Pakistan militarily become strong. Coincidentally, the Iranian speaker arrived in Islamabad the same day the U.S. delegation was departing from Pakistan. On that occasion, Pakistan and the United States issued a joint statement expressing their resolve to continue to strive to strengthen bilateral relations. The U.S. delegation was led by Deputy Secretary of State Strobe Talbott, who had come to Pakistan after visiting India. The main purpose of his visit was nothing but to help eliminate the nuclear arms race in the Indian subcontinent. However, it is ironic that while in India nobody listened to what Mr. Talbott had to say, in Pakistan his delegation was welcomed and heard very enthusiastically. Even though the Government of Pakistan asserts that no national interest was compromised, the wording of the joint statement and the expression of happiness on the face of Talbott during his meetings with the president and the prime minister and at the news briefing rightly lead one to suspect what success led the U.S. representative to display such a sense of happiness. The use of vague language in the joint statement itself reflects the success of the U.S. stand. The foreign media is also giving the same impression. Even though the supply of F-16's has not been tied to the capping of the nuclear program, Pakistan has also not openly showed a red signal to the U.S. moves; it is worth noting that the two countries will adopt the path of "quiet diplomacy" to resolve the nuclear issue. This method is definitely useful for the United States, but it is also tantamount to openly deceiving the Pakistani people. The Government of Pakistan, therefore, must remain cognizant of the fact that the people will never accept any compromise -- open or secret -- on their independence, security, and sovereignty! The government must not commit even the slightest mistake in understanding the feelings of the nation. Similar are the sentiments of the neighboring Islamic states and further progress in the nuclear program is essential for enhancing the strength of both Pakistan and the Islamic community.
