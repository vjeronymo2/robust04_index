CSO [Article by Wesley Gibbings: "Dollar Laundering Law Coming, Says Huggins"] [Text] Government is considering the introduction of banking regulations which will make deposit declarations for large sums of money mandatory. National Security Minister Russel Huggins made the revelation yesterday, saying the measure would be one instrument in the campaign against money laundering. The matter arose while Huggins steered a motion on the granting of diplomatic immunity to the Caribbean Financial Action Task Force through the Senate. Huggins was responding to a query from Opposition Senator Wade Mark who said commercial banks should not be left on their own to monitor a situation that provided vital clues to the scale of money laundering activities in the country. "Don't leave it up to commercial banks," Mark said. "One important area of money laundering is the commercial banking sector." Mark described the current arrangement as a "gentleman's agreement." Bankers have however reported some success with the system and a number of depositors, according to banking sources, have reportedly been turned away as a result. Huggins said regulations would be taken to Parliament on the matter "soon." He also defended the country's invitation to the Task Force to set up office here, saying it offered a range of resources to enable this country to battle money laundering more effectively. He told Senators that the body was not a policing agency and that it was established to offer technical advice and expertise to counter the incidence of money laundering in Latin America and the Caribbean. Independent Senator Martin Daly expressed scepticism about the institution and said a trial period of two years should be built into the motion granting it diplomatic immunities and privileges. He however refused to pursue the amendment when the vote was taken. Huggins also addressed fears by Mark that the industrial relations climate of certain regional institutions left much to be desired. The Opposition Senator contended that the managers of some institutions often hid under the cover of diplomatic immunity in order to commit serious breaches of the constitutional rights of workers. Huggins however said the terms of such diplomatic arrangements were dictated by international conventions and practices. The motion was passed unanimously. Last Friday, it gained similar consent from the Lower House.
