930507 FT 07 MAY 93 / Parliament and Politics: Labour wrangle over VAT INTERNAL wrangling over economic policy left the Labour leadership embarrassed yesterday as the government seized on an article by a party spokesman suggesting the ending of VAT zero rating for all items. Lord Desai, Labour economic spokesman in the House of Lords, also argued that Labour 'should oppose spending cuts but be bold in proposing suitably packaged tax increases'. Senior party figures sought to play down the significance of the remarks but the insistance of Mr Gordon Brown, Treasury spokesman, that Labour was opposed to extending VAT to food, children's clothing, fuel and transport, only highlighted tensions over the party's economic strategy. To the annoyance of some Labour MPs the party opposes tax increases but also complains about cuts in public spending. A revised tax and spending policy will depend on the outcome of the independent commission on social justice set up by Mr John Smith, party leader. Lord Desai, writing in the left-wing Tribune newspaper, said: 'It is time to debate whether Labour favours a high spending and high taxation policy, balancing the budget over the cycle, or whether it is always for high spending and low taxation.' He added: 'The latter alternative, although attractive, is in the long run neither economically rational nor politically credible.' Lord Desai said Labour had to think about taxation that would hit consumption by the better-off. He favoured higher band income tax and ending of the ceiling on national insurance contributions, proposed in Labour's 1992 election manifesto. 'Failing that, I would remove zero rating for VAT on all items.' Benefit entitlements could be doubled to compensate the poor, he added. At Treasury questions, Mr Norman Lamont, the chancellor, taunted Labour over the Lord Desai article: 'I think the whole country and the House will observe that the Labour party seems to have one policy in the House of Commons and another policy in the House of Lords.'
