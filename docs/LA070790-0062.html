July 7, 1990, Saturday, Home Edition

LIBERIAN REBELS SEIZE PORT NEAR DOE'S MANSION

Rebels firing heavy artillery attacked the port of Monrovia and fought a sea battle Friday near the fortified mansion where Liberian President Samuel K. Doe is trying to cling to power with a crumbling army.

Guerrillas moved in to the port, less than 2 miles from Doe's Atlantic beach fortress, telling soldiers to surrender, residents said.

"Don't die for Doe," rebels told the demoralized and disorganized troops.

One witness said he later saw three carloads of soldiers, dressed in civilian clothes, returning to central Monrovia, a city on a peninsula joined to the port by two bridges.

Plumes of smoke from explosions rose from the city outskirts, and automatic rifle and artillery fire echoed across the besieged capital from dawn until noon.

A rebel fishing trawler fired at a coast guard cutter about 5 miles offshore. The trawler, which hijacked a shipload of rice for the rebels Sunday, then gave chase, firing again before disappearing over the horizon.

The United States, which has offered to evacuate Doe, supplied water to his mansion under heavy Marine guard. Four American vehicles, with black tinted windows, escorted a water tanker to the mansion, a witness said.

The rebels, who invaded from Ivory Coast six months ago, now control all land routes out of the capital.

Doe, who seized power in 1980, has declined a U.S. offer to help him leave Liberia as rebels led by his former associate, Charles Taylor, tighten their grip on Monrovia.

Doe has offered to resign, on condition that his safety and that of his minority Krahn tribe is guaranteed.

Washington has a task force of 2,300 Marines and four Navy ships off the coast to evacuate foreigners if necessary.

Monrovia's 500,000 people spent much of Friday searching for food and carrying buckets of water from dwindling wells. The capital has been without water for nine days and without electricity for a week.

Most shops and markets have been closed since Monday and some people have been begging for food, or foraging for leaves to eat from waste ground.
