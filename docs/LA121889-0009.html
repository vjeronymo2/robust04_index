December 18, 1989, Monday, Home Edition

THIS SONG IS YOUR SONG AND SHOULD BE EVERYONE'S;

ANTHEM: WE OUGHT TO SCRAP 'THE STAR-SPANGLED BANNER,' BUT NOT FOR 'AMERICA THE BEAUTIFUL.' LOOK INSTEAD TO OUR MASTER SONGWRITER, WOODIE GUTHRIE.

A font of originality, Rep. Andrew Jacobs (D-Ind.) is again watering the nation's imagination. He wants to change the national anthem from "The Star Spangled Banner" to "America the Beautiful." His idea, proposed as legislation in Congress last January, has won the support of 315,000 Parade magazine readers who responded favorably to the question, should we change our national anthem? About 74,000 said "no".

The Jacobs theory -- that "The Star Spangled Banner" is a martial song that should be scrapped in favor of "America the Beautiful," which "celebrates our power and our ability to live and work in peace" -- is on the mark, but only by half.

The Star Spangled War Song deserves to be ditched, or at least dispatched to the parade grounds at West Point. But its replacement with "America the Beautiful" is little improvement. What's the appeal of a psuedo-patriotic hymn that turns God into a nationalistic prop and America into a surreal Shangri-La?

"America the Beautiful" is provincial, not national. It's about Colorado. The words were written by a sub-minor poet -- Katherine Lee Bates -- who ascended Pikes Peak in 1893 and was apparently turned giddy by the rarefied heights.

What's odd is that the congressman, strong in democratic populism, didn't come up with the one song that gives full expression to the popular will: "This Land is Your Land."

Woodie Guthrie's treasure, called "the greatest song ever written about America" by Bruce Springsteen, begins with the familiar lines:

This land is your land, this land is my land,

From California to the New York island

From the redwood forests to the gulfstream waters,

This land was made for you and me.

Then, these verses:

As I was walking that ribbon of highway,

I saw above me the endless skyway,

I saw below me that golden valley,

This land was made for you and me.

I've roamed and rambled and I followed my footsteps

To the sparkling sands of her diamond deserts,

And all around me a voice was sounding,

This land was made for you and me.

Singable, hand-clappingly lively and populist, Guthrie's work is a fit national anthem because it is a song of the folk. "I am out to sing songs that will prove to you that this is your world," he wrote in the early 1950s when the Library of Congress established a collection of his recordings. "I am out to sing the songs that make you take pride in yourself and in your work. And the songs I sing are made up for the most part by all sorts of folks like you."

From 1936 to 1952, Woody Guthrie produced about 1,000 songs, every one of them as truthful as the minstrel who sang them. The songs were messages of affirmation. "The main secret about singing," Guthrie wrote, "ain't so much to have other folks listen as it is to pick up your own spirits."

With such a national anthem as "This Land is Your Land," the picking up will lift the country higher than any bombs bursting in air or purple mountain majesties, whatever they are.
