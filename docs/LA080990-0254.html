August 9, 1990, Thursday, P.M. Final

LIZ SMITH: POITIER, LANCASTER TEAM UP FOR ABC MINISERIES

SIDNEY POITIER and Burt Lancaster, two great Oscar-winning movie stars, will join forces and appear together for the first time on television in ABC's miniseries "Separate but Equal."

Poitier portrays Supreme Court Justice Thurgood Marshall. Lancaster appears as John W. Davis, Marshall's chief opponent in the historic civil rights case known as Brown vs. the Board of Education. Marshall was counsel for the NAACP Legal Defense Fund and led the fight to end school segregation in the United States.

So who persuaded these two super-actors to pool their talents for this small-screen blockbuster? George Stevens Jr., that's who. Stevens, a talented man, is to television what his father was to movies. He will direct and produce "Separate but Equal."

Shooting on the ambitious project began early this week in South Carolina.

DON'T MISS the Aug. 12 issue of Parade magazine and writer Ed Klein's incisive, candid interview with former President Ronald Reagan.

In this piece, Reagan defends his oft-criticized wife Nancy: "Our marriage is the kind of thing that an adolescent believes marriage should be. . . . I don't think either one of us thinks of making a big decision without the other." Reagan says that even he was shocked by the controversial $2-million fee he received for a speaking engagement in Japan but insists he was not "cashing in on the presidency. . . . If I'd been of a nature to cash in, I would have returned to the industry that I left."

Reagan readily admits to having enjoyed being President, though he often felt trapped and confined because of all the security measures, "like a bird in a gilded cage." Still, if Reagan could have had his way, he would have lingered a while longer in that cage. He wanted very much to run again in 1988.

The 22nd Amendment, limiting a President to two terms in office, ended his reign. The 40th President of the United States declares emphatically: "That amendment is an infringement on the democratic right of the people. The people have a right to vote for whomever they want and for as many times as they want!"

NOW HERE is a bit of amusing movie trivia. All you "Casablanca" fanatics out there already know that young actor Reagan was once briefly considered to play the bitter cafe owner Rick in that famous film. But were you aware that Dooley Wilson was not the first choice as Rick's piano player?

No, originally, the producer, Hal Wallis, considered either Hazel Scott or Lena Horne for the part. When the legend that is Lena was recently told about this, she laughed and said, "Well, I guess they would have had to change that line to 'Play it again, Mandy.' "
