September 16, 1990, Sunday, Home Edition

Q & A

Q: During the past two years, I have put almost $500,000 into penny stocks through a San Diego brokerage firm. I happened to speak with a broker from a different firm, and he told me what some of my shares were worth. Then I talked with the San Diego broker who sold me the shares, and he quoted me prices that were higher. In fact, the first broker's prices are always higher. Why? -- San Diego

A: The first thing you should do is contact the Los Angeles office of the National Assn. of Securities Dealers and report what's going on. The next thing you should do is make an appointment with a psychiatrist to find out why you would invest $500,000 in penny stocks.

Your next call should be to the Securities and Exchange Commission office in Los Angeles. The number is (213) 965-3998. You might also get help from California securities regulators. And you might want to hire a lawyer.

I spoke with Joseph Goldstein, who heads the SEC's penny stock unit in Washington, and he says it is not unusual for stock quotes from different brokers to vary in thinly traded securities. "But quotes should be fairly close," he said.

Goldstein thinks that you should alert authorities to see if any law is being broken, and also so they can determine whether the practice you mention is widespread.

Not all penny stocks, of course, are worthless. But a lot of them are.

Q: A broker suggested that I invest $25,000 in Nestle, the Swiss company, because of its high earnings ratio and because it is one of the largest food distributors in Europe.

With more and more countries getting freedom from communism, it is hoped that their income will increase demands for products from which they had been deprived. What's the opinion of Wall Street about this stock? -- Lakewood, Colo.

A: That's an interesting thought: The Berlin Wall came down so that all the deprived people in Eastern Europe could run out and buy chocolate candy.

Ronald Morrow, the Smith Barney & Co. food and beverage analyst, calls Nestle S. A. of Vevey, Switzerland, a "very well run, high-quality company."

He's been recommending purchase of the stock, even though he sees some problems. Forget about Eastern Europe. Think about America, says Morrow, because Nestle gets 40% of its income here.

With the U.S. economy slowing, the big question is: Will strapped Americans cut back on their consumption of Nestle food products? Nestle's earnings could also be hurt by the weak dollar. The numbers could look worse than they really are when the company's sales in dollars are translated back into Swiss francs.

But Nestle's stock has already retreated some, so maybe investors are taking the company's potential problems into account.

By the way, Morrow is one of the few analysts in the United States who follows Nestle.

Q: Several years ago, I inherited 16,666 shares of Overthrust Resources Ltd. stock. The company was supposed to merge with Polaris Resources. I have been trying to locate Overthrust to see if the stock has any value. If it is worthless, I would like to take a tax loss. Letters that I've written to the company are returned, stamped "undeliverable." Can you help me find out if this stock has any value? -- Denver

A: Overthrust Resources seems to still exist legally, but the company is inactive. Robert Fisher of R. M. Smythe & Co., a New York company, says Overthrust last traded on Aug. 22, 1989, at 1 cent a share. There don't appear to be any bids for the stock at this time, and shares are being offered at 2 cents.

The company is still listed in the Denver phone book, but its number, (303) 298-0430, isn't manned. A recording at that phone number refers you to (303) 592-7077, which is the office of Polaris Resources.

The woman who picked up the phone at Polaris, but who didn't give her name, said Polaris doesn't own Overthrust. "Overthrust just works out of the same office," she said.

The woman confirmed that Overthrust's stock isn't trading and that it isn't worth "much more" than 2 cents a share. She also says Overthrust doesn't plan any expansion and won't file for bankruptcy.

You'll have to consult a tax adviser on what to do about the stock in an ongoing company that you are unable to sell. Even if you can claim a loss, the stock's price at the time you came into possession of it might have been so low as to make such a move unprofitable. JOHN CRUDELE
