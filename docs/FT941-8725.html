940218 FT 18 FEB 94 / Acquittal ruling fails to ease wariness in City Investment analysts are thought unlikely to become any less wary of infringing the laws on insider dealing than they have been since last year's conviction of Mr Thorold Mackie, a Scottish investment analyst, in spite of the quashing on Wednesday of the conviction. The Court of Criminal Appeal in Edinburgh also cancelled Mr Mackie's Pounds 25,000 fine. Lord Hope, the Lord Justice General of Scotland, said the trial judge had misdirected the jury. The conviction of Mr Mackie sent a shudder through the UK financial community, which changed its way of doing business. Mr Hamish Buchan, a leading analyst, with NatWest Markets said: 'An analyst might now begin a session with a company chairman by saying: 'Please don't make me an insider'. I believe they will remain guarded despite Thorold's acquittal.' Many analysts now like to have a colleague with them when they talk to a company to corroborate an account of the meeting if there were a subsequent dispute. Mr Mackie was convicted under the 1985 Company Securities (Insider Dealing) Act of 'counselling and procuring' two salesmen at Bell Lawrie White, the Edinburgh stockbrokers for which he worked, to deal in shares of Shanks & McEwan, the waste disposal company, on behalf of clients. Mr Peter Runciman, chairman of Shanks & McEwan, had testified that he had informed Mr Mackie that the company was soon to issue a profits warning. Mr Mackie said that Mr Runciman had only said that there would be 'little by way of growth in earnings per share' for the company in the current half year. By selling ahead of the profits warning that was later issued the shares realised Pounds 1.4m more than they would have done after the warning was issued. Lord Hope, backed by Lords Cowie and Mayfield, said this week that the trial judge, temporary judge Sheriff Robin McEwan QC, was wrong to say that Mr Runciman's evidence was corroborated by the fact that Bell Lawrie White sold so many shares just after the meeting. He said the circumstantial evidence was also entirely consistent with Mr Mackie's innocent version of events. 'In my opinion there was a miscarriage of justice because there was insufficient evidence in law for a conviction.'
