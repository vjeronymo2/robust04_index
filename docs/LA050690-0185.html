May 6, 1990, Sunday, Home Edition

Q & A

Q: I'm watching the stock of Wendy's. It appears to have been forming a base at about $4 a share. Is this company a sound investment or am I just too fond of burgers? -- Glenview, Ill.

A: Wendy's did bounce off the $4 level and was recently selling for nearly $5. But don't interpret that to mean that this company isn't still in a pickle trying to compete with the likes of McDonald's. Edward M. Tavlin, an analyst with Josephthal & Co., has a neutral opinion on Wendy's right now. "Wendy's is a survivor involved in a continuing struggle in a very competitive market," says Tavlin.

Wendy's earned 25 cents a share in 1989, plus it had 7 cents in extraordinary gains for a total of 32 cents. It will probably come close to that performance in 1990, according to Tavlin.

Q: My inquiry has to do with Safeway Stores' 1996 warrants. A friend of mine told me to buy them. Since Safeway has announced its intention to issue stock, are my warrants being called? Should I buy more warrants? I'm neither a widow nor orphan, so I don't worry about the risks involved. -- Larkspur, Calif.

A: You didn't give any details about the warrants, so it took some digging. But here goes:

The warrants are callable by the company only after 1991. But after you wrote your letter, Safeway indeed issued 10 million shares, representing 10% of the company. They were priced at $11.25 a share on April 26 and closed that day in New York at $12.375. The warrants you hold can be converted into stock at your option. As you probably know, each warrant represents the right to purchase approximately 0.279 common shares.

Q: Executives of Ames Department Stores have been buying a lot of the company's stock lately, starting at around $12 a share. Now it sells for just above $4. Can all these insiders be wrong? Or is Ames a smart buy? -- Grand Island, N.Y.

A: If Ames executives have been buying a lot of their company's stock as you say, it just proves that insiders can be as dumb as anyone else. Since you wrote, Ames stock has halved in price. It's now selling for just $2 a share. And even that may be too expensive.

The Connecticut-based retailing company has lots of problems that began with its 1988 acquisition of Zayre Corp. for $800 million. It couldn't turn the discount store chain around and has been closing stores ever since.

Q: My question involves Skyline Corp. Why are its assets so liquid and its stock value so low? -- Skokie, Ill.

A: Robert Curran, an analyst at Merrill Lynch & Co., says Skyline's stock has never really reflected the fact that the company has a lot of cash and very little debt. And he can't explain why.

According to Curran, Skyline -- which makes recreational vehicles and manufactured housing -- has more than $9 a share of cash on its books. Even so, its stock was recently selling for a little over $14. Curran says the same relationship between the amount of available cash and the stock price has existed for years. Not too many years ago, some enterprising corporate raider would have had his eyes on all that cash. But today, as you've probably read, the raiders have all been put to sleep.

One factor that's been weighing heavily on Skyline's stock is slipping profits. The company earned $1.53 a share in the fiscal year that ended in May, 1989. Curran is estimating that profits will be down to 85 or 90 cents this year. Institutional Brokers Estimate System says the most optimistic analyst is predicting a 25-cent decline.

Skyline's business has been hurt by tax law changes in the mid-1980s, which promoted the construction of apartments in suburban areas. These apartments proved attractive competition for mobile homes. Also, when mortgage rates declined from their peaks a decade ago, many consumers opted to splurge on houses rather than buy mobile homes. And now, with home prices declining in many parts of the country, the conventional single-family dwelling is an even more attractive alternative. JOHN CRUDELE
