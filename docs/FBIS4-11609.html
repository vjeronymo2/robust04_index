BFN [Editorial Report] The following is a compilation of reports on economic activities monitored through 28 April: Telebras, Brazilian Telecommunications, Inc., has opened the social capital of its state subsidiaries expecting to make $260 million this year. This was announced yesterday when the company announced its 1993 balance which showed a $1.143 billion net profit. (Sao Paulo O ESTADO DE SAO PAULO in Portuguese 21 Apr 93 p B7) Yesterday, the government reduced the import duty on 17 products to 2 percent. Economic Policy Secretary Winston Fritsch said the objective is to warn national industry from committing price abuses. The products are soluble coffee; soft drinks; beer; cheese and milk byproducts; sugar; tire tubes for cars and trucks; tires for cars, trucks, and tractors; car generators and dynamos; disc plows; lampblack; ceramic bathroom parts; shaving machines; shaving cream; razor blades; oil; olives; and seeds and plants. Import duty on aluminum cans and condoms were reduced to zero. (Sao Paulo GAZETA MERCANTIL in Portuguese 21 Apr 94) Central Bank net dollar purchases totaled $1.7 billion in March, while international cash reserves grew by $1.4 billion in February, to reach $30.5 billion. At the end of January, cash reserves stood at $29.1 billion. Liquid reserves, which include medium and long-term credits, delayed credits, and provisions for the return of capital, stood at $36.5 billion at the end of February, while at the end of January they stood at $35.4 billion. The Central Bank bulletin states that the slower reserve growth compared to January, when reserves grew by $3.2 billion, was caused by a drop in the exchange operations surplus registered in February. (Sao Paulo O ESTADO DE SAO PAULO in Portuguese 22 Apr 94 p B6) German enterprises control 1,027 enterprises in Brazil. This is equivalent to 12 percent of the 8,567 foreign capital enterprises registered in Brazil. Germans control 65, 47, and 35 percent, respectively, of the car parts, vehicle, and medicine sectors. (Sao Paulo O ESTADO DE SAO PAULO in Portuguese 23 Apr 94 p B2) Yesterday, Citibank Senior Vice President Alcides Amaral said Brazil is again being granted short-term credits. He estimated that so far the country has obtained short-term credits worth $16 billion, especially for financing exports. The flow of short-term credits increased after Brazil began to negotiate its foreign debt. In connection with Brazil's debt to private banks, on the day the debt negotiation was concluded, the country received $352 million. Of this, Citibank provided $220 million.
