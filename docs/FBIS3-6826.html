Language: English Article Type:BFN [Text] Tokyo, March 22 KYODO -- Government officials met unofficially Tuesday [22 March] to start discussing possible measures to halt the flow of Japanese money to North Korea, which allegedly funds a suspected nuclear arms development program. The officials are considering banning cash remittances, grounding North Korea-bound sea vessels and aircraft, conducting export checks at Niigata on the Sea of Japan and refusing to issue reentry permits to travelers going to the isolated socialist state, government sources said. North Korea denies that it is trying to build an atomic bomb but continues to deny international inspectors access to its more suspicious facilities. On Monday, the UN nuclear monitor International Atomic Energy Agency (IAEA) urged Pyongyang to comply with its safeguard measures and referred the issue to the UN Security Council, which could impose sanctions. Analysts have suggested that the sanctions would have little effect on the already impoverished nation, which receives backing from China and an estimated 60 billion yen annually from pro-Pyongyang Korean residents in Japan. Attempts to halt the money flow are likely to be thwarted by a confusing array of routes the money takes through banks, in cash, in trade transactions and through third nations on its journey from Japan to North Korea. Some is sent to relatives of Korean residents living in Japan but the U.S. insists the money is being used to fund the alleged arms program. If sanctions are imposed, the government intends to do what it can within the limits of the law but worries about remittances making their way to Pyongyang through third nations, informed sources said. Government sources suggested the Security Council will likely seek a way for the inspectors to complete the inspections in North Korea and is not immediately discussing a resolution on sanctions. The sources said Japan intends to keep close contacts with the U.S., China and South Korea in an attempt to reach a peaceful resolution to the nuclear problem through dialogue.
