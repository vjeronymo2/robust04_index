BFN [Commentary by H.S. Rao of PTI] [Text] A great deal of importance was attached to the visit of the U.S. deputy secretary of state, Mr. Strobe Talbott, to India and Pakistan. In New Delhi, the first (?stop) on his first foreign trip as U.S. deputy secretary of state, Strobe Talbott has had cordial and frank exchange of views with Indian leaders, including the prime minister, Mr. P.V. Narasimha Rao. During the discussions spreading over two days, Indian leaders conveyed India's concern on the proposed U.S. move to transfer F-16 aircraft to Pakistan. There were even indications that the U.S. persistence with the supply could cast a cloud over the prime minister, Mr. P.V. Narasimha Rao's visit to Washington. As New Delhi sees it, F-16's would adversely affect the strategic balance against it in the region, forcing India to take appropriate measures diverting the much-needed resources from economic welfare programs and setting back the process of economic reforms. The future course of Indo-U.S. relations, it was clear, would be shaped by Washington's response to India's concern. As expected, Mr. Talbott defended the U.S. proposal in his talks at various levels, the ground that it was linked with the nuclear nonproliferation obligations on the part of Islamabad and that the aircraft would be transferred only if Pakistan agreed to verifiable cap on the production of weapon grade material. [sentence as heard] However, he told his hosts in New Delhi that he had noted India's strong views. During the talks, Mr. Talbott was plainly told of the prevailing popular sentiment in India against the U.S. tilt toward Pakistan in [word indistinct] of the cold war attitude. India as was told did not like third parties to look for equality, parity, or balance between India and Pakistan. The territorial integrity of the country would not be sacrificed, the Indian side said, while emphasizing that Jammu and Kashmir was part of India. Mr. Talbott dealt with the tilt perception at his press conference. As he put it, the very word tilt belongs to the vocabulary of the past and specifically to the vocabulary of the cold war. The U.S. has good relations with a number of countries in the area -- certainly with India and certainly with Pakistan. On Kashmir, the position of Clinton administration, he said, is very simple and consistent. The United States hopes very much that India and Pakistan will be able to resolve this matter themselves as per the Simla Agreement. Forty-seven year-old Talbott, a former TIME magazine editor and columnist, is a close friend of President Clinton. They shared a room as Rhodes scholars at Oxford University a quarter century ago. When Clinton visited Washington as governor of Arkansas, he often stayed at Talbott's home. One of the Talbott's assignments was to remove the sense of frustration in Indo-U.S. relations. After his one-hour long discussions with the prime minister, Mr. P.V. Narasimha Rao, Mr. Talbott observed that no one person (?is fit) to bring about better understanding and cooperation between India and the U.S. than Mr. Rao himself. He also announced that Mr. Rao has accepted in principle an invitation to visit the United States and Mr. Clinton looks forward to receiving Mr. Rao to make the most of what the two countries have in common. This itself is an indication of the positive aspect. But the same cannot be said about Mr. Talbott's diplomatic mission to Islamabad. In Islamabad, Mr. Talbott held intensive talks with the Pakistan prime minister, Ms. Benazir Bhutto, President Farooq Leghari, and other leaders. On the whole, the talks were held in business-like fashion although cordiality and nostalgic memories of an old strategic relationship marked the discussions. Three major areas were covered. The issue of nuclear disarmament in the South Asian regional context, Kashmir, and the U.S.-Pakistan bilateral framework on the question of inspections to ensure the capping of Islamabad's nuclear armament program. A noticeable degree of convergence of objectives came into focus in respect of the U.S. proposal for multilateral forum to discuss nonproliferation in South Asia. On the other two issues, though Pakistan appeared to have given a patient hearing, no appreciation and nod from the U.S. was available. On Kashmir, he reiterated the U.S. stand that India and Pakistan should resolve it in accordance with the Simla agreement. This indicates Pakistan's failure either to secure a recognition of the linkage between Kashmir issue and nuclear nonproliferation in South Asia or to elicit a view that Kashmir, more than nuclear proliferation, is a complicating factor in this region. Mr. Talbott also refused to budge on the issue of verifiable cap on Pakistan's nuclear program and maintained that discussions would continue between the U.S. and Pakistan.
