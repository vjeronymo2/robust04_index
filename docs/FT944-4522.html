941208 FT 08 DEC 94 / Global harmony begins at home, warns Pennant-Rea Exchange rate stability requires micro-economic reforms to improve productivity, as well as macro-economic measures to control inflation, the deputy governor of the Bank of England said yesterday. Giving the third Bank of England/London School of Economics lecture, Mr Rupert Pennant-Rea said that 'to achieve international monetary harmony, every country should start at home'. Reviewing the history of exchange rate management, Mr Pennant-Rea said: 'It has become virtually impossible for any central bank or government to withstand the pressures of a sceptical market.' He added that systems, such as Bretton Woods or the European Monetary System, which 'clustered' around core currencies such as the dollar or the D-Mark, did not last forever. Mr Pennant-Rea said that exchange rate targets were targets 'not just for the authorities but for currency dealers as well; and like all targets, they invite pot shots'. However, he said that the low inflation rates of 1993 and most of 1994 had been 'due to policy decisions made while sterling was in the exchange rate mechanism. Only now is Britain in a position to prove that it can keep inflation down solely by virtue of its domestic resolve to do so - and it is far too early to judge how we will fare in the test.' Apart from the difficulties caused to macro-economic management by free international capital markets, Mr Pennant-Rea also addressed the problems posed to prudential supervision. He stressed the importance of real-time settlement which, he said, 'would significantly reduce the risks in global finance, bringing benefits to everybody'.
