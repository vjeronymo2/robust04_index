Language: English Article Type:BFN [Text] Vienna, March 21 KYODO -- The International Atomic Energy Agency (IAEA) convened a meeting Monday [21 March] in Vienna of its Board of Governors to discuss North Korea's refusal to allow IAEA inspectors to make a full inspection of its nuclear facilities. The Board of Governors will hear a report from IAEA Director General Hans Blix about the inspection work by a group of inspectors on North Korean facilities but North Korea threatened Monday to quit the Nuclear Nonproliferation Treaty (NPT). A North Korean Foreign Ministry spokesman said in a statement that Pyongyang has "no alternative" but to (?reactivate) 1993 decision to leave the international pact on the nonproliferation of nuclear weapons. The U.N. nuclear watchdog body is expected to pass a resolution that will refer the North Korean nuclear issue to the U.N. Security Council, Agency sources said. If the matter is referred, the Security Council may discuss possible economic sanctions against North Korea, the sources said. China, one of the five permanent members on the Security Council, may oppose any tough action against North Korea with which Beijing has maintained close relations, they said. Japanese Prime Minister Morihiro Hosokawa, during weekend meetings with Chinese Premier Li Peng, voiced grave concern over North Korea's suspected development of nuclear weapons, saying the international community's patience is nearing its limit. Li told Hosokawa that the international community should try to solve the problem through patient negotiations. Li also said it is important to give North Korea "what it needs." The Chinese premier, however, did not specify what North Korea needs. A group of IAEA nuclear experts returned from North Korea earlier this month after being unable to verify whether nuclear material had been diverted from a radiochemical laboratory in Yongbyon, about 90 kilometers north of Pyongyang. Amid mounting tensions on the Korean peninsula over North Korea's suspected nuclear development program, the United States and South Korea are moving toward resuming the Team Spirit military exercises. South Korean President Kim Yong-sam instructed his ministers Monday to consult with the United States about when to hold the Team Spirit military exercise, Presidential Press Secretary Chu Ton-sik said. Seoul's YONHAP NEWS AGENCY reported South Korea and the U.S. plan to deploy Patriot air defense missiles at U.S. military bases in South Korea next month.
