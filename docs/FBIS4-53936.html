BFN [By Yi Tong-min] [Text] Jakarta, May 23 (YONHAP) -- The diplomatic importance of the Non-Aligned Movement (NAM) to South Korea was driven home during Foreign Minister Han Sung-chu's visit here this week, especially with the North Korean nuclear controvery looming in the background. Han came to Jakarta eight days before a meeting of NAM foreign ministers, held every three years, in Cairo where the North Korean nuclear issue, in connection with an overall review of the situation on the Korean peninsula, will be addressed for the first time. "It's important that we make clear where we stand on the North Korean nuclear problem, that NAM take our position into account when the foreign ministers' meeting convenes," said Yu Pyong-u, director-general of the Asian Affairs Bureau at the South Korean Foreign Ministry. The foreign ministers are expected to adopt a final document through consensus and the wording of the clause on the nuclear impasse, if it in any way leans toward South Korea, will have no small impact considering Pyongyang's ties with NAM. North Korea keeps its third largest embassy in Jakarta with a 25-member staff. Current Ambassador Ham Pong-ha has been here for four years. Diplomatic officials say the embassy functions as a base camp for North Korea's diplomatic lobbying activities in Southeast Asia as well as Australia and New Zealand. Indonesia, as the chair of NAM, could serve as South Korea's window to the group by reflecting its position without Seoul actually being there, bringing the movement more toward the center between the two Koreas. It is hoped that Han's visit will add force to Seoul's efforts to achieve this objective. Indonesia's stance on the nuclear issue was too ambivalent from Seoul's point of view as late as last January, when Jakarta kept visibly silent as the UN Security Council adopted a resolution urging Pyongyang to comply with International Atomic Energy Agency (IAEA) inspections. South Korea felt compelled to step up its persuasion of Indonesia, hoping in turn to reach NAM. Then in March, President Suharto sent one of his ambassadors to North Korea as a special envoy. The following month, North Korea sent Vice Foreign Minister Kim Chang-kyu to Jakarta. This activity produced a letter from President Suharto, speaking as NAM chairman, to UN Secretary-General Butrus Butrus-Ghali saying the group wanted to see the problem solved through dialogue, but that North Korea should accept IAEA inspections and remain within the nuclear Nonproliferation Treaty (NPT) regime. Seoul officials say this stance was the product of much convincing by South Korea and international organizations. The global consensus obviously helped as the issue has been recycled through IAEA, United Nations and Security Council statements and resolutions, they say. South Korea's ties with NAM will receive a more potent boost when President Kim Yong-sam comes here for the Asia-Pacific Economic Cooperation (APEC) summit in November. The Seoul government expects a Kim-Suharto summit during the APEC conference. The presidents will likely go over again, at the highest level, where they stand on the nuclear controversy while strengthening their two-way relationship.
