BFN [Text] Belmopan [dateline as received] -- Belizean Prime Minister Manuel Esquivel today announced that at the upcoming meeting of the Caribbean Common Market [Caricom] scheduled for July, he will propose the creation of a regional commission to establish a dialogue with the Haitian military. Esquivel voiced his desire for such a commission to serve as mediator in the search for a solution to the crisis that Haiti has been experiencing since 30 September 1991, when a military junta overthrew President Jean-Bertrand Aristide. Esquivel, who is taking part in a meeting of representatives and governors of Central America, the Caribbean, and the United States, said the proposal may be supported by other countries of the region, including Jamaica, the Dominican Republic, and others. Delegates of 13 Caricom member-states, Central American economics ministers, and eight governors from U.S. southern states are participating in the two-day meeting. Esquivel said there is a great deal of resistance on the part of the Haitian military, perhaps because it views the United States as a great power. With the creation of the commission, which would not attempt to displace Washington, "there could be other alternatives," he said. He added: "It is perhaps easier for the Haitians to talk with the small countries of the region rather than with the United States. The message would be the same; only the messenger would be different." He believes the U.S. Government has dealt with the problem well. He said: "Its ideas are good. The problem is that the United States is the world's most powerful country. It is difficult for it to talk with one of the smallest countries in the region. I think it is also easier for the rest of the countries to talk with one another than with a larger country."
