October 9, 1989, Monday, Home Edition

PROTECTING WATER SUPPLY

Brown-Ferris Industries, operator of Azusa landfill, cannot be faulted for promoting its case against the combined forces of the water industry and state environmental groups before the State Water Resources Control Board. A potential 40-million ton refuse depository (editorial, "Not the Right Spot to Begin With," Sept. 30) generating a rate/ton averaging $15-$25 per ton until the expected fill completion date of 2010 adequately reveals the motive. Considering the legendary low overhead of the landfill industry, the situation looks like a real money machine.

However, Region IX Environmental Protection Agency estimates a 30-year $800-million bill required to purify the main San Gabriel basin water table, an extensive underground "water tank" or reservoir, which could serve parched Los Angeles regional needs if it were adequately cleaned up to contain excess winter flows from the State Water Project and Colorado River.

The State Water Board's own staff has documented heavy concentrations of a great variety of pollutants, including industrial solvents, which amount to the greatest underground catastrophe in the state. By a substantial margin, we are No. 1. These financial facts expose (former EPA administrator and current) Brown-Ferris Chairman William D. Ruchelshaus' offer to build $20 million in water treatment facilities at three sites to be puny indeed.

Despite Public Utility Commission regulation, our own water industry might also be considered a money machine. Why haven't all the adjudication participants of the Main San Gabriel Basin Water Masters installed these suggested water treatment facilities on their own or with EPA financial assistant when we became a Superfund cleanup site over 5 years ago? The voters of the San Gabriel Valley await the answer to this question.

Our basin, critical to the future of Southern California, must be protected. Both parties to the dispute have been negligent by not showing adequate diligence. The state board should have denied the landfill expansion permit. Local control has failed. The state's natural resources are burdened by uncompensated costs borne for decades and the cleanup bill is now due.

WILLIAM ROBINSON

Director, Division 4

Upper San Gabriel Valley

Municipal Water District

West Covina
