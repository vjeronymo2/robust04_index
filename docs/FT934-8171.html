931117 FT 17 NOV 93 / Third of companies 'register pre-tax losses' ABOUT ONE THIRD of British companies registered a pre-tax loss in 1992, casting doubts on government claims that UK industry had survived the recession with profits at a high level, according to a report from two City institutions. Robert Fleming Securities, a stockbroker, and Risk & Opportunity Intelligence (ROI), an independent corporate analyst, said official figures highlighting robust real rates of return on capital employed and strong non-North Sea gross trading profits 'seriously misrepresent the traumas of the corporate sector during the past four years'. Drawing on figures provided by Dun & Bradstreet, the business information group, Mr Peter Warburton of Robert Fleming and Mr Niall Fraser of ROI said that 'healthy profits are the preserve of a few thousand firms, while tens of thousands have battled for survival'. Taking 1991 data on 113,600 companies, they said the top 250 UK companies contributed more than 30 per cent and the top 1,600 some 70 per cent of the pre-tax profits earned by British business that year. In an analysis of 16 UK industrial sectors, Messrs Warburton and Fleming said a 'staggering' 37.6 per cent of all companies registered a pre-tax loss in their 1991 accounts. A further 27.9 per cent reported 'trivial' profits of less than Pounds 50,000. On the strength of provisional data, the researchers estimated that at least 33 per cent of British companies reported a pre-tax loss last year. Messrs Warburton and Fraser said that large companies, with annual sales of more than Pounds 50m, had emerged from the recession in a much stronger position and with much greater credit-worthiness than small companies. They said their sectoral analysis showed that manufacturing companies had the highest average credit standing. The intermediate and capital goods industries contained the highest proportion of low risk companies, followed by financial services and holding companies and wholesalers of non-durable goods. During the recession property companies supplanted those providing hotel, accommodation and leisure services at the bottom of the credit quality league with the greatest proportion of companies considered to be risky trading partners. Mr Warburton took issue with Mr Eddie George, the governor of the Bank of England, who recently warned companies not to boost their margins excessively. The quest for high margins was an 'essential process in the rehabilitation of the corporate sector', he said.
