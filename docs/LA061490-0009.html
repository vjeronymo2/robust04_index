June 14, 1990, Thursday, Home Edition

CONTINUING CONTROVERSY OVER FEDERAL FUNDS FOR ARTISTS

The essay by Edwin Meese III and Bruce Fein ("High Clerics of Art Have Had Enough," Commentary, May 21) shows that, when it comes to art, everyone has an opinion. Their opinion, to which they are entitled in this free and diverse country, is that the National Endowment for the Arts ought to be abolished. The case upon which they build it, however, is not one to inspire confidence in them as either art critics or lawyers.

They assert that the public would have been better off investing in "defaulted junk bonds" than in government-assisted art because the endowment has not produced "artistic masterpieces that promise to live for the ages," that there have been no "NEA-funded artists who would do honor to Van Gogh, Picasso, Mozart, Rubens, Monet, Beethoven, Voltaire or Shakespeare."

How to address this remarkable conclusion? With the fact that the authors are comparing contemporary artists helped in the last 25 years with recognized masters, many of whom were long dead by the time their work was fully appreciated? With the irony in their naming only European artists, implying that America is a cultural wasteland? With their criticism of NEA Chairman John Frohnmayer, whom they say "shows no glitter as a talent scout for fledgling genius?" True enough, Frohnmayer has been on the job for seven months and hasn't a single Shakespeare to his credit. Get with it, John!

In commenting on what they perceive as the "arrogance" of the arts community in its request for a comparatively small amount of federal leadership and assistance, Meese and Fein assert that only freeloaders accept even meager support from their government. Is it not ironic, if not arrogant, for those who have made part of their own living and reputations on taxpayer support to suggest that the only good artist is a starving artist?

The nation would have been better served had public officials paid more attention to the criminal conditions and bungling that led to the "defaulted junk bonds" and savings and loan crisis. The $2,000 bill each American likely will have to pay to remedy that fiasco pales next to the 68 cents per year each pays for the arts endowment and its support of arts education programs, museums, local orchestras, theater and dance companies and public television programs. What will it be, folks: 68 cents for the arts or $2,000 to help defray the cost of a junk bond dealer's Mercedes?

I can hardly wait to see their next opinion piece, "Meese and Fein on Investing."

JACK LICHTENSTEIN

Director, Office of Public Affairs

National Endowment for the Arts

Washington, D.C.
