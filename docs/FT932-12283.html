930429 FT 29 APR 93 / Jordan forecasts new strike wave ECONOMIC RECOVERY could be accompanied by 'a return to industrial disputes of a combative kind' such as the bitter dispute at Timex in Dundee, Mr Bill Jordan, the president of the AEEU engineering union, warned last night. He told the Commons employment committee that trade union and individual worker rights had dramatically worsened over the past 14 years. Throughout the 13-week Timex dispute, he said, his union had fully complied with the law but appeared to be 'not one inch nearer a settlement'. The union has been seeking a negotiated agreement with Timex over the reinstatement of 341 workers dismissed nearly three months ago for refusing to accept new terms and conditions of employment. Mr Jordan said the union agreed with Timex's aim of turning its lossmaking plant into profit and urged the company to join the union in calling in the conciliation service Acas to try to resolve the dispute. Timex said in its evidence it wanted to see a toughening in the labour laws. Mass picketing should be turned into a criminal offence, it said. In its written submission the company said that controlling picketing by a voluntary code of practice would 'continue to be ineffective'. It said it had not been able to use the law against outsiders who came to picket because it had failed to establish their identity. Mr Peter Hall, the company's chief executive, said there had been widespread violence during the dispute. The company said the present labour laws had made it impossible to change its workers' contracts of employment without going through a 12-week notice period, which it claimed would have jeopardised its financial survival. Mr Hall said that the company would reconsider its position on May 17, which would be 90 days after the workers were sacked. Under existing law a company cannot rehire workers who have lost their jobs in a mass dismissal without facing claims of unfair dismissal by those not offered re-employment. Mr Greville Janner, the Labour MP who chairs the select committee, appealed to Mr Hall to settle the dispute. 'Could you not reconsider the situation having heard what the union had to say?' he asked. Mr Hall said: 'We have stated our position and that is that.'
