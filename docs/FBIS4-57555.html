BFN [Report by Yonis Garcia] [Text] Upon leaving Miraflores, Finance Minister Julio Sosa Rodriguez asserted that his office estimates the country will earn additional income of approximately 6 billion bolivares from PDVSA [Venezuelan Petroleum] thanks to an increase in oil prices in May. According to the minister, this income will help efforts to balance the budget deficit. "But I don't have too many hopes. I believe we will earn enough foreign exchange to be able to meet the country's needs--I mean, all its exports and the debt servicing. I should point out that the increase in the price of oil is due to an increase in demand for oil and the improving U.S. economic situation." The minister reported the government has collected 3.1 billion bolivares in bank debit taxes. "We have not had any problems in collecting those revenues. The system has worked satisfactorily, and the banking sector has not had any computer problems." Regarding the concession law approved by the Council of Ministers, Sosa Rodriguez said the legal mechanism for construction of public works will attract foreign investors to the country and will provide economic compensation for the budget cuts made to several national interest projects. Referring to the sales tax law, the finance minister affirmed that this law will be approved by the executive on Wednesday. When he was asked if they planned to extend the tax on banking debits, he replied: "I don't think it's right to extend this tax." The IMF visit to Venezuela has triggered public expectations regarding the economic conditions and programs it plans to suggest to the government. In this respect, Sosa stated: "From a psychological point of view, it would be good for the IMF to officially prop up the level of our reserves and make a public statement on the situation. This would have a positive impact on our economy and deter the negative influence of speculators and rumors about our currency. In conclusion, it would make it more difficult for speculators who work in our markets." Likewise, he added that the only point the IMF could question is the suspension of the value-added tax (VAT). "Everyone knows this tax had to be lifted because the country was neither prepared nor organized to collect this kind of revenue. The methodology was used for commercial speculation to the detriment of the citizens." On the same subject, the minister said: "Another thing the IMF could ask is why we have not increased the price of gasoline. On this point, I have always said we are going through an economic crisis, an inherited chaos, and to straighten things out we need to grant several administration subsidies to the collective transportation sector." On another topic, Sosa Rodriguez asserted that he is still studying the possibility of requesting support or other types of help from the IMF. "For the time being, however, both the IMF and the government, as had been planned, will work together for two weeks to study the economic situation and come up with a joint assessment." Another international financial organization that recently visited Venezuela was the World Bank. Julio Sosa Rodriguez said that the World Bank officials met with President Caldera. "They came to help us with the subsidized banks, and I believe their observations have been very positive. They offered to send us this week, a group of technicians to help Fogade [Deposit Guarantee and Banking Protection Fund] and the Banking Superintendent's Office in solving management and financial aid problems." Sosa reported the executive had requested credits, which are fully justified, for 156 billion bolivares from Congress. This money includes the resources for paying all government employees, including those in the Judiciary Council. "The only observation the Congress's Finance Commission had on my request was that I had not included allotments for corresponding social benefits."
