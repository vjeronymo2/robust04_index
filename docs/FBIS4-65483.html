BFN [Interview with UN Secretary General Butrus Butrus-Ghali by correspondent Franz-Olivier Giesbert in the Paris studio -- live] [Excerpts] [Giesbert] Good morning, Butrus Butrus-Ghali. [Butrus-Ghali] Good morning. [Giesbert] The United Nations is taking a lot of flak right now. You will have heard what [Prime Minister] Edouard Balladur and [Foreign Minister] Alain Juppe have said: If things continue like this in Bosnia, we shall withdraw our peacekeeping troops. What do you think about this? [Butrus-Ghali] I think that any peacekeeping operation must be of limited duration, and that we must encourage the protagonists in the conflict to find a peaceful solution as quickly as possible, because there is fatigue on the part of the member states, on both the military front and the financial front. [Giesbert] What will happen if France puts its threat into action? [Butrus-Ghali] It is for the Security Council to decide what we must look at, to which troops we should turn to replace the troops that wish to leave. A similar operation was conducted in Somalia, where the French, American, German, and Belgian troops withdrew, and we were able to continue the operation with African and Asian troops. [Giesbert] You saw Francois Mitterrand yesterday, as well as Edouard Balladur. Did they tackle you about it? [Butrus-Ghali] No, on the contrary; I received support from both the prime minister and the president for the action the United Nations is taking worldwide, whether in Angola, Mozambique, Rwanda, Burundi -- more or less throughout the world. [Giesbert] But in order to make progress in this matter of Bosnia, should one not in fact send more peacekeeping troops? [Butrus-Ghali] Yes, but if the states do not wish to do this, then we are dealing with an established fact. We have to try to continue with our action with the number of peacekeeping troops at our disposal at the moment. [Giesbert] With regard to your action in Bosnia, when you are accused, as happens often, of playing the Serbian card, how do you answer this? You have a representative there who is very fond of the Serbs. [Butrus-Ghali] No, not at all. We are neutral, we represent the international community. Our presence there is made up of French troops, British troops, Egyptian troops, Jordanian troops. You have an international group, it is a multinational force, and the decisions which are adopted are not decisions adopted by one or two states. They are adopted by 15 states, including the five big powers. So, we cannot be accused of subjectivity in so far as we are implementing decisions which are adopted by the international community. [Giesbert] You are not being accused of subjectivity, Mr. Secretary General, but your representative there is accused of subjectivity. [Butrus-Ghali] No, it is not true. The representative who is on the spot is applying the recommendations and the resolutions which are adopted by the member states of the United Nations, and it is a normal thing that the various protagonists are going to accuse us of subjectivity when this or that action is not in keeping with their particular objectives. [Giesbert] The Bosnians are howling. [Butrus-Ghali] The Bosnians are howling, the Serbs are howling, the Croats are howling, and we have had problems with all three protagonists at different moments. [Giesbert] What about the basic question raised by the Bosnia problem, because you are accused of many things, but in the final analysis, you do not have the necessary means? Does this not raise the question of this real army which the United Nations ought to have, a real army which would be capable of making war? [Butrus-Ghali] I agree, but this real army depends on the willingness of the member states. The United Nations does not have an army, arms, or the financial resources to be able to act, if the member states do not decide that this should be the case. [Giesbert] And what do the member states say? They say if we cannot do that, it is because of the United Nations. In other words, you are being used as a scapegoat. [Butrus-Ghali] Yes, certainly the United Nations is often used as a scapegoat, but I believe everyone knows very well that, in the final analysis, when a resolution which demands 10,000 men is adopted by the Security Council and we are unable to obtain the 10,000 men, this is not the fault of the UN Secretariat. It is due to the fact that the member countries do not wish to put the 10,000 men demanded by the Security Council resolution at the UN Secretariat's disposal. Or, alternatively, if members of the United Nations, after having put certain forces at UN disposal, decide to withdraw these forces, then I have no means of preventing these members from withdrawing their forces. [Giesbert] You, the secretary general, the United Nations, are insulted all the time. There are even French ministers who say it is this, it is that. There is talk of impotence, cowardice -- do you have the impression that you are being supported in your action? For example, do the states willingly help the United Nations -- in all ways, I am not just talking about financially? [Butrus-Ghali] No, no, no, the financial problem is another aspect of the problem. States do not pay their contribution. The United States owes us more than a billion dollars, so we have financial difficulties. [Giesbert] Are you saying that it is in fact the Third World that pays the United Nations? [Butrus-Ghali] To a certain extent it is the Third World which sends the troops, but obviously the logistics, the means of transporting these troops, the arms required for these troops, the means of communication, are those of the big states. [Giesbert] Mr. Secretary General, let us talk about Rwanda now. There is a dual genocide taking place there, and all that you are doing at the United Nations in the end is to reduce the contingent. [Butrus-Ghali] No, we are not reducing the contingent. Here again we are talking about a decision by the Security Council. I gave a report to the Security Council... [Giesbert, interrupting] In other words, it is not you? [Butrus-Ghali] On the contrary. [sentence as heard] I asked the Security Council to replace immediately...[pauses] To give you an example, the Belgian troops withdrew, and they have the right to withdraw, because the contribution is a voluntary one. I read a report to the Security Council, calling on it as a first option to replace the Belgian troops immediately by other troops. [Giesbert] In other words, you are the UN Secretariat General. In other words, it does not care about anyone else. [Butrus-Ghali] No, the member states made a choice, but if the United Nations is to be used as a black sheep and this helps the member nations, then highlighted whereas the successful operations are generally overlooked] [Giesbert] You still have a lot of work on your plate. For example, as an Arab, are you not afraid of what is going to happen in Algeria? [Butrus-Ghali] No, at present Algeria is a matter of an internal conflict. But if the Algerian Government should ever ask for our assistance, then we are ready to give it -- just like any other country, just as the Georgian Government asked for our help. We have observers on the spot helping to find a solution to the conflict between the Georgians and the Abkhazians. [passage omitted] [Giesbert] Thank you, Mr. Secretary General. I wish you success and a good day. [Butrus-Ghali] Thank you.
