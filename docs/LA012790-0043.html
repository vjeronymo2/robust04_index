January 27, 1990, Saturday, Home Edition

DR. JOYCE BROTHERS: SENSE OF HUMOR IS NO MEAN JOKE

DEAR DR. BROTHERS: My 19-year-old son has a wonderful sense of humor, but he sometimes uses it to attack his sister and his grandmother. He always claims he's only teasing, but his barbs hurt. Whenever I try to talk with him seriously about anything, he turns what I'm saying into a joke. Obviously, this makes serious conversation difficult. -- B.D.

DEAR B.D.: While it's true that there's usually some hostility in all humor, people who use humor to attack are like hit-and-run drivers: They escape by claiming they were only kidding and often end up blaming the victim for not having a good sense of humor. It's important to put a stop to this pattern and to point out to your son that what he's doing is cowardly and cruel.

If he's angry with his sister or his grandmother, then he should discuss the problem directly without masking his aggression in humor. Whatever it is that disturbs him in these relationships won't be solved until he uses a more honest, constructive approach.

DEAR DR. BROTHERS: My 5-year-old son has a terrible fear of dogs. My boyfriend wants to get him a puppy for his birthday because he thinks this will get him over his fear immediately. I'm not so sure. What's your view? -- R.C.J.

DEAR R.C.J.: The key to overcoming any fear such as this is to do it gradually. I believe it would be a mistake to bring a pet into your son's life until he feels more secure. This will take time. It would be fine to take him to visit a cuddly puppy and see if he'd like to try stroking the dog's fur. If he's frightened and doesn't wish to do this, his wishes should be respected. No one should make fun of him or force him. It might even be a good idea to start him out with a toy puppy, one that's soft and has a coat that feels like fur. This might be a totally non-threatening way to introduce him to what a real puppy might be like.

I'm wondering if you know the source of your son's fear of dogs. If he was attacked or frightened by a dog, encourage him to talk about this experience. Let him know that fear is normal but that it's possible to conquer our fears so that they don't cripple us or limit our freedom.

You might also take him to a children's zoo where he can see other children touching and petting the animals. Again, however, if he doesn't want to join them, don't force him. Eventually, if this is handled with sensitivity, he may welcome a new puppy, but wait until he's ready.
