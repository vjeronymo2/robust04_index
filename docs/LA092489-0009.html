September 24, 1989, Sunday, Home Edition

FOR MY BROTHER

We knelt in the recess of a hollowed rock.

We lit a driftwood pyre with your magnifying glass.

The roasting pinecone opened itself

like a mother's hand. We ate the flesh

of its seeds. Nearby

the Mediterranean ticked without stop

above the angelfish

while behind our cave, in the cliff-side house,

she placed a note in Father's typewriter:

I give up,

since you want me still

and silent.

I believe the ink unfurled, frail as a spider,

when he smoothed out the sheet.

Then he crushed it

in his palm. Yes, I know, you and I had filled

our ears, like conch shells,

with noise. But no matter:

What happened next

we could not have stopped.
