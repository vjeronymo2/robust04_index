July 8, 1990, Sunday, Home Edition

ON THE GRAND TOUR WITH CANALETTO;

CANALETTO BY KATHERINE BAETJER AND J.G. LINKS (METROPOLITAN MUSEUM OF ART, HARRY N. ABRAMS, DISTRIBUTOR: $60; 387 PP., 167 COLOR ILLUSTRATIONS, 13 BLACK-AND-WHITE; 0-8109-3155-9)

The 18th Century was the age of the Grand Tour, and one product of the new passion for travel was the proliferation of view paintings (vedute), which recorded specific and recognizable sites. Among the foremost masters of the genre was the prolific Venetian painter, Giovanni Antonio Canal, called Canaletto (1697-1768), whose work caused his first patrons to marvel: "You can see the sun shining in it."

The artist's reputation was established by his luminous views of Venice, though he was equally proficient in creating imaginary, but plausible, landscapes. The perfect congruence between his talents and contemporary taste was recognized first by Owen McSwiney and then by Joseph Smith. These enterprising gentlemen promoted Canaletto's paintings among British collectors, who cherished these dazzling mementos of the most opulent city on the Grand Tour.

In 1746, the artist traveled to England where he remained for about 10 years. He expanded his repertoire to include portraits of London and the estates of the aristocrats who were his most consistent patrons. His sketchbooks allowed him to paint Venetian scenes while in England and to paint British scenes when he returned to his homeland.

Today, his pictures are generally appreciated not only for their intrinsic charm but also as records of England before London cannibalized its countryside, and as tantalizing visions that recall the splendors of Venice prior to the depredations of pollution. But as this handsome book effectively demonstrates, Canaletto's paintings transcend their utility as topographical records. Their beauty reflects the vitality and geometric clarity of the artist's imagination.

"Canaletto" is the catalogue for the exhibition organized by the author, a curator at New York's Metropolitan Museum of Art, in collaboration with the eminent British scholar, J. G. Links. Because Canaletto's oeuvre has been authoritatively established in W. G. Constable's catalogue raisonne (revised by Links in 1989), the authors were not concerned with questions of attribution or with the need to distinguish his work from that of other view painters, as in Vittorio Moschini's 1954 monograph. Rather, they address issues raised as early as 1771, when Antonio Zanetti the Younger assessed the artist's accomplishment: "Canaletto combined nature and artistic license with such skill that his works appear to be absolutely accurate." But the Venetian connoisseur emphasized that this is an illusion achieved by disciplined artistry.

Katherine Baetjer and Links bypass the beguiling subjects that make Canaletto's paintings so deceptively accessible and explore the intellectually rigorous structure and meticulous craftsmanship that underlie their capacity to fascinate.

In support of this enterprise, the authors offer an impeccable selection of acknowledged masterpieces. The 85 paintings and 42 drawings all are reproduced in beautiful color plates, which make the volume a visual delight. Detailed catalogue entries provide information on the patrons and provenance of each object and on the history of the landmarks, noting especially the artist's revisions of visible reality.

Five introductory essays discuss Canaletto's reputation and analyze the methods by which he consistently engages our imagination. Links incorporates a lifetime of study in a biographical sketch that reveals much about Canaletto's relations with his patrons.

The essay by Michael Levey, former director of the National Gallery, London, emphasizes the imaginative artistry underlying the "powerfully factual air" and "pattern-making precision" that constitute the hallmarks of Canaletto's style. Levey characterizes Canaletto as an idealizing rather than as a representational painter, arguing that he consistently "asserted his artistic right to modify" the facts of visual reality in the interest of creating a picture while retaining "the general air of verisimilitude in the finished work." Furthermore, the organization of actual and invented details is entirely "subordinated to the major concern with coherent composition." Topographical facts are manipulated as part of the comprehensive reordering that is essential to the harmonious reality created within each picture.

The insights offered by Viola Pemberton-Pigott, senior restorer for the Royal Collection, derive from her microscopic scrutiny of individual paintings. She draws attention to the mechanical devices that Canaletto employed to achieve an illusion of crystalline perfection and describes his technique of "sculpting" in paint to enhance the naturalistic appearance of surfaces. Most surprising is her observation that the brilliant luminosity and coloristic effects for which Canaletto is famous are actually achieved with a very limited palette.

Surveying his career, the conservator confirms Levey's remarks regarding Canaletto's Cezanne-like devotion to the mastery of composition. She concludes that Canaletto "deliberately rejected the brand of realism that had characterized his early painting. He passed from the meticulous observation of every detail to exploring the scope of composition within color, pattern and texture, and the extreme stylization of his later works became a further stage of Canaletto's unflagging creativity."

Altogether this is a wonderful book. The texts complement the multitude of illustrations, enhancing our appreciation for the sparkling pictures by revealing the sophisticated artistry Canaletto carefully veiled.

My only quibble is that throughout there are references to prints by and after Canaletto, and some effort should have been made to include relevant examples. Otherwise, this substantial volume admirably serves the artist it celebrates.
