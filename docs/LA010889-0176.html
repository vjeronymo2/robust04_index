January 8, 1989, Sunday, Home Edition

'YELLOWSTONE' THE NOVEL

I am disappointed in Michael J. Carroll's review (Book Review, Nov. 20) of my novel, "The Yellowstone."

Carroll doesn't take care of the first piece of business of any review, to report to the reader the basic facts about the book. So here they are.

"The Yellowstone": The first of a series called Rivers West, is a generational novel set in the Yellowstone country about the trader Mac Maclean and his family of Indian wives and half-breed children. In the first part, at the end of the fur trade era, Mac tries to establish a life for himself among the Cheyennes. A generation later, the family struggles to keep its native life way in the face of the Montana gold rush. The book has a bizarre cast, from idiosyncratic mountain men to a Scots lord to a Cheyenne male who lives his life as a female to a Jewish peddler, of all things, who traipses the Great Plains pulling a handcart full of sewing gear.

Aside from underreporting, Carroll does some misreporting: He invents a rape that isn't there, and calls my book a frontier novel when it's set a thousand miles beyond the frontier. He also chastises me for my descriptions of the Yellowstone country. But those descriptions are from the point of view of the central character, Mac, so the language is not mine but his.

I'll grant Carroll whatever opinion of my novel he likes, but not his critical ineptitude. If I'm going to get knocked, I want to get knocked by somebody good.

WINFRED BLEVINS

JACKSON, WYO.
