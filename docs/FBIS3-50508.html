Language: Spanish Article Type:BFN [Text] La Paz, 30 Dec (EFE) -- Bolivian President Gonzalo Sanchez de Lozada today announced that 1994 will be the year of great structural changes, when the traditional "come back tomorrow" of public administration will become a thing of the past. Sanchez de Lozada today, Thursday, met with the local press at a rural spa in Cochabamba, where he is resting for a few days to "recharge his batteries" before facing the challenge of the change beginning with the preparation of the nation's general budget. The president explained that the budget proposal he will send to congress will be accompanied by several draft laws "which will give meaning to the budget" and which will implement the structural changes. "The first drafts show that more than half of the budget will be earmarked for the social area," Sanchez de Lozada said, because "great structural changes must be made to cover the growing social needs, to serve as the basis of the coming year: capitalization, educational reform, law against corruption, people's participation, and simplification of bureaucracy." The 63-year-old Bolivian president said that for 1994 there will be an increase [in salaries] of 7.5 percent in order to maintain the purchasing power of the salary and that will be equal to the inflation rate calculated for this year. "If we begin with greater increases, we will have higher inflation, greater deficit, and a lower level of economic growth," Sanchez de Lozada said, and asked whether it was worth having considerable increases, which would later be overcome by the increases in prices. He ruled out the reported increase in fuel prices which prompted the increase in prices and hoarding of other products, and added that he will adopt other measures to improve income, such as reducing the tax rates and increasing the tax base. Sanchez de Lozada said that a system to simplify state administration transactions will be implemented next year, thus seeking "to eliminate the torment the citizens" must go through when they are involved in these transactions and to eliminate from public offices the "come back tomorrow." He ruled out any change in his cabinet. "It was so difficult for me to organize it," he said. He added that if it does not yield the expected results in the future, there might be some changes. He compared the structure of the Council of Ministers he inherited from the previous government with a "Model-T Ford with which we could not win a formula one race." He added: "I built a race car that could not start because it was not well tuned." Sanchez de Lozada was optimistic over the state of his "race car" although he admitted that "if we begin to run strong and if some part breaks down we will have to fix it quickly" to meet the expectations of the Bolivians who want to see the government working. The Bolivian president will return from Cochabamba, 383 kilometers east of La Paz, next week.
