May 1, 1990, Tuesday, Home Edition

SPECIAL SCREENINGS: UCLA FILM ARCHIVES OFFERS GLIMPSE OF COLOMBIAN CINEMA

"Colombian Film Today: From Magic to Realism," composed of a dozen films that the UCLA Film Archive is presenting in Melnitz Theater today through May 15, introduces a national cinema all but unknown in the United States.

By chance, the three films available for preview focus on men who may not be what they seem. The first of the three to screen (on Tuesday), Mario Ribero's "Ambassador From India" (1986), is an amusing, lighthearted comedy loosely inspired by Gogol's "Inspector General." Hugo Gomez stars as an amiable ne'er-do-well who passes himself off as none other than India's ambassador to Colombia traveling incognito. Arriving by bus at a small Colombian town, Gomez finds himself treated with such pomp and ceremony that even the British royals or Donald Trump would find it excessive. Indeed, Gomez, who is a sly comedian, finds himself caught up in a nonstop succession of entertainments as tedious and pretentious as those Imelda Marcos used to stage. As funny and likable as "Ambassador From India" is, it is somewhat meandering, and its satire is broad and affectionate rather than sharp and reflective.

You may at first feel that Francisco Norden's relentlessly somber 1984 "A Man of Principle" (Saturday) requires considerable familiarity with Colombian political history. While that certainly wouldn't hurt, the film soon becomes a clear-cut study of a rigid family man (Frank Ramirez) who, while struggling to keep his position on the lower rungs of the middle-class, emerges as an iron-willed political terrorist. The film, set in the late '40s, is doggedly austere and tedious.

Genuinely unpredictable, Pepe Sanchez's seemingly artless 1985 "Little San Antonio" (Sunday) is set at the turn-of-the-century. A handsome, impoverished country youth (Carlos Jaramillo) charms a succession of women, first in his home village and later in town, into backing his education at a seminary, where he is to study for the priesthood. Sanchez evokes with both humor and compassion a time when ladies of refinement but straitened circumstances had little recourse but to take in boarders and spend their days in tedious daily religious rituals and considerable indolence -- a perfect atmosphere for the repression of sexuality. Never mind that "Little San Antonio" is marred by a truly terrible jangly score -- it is the rare film in which a protracted buildup ends with a dynamite payoff.

A fresh print of Stanley Donen's glorious 1957 "Funny Face" closes AFI Film Fest Thursday and then opens a one-week revival at the AMC Century 14. Fred Astaire stars as a Richard Avedon-ish fashion photographer and Audrey Hepburn is a mousy Greenwich Village bluestocking transformed into a radiant model. Featuring Gershwin standards, "Funny Face" is even more enjoyable than when it was new; they don't make witty, high-style sophisticated romantic musicals like this anymore.

Meanwhile, a Norma Shearer retrospective gets underway at LACMA Friday at 1 p.m. and again at 8 p.m. with "Marie Antoinette" (1938), a landmark costume pictures in which Shearer nevertheless gives a remarkably shaded portrayal of a frustrated, frivolous but finally gallant and brave queen.
