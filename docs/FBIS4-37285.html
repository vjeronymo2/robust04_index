BFN [Text] Khartoum, 17 June (SUNA) -- U.S. President Bill Clinton's envoy, Melissa Wells, will head for Washington to submit her report to the U.S. Administration on the talks she held with President Lieutenant General al-Bashir and the heads of the four Intergovernmental Authority on Drought and Development states, which concentrated on the issue of peace and humanitarian affairs. The U.S. envoy left Sudan on 15 June for Nairobi to meet with rebel leaders, thereby completing her report, which will embrace the different viewpoints on the issues of relief and peace. This was stated in Sudan by Mityang Malwal, the director of the peace department in the Ministry of Foreign Affairs, speaking in today's edition of AL INQADH AL-WATANI. He said the U.S. envoy's program included another mission on the issue of relief, which would take her to Geneva to hold talks with international donor agencies. His excellency the ambassador said that he accompanied the U.S. envoy to Kadugli in southern Kurdufan state, and to Wau in the western state of Bahr al-Ghazal, where they toured refugee camps and learned about different relief organizations' activities. His excellency said the situation was stable, and that relief work and services to refugees were continuing. He said the envoy listened to a report from the Catholic archbishop in Wau, workers from the Islamic Call organization, and the Muwaffaq Charitable Organization, which he said confirmed that there was a great degree of peaceful coexistence between Muslims and Christians. Mr. Mityang reiterated the government's commitment to the passage of relief supplies, agreements on humanitarian activities, and the provision of food to all war victims in the south. His excellency said the government delegation was prepared for the forthcoming round of negotiations in Nairobi, and denied reports carried by western media that hopes had dimmed over Sudan's participation in the forthcoming negotiations, especially if the government recaptured Nimule. He said Sudan would participate in the negotiations even if all Sudanese territory was liberated, because peace is the principal pillar upon which stability and development rest.
