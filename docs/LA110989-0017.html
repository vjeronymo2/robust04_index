November 9, 1989, Thursday, Home Edition

...AS BUSH'S VISION DIMS;

ELECTIONS: IT WASN'T ABORTION THAT SUNK THE GOP. IT WAS THE FAILURE OF GOP LEADERS TO OFFER A VISION.

Tuesday's "off year" election was not a referendum on abortion, race or any other single issue. It was a referendum on vision -- and Republicans were defeated all over the country because they have lost theirs.

Republican inability to read the political eye chart was revealed by the party's leader at his Tuesday news conference. Speaking on the abortion issue (though the subject could as easily have been capital gains taxes), President Bush, who had made some gains among conservatives for what they had perceived as a principled pro-life stand, said, "We have room in our party for people that feel one way, pro-life or pro-choice. The Democratic Party is the same way."

The Democratic Party is not the same way. Few staunchly pro-life Democrats could be elected to anything above the level of dog catcher, and no one could hope to be nominated for President in the Democratic Party if he or she is not convincingly pro-choice.

This is what is killing Republicans from coast to coast. Voters don't want accommodaters. They want leaders with strong convictions who will tell them what is right and wrong and why. They want visionaries who will lead them toward a defined goal.

During last year's election campaign, President Bush didn't say that there was room in the Republican Party for those who thought Willie Horton ought to be out on parole, or room for flag burners along with those who would protect the national symbol. He took a stand. He had a vision. And the people followed.

Now he complains that Congress is "blocking everything I try to do." And why shouldn't they? The "Mr. Nice Guy" image may work at country clubs, but it is suicide in politics. At a prize fight the biggest cheers don't come when the combatants shake hands before the fight, but when the slugging starts.

There is only one reason for winning the presidency and it has nothing to do with promoting kindness and gentleness. It has to do with exercising power to promote the general welfare.

In order to promote the general welfare, though, one must define the general welfare. It does not excite conservative voters, who provided President Bush with his margin of victory, when they hear him say that abortion, for example, is not the most important issue "for me . . . absolutely not." There is only one reason to oppose abortion and that is because one believes it is the taking of innocent human life.

If President Bush can't get worked up over the most emotional issue our society faces, what issue will light a fire under him?

Rep. Jim Courter lost support early on in the New Jersey governor's race because he tried to accommodate the pro-choicers by softening his pro-life position. Pro-choicers already had one of their own in Rep. James Florio, so why should they vote for Courter? They didn't and neither did many pro-lifers, who stayed home and watched Courter lose by a 2-1 margin.

In Virginia, though the margin was tight, Marshall Coleman assured voters that if elected governor he would not do anything to change the abortion status quo.

This is not the kind of talk that energizes conservatives to go out and vote, and it is not the kind of talk that will produce victories for Republicans.

Republicans have a case of political cataracts. If they don't have them removed, they risk going blind and having to be led around by Democrats.
