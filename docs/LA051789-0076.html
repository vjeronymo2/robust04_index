May 17, 1989, Wednesday, Orange County Edition

SUSPECT IN SLAYING OF WIFE RETURNED TO COUNTY

On a daylong airplane flight from the Montana wilderness where he was captured, David Lee Schoenecker returned to Orange County late Tuesday to answer charges that he fatally shot his wife and plotted against more than 50 other people.

The 48-year-old Anaheim Hills chemical engineer, who waived extradition Tuesday morning before a judge in Missoula, Mont., was booked in Orange County Jail under $500,000 bail on charges that he murdered his wife, Gail Schoenecker, 40, at the couple's home on May 5.

Schoenecker, who also threatened in a letter to "punish" 54 acquaintances and relatives, authorities say, was captured last week by an Old West-style posse while trekking through deep snow in mountains along the Montana-Idaho border. Schoenecker initially refused to waive extradition but after consulting with an attorney Monday agreed to return to California voluntarily.

Schoenecker, wearing an orange jumpsuit, signed extradition papers Tuesday morning during a brief appearance before Montana State District Judge John Henson. He was subsequently placed in the custody of two Anaheim police detectives, who took him aboard an early afternoon flight from Missoula -- where Schoenecker had been detained since the weekend -- to Orange County's John Wayne Airport.

For the flight, Anaheim detectives Dave Tuttle and Joe Reiss allowed the fugitive to wear civilian clothes, although they kept his hands cuffed in front of him as they led him through passenger terminals in airports in Missoula and Salt Lake City, where they had to change planes.

Through it all, Schoenecker -- wearing a blue-plaid shirt, gray dress slacks and loafers -- appeared calm and unemotional.

On the flight between Salt Lake City and Orange County, the suspect immersed himself in a newspaper, leafing through page after page. Detective Reiss, who sat next to Schoenecker on the last leg of the flight, uncuffed the suspect's hands so he could eat a snack. Schoenecker said little during the flight, exchanging some small talk with the detectives.

Fellow passengers were apparently unaware that a fugitive was in their midst, as the detectives arranged to take Schoenecker aboard before others took their seats. And the detectives took great care to seat him away from other people while awaiting flight departures in the airports. Reiss and Tuttle had asked a Times' reporter and photographer aboard the flight not to approach them on the jet.

"This is an extradition, and we don't want to upset the other passengers," Tuttle had said in Missoula.

The anonymity faded, however, as soon as the Skywest jet touched down at John Wayne Airport about 7:15 p.m. Awaiting Schoenecker's arrival were a throng of news reporters and photographers. He repeatedly ducked and turned his head to avoid having his picture taken.

Detectives Reiss and Tuttle, joined by a third Anaheim police officer waiting at the airport, hustled Schoenecker off to a waiting car and drove him to Orange County Jail, where he now awaits an as-yet unscheduled arraignment.
