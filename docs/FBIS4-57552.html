BFN [Editorial Report] The following is a compilation of reports on economic activities monitored through 19 May. The National Statistics Institute has reported that the Consumer Price Index increased by 3.52 percent in April. The accrued inflation in the first quarter of the year added up to 11.65 percent as compared with 16.1 percent in the corresponding period in 1993. Food and beverages, transportation, and communications registered the highest price increases. (Montevideo EL OBSERVADOR ECONOMICO in Spanish 3 May 94 p 8) The trade deficit continued its upward trend in February, reaching $51.8 million. Exports grew by 6.5 percent while imports increased by nearly 17 percent in the first two months of 1994, resulting in a $122.3 million deficit. The reasons behind the trade deficit are: lack of competitiveness, the increase in salaries, the cost of living, the decline in the price of some products on the international market, and the reduction of tariffs that spurred purchases of imported goods. (Montevideo EL OBSERVADOR ECONOMICO in Spanish 4 May 94 p 9) The Central Bank's net international reserves increased by $2.3 million in April, totaling $1.2587 billion. International reserves have increased by $56.8 million so far this year. The monetary base shrunk by $201.5 million last month due to a $208.8 million drop in the net domestic credit granted by the Central Bank. (Montevideo EL PAIS in Spanish 5 May 94 Market Indicators Section p 1) Despite the fact that Uruguayan exports to Common Market of the South, Mercosur, member countries increased by 25.1 percent in the first two months of the year, its trade deficit with these countries added up to $78.7 million, thus registering a 43.1 percent increase compared with the corresponding period in 1993. There is a trade deficit of $48.3 million with Brazil and a deficit of $32.7 million with Argentina, while the surplus with Paraguay amounts to $2.3 million. (Montevideo EL PAIS in Spanish 5 May 94 Market Indicators Section p 1) According to figures released by the Central Bank, Uruguay grew by an average 3.3 percent per annum in the four years of the Blanco Party administration. (Montevideo EL OBSERVADOR ECONOMICO in Spanish 6 May 94 p 10) The State Board of Sanitation chairman has reported that public water rates increased by 11 percent as of 1 May. (Montevideo EL OBSERVADOR ECONOMICO in Spanish 6 May 94 p 10) The Central Bank has reported that the public sector--including government-owned enterprises, town halls, and other decentralized agencies--registered a $9.1 million deficit in January 1994 despite the central government's $6.9 million surplus. The financial position of the public sector has been deteriorating since last year, when after recording a $51 million surplus in the first quarter, it showed a deficit amounting to 1.5 percent of GDP by the end of the year. (Montevideo EL OBSERVADOR ECONOMICO in Spanish 9 May 94 p 11) According to data released by the Central Bank, private consumption in Uruguay grew by 25.7 percent from 1990 to 1993. (Montevideo EL OBSERVADOR ECONOMICO in Spanish 10 May 94 p 8) The Central Bank reported on 9 May that the central government registered a $12.6 million surplus in March. The accrued government surplus in the first quarter of the year totals $29.2 million. Despite these positive economic indicators, the government's accounts continued to deteriorate in the first quarter of the year, as revenues grew by 1.9 percent while expenditures increased by 11.6 percent. (Montevideo EL OBSERVADOR ECONOMICO in Spanish 10 May 94 p 8) Basing its information on data released by the Central Bank and the IMF, the Finance Ministry disclosed on 10 May that imports of capital goods have grown by 121 percent over the last four years. Purchases of capital goods, however, declined by 2.7 percent in the first two months of the year as compared with the corresponding period in 1993. (Montevideo EL OBSERVADOR ECONOMICO in Spanish 11 May 94 p 9) Uruguay's net external debt has dropped by 27 percent over the last four years due to a 47 percent increase in net international reserves. The per capita debt has declined from $1,021 to $753 over the last four years. Nevertheless, the total gross external debt has increased by 12.9 percent in the period under consideration. It amounted to $7.89 billion at the end of 1993, thus representing 57 percent of GDP. The net external debt totals $2.373 billion. (Montevideo EL OBSERVADOR ECONOMICO in Spanish 11 May 94 p 9) The Central Bank has reported that investments in fixed capital grew by 11.8 percent in 1993 while private consumption increased by 4 percent. This led to a 5.5 percent expansion in domestic demand. (Montevideo BUSQUEDA in Spanish 12 May 94 p 26)
