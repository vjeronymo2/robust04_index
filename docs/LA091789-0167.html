September 17, 1989, Sunday, San Diego County Edition

UCLA CATCHES SAN DIEGO STATE;

BRUINS WIN, 28-25, ON SNEAK BY JOHNSON WITH 2:39 LEFT

No. 20 UCLA needed a one-yard touchdown sneak by quarterback Bret Johnson with 2:39 to play to score a 28-25 comeback victory over winless San Diego State Saturday night before 31,639 at San Diego Jack Murphy Stadium.

Johnson's touchdown came a little more than four minutes after running back Darrin Wagner's 31-yard touchdown run had given the Aztecs (0-2) a 25-21 lead.

The touchdown capped a nine-play, 57-yard drive that allowed the Bruins (1-1) to avoid their first loss ever in 13 games against the Aztecs.

"I'm proud of these kids," said SDSU Coach Al Luginbill, "and tonight everybody saw why. I see it in practice every day."

Said SDSU quarterback Dan McGwire: "This offense can explode any time. It's volatile, especially the running game. We just fell short tonight. These games are hard; we're trying to keep our heads high."

If there were any question that this would be some UCLA team other the one that lost to Tennessee, 24-6, in its opener the week before, it was dispelled early.

All it took was a 73-yard run for a touchdown on a fake punt by senior nickel back John Wesselman to give the Aztecs a 7-0 lead with 3:49 left in the first quarter.

It was the first time since this 67-year-old series was resumed in 1984 that San Diego State led in a game. The run was a warning that this game would be closer to UCLA 18-15 victory in 1984 than than the lopsided UCLA victories of the next four years.

In the early going, the Bruins looked like anything but the top 20 team they are. They struggled to take a 14-10 halftime lead against a San Diego State team that had lost at Air Force Sept. 2 in its opener, 52-36.

This also was the same Aztec program, though not the same team, that the Bruins had beaten the past three years by a combined score of 151-34. But regardless of improvement the Aztecs showed this time, the Bruins demonstrated many of their problems were their own doing.

They again had difficulty defensively. They yielded 234 yards to the Aztecs in the first half; that was as many as the Bruins gave up to the Aztecs in their entire 59-6 victory in the Rose Bowl last year. And unlike past years when they were able to physically overmatch the Aztecs on both lines, San Diego State held its own up front.

This appeared to be a much more brazen Aztec team than the Bruins were used to playing. Luginbill, was not afraid to gamble on big plays.

He not only successfully used the fake punt, but risked a throw to the goal line late in second quarter on fourth-and-one from the UCLA 21. That pass by McGwire was intercepted by strong safety Matt Darby, but the message again was clear: This San Diego State team was not about to roll over.

The Bruins appeared ready to exert their traditional dominance of the Aztecs midway through the first quarter, starting a long drive that took them deep into San Diego State territory. But the drive ended abruptly with an interception and the Aztecs then turned to a touch of trickery.

UCLA had moved to the Aztec 27 when Johnson attempted to complete a pass to tailback Brian Brown. But the ball was high and Brown could only tip it over his head and into the hands of San Diego State cornerback Clark Moses at the Aztec 17.

Moses returned the ball five yards to the San Diego State 22. The drive stalled after three plays netted five yards, and the Aztecs went into punt formation. But instead of snapping the ball to punter Joe Santos, the ball was hiked to the short man, Wesselman.

Wesselman, a senior from West Torrance High School, took the snap and dashed around the right side untouched. He eluded Shawn Wills, who had been set up as the return man, at the UCLA 45 and then outraced strong safety Mark McGill to the end zone.

"We worked very hard on our kicking game," Luginbill said. "I told me club if we work on something very hard, we're going to use it in the game."

The Bruins wasted little time responding. They went on their longest drive, moving 79 yards in eight plays to tie the score at 7-7 on a 38-yard pass to flanker Scott Miller from Johnson with 12 seconds left in the first quarter.

The Bruins went ahead 14-7 on their next possession. This time they went 71 yards in 10 plays, the drive ending on a one-yard dive by freshman tailback Kevin Williams.

The touchdown drive put the Bruins ahead for the first time, but it had a price. Wills sprained his right ankle midway through the drive and did not return. Wills, who led the Bruins with 62 yards on 11 carries against Tennessee, had 29 yards on four carries against the Aztecs. His departure left Brown to carry the rushing load.

Brown finished with 99 yards on 32 carries.

The Aztecs tried their second gamble on the next series. Faced with a fourth-and-one at the UCLA 21, the Aztecs not only elected to go for a first down rather than attempt would have been a 38-yard field goal but tried to gain the yardage through the air.

McGwire attempted a pass to Jimmy Raye near the left front of the end zone, but Darby stepped in to make the interception.

But the Bruins could do nothing with that bit of good fortune and the Aztecs took over at their 40 with 2:56 left in the half after a 52-yard punt by Kirk Maggio.

The Aztecs went into their two-minute offense, driving for a fourth-and-one to the UCLA 9 with 10 seconds left. The Aztecs used their last time out to set up for a 26-yard field goal by Andy Trakas that cut the lead to 14-10.

The Bruins had a chance in the third quarter to again go ahead by a touchdown, but Alfredo Velasco's 42-yard field goal attempt with 10:54 left was low and was blocked at the line by lineman Brad Burton.

The third quarter ended scoreless, but that was only because the clock ran out two plays before the Bruins could add their third touchdown.

But like most else in the game, this did not come easily either for the Bruins. They needed two tries from less than a yard away before Williams finally snuck around the left side for his second touchdown and a 21-10 lead at 14:18.

The first attempt ended with fullback Maury Toy being stopped as he attempted to crash the middle of the line. The second was successful but only because of the persistence of Williams, a freshman from Spring, Tex. He tried the left side, and when he found no opening, he kept sliding farther outside, eventually getting by two Aztec defenders and into the end zone.

But the Aztecs would not go away. They drove 74 yards on 10 plays on their next possession, which ended when Wagner slipped around the right side for a one-yard touchdown run with 11:24 to play.

The gambler in Luginbill came out again on the conversion, but not before a momentary mix-up. Luginbill apparently wanted to try for a two-point conversion but before the team could get the word, Trakas attempted a conversion kick while McGwire was running on the field and the Aztecs were called for too many men on the field. But the penalty turned out to be the break the Aztecs needed.

That gave them a second chance to get McGwire on the field. And this time he completed the two-point pass to Raye in the end zone. That brought the Aztecs to within 21-18 where a field goal could have given them a tie and a moral victory.

But safety Morey Paul and Wagner would combine a few minutes later to give them more than that.

On the third play after Wagner's touchdown, Paul intercepted the second Johnson pass of the game at the San Diego State 47.

At first, it appeared as if the Aztecs would not benefit as a holding penalty and sack forced them into a third-and-31 at their 27. But McGwire connected on a 30-yard pass to wide receiver Dennis Arey on third down. And then another Aztec gamble worked when McGwire snuck over for a first down on a fourth-and-one to keep the drived alive at the UCLA 42.

Three plays later, Wagner dashed down the right side for his second touchdown -- a 31-yarder that included a move that left free safety Eric Turner on his rump.

That gave the Aztecs a 25-21 lead and forced the final Bruin rally.
