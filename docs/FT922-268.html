920629 FT 29 JUN 92 / Recession overturns southern and regional divide THE lingering recession has overturned the caricatured relationship between Britain's southern economic core and its regional periphery. The southern regions have fared badly while Scotland and Northern Ireland have escaped relatively unscathed. Yet the slow spread of the recession from services into manufacturing means the west Midlands has suffered the sharpest fall in output over the last eight quarters, according to Business Strategies, the regional consultancy. The service and consumer-oriented recession was largely a punishment for the excessive growth rates enjoyed by London and the south-east in the latter half of the 1980s. Rapid southern growth and the lingering side effects of the last, northern-biased recession, opened wide north-south gaps in unemployment rates, house prices and incomes per head. But Britain's economic boom proved unsustainable and the combination of heavy debts, high real interest rates and falling house prices affected the spending power of southern consumers worse than less indebted consumers elsewhere. Across a wide range of economic indicators - unemployment, house prices, bankruptcies - the southern regions have fared worst. The south-east may still have a lower unemployment rate than the average, but the difference has closed to 0.5 percentage points from 1.5 percentage points when the recession started, and 2.8 percentage points in 1987. The south's hangover is likely to impede economic recovery this year. National Westminster Bank expects a sluggish recovery to be led by Wales, the north of England and Scotland. The wealth and economic power of southern Britain means that those regions are expected to experience above-average growth rates when the economy shakes off its lethargy.
