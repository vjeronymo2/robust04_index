January 2, 1990, Tuesday, Home Edition

JUSTICE FOR NORIEGA ISN'T IN THE CARDS;

LAW: OLLIE NORTH'S CLOAK OF DISPENSATIONS WOULD FIT THE GENERAL JUST AS WELL. THE GOVERNMENT CAN'T AFFORD TO TRY HIM -- OR TO LET HIM FREE.

The one country we can be certain Manuel Noriega will not ask to be sent to is Romania. The new revolutionary government's concept of justice -- secret "trial," no appeal, quick execution -- is unlikely to appeal to Noriega's sense of survival.

Like many tyrants of the past, Noriega will be seeking a peaceful and luxurious retirement. And if history is a guide to the future, he will find some fellow dictator willing to extend him a professional courtesy. Among the modern precedents is the hospitality extended to mass murderer Idi Amin by Saudi Arabia, and the welcome mat rolled out for Nazi war criminals by Paraguay and Syria. Even civilized countries like the United States and France have given sanctuary to despots -- like the Shah of Iran, Ferdinand Marcos and Jean-Claude "Baby Doc" Duvalier, wanted for trial in their own countries.

Administration sources, pointing to Noriega's indictment on drug charges, have tried to distinguish the case by arguing that he is "a common criminal." But the Shah, Marcos and Duvalier were also regarded as criminals by their own people.

The fact that we sent in the U.S. Army to capture Noriega weakens our claim that he is a "common criminal" as distinguished from a political opponent. He may well be both. The U.S. government certainly did not regard him as a common criminal when our CIA worked hand-in-hand with him over a considerable number of years. We treated him as the powerful political figure he was. He may have been an s.o.b., but as Franklin Roosevelt once said of another U.S.-supported Central American dictator: "At least he's our s.o.b."

Manuel Noriega is no longer our s.o.b., but he apparently knows a lot about other s.o.b.'s with whom we are, and have been, dealing. He also knows about our CIA operations throughout Central and South America. It is for this reason that knowledgeable sources are doubting that President Bush really wants him to be tried in an American court. Under the rules of evidence, Noriega's lawyers could demand disclosure of highly embarrassing government files. If our intelligence services refuse to produce those files, the charges against Noriega might have to be dropped, as several charges were dropped against Oliver North, John Poindexter and others.

The difference is that the Bush Administration and the Central Intelligence Agency wanted the charges dropped against North and Poindexter. They used the cloak of national security as an excuse for helping their friends. Noriega is nobody's friend. The last thing the Administration wants now is a trial in an American court that results in a dismissal of charges against him. Our legal system is based on precedents. The crowning irony would be for the precedent established in the case of hero Ollie North to be used to free villain Manuel Noriega.

There were many officials in Washington who were hoping that Noriega would suffer the fate of Nicolae Ceausescu, the Romanian dictator executed by his own army, or of Jose Gonzalo Rodriguez Gacha, the Medellin drug boss killed in a shootout.

Short of Noriega being killed, the next best outcome for the Administration may well be the one that is currently playing itself out. The problem is now squarely in the hands of the Vatican. No one expects us to use gunboat diplomacy on the nunciature. Our hands are tied. We did the best we could. Or did we? Is it possible that Noriega's all-too-obvious Vatican option did not occur to a single Washington official? Could we not have surrounded the nunciature to prevent Noriega from sneaking in? Or did we really close our eyes to this possibility?

It's not too late to get our hands on the fugitive who has continued to make a fool of us. We can simply wait him out. Although international law may forbid us from physically entering the nunciature and grabbing Noriega, he remains subject to capture as soon as he tries to leave. Cars, helicopters and airplanes -- even those bearing the seal of the Vatican -- are not the "territory" of the Vatican as the nunciature is. If the Vatican were to decide to allow Noriega to seek asylum in Cuba or some other country, we need not allow him safe passage.

Remember that not so long ago, we forcibly stopped an Egyptian aircraft carrying terrorists and made it land in Italy, where the terrorists were apprehended. We could do the same with Noriega. But there are many who believe that we will not do everything in our legal, diplomatic and military power to see to it that Noriega is brought to trial in a America. courtroom.

As a Noriega lawyer put it to me: "They know that the case of the United States vs. Manuel Noriega would really turn out to be the case of Manuel Noriega vs. the CIA, Ronald Reagan and George Bush."
