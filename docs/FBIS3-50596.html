Article Type:BFN [Editorial Report] The following is a compilation of reports on Guatemala economic developments monitored through 3 January. Source is cited in parentheses following each item. Finance Minister Richard Aitkenhead has announced the government has felt it advisable to allow competition in some services provided by state enterprises such as INDE (National Institute of Electrification) and Guatel (Guatemalan Telecommunications Enterprise) to make these enterprises more operational and profitable. He added this is the first step before privatization. (Guatemala City PRENSA LIBRE in Spanish 28 Dec 93 p 53) William Stixrud, president of the National Coffee Association (Asociacion Nacional del Cafe) has said that approximately 389,560 quintals of coffee, worth over $24 million, were exported during the 1992-93 harvest. He added that Guatemala continues to withhold 20 percent of its coffee exports. (Guatemala City PRENSA LIBRE in Spanish 29 Dec 93 p 62) As of 30 November, approximately 119,000 export permits, totaling $1.8128 billion, had been issued. Of this amount, $1.2395 billion were nontraditional exports. With regard to traditional export values, coffee rose $17 million, cotton dropped $6 million, sugar rose $8 million, meat rose $3.4 million. During 1992-93, exports of traditional products rose $92 million in markets outside Central America and $24 million in the regional market. (Guatemala City PRENSA LIBRE in Spanish 30 Dec 93 p 63) Guatemala Bank President Willy Zapata said on 30 December that the exchange rate fell from 5.25 at the beginning of 1993 to 5.77 percent on 29 December, a 9.9 percent depreciation. Zapata also said the money supply for 1993 grew 11.7 percent, or 348.7 million quetzales. He added that in 1994 the bank will seek to induce an interest rate reduction so as to generate increased investment. (Guatemala City PRENSA LIBRE in Spanish 31 Dec 93 p 46)
