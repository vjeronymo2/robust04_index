January 8, 1989, Sunday, Valley Edition

L.A. WORKS TO SAVE TROUBLESOME TREES IN WOODLAND HILLS

In a switch from what happened the last time tree roots damaged a street in Woodland Hills, officials are taking concrete steps to save hundreds of trees that have buckled pavement in the community's oldest neighborhood.

Los Angeles city street maintenance crews are cutting out and replacing damaged curbs and gutters -- not cutting down the 65-year-old eucalyptus and pepper trees whose roots have caused cracks and buckling on 10 streets.

The streets are in Woodland Hills' original 1923 Giraud subdivision south of Ventura Boulevard. The neighborhood is half a mile from where state highway workers caused a furor eight months ago by chopping down trees that were beginning to crack sidewalks.

State workers removed 25 ash trees along Topanga Canyon Boulevard near Warner Center Park in May before a public outcry forced them to stop. Department of Transportation officials later apologized and told nearby homeowners that the trees could be replaced.

But Caltrans administrators continue to back their policy of removing troublesome trees.

Cannot Afford Repairs

In subsequent meetings with Woodland Hills residents, state officials -- who have jurisdiction over Topanga Canyon Boulevard because it is a state highway -- said they cannot afford to make repeated repairs to tree-damaged pavement.

James L. McCullough, the senior Caltrans maintenance engineer who authorized the tree removal in May, said other trees along Topanga Canyon Boulevard are beginning to damage pavement. He said the state will give adjacent property owners a chance to save the trees by assuming responsibility for them before Caltrans resorts to the chain saw.

"It is not a dislike for trees that makes us do this," McCullough said. He added that Caltrans' limited highway-repair budget precludes spending money on trees.

City street maintenance administrators said their policy is different.

"We do whatever we can to save trees," said Bob Kennedy, street tree superintendent for the city's Bureau of Street Maintenance. "It's easy to cut the tree down and eliminate the problem, but we don't take that posture."

In Woodland Hills, city tree experts have carefully cut away roots that are damaging the pavement. Then they have pruned the tallest trees to keep them from toppling over because of a lack of root support.

Kennedy said the city in recent years has increased inspections of the 680,000 trees that line Los Angeles' streets. It has also increased tree trimming in hopes of keeping top-heavy trees from blowing over in storms.

In 1983, when the city scheduled pruning of all street trees over a 16-year cycle, about 300 trees were uprooted by one storm, Kennedy said Friday. The city now tries to trim all trees every six years or so. As a result, only 20 street trees were lost when a major windstorm struck Los Angeles two months ago, he said.

Dennis Weber, street maintenance supervisor in charge of the Woodland Hills repair project, said workers are installing new gutters along 2.3 miles of roadway bordered by Providencia and Crespi streets and Don Pio and San Feliciano drives.

Each curb-side tree in the area is being inspected by city arborists after the old gutters, curbing and pavement have been removed and the roots have been exposed, he said.

Only two aging eucalyptus trees have been found to have rotted roots that put them in danger of falling over after the root system had been trimmed to make way for the new paving. After city workers consulted homeowners near the trees, officials said, the trees were reluctantly removed.

"I'm happy to see trees saved," Weber said Friday. "This place would look kind of desolate without them."

Replacing Curbing

Although new concrete gutters are being installed along all 10 streets, only the most seriously damaged curbing -- sections with broken concrete that could cause tire blowouts -- is being replaced, he said.

The project is costing about $440,000, said Neil Spiva, chief projects coordinator for the Bureau of Street Maintenance.

The work will take another two months to finish. But it has already drawn praise from Woodland Hills residents who had decried May's tree-chopping by Caltrans.

"I'd rather save the trees than the sidewalks," said Doug McClung, who is one of the neighborhood's oldest residents. He built his home on Martinez Street in 1948, when there were only a few houses in the Giraud subdivision.

Giraud's eucalyptus and peppertrees were mature giants even then, McClung said. By the '40s, Giraud's original development company had gone broke and the name of the entire 4,000-resident community had been changed from Giraud to Woodland Hills because of the trees.

McClung said he has teased the city street repair crew that is working in his neighborhood by showing them a 1929 photo that depicts nearby Dumetz Road being graded by workers using a team of four mules.

Gordon Murley, president of the Woodland Hills Homeowners Organization, also applauded the city's tree-saving efforts. He said the city's street-tree maintenance staff has begun to work with homeowners after being involved in a tree-pruning controversy a few years ago.

"I'd have to say that Mr. Kennedy is less a bureaucrat and more sympathetic than most people in the city we deal with," said Murley, who bitterly clashed with Caltrans over the Topanga Canyon Boulevard chopping incident. "People are finally coming to realize that to denude a neighborhood has no value."
