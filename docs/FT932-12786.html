930427 FT 27 APR 93 / Parliament and Politics: Lilley pressed over benefits policy PROPOSALS aimed at focusing social security benefits increasingly on those in need were among the options being considered by the government, Mr Peter Lilley, social security secretary, told MPs yesterday. He also confirmed that during the lifetime of the present parliament the government was committed to maintaining the basic retirement pension and uprating child benefit in line with inflation. Mr Donald Dewar, the shadow social security secretary, pressed in vain for more information about the policy papers discussed at the week-end by ministers engaged in the long-term review of social security policy. Mr Lilley declined to comment on whether a decision in principle had been taken that those on middle incomes and above should be asked to opt out of the basic state retirement pension, and, ultimately, from sickness and unemployment benefit as well. Miss Ann Widdecombe, junior social secretary, attacked Mr David Winnick, Labour MP for Walsall North, for saying that the basic retirement pension was no longer safe in the government's hands. The Treasury estimates that only about 1 per cent of the additional yield from extending VAT to all fuel and power is likely to be paid by charities, Sir John Cope, the paymaster general, said yesterday. 'We expect almost all of the remaining additional yield will be in respect of private households, though there is no reliable information on the expenditure by non-private households on fuel and power.'
