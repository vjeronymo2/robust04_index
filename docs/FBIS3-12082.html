Language: Spanish Article Type:BFN [Text] Guatemala City, 29 Mar (ACAN-EFE) -- The agreement on human rights reached in Mexico by the Guatemalan Government and the guerrillas is the greatest outcome in three years of negotiations and opens the possibility for President Ramiro de Leon Carpio to meet his objective of pacifying the nation. The former human rights ombudsman -- who became Guatemala's president on 6 June 1993 in the midst of a terrible political crisis originated by ousted President Jorge Serrano's failed self-coup -- has paved the way to peace with the signing of an overall agreement on human rights with the rebels. The Government Peace Commission (Copaz) and the Guatemalan National Revolutionary Unity's (URNG) "military command" reached this agreement in Puebla (Mexico) after overcoming several obstacles throughout three years of conversations. The agreement with the rebels strengthens Ramiro de Leon Carpio's administration, which during his 10 months in power has lost credibility and the support of the diverse sectors of society that demand an end to the conflict and better living conditions. People's leaders consulted by ACAN-EFE indicated that light can now be seen at the end of the tunnel and that even though the agreement has been signed true hope lies in its fulfillment and the beginning of the end of the massacre. "The signing of the agreement is a victory for the whole community that pressured the government and the guerrillas. It is not a voluntary gift, although it fell on Ramiro de Leon Carpio," stressed Rosario Pu, leader of the Committee of Peasant Unity (CUC). Meanwhile, Rosalina Tuyuc, leader of the National Commission of Guatemalan Widows (Conavigua), told ACAN-EFE that a first step has been taken for a constitution of civilian communities [pueblos civiles] and that this agreement will stimulate and support the people. "For us the signing of this accord is very important because it opens up a space for society," she highlighted. Meanwhile, Human Rights Ombudsman Jorge Mario Garcia Laguardia said the agreement gives "a new glimmer of hope to the population" because the parties have demonstrated their democratic calling to reach an understanding. Garcia recognized that the work of Frenchman Jean Arnault was a key to reaching the accord, with which the incipient democracy will be strengthened in the medium- and long-term. For the human rights ombudsman now "it is necessary to form a national front of peace to discuss an agreement of social conditions as the only way to end the instability." Nineth Montenegro, president of the Mutual Support Group (GAM), also told ACAN-EFE that after 34 years of war the agreement represents "a significant step forward" in the attainment of peace. "I am very happy to hear there is talk of democratizing and demilitarizing the country," the human rights leader remarked. The issue of human rights was the most complex one of the 11 points on the agenda agreed upon in Mexico in 1991 by the government and the URNG under the mediation of Catholic Bishop Rodolfo Quezada Toruno. According to Quezada Toruno, who was dismissed from the negotiations by de Leon Carpio, who in turned requested the UN mediation, under his mediation the parties were nearing the signing of an accord, but the foiled coup perpetrated by Serrano on 25 May 1993 ruined all the efforts. The peace negotiations became bogged down several times during the coupist government of Serrano, and although de Leon gave new momentum to the process, it was not until last January that his representatives were able to meet for the first time with rebel leaders in Mexico. De Leon removed the main players of the government in the process and appointed Hector Rosada to head the government delegation. Rosada has been charged with exerting pressure to force Quezada Toruno's removal and dissolve the National Reconciliation Commission. The signing of the human rights accord and its international verification mark the end of a 34-year-old armed confrontation in Guatemala, the only country in Central America where tension remains. The undeclared civil war has left more than 150,000 people dead, some 250,000 widows, and 45,000 missing persons due to political reasons, as well as thousands of refugees.
