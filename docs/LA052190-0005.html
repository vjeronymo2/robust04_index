May 21, 1990, Monday, Home Edition

MAYOR'S ACTION IN SHOPLIFT CASE

As your reporters well know, many days I bite my tongue upon reading the morning's news. Much of what I read is a snippet of fact nestled in so much speculation and distortion that, even when I directly witnessed or caused the event under discussion, I don't recognize the story.

Today, I am having a tougher time than usual biting my tongue. On a Saturday afternoon in April, I received a call from one of my executive assistants, Jeff Matsui. I learned that a Japanese official was in trouble at the airport, and that the trouble had started with a claim of shoplifting. From that moment until now, I proceeded to do my job. As the ranking officer in this city with responsibility for every constituent, and with particular sensitivity to our foreign visitors, I called the airport to learn the circumstances firsthand.

I quickly learned the answers to my questions from the sergeant of the airport police. My staffer, who had gone to the airport in response to a call from a member of our sister city committee hosting the official, did too. The foreign dignitary was in custody for shoplifting. There was no accompanying disturbance. He had a lawyer representing him. The parties, the store and the visitor, were discussing how they wanted to proceed. The store authorities decided that they would drop the matter, not because of any pressure or suggestion from me or my representative, but of their own volition.

In the course of my duties, I have often been called upon to make inquiries. But in 27 years in elective office, I have never urged anyone not to prosecute a criminal case and I didn't in this instance. Should the foreign dignitary have been prosecuted? Should the police have placed the man in jail? These were not questions for me to address, and if these options are still being considered, they still are not for me. But, I'll be damned if I'll stop doing my job to make inquiries in appropriate circumstances, no matter how controversial.

So, for those of you who live, work in or visit this city, please know that if you run afoul of the law, you will have to take responsibility for the predictable consequences. But if you need me, or simply think you need me, to make inquiry about a matter, I'll be there and will not be discouraged or intimidated out of fear that some irresponsible reporter may distort the facts to suggest that my actions were improper.

TOM BRADLEY

Los Angeles Mayor
