May 20, 1989, Saturday, San Diego County Edition

DANCE REVIEW;

ANIMALS ABOUND, BUT ENSEMBLE'S INCREASING PROWESS IS EVIDENT

Although San Diego is a border town, cross-cultural exchanges between local arts organizations and their Mexican neighbors are few and far between.

A welcome exception is San Diego-based American Ballet Ensemble's ongoing relationship with Tijuana and other cities south of the border.

The school-based company, directed by former Balanchine ballerina Lynda Yourth, performs regularly for Mexican audiences and always features Mexican dancers.

When the ABE returned to Tijuana last Saturday for two concerts of "Light Classics," the program included the world premiere of Yourth's "Peter and the Wolf," a dance drama driven as much by the narration as by its famous Prokofiev score.

American Ballet Ensemble will make its debut at the Lyceum Theatre this weekend with the same program. Performances will take place at 2 and 8 p.m. today and 2 p.m. Sunday.

Although the one-act ballet was conceived for use in outreach programs around San Diego, Yourth designed an adaptation for Tijuana audiences that included a Spanish-language narrative of the fable.

The choreography was about what you might expect from a ballet "peopled" with birds, ducks, wolves and other woodland creatures. Paying short shrift to the canons of classical ballet, Yourth stressed the libretto's storybook elements in her movement motifs.

The duck (Jana Emery) waddled, the fragile-looking bird (Aura Dixon) fluttered and flew -- at least fleetingly -- the cat (Claire Taber) slinked through her feline motional patterns, and frogs (there were three of them) crouched and jumped on cue.

Only Peter, danced with athletic vigor by Christopher Mac Dougall, had a chance to shine in dance terms during this little diversion.

Judging from the program notes (which credits the dancers for contributing to the choreography), and the predictable nature of the moves, the animal images must have been hatched in improvisation sessions.

Visually, the ballet was a lot more interesting. Clark Mires' costumes, particularly the masks, were delightful. Biff Baker's forest backdrop was effective in evoking the sylvan setting, although it seemed all too realistic to complement the stylized tree that served as the anchor for the action.

Yourth added a more upbeat ending to the old tale by shipping the trussed-up wolf off to the zoo for the denouement -- an appropriate bit of poetic license by the storyteller, considering San Diego is home to one of the world's most famous zoos.

John Williams' theme from the movie "Jaws" provided the eerie accompaniment to another storybook ballet on the program, Clarissa Boeriu's "The Fisherman and the Crab." This very contemporary ballet may have taken its thematic content from the fairy-tales, but that was all.

A willowy-slim Aura Dixon and her bare-chested partner, Peter Schetters, paired off in a series of complex couplings that vaguely suggested the conflict between the characters. Although Dixon is only 15 years old, she held her own very nicely with the veteran danseur. Schetters is a former member of the Dutch National Ballet.

The last two pieces on the program gave the dancers (there are 30 in the ensemble now) better opportunities to show off their ever-increasing technical prowess.

"The Blue Danube Waltz," an excerpt from a full suite of Strauss waltzes choreographed by Mieczyslaw Morawski, was well-rehearsed and impressively danced -- considering the student status of the dancers.

A 20-member cast crowded the stage with an amplitude of movement that almost matched the breathless surge of the music. It was a return to genteel elegance as the girls made sweeping circles with their frothy chiffon skirts, while their partners held them gallantly, if not always securely, aloft.

With pianist Douglas C. Buurma on stage to accompany the dancers, Yourth's revival of "Rag Suite" was more than just a string of spirited dances. It was a playful period piece -- set in an old-time ice cream parlor -- that kept the dancers on their toes from start to finish.

This fast-paced, witty and totally unself-conscious ballet spoofed the symbols of the period and some of ballet's sacred cows as well. It also proved to be a fine showcase for the energetic ensemble.
