April 28, 1990, Saturday, San Diego County Edition

5 WHO HELPED BRING CRIMINALS TO JUSTICE CITED;

CRIME: DISTRICT ATTORNEY HONORS TWO WHO CAUGHT A CRIMINAL AND THREE WHO HELPED CONVICT CRIMINALS.

During the early morning one day last December, two construction workers were in hot pursuit of a hit-and-run driver who had just killed a 78-year-old woman as she and her husband crossed an Oceanside street.

Francisco Parada y Maldonado and Charles Long were on their way to work in a truck when Maldonado saw the woman hurled into the air by the hit-and-run driver's car.

Maldonado did the only thing he could do -- he turned his truck around and chased the car, while Long called the police on a the truck's telephone.

They chased the man, Richard Santillan, to a cul-de-sac about 2 miles away. When they finally reached him, they saw him toss several beer bottles from his car.

Maldonado said they both told Santillan to go back to the scene. To their relief, Santillan agreed to return.

"I was scared," Maldonado said Friday. "I was shaking, and I didn't realize it until we went back to where the accident happened."

Santillan, 23, an illegal alien who had lost his license as the result of an earlier hit-and-run accident, was later sentenced to four years in prison.

Maldonado and Long have been named Good Samaritans by the San Diego County district attorney and were among those honored Friday at the third annual District Attorney's Crime Victims Luncheon.

Some of the others who received words of praise from Dist. Atty. Edwin Miller were ordinary people who became victims of crimes. The crimes pushed them to the limits of emotional endurance.

Lisa Clark was a rape victim who had to endure the threats and taunts of classmates at Monte Vista High School in Spring Valley when she testified against two very popular boys who raped her on an April night two years ago.

Her attackers, Tim Clay, now 20, and Courtney Hallman, now 21, were sentenced, respectively, to six and three years in jail. They were 1987 Monte Vista graduates.

Jodie Robertson was abducted in 1984 by serial killer David Allen Lucas. He slit her throat and left her to die.

"It's a miracle that she is alive," Miller said. He praised her tremendous spirit for "simply living" after her throat was cut. And he lauded Robertson's endurance -- because she had to relive the attack seven times on the witness stand during Lucas' trial. In September, Lucas was convicted of three murders and sentenced to death.

Robertson thanked Miller and added that she could not have endured the ordeal without the support of her family and the prosecuting attorneys.

Kathy Minnifield had similar praise for Miller and his staff. Minnifield's 2-year-old daughter was beaten to death and stuffed into a trash bin in El Cajon in 1988 by Matthew LeSure.

Minnifield and LeSure had been living together.

LeSure was convicted last year, and sentenced to 25 years to life in prison, after Minnifield and her 7-year-old son, Derwin Anderson, testified against him.

In a tearful voice, Minnifield thanked Miller's staff and her family "for helping me to stay strong."

Miller also singled out two organizations that work within the criminal justice system to ease the pain of crime victims and to help them wend their way through the criminal justice system .

One is the Crisis Intervention Program, whose volunteers counsel victims of crime, most often violent ones, in those first moments of trauma. The other is the Domestic Violence Prevention Project, which since February, 1989, has helped more than 6,000 victims of domestic violence obtain temporary restraining orders against their abusers.

Miller said he hoped that the recognition of the people and the organizations would enable the public to understand the problems of victims -- the trauma of crime and the frustration brought about by delays in the courthouse.

"Anything that we can do to ease the pain. . . . It's very emotional for me. I'd like the public to know the problems they encounter," Miller said.
