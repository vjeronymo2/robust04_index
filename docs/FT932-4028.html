930611 FT 11 JUN 93 / Parliament and Politics: Press and privacy white paper 'soon' A WHITE paper setting out the government's views on press self-regulation and invasion of privacy by the media will be published before the end of next month, Mr Peter Brooke, heritage secretary, announced last night. He also said that the government remained reluctant to accept the recommendation by Sir David Calcutt that a statutory press complaints tri-bunal should be established. Mr Brooke promised to take into account the strength of support among MPs for the appointment of a press ombudsman reflected in a debate on a report by the Commons heritage committee on privacy and media intrusion. The committee suggested that the ombudsman should be able to adjudicate on complaints about decisions reached by a new voluntary press commission able to require newspapers to correct in-accuracies and impose fines. Newspapers and complainants would be able to appeal to the High Court to overturn decisions made by the ombudsman. Mr Gerald Kaufman (Lab Manchester Gorton), chairman of the cross-party committee, envisaged the ombudsman having a 'back-up' role which provided a stimulus for voluntary regulation. He welcomed moves already made by the existing Press Complaints Commission to give effect to some of the recommendations made by the committee. Ms Ann Clwyd, the shadow heritage secretary, said a future Labour government would order an inquiry by the Monopolies and Mergers Commission into the ownership of the press. She said: 'When Mr Rupert Murdoch can own 35 per cent of weekday newspaper circulation that, in the eyes of most people, is a quite unacceptable concentration of power.'
