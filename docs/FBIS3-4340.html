Language: English Article Type:BFN [By Debbie Kuo] [Text] Taipei, March 26 (CNA) -- The Communist Chinese military will not attack Taiwan before the year 2000, a military expert said Saturday [26 March]. However, Taiwan will be at an increased risk of attack during the years 2001 to 2010, Niu Hsien-chung, a military strategist and author of several war game books told legislators at a public hearing on security issues. Beijing will be preoccupied with resolving thorny problems related to the retrocession of Hong Kong and Macao until the turn of the century, ruling out the possibility of any attack on Taiwan, Niu said. Niu was at the Legislative Yuan to comment on the recently published Ministry of National Defense White Paper. Niu said that the white paper lists six reasons and ways for a communist offensive against Taiwan, but did not discuss when such attacks would take place. "Timing is vitally important," he said. On the ministry's plan to size down the armed forces to about 400,000 in 10 years, Niu said if the plan is a must, it should be carried out in five years rather than 10. He added that the White Paper only dealt with military maneuvers against Taiwan. "As a matter of fact," he said, "non-military attacks would be even more deadly." The white paper claims that the foremost event which might trigger a military attack from Beijing would be a declaration of independence by Taiwan. Other major events include internal large-scale chaos or riots within Taiwan, foreign intervention in the internal affairs of Taiwan, refusal to hold reunification talks with the mainland, the development of nuclear arms on the island, and political instability in the post-Deng Xiaoping era, which might lead to a communist attack on the island. According to the white paper, some of the six ways the mainland could possibly attack Taiwan include a sudden missile offensive against targets on Taiwan's western coast or seizing upon fishing disputes to trick Taiwan's Navy into a maritime confrontation. The communists may lure Taiwan air and naval forces into battle after first attacking Taiwan-held offshore islands. Mainland forces could also launch a rapid-fire invasion and take over Taiwan-held islands, the white paper said.
