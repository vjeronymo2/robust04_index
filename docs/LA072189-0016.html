July 21, 1989, Friday, Home Edition

MODERATE CAFFEINE OK FOR SOME HEART PATIENTS

Question: A friend of mine is being treated by a doctor for what he says is a heart rhythm problem. In general, I'd say that he's a good patient because he follows an exercise routine that his doctor set up for him and he watches his diet and has lost several pounds. But he still drinks coffee. Either his doctor has told him that he can have coffee, or he's drinking it against his doctor's advice. Isn't he asking for serious trouble by using a caffeine?

Answer: Many physicians do warn their patients with heart disease about the possibly dangerous effects of drinking coffee or, generally, using caffeine. However, the results of one study suggest that caffeine used in moderation will not have an adverse effect on patients in relation to heart rhythm disturbances.

This does not mean that all heart disease patients can use caffeine with impunity. Some patients may be particularly sensitive to the effects of caffeine and may have to abstain completely. But these probably constitute a small group. Of course, patients who have been told by their physicians to avoid caffeine should follow this advice.

Q: If lung cancer can't be seen on X-rays until it's practically too late to do anything about it, and if there are no other tests to show if a person has lung cancer, what should a person look for in deciding whether to see a doctor?

A: Smokers should contact a physician without delay if they have a cough that has persisted for a while, if they cough up blood and if they have experienced an unexplained weight loss.

Q: My skin often gets dry, and when it gets dry, it itches. I know what to do about it, but why would a doctor warn against scratching? It's not as if I have blisters which can break if you scratch them.

A: Dry skin may not be blistered, but scratching dry skin may cause the skin to break. And if this happens, bacteria may enter and cause an infection.

Q: Some friends and I have been reading about risk groups in relation to AIDS, and we got to wondering which women are most likely to get the disease. Is there any information gathered that would answer the question?

A: In the United States, women who use intravenous drugs make up the largest group of women with AIDS.

Q: I would like to know if diet has any effect on the premenstrual syndrome. Are there certain foods that women with the syndrome should avoid?

A: Some foods may tend to aggravate the symptoms of the premenstrual syndrome. Salt, for example, may cause bloating and headaches, while sugar tends to trigger a craving for certain foods. In addition, caffeine may lead to anxiety and irritability.

Conversely, the symptoms of premenstrual syndrome may be eased by the adoption of a nutritionally sound, well-balanced diet that emphasizes fruits and vegetables, and lean meats and fish.
