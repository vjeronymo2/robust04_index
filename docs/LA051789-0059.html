May 17, 1989, Wednesday, Home Edition

SHUTTLE SKIPPER HAS CLOSE CALL WHILE FLYING JET

A NASA jet piloted by the commander of the space shuttle Atlantis reportedly came within 500 feet of a Pan Am wide-bodied jetliner about 30 miles west of Washington, officials said Tuesday night.

The NASA T-38 training jet piloted by David W. Walker came close to the Pan Am jet at about 6:35 p.m. Monday about five miles west of Washington Dulles International Airport in the northern Virginia suburbs, said John Leyden, a spokesman for the Federal Aviation Administration.

Navy Capt. Walker, who was commander of the shuttle that returned May 8 from a four-day mission, flew to Washington on Monday night in a T-38 to attend a White House ceremony honoring the shuttle astronauts, NASA spokesman David Garrett said.

Leyden said the pilot of the T-38 was listed as "David Walker," but he did not know if it was the same David Walker. However, Garrett said there is no other NASA pilot by that name.

The shuttle captain, reached at a Washington hotel, declined to confirm if the incident occurred.

"That's something that I need to probably talk with the FAA about, not with the news organizations," Walker said. "I'm going to have to get my information from them before I start talking to you all."

NASA's Garrett said he had heard reports of a possible close call involving Walker but had been unable to reach the pilot.

Describing the incident, Leyden of the FAA said: "The pilot (of the National Aeronautics and Space Administration craft) was given an 8,000-foot assigned altitude. He was descending below his assigned altitude of 8,000 feet to 7,000 feet, and that caused him to come close to a Pan American Airbus 310."

Pan Am spokeswoman Pamela Hanlon confirmed that the small plane crossed the path of Flight 140, which was en route to Paris from Dulles.

"It had taken off from Dulles and was at 7,000 feet when it reported a T-38 aircraft came within what the captain believed to be approximately 500 feet of the Airbus aircraft," Hanlon said. The Pan Am jet was carrying 166 passengers and a crew of 10, she said.

"The pilot did not take any evasive action," but immediately reported the incident to the Dulles Airport tower, Hanlon said.
