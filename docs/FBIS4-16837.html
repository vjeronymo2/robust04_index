BFN [From the "Diplomatic Panorama" feature: Reports by Diplomatic Correspondents Igor Porshnev, Vitaliy Trubetskoy, and others] [Text] The Korean Peninsula is the only hot spot in Asia and the Pacific which may seriously destabilize the situation in and outside this region. Given this, talks with the South Korean President Kim Yong-sam in Moscow on June 1 to 4 will deal with ways of easing tensions in the Korean Peninsula and with South Korean-Russian relations, Russia's Deputy Foreign Minister Aleksandr Panov told INTERFAX on Tuesday. He believes that the South Korean president's visit to Moscow provides "one more proof of our stable and broadening relations." "We hope that the forthcoming visit will give a fresh impetus to them," he said. To quote Panov, relations between Moscow and Seoul develop normally and have no unsurmountable barriers, which can be judged from a 40 percent increase in bilateral trade last year. Panov emphasized that Russia's initiative on the convocation of an international conference on the Korean Peninsula remained in force. "This idea has not been rejected by anyone, although most of our partners give preference to bilateral talks," he said. He told INTERFAX that Russia had already held consultations with China, Japan and other parties concerned. "No one has removed this idea from the agenda. Moreover, everyone thinks that at a certain stage it may become rather efficient. We don't think that bilateral talks, the way they have been conducted until now, hold any promise. They are not making any progress and only complicate the situation each time they find themselves in a deadlock," said Panov. He announced that Russia would like the People's Democratic Republic of Korea to adhere to the treaty on the nonproliferation of nuclear weapons. On the other hand, we think that it is high time that all the parties concerned worked out a single policy instead of holding consultations on the Korean problem, he continued. A balance of interests must be found, whereby the security interests of both Korean states will be observed, said Panov. It is difficult so far to talk about the date of the international conference, he went on to say. "We propose that a consultative meeting be held to discuss the agenda," he said. Asked if the government crisis in Japan would influence Russian-Japanese relations, Panov said that Russia had no fears on this score. Judging by all accounts, Tokyo will not change its policy in relation to Russia whoever heads the Japanese government and whichever parties are represented in it.
