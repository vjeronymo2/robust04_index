November 5, 1989, Sunday, Home Edition

MORNING BRIEFING: WILLIAMS KNOWS A LOT ABOUT RUNNING TO -- AND FOR -- OFFICE

Reggie Williams burns with envy every time he sees Bo Jackson's television commercial.

Bo may know a lot of things, Williams admits, but at least he's not doing them all simultaneously.

Williams, on the other hand, is doing two things at once: trying to hold onto his job as starting outside linebacker with the Cincinnati Bengals while at the same time campaigning to remain a member of the city's City Council in Tuesday's election.

"Bo at least gets to separate them," says Williams, a 14-year NFL veteran closing out his career this year with the Bengals, and a council member since last year. "He doesn't have to run from the football field to the baseball field. I'm doing both at the same time."

Now it can be told Before baseball's playoffs, well before the Bay Area World Series and the disastrous turn of events resulting from the devastating earthquake, long-time sportswriter-columnist Leonard Koppett invoked what he calls Koppett's Law.

It appeared in the Oct. 1 issue of the Boston Globe, simply stating that "Whatever will cause the greatest inconvenience to the most people is what will happen."

Out for the count Talk about freak accidents . . .

While sparring recently at Ontario's North Bay Martial Arts Club, two kick-boxing opponents clinched, then tumbled out a third-story window, landing on a pedestrian below.

The pedestrian was treated at a hospital and released. The two combatants, Jim Lawrence, 26, and Bradley Sayeau, 23, were hospitalized with serious injuries after the 30-foot fall.

"It was a freak accident," Ontario Provincial Police spokesman Rick Sapinski said, before stating the obvious: "It was absolutely unintentional."

Fortunately for the passer-by on whom they landed, the athletes were kick boxers, not sumo wrestlers.
