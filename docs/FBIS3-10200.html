Language: Spanish Article Type:BFN [Text] Human Rights Ombudsman Dr. Jorge Mario Garcia Laguardia requested the Army's and the Foreign Ministry's intervention to protect the nation's territorial integrity and the lives of Guatemalans settled along the Belizean border. During his visit to the region, the ombudsman personally learned about the threats that Belizean soldiers issued against 35 families who live at the border town of Rio Blanco, Dolores, Peten Department. The high-ranking official met with peasants of the aforementioned town and the mayor of Dolores who gave a detailed account of the threats issued by the Belizean military. Sebastian Tux, a peasant, asked the ombudsman to clearly demarcate the boundary with Belize, because last year Belizean soldiers also forced them to abandon their land, where they left behind a crop of approximately 2,500 quintals of beans and 2,000 quintals of corn. He added: If necessary we, the peasants, offer our labor to build a boundary along the border. We have looked for another place to relocate, but have not found one. He said they would rather stay than emigrate from the lands they have tilled for many years. Moreover, Ombudsman Garcia Laguardia said it all began when former President Jorge Serrano accepted Belize's independence and established diplomatic relations with its government, an unconstitutional act that would have never been accepted by the Guatemalans if a referendum had been held on the issue. However, he added, two key institutions have to intervene: the Army and the Foreign Ministry. Besides, the Armed Forces are duty bound to maintain strict control of the border, which it has apparently neglected; and the Foreign Ministry has to mediate with the Belizean Government through diplomatic channels to prevent these actions. Referring to this, he asked the Army to permanently protect the border zone and guarantee Guatemalan rights; and he asked the Foreign Ministry to mediate diplomatically to put an end to hostilities and provide guarantees to the Guatemalan citizens, so they may live in their country and exercise their rights.
