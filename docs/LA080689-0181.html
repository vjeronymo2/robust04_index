August 6, 1989, Sunday, Home Edition

RESPECT, CONTEMPT FOR GUEST CONDUCTORS -- FROM THE MUSICIANS UNDER THEIR BATON;

WHAT HAPPENS TO THE MYSTERIOUS LINK BETWEEN CONDUCTOR AND ORCHESTRA?

A conductor should reconcile himself to the realization that regardless of his approach or temperment, the eventual result is the same -- the orchestra will hate him.

-- Oscar Levant, "A Smattering of Ignorance"

As a summer sport, it hardly ranks with water-skiing or body-surfing for sheer visceral excitement. But for thousands of orchestra fans, the annual game of conductor watching at the Hollywood Bowl offers its own special thrills.

Until mid-September, the Los Angeles Philharmonic plays host to an array of guest conductors, some well-known to the orchestra, some appearing with the orchestra for the first time.

In some ways, each series of rehearsals and concerts is a little like a blind date. Each visitor on the podium undergoes intense scrutiny by the players.

"A new guest conductor in front of an orchestra is pretty much stripped naked," says Roy Tanabe, Los Angeles Philharmonic violinist and assistant personnel manager. "We're all thinking, who is this person? What does he or she have to offer?

"We have to establish our sig

nals. We have to determine not only what the conductor wants musically but also how he's going to convey it. I have a job to do. I need to know how much I'm supposed to contribute. Will I have to carry him?"

There isn't much time to form an opinion. The Philharmonic typically has only one or two rehearsals for each Hollywood Bowl concert during its 11-week summer season, as opposed to three to five run-throughs for a subscription concert at the Dorothy Chandler Pavilion.

The distractions of working alfresco at the Bowl can also make it hard to concentrate. For this reason, Los Angeles Philharmonic violinist Edith Markman says the orchestra's relationship with Bowl guest conductors is enhanced greatly if the visitor has a sense of humor. Soviet conductor Yuri Temirkanov, who conducted several concerts at the opening of the summer season, rated high on this point.

"He had a great perspective," Markman says. "He got terrific results by using exaggeration when he rehearsed with us. So that by the time the concert came around, we'd clearly remember the musical point he was making. And even if you gave him only a little of what he wanted, it would come across."

And, of course, it helps if a visitor is well-prepared. A guest conductor must know his repertory perfectly, says John Mack, principal oboe of the Cleveland Orchestra. "He mustn't shy away from addressing problems of ensemble, pitch, style, note-length, anything. The more convinced a conductor is about what they're doing, the more the orchestra will respect the conductor."

According to the Philharmonic's principal concertmaster Sidney Weiss, that respect is won or lost quickly. "We know within a matter of seconds whether this is going to be a rewarding experience or just time we have to put in."

How do they know? "It's an elusive thing to define," Weiss admits.

What activates this mysterious link between an orchestra and its conductor? Does it always end in bitterness, as Oscar Levant insisted? Does a warm rapport between conductor and players necessarily lead to satisfying Schubert? Does mutual distrust inevitably result in muddled Mahler?

Finally, as far as orchestral musicians are concerned, what makes a great conductor?

A random poll of players around the country elicited some surprising answers.

The first myth to be shattered was that of the clearly defined beat. Superior baton technique apparently is not a prerequisite for superb music-making.

Many esteemed conductors, from Wilhelm Furtwangler to Carlo Maria Giulini, were notoriously lacking in the clear beat department.

"I used to think it was crucial," Weiss says. "But as the years pass, I find it's less and less important. For the great bulk of the standard literature -- a Mozart or Brahms or Tchaikovsky symphony -- once you start playing the movement, the pulse is self-explanatory. We don't need a traffic cop."

Mack cites the example of his orchestra's principal guest conductor: "Vladimir Ashkenazy is a magnificent musician. But my friends in the audience always complain that he makes awkward stabbing gestures with the baton. So what if he makes stabbing gestures? He's making great music."

Still, there are technical skills that a first-rate conductor can't do without.

For Nathan Stutch, who retired this spring as associate principal cello of the New York Philharmonic, they include a strong rhythmic sense; an ability to adhere to a strict tempo; a highly developed ear for intonation, balances and different timbral possibilities; vast insight into varying musical styles, and an intuitive musicality.

"Ideally, a great conductor must be able to hear all, see all and know all about the music he performs," says the 42-year veteran of the New York Philharmonic. Stutch says musicians rarely encounter such a person.

Surprisingly, articulate speech rated low on everyone's scale.

The way one uses language is a big part of one's personality, Tanabe said, "but a conductor has his whole body to use, his facial expressions, his sheer energy. And during a concert, there are no words.

"A conductor should be able to rehearse the orchestra with a minimum of verbal communication. We don't want him to tell us, 'Play it like the blue waters emanating from the source of eternal light.' We want to hear 'slow, fast, loud, soft.' If he achieves a distinctive sound, then we allow him his blue waters and eternal light."

Violinist Arnold Brostoff, a 25-year veteran of the Chicago Symphony Orchestra, concurs.

"Look, Moses was tongue-tied, he could hardly talk. All he had to say was 'Let my people go!' And said (it) with intensity. That certainly meant something!"

Loquacious conductors can "drive an orchestra crazy," Brostoff says, particularly when the gift of gab isn't complemented by serious conviction. He singles out three noted Americans for special scorn: James Levine, Leonard Slatkin and James Conlon. "The dishonesty, the posturing," Brostoff exclaims. "These people are not making a connection with the orchestra."

That connection does appear to benefit from a conductor's personal warmth and spirit of camaraderie.

JoAnn Falletta, who officially begins her first season as music director of the Long Beach Symphony on Sept. 16, gets high marks from many members of the orchestra, not only for her musicianship, but also for her engaging manner.

"She treats us like human beings," says Long Beach violinist Jeff Gauthier. "She seems interested in getting to know us. There's a strong commitment there, both to the orchestra and to the music."

Yet some of the most celebrated conductors of this century were not exactly proponents of the warm fuzzies.

Sidney Weiss, who performed in several major orchestras before joining the Los Angeles Philharmonic in 1979, remembers a rehearsal with the Cleveland Orchestra at which the late George Szell fired the entire trumpet section, player by player, as each one failed to perform a certain phrase in Debussy's "La Mer" to the famed Hungarian conductor's satisfaction.

"Fritz Reiner was even worse," Weiss says. "This was a truly sadistic man. I'm convinced he would try to think up ways to humiliate the players."

Weiss freely acknowledges that Szell and Reiner got phenomenal results from their unhappy bands.

The late Herbert von Karajan was also well-known for his uncompromising attitude, both on the podium and off.

Stutch vividly remembers a performance of Beethoven's Ninth Symphony that he played under Karajan in 1958 in New York. "He definitely had a cool, detached presence on the podium," Stutch said. "It was the antithesis of Bernstein, who hugs and kisses and wants everyone to love him. With Karajan, there was no fraternizing.

"Yet you knew you were in the presence of a giant. Such economy of motion, such command. You wanted to be enmeshed in this great musical mind."

So if personal charm, baton technique and a way with words are not essential, what makes a great conductor? Weiss says it's an innate gift that is rare.

"A lot of young people are getting a chance to conduct who don't deserve to," he said. "There's a common misconception that if a person has enough experience on the podium, he's going to excel. It just doesn't happen. A conductor is trained in his own home, studying scores.

"Most conducting courses are a waste of time. You really can't teach this art. The real talent is the ability to inspire -- to impart one's musical ideas to every musician in the orchestra in such a way that

they play better than they thought they could . . . better than they can."

The means by which this inspiration is imparted varies widely.

Leonard Bernstein sweats, swoons, cries and dances, Tanabe says. "It's a carnal experience. Part of his conviction is to call attention to himself. He never holds himself back.

"To explain the feeling of buoyancy he wanted in a Beethoven symphony, he exploded in a series of belly laughs."

Doriot Anthony Dwyer, since 1952 the principal flutist of the Boston Symphony, is still haunted by a glance she received from Bruno Walter more than 30 years ago.

"At Tanglewood, we were (recently) playing Dvorak's New World Symphony, a piece we've done hundreds of times," Dwyer said. "There isn't a time we play it that I don't see Bruno Walter's face looking at me during the first big flute solo. It was such a beautiful expression. I knew exactly what he wanted. When the spirit of the music lights up a conductor's face before you play, there's nothing like it."

Recent Bowl guest Neeme Jarvi capitalizes on his physical stature, Markman said. "He has huge hands, he's a very large man, and he just envelops us with his interpretation.

"Erich Leinsdorf, a much smaller man, also does this beautifully. His hands, arms, head, body are all working together with such a strong sense of purpose. You feel like you're on Ninja at Magic Mountain. He's

taking you away and there isn't any other way to go. You can't fight the G-force. For me, that makes playing in the orchestra very exciting."

Concept and heart are the most important conductorly qualities as far as Markman is concerned. "If the concept is deeply felt, and if a conductor has the ability to produce that concept in the orchestra, I'm willing to risk all to go with him."

"It has to do with an insight beyond the work itself," Tanabe said. "Way beyond the achievement of a good, technical performance. It's a gut-level emotion. The whole orchestra feels it. And we all hope it will happen."
