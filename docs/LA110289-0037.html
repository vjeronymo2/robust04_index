November 2, 1989, Thursday, Home Edition

FOOD AND COMMON SENSE: REDUCING FAT INTAKE A CONFUSING ISSUE, BUT GUIDELINES CAN HELP CUT USE

"Why is it," a health-conscious friend complained recently, "that you nutritionists are always making it more complicated to choose a healthy diet?"

His lament referred to selecting fats for cooking and table spreads. For the past several years, he had paid close attention to reports on TV and radio and in newspapers chronicling research findings on how different types of fat affect serum cholesterol. The payoff, he observed wistfully, is that he feels confused about exactly what he should be doing.

He's not alone. Experts agree that reducing saturated fat is the most critical step in modifying fat intake to lower serum cholesterol. Yet the role of unsaturated fat, and the mix of polyunsaturated and monounsaturated fats we should consume, is far less clear. For such a complex question, it will take years of study, not the findings of any single project, to reach an answer.

The other confusing factor in fat selection is the proliferation of butter alternatives and the advertising that accompanies them, tapping into some piece of the message for a healthy heart.

Despite the confusion, we have to make decisions at the grocery store, in the kitchen, and all other places where we buy, prepare and eat food throughout the day. Luckily, there are some simple guidelines recommended to ease the process.

Start by assuming you could use less fat in your diet. Although consumption has been dropping, the typical American gets considerably more fat than the current recommendation of 30% of total calories. The best approach to trimming fat is simply to eat lean: Choose low-fat or skim-milk dairy products, buy lean cuts of meat and eat smaller servings, use more chicken and fish, cook with a minimum of fat and use it sparingly at the table.

For many people, this strategy might be all that's needed to reach the target of 30% of total calories, as well as to create a diet where no more than 10% of the fat is saturated, with the rest evenly split between monounsaturates and polyunsaturates. At nine calories per gram, fat is the most calorie-dense of the energy nutrients, compared to four per gram for either carbohydrate or protein. Thus, cutting back on fat might also mean you'll shed extra pounds.

If step one is to take a well-sharpened paring knife to the fat -- especially saturated -- in your diet, step two is to make informed choices about those fats you do buy for cooking and table spreads. Where oils are concerned, you can choose according to usage.

For sauteing, take one of the many flavorless oils available. For salad dressings or dishes with a Mediterranean flavor, olive oil makes the zestiest choice. It should be used sparingly, so the fruitier the better. And in many Oriental dishes, peanut oil -- like olive, rich in monounsaturates and also carrying a distinctive flavor -- might be your pick. Again, a little goes a long way.

So far, so good. When you hit the dairy case, however, you may feel lost in a jungle of products. Bear in mind this generalization: A tablespoon of regular, stick margarine, 80% fat and 20% water by weight, is calorically comparable to butter, providing 100 calories per tablespoon.

Checking at the market, we found that all but three stick margarines provided labeling information. All except one contained two grams of saturated fat per tablespoon and one to three grams of polyunsaturated fat. Curiously, all but one manufacturer used a tablespoon as the serving size. This one exception used a teaspoon, which contained a gram of saturated fat. Multiply that by the three teaspoons to a tablespoon and you'll find the product more highly saturated than other brands.

Of the three unlabeled margarines, two were made from partially hydrogenated vegetable oil, and it's unknown how saturated they are. The label on the third states that it "contains liquid corn oil," but again, we don't know whether it's hydrogenated and therefore more saturated. Among tub margarines, the one containing the most saturated fat used a teaspoon rather than a tablespoon as the portion of choice, thereby misleading the unwary consumer.

What happens when these margarines are modified by whipping in water? Calories are reduced, but how much varies by brand. Consumers can make their selection both on taste and on desire to cut calories. Clearly, there's no getting around not reading the labels.

Finally, there is a line of products whose appeal is unabashedly based on being a butter taste-alike. It may or may not achieve that objective. But, not surprisingly, the addition of butter boosts its quota of saturated fat. In making comparisons between this and other margarines, always remember to check for portion size. Otherwise, you may be getting more saturated fat than you bargained for.

Not All Shellfish Have Excessive Cholesterol

Question: I was sure I'd read in your column that most shellfish are no richer in cholesterol than lean meat or chicken. The other night I dined with a friend who says he knows all about the ins and outs of a cholesterol-lowering diet. He passed up scallops because, he said, they are a potent source of cholesterol. I thought he was wrong, but he was adamant. Who's right?

Answer: You are. Assuming that the scallops weren't prepared with a lot of oil or margarine and weren't afloat in either melted butter or a rich cream sauce, they are not only low in cholesterol but also low in both fat and saturated fat. The scallops themselves, before any fat is added, contain less than a gram of fat and just 95 calories.

To spare your friend's feelings, you can explain that his confusion is understandable. The problem is that at the time a link between dietary factors and serum cholesterol was first established, laboratory methods for analysis of dietary cholesterol were relatively primitive compared to today's fine-tuning. As a result, other compounds, which are chemically similar, were measured as cholesterol.

This error inflated the early values for shellfish. Although the mistake was later rectified, old learning has died a slow death. Many people, such as your dining companion, cling like limpets to the belief that shellfish are all rich in cholesterol.

In truth, many shellfish contain little. For example, three ounces of clams, mussels, oysters or scallops provide between 30 and 50 milligrams. That amount is considerably less than the 80 milligrams you would get from the same portion of veal, beef or chicken.

The same amount of lobster contains somewhat more, about 90 milligrams. How much there is in shrimp depends on species, anywhere from as little as 60 milligrams in Japanese prawns to three times that amount in the white Atlantic varieties. Squid, a mollusk most people do not recognize as such, contains 180 milligrams of cholesterol.

Q: I know that ground beef does not keep as well as whole cuts, but I haven't the foggiest notion why. Can you enlighten me?

A: There are two reasons why ground beef deteriorates more readily than a roast or even chunks cut for stew. For one thing, ground beef is often made from trimmings, so it has been handled more than larger pieces of meat. The handling process increases exposure to bacteria. For another, grinding the meat greatly multiplies the exposed surface area. That exposed area is an open playing field for bacteria naturally present in the environment. While these organisms are not harmful, they can cause a breakdown in quality, and even spoilage, if handled improperly.

Because ground meat deteriorates so quickly, it should be held in the refrigerator for no more than a couple of days. If you don't get a chance to use it up within that time, you have several choices. One is to brown the meat well and use it in a cooked dish such as spaghetti sauce or chili, which can be eaten within a few days.

Alternatively, it should be frozen. If you do choose to freeze it, remember that the best way to thaw it is in the refrigerator. Thawing meat on the counter is not recommended. That method allows the outer layer to warm to a temperature where bacteria thrive, while the inside remains solid.

Q: I was preparing a batch of corn muffins when my mother offered to "help." She had never used corn meal before and when she opened a new box she wailed in dismay at the black specks inside. I assured her that it was perfectly fine and that those specks were always present in corn meal. She was dissatisfied with my answer. Can you tell my mother that I know what I'm talking about?

A: Those black specks are indeed a natural part of the corn kernel. Corn kernels, like other grains, have three basic parts: the bran (or outer husk), the germ and the endosperm. These black specks, called the "hilar layer," are found at the base of the germ and connect it to the rest of the kernel. During milling, the hilar layer remains with the endosperm, and shows up as the dark specks you describe.
