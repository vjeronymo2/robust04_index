March 16, 1990, Friday, Home Edition

JOE ROTH -- A 20TH CENTURY MAN;

FILM: THE DIRECTOR OF 'COUPE DE VILLE' IS UNCONVINCED HE HAS A TALENT FOR DIRECTING, SO HE'S HAPPY HE HAS A DAY JOB -- AS CHAIRMAN OF 20TH CENTURY FOX.

The Hard Rock Cafe was jumping. To celebrate an invitation-only screening of Universal's new film, "Coupe de Ville," the chic West Hollywood eatery was packed with Hollywood's finest -- actors, producers, agents and lawyers -- all eager to meet one person.

Joe Roth.

Wherever the director of "Coupe de Ville" stopped on his zigzag course through the restaurant, a crowd gathered. Shelley Long squeezed his hand and paid her respects. Universal Pictures chief Tom Pollock offered hearty congratulations. Directors Amy Heckerling and Neil Israel, both old Roth pals, gave the 41-year-old director affectionate hugs.

As he struggled toward the buffet table, Roth bumped into Billy Crystal, who offered praise for "Coupe," which opened to mixed reviews last weekend (Universal is broadening its run next Friday to about 150 theaters). Without missing a beat, Roth impishly retorted: "How much are you lying?"

"Oh, maybe a little bit," Crystal said slyly, quickly adding: "Joe, you did great."

Roth wasn't enjoying this regal treatment because the crowd here smelled a hot new director. In fact, when Israel asked if he was eager to direct again, Roth furiously wagged his head. "Oh no. No. No. I'm happy where I am. Whenever I watch the movie, I keep saying, 'Thank God, I've got a job. I've got a job!' "

And what a job. Since August, Roth been the chairman of 20th Century Fox Films, the first time a director has run a major studio in more than half a century.

But even Roth's new-found clout couldn't guarantee a successful celebration. Just as the bash hit its stride, a phalanx of police burst into the room, accompanied by a local fire marshal, who closed down the restaurant, citing overcrowding.

As several burly firemen, wearing big yellow slickers, made their way through the Hollywood crowd, Roth reluctantly headed outside, accompanied by his wife, Donna. "Geez, what a party," he sighed, eyeing a fire truck parked at the curb. "I never even got a burger."

Joe Roth's 20th Century Fox office doesn't have the palatial glaze of most movie-mogul throne rooms. Sitting on a scuffed white couch, eyeing his most distinctive office props -- a plain wooden desk and a mini-basketball hoop -- you get the feeling Fox hasn't wasted any money on decorator's fees for its new film division chairman's office. Roth's shelves brim with pictures of his family and a photo of his directorial idol, Preston Sturges, playing pool.

Roth's attention is focused on the wall near his desk, where a production chart follows the flow of Fox film releases. The batting order gives you a pretty good idea of Roth's strategy for turning around the slumping studio.

"The Adventures of Ford Fairlane," Andrew Dice Clay's first starring feature, and "Die Hard II," the sequel to the 1988 box-office hit, are slated for June. In July, the studio has a pair of high-profile sequels, "Young Guns II" (Roth produced the original 1988 hit) and "The Exorcist III." Later in the fall, the studio will deliver "Pacific Heights," starring Michael Keaton and Melanie Griffith, followed by Tim Burton's "Edward Scissorhands," with "Predator II" due at Christmas.

It's a fairly conventional production strategy -- an occasional offbeat film mixed in with a hefty dose of brand-name sequels and distinctive movies made by strong-minded directors. It reflects Roth's strategy during his two-year stint running Morgan Creek Productions, where he sandwiched a daring thriller like David Cronenberg's "Dead Ringers" between lowbrow crowd-pleasers like "Young Guns" and "Major League."

Still, the question is inevitable. How much room does this formula leave for a personal film that didn't have a top-name actor -- a picture like "Coupe de Ville," which Roth directed for Universal before taking the job at Fox? How much room would there be for Roth, whose previous directorial efforts were a critically praised flop, "Streets of Gold," and the critically dismissed "Revenge of the Nerds"?

"If the person who'd directed 'Streets' and 'Revenge' had come in here and said I want to direct a $10-million film with no real stars, I'd probably say no," acknowledged Roth, an informal, candid man who dresses simply and has retained his lean, high school athlete build. "On the other hand, would I have liked the material? Would I have liked the cast? Yes. But is it an obvious sell? No."

But don't expect Roth to look for advice from Hollywood's entrenched market research consultants, whom he has derisively dismissed as "voodoo makers."

"This business has become so driven by money and fear that it's become overly reliant on testing and research," he said, his voice taking on a steely edge. "It's an excuse. A crutch. Worst of all, it pits you against your instincts. We've become so statistically driven that we can be ecstatic when we watch a movie -- and get depressed when we get bad numbers a half-hour later. That's crazy! The sheer, overwhelming data for 'War of the Roses' suggested it was not going to be a hit. But it'll end up doing $85 million in the U.S. alone."

Paul Mazursky, who says he "lights candles for Joe every night" for making his critically lauded, post-Holocaust romance, "Enemies, a Love Story," confirmed this account.

"Joe loved 'Enemies' when he first saw it, but when the research people screened it, it didn't get great numbers from a big chunk of the audience. And Joe said to me, 'I love the movie more now than I did the first time I saw it. Don't worry about the numbers -- I regard them as useless. They're for advertising purposes alone. I wouldn't touch your movie.' "

The busy studio chief has green-lighted 16 films during his first seven months at Fox. He projects that he'll make 22 movies this year and says he has a mandate from Fox Inc. Chairman and CEO Barry Diller to make as many as 30 films in 1991.

But what has really made Roth an object of intense industry curiosity is his maverick status. The moguls who run Hollywood's other studios have all been in place -- or key players in the system -- for more than a decade. Even David Puttnam, when he took over Columbia, was a highly visible, Oscar-winning producer. But as recently as 1987, before his Morgan Creek films began to attract attention, Roth was a virtual unknown operating out of a small trailer-office on the Fox lot.

"He's the first real outsider to run a studio in years," said Larry Gordon, a leading producer who was one of Roth's predecessors running Fox. "He's not a member of the club. Everyone else in town -- Eisner, Diller, Katzenberg, Sheinberg, Daley, Semel, Pollock, Mancuso, Medavoy, Guber and Peters -- have all been around for 20 years. But Joe's from a different crowd.

"When I go in to see those other guys, I'm going into combat, with my flak jacket on. There's so much history there. But Joe doesn't carry all that baggage around with him."

Still, industry skeptics remain surprised by Roth's swift ascent to executive stardom. Before his brief stint at Morgan Creek, he had produced a batch of largely undistinguished films, including teen hits ("Bachelor Party"), critic's favorites ("The Stone Boy") and such forgettable flicks as "Americathon," "The Final Terror" and "P.K. and the Kid."

"Give Joe two years," said one veteran producer. "Then we'll know whether the hits he had at Morgan Creek were the result of being truly shrewd or just lucky."

For now, Roth is trying to revive Fox's reputation with exhibitors by delivering a host of star-driven films (like Bette Midler's "For the Boys") and sequels.

"It's very competitive to get theaters," he said. "So they need to know that we can bring them event films -- both for summer and for Christmas -- that will generate audience interest. But I don't want to make what we call 'programmers.' You're not going to get people out of the house unless you make stories that have a strong emotional hook. The kind of hook that if you had a seismograph, it would cause a real blip."

But can Roth run a film studio without eventually getting removed from reality? How long will it take before the cagey outsider becomes the complacent insider?

"I worry about that every day," he said. "I try to drive into this lot every morning thinking as a producer, trusting my instincts. But it's hard. I wish I could enjoy it more when people come over and tell me how much they liked my movie.

"But you never know who's sincere and who's not. So one of the ways I guard against that is pretty simple -- I figure no one is telling the truth."

When Hollywood executives describe Roth, they use the same phrase -- competitive. Several years ago, when preparing to produce a baseball film, he played center field at a Chicago Cubs fantasy baseball camp. It's somehow fitting that Roth's most vivid memory isn't the joy of schmoozing with his heroes but the thrill of matching his athletic skills against them.

"I hit against Ferguson Jenkins," he says. "Well, I tried to hit against him. The first pitch was a fastball." Roth signals the whoosh of a ball hurtling past him. "No chance."

With his checkered past as a director, many thought Roth might be equally overmatched when he went off last summer to make "Coupe de Ville," which stars Patrick Dempsey, Daniel Stern and Arye Gross as three estranged brothers traveling to Florida to meet their father.

A week into shooting, Roth collapsed. "I was so eager to direct that I had us shoot in the South in the heat of summer," he said. "I went out there with no hat, no sunglasses, no protection. I just snapped. They thought I had a stroke. I curled up in a ball and said, 'Let's just pack up and go home. I'm finished.' Finally, they put me on an I.V. and my body started sucking up salt. I took the next day to recover and then finished the film."

It's revealing that Roth picked Dempsey not simply for his comic flair. "What really got Patrick the part was the pain on his face," Roth said quietly. "He'd been an unhappy kid who left home early, so he lived out that part. That's something all the brothers share -- they're alienated from each other."

Roth identifies with this emotional distance. He grew up in New York, where his parents were active unionists. Again Roth was an outsider. "My family was a left-wing, artsy family. I was very emotionally cut off from them -- I was more comfortable with sports. In school, I'd sit in the front row of class, over in the corner, out of the teacher's peripheral vision, and I'd make up all-star lineups in my mind."

For most of his 15 years in Hollywood, Roth was also off on the fringe, far from front-row center. "I had an awful lot of frustration," he admits. "I remember being so frustrated about five years ago that I talked to my accountant about going into the cable field."

Finally, when he was producing a film on location in Brazil, he snapped. "I was in the middle of the 100-degree heat, in a horrible rainstorm, standing in the jungle surrounded by bugs and snakes that could kill anyone within 100 feet," he said with a frown. "And I thought, 'I'm not even directing. I'm just watching the damn movie being made!' "

When Roth returned, he put his house for collateral and made "Streets of Gold." "I love the singularity of focus with directing," he says. "Night shooting is so wonderful -- it's just you with the actors. You have a peacefulness you don't have in this other job I do."

But Roth has no illusions. "When I watch my movie, I really feel someone else can do it better. If I thought I had the talent of a Larry Kasdan or Barry Levinson, OK. But I don't think I have that gift."

Roth shrugged. "After all, I didn't give it up to go back and teach at the local education center. I have a great job. I'm one of the few guys who can say 'yes' to making a movie."

Still, you get the feeling Roth's still not entirely comfortable in his new role. He admits that when Barry Diller first offered him the Fox job, he turned it down.

"I'd spent my whole life railing against these institutions, making movies about characters who were outlaws," he said. "But Barry convinced me I had a mandate to run the company my way -- and to trust my instincts."

Roth gazed wistfully out his office window. "You can't predict anything in this business, but here I am."
