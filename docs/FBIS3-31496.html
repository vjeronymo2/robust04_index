Language: English Article Type:BFN [Text] Budapest, 17 February (MTI) -- Hungary's Government has decided NATO's AWACS surveillance aircraft may not stay in Hungarian airspace during any air strikes on Bosnian Serb positions, Andras Kelemen, state secretary of foreign affairs, told reporters today. The government has asked Parliament's Defense and Foreign Affairs Committees to discuss this position. Prime Minister Peter Boross will inform NATO Secretary-General Manfred Worner on the committees' conclusions immediately, the state secretary said. The Hungarian Government agrees that the bloodshed in Bosnia should be brought to an end, if required, even by resolute military action. In the absence of a NATO guarantee, however, Hungary cannot take part in such an act. In view of the 400,000 ethnic Hungarians living in Vojvodina and other parts of Serbia, Hungary would like to avoid even the suspicion of a connection with any military action. Under the Hungarian Constitution, participation in a military action requires parliamentary approval. Since the government has not asked for such approval, the idea that someone use Hungarian airspace for an air strike in Bosnia cannot be raised in the framework of constitutional order, Kelemen said. The AWACS aircraft patrolling in Hungary are monitoring the fronts in Croatia and Bosnia-Herzegovina but are not authorized to provide logistical support for any air strike, Prime Minister Boross told NATO Headquarters last week. He indicated that modifying the mandate of AWACS fell within the scope of the Hungarian Parliament. "NATO has not asked us to allow AWACS aircraft to control an air strike on Bosnian Serb positions. Nor will the government ask Parliament to extend the AWACS patrols' mandate," Kelemen said. The state secretary added that a Hungarian role in a possible NATO air strike in Bosnia could trigger anti-Hungarian hysteria in both Serbia and the "Krajinas" under Serbian control. Hungary's charge d'affaires in Belgrade has duly informed the Yugoslav Foreign Ministry on the Hungarian Government's position. On Monday [14 February] the Hungarian Foreign Ministry informed Yugoslavia's charge d'affaires in Budapest. Kelemen said the Hungarian Foreign Ministry did not plan to recall the families of Hungarian diplomats accredited to Serbia and Montenegro. The Belgrade Embassy, he said, has not reported that any Hungarian citizen would be in danger. In reply to a question, the state secretary said that the Hungarian position on the AWACS planes did not influence the peace partnership established between Hungary and NATO last week.
