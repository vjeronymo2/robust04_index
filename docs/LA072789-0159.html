July 27, 1989, Thursday, Home Edition

FOOD AND COMMON SENSE: THERE ARE LEANER AND LIGHTER ALTERNATIVES FOR BACK-YARD BARBECUES

We hate to risk being accused of raining on that great American institution, the summer barbecue. But as nutritionists, we must point out that hamburgers and frankfurter, accompanied by macaroni, potato salad and potato chips -- the stuff of traditional barbecues -- are best reserved for a very occasional treat.

But hold on, and don't put the grill in storage: Believe it or not, you can find culinary happiness in alternatives that are lower in fat, calories and sodium.

These leaner, lighter choices begin with a focus on chicken and fish, both excellent candidates for the outdoor grill. For chicken, the key to variety lies in marinades that extend well beyond the usual sauces (invariably some shade of red) sold in the supermarket.

One favorite, reminiscent of the Caribbean, is a mixture of oil, lime juice and minced onion seasoned with tarragon and hot pepper sauce. Teriyaki sauce, seasoned with a little brown sugar and mixed with a few drops of sesame oil, white vinegar and mustard is another good option to brush onto chicken.

An Indian Touch

For a marinade of chicken legs with an Indian touch, season one-quarter cup each of lemon juice and oil with six cloves of minced garlic, one tablespoon minced ginger root, one tablespoon ground cumin and several generous dashes of hot pepper sauce. Indeed, it is possible to grill anything from chunks of white meat on skewers or pounded breast, brushed lightly with oil, to quarters with the bone left in. To get the job done more quickly, precook chicken in the microwave oven.

For fish, the sheer variety of choices, including shellfish such as shrimp and scallops, and the different combinations of herbs are all you need to create an endless parade of tastes. The teriyaki mixture we've suggested for chicken goes equally well on fish, especially tuna steaks and bluefish. The flavor of salmon steaks may be enhanced with sprinklings of thyme, sage, parsley and pepper before grilling. If you want to serve it with a sauce, try yogurt with plenty of minced cucumber folded in and seasoned with dill, grated onion and white pepper.

Rosemary, lavender leaves, crushed fennel and cumin seeds are another good herb blend for seasoning fish. And, of course, in the summer months nothing is better than fresh, chopped basil.

Mayonnaise Alternatives

As for accompaniments, our program begins with a farewell to mayonnaise. Yes, it does a nice job of holding potatoes, macaroni, cole slaw and even rice together, but at around 100 calories a tablespoon, it's a good idea to seek alternatives. A delicious side dish to go along with chicken is a lentil salad, popular in France in the summer but rarely seen here. To prepare it, a cup of lentils, providing six servings, is cooked with an onion stuck with two cloves and a bay leaf until tender. They are then drained, tossed with a minced onion, about two tablespoons each of oil and wine vinegar, chopped parsley, a little salt and freshly ground black pepper.

A rice salad is another neglected option. Cooked rice is simply tossed with a little oil, lemon juice or wine vinegar, minced parsley and green onions. If you want more volume for calories, extend the salad by adding sliced water chestnuts, blanched bean sprouts and some lightly cooked green beans. You might also try seasoning it with cilantro, or Chinese parsley leaves.

We also recommend a French potato salad, prepared by tossing sliced, small cooked potatoes with white wine vinegar, a little oil, and seasoning it with chopped parsley, chives and tarragon.

Grilled vegetables are an increasingly popular feature at barbecues, and that is all to the good. Easily cooked on skewers or in pans specially designed for the task (or sometimes right on the grill itself), they require only a slight brush of oil and, if desired, a strategically chosen herb or two. At a cost of very few calories they provide taste, color and texture, not to mention vitamins, minerals and fiber. If you'd rather stick to raw vegetables such as carrot sticks and pepper rings, or even raw broccoli and cauliflower, try a dip made by adding a teaspoon of curry powder to a cup of low-fat yogurt.

In Favor of Fruit

For dessert, we strongly favor fresh fruit. But if you're in the mood for something more elaborate than a watermelon slice or a bowl of cherries, we suggest fruit lightly poached in a syrup of equal parts of sugar and water. Our favorites include nectarines, apricots or peaches served with a puree of raspberries or strawberries. And if you're feeling extra fancy, a small bit of liqueur goes a long way toward enhancing the flavor.

As you can see, it's well within your grasp to eat festive, outdoor meals without either throwing nutritional principles out the window or sacrificing the great tastes of summer.

Metamucil Fares Well in Cholesterol Study

Question: I recently heard on the radio that a "bulking agent" commonly used to promote normal bowel movements also lowers cholesterol. Is that true?

Answer: Results of recent studies do suggest that psyllium hydrophilic mucilloid, more familiar as Metamucil, does lower serum cholesterol. Psyllium is a water-soluble, gel-forming fiber derived from the husks of blond psyllium seeds. It belongs to a group of soluble fibers that include gums, pectins, mucilages and certain hemicelluloses that have been shown to lower serum cholesterol.

In the trial reported most recently, a group of men and women with mild to moderate elevation of serum cholesterol were placed on the National Cholesterol Education Program's "Step One Diet" to reduce serum cholesterol. On that diet, 30% of the calories come from fat, 55% from carbohydrate and 15% from protein. It provides no more than 300 milligrams of cholesterol daily.

After 12 weeks, subjects judged appropriate to the trial were divided into two groups. One group was given packets of Metamucil, the other an inert cellulose fiber. They were instructed to take the fiber in water three times a day before meals.

The group adhered to the regimen remarkably well. Compared to those who received the inert fiber, subjects who took the psyllium achieved an additional reduction of almost 5% in total cholesterol level, and an 8% reduction in low-density lipoprotein associated with increased risk of heart attack. Moreover, the use of psyllium did not have adverse effects on other important health indicators, among them blood pressure, blood sugar levels and HDL cholesterol levels, associated with decreased risk of heart attack.

Some subjects did suffer mild gastrointestinal complaints, but these were considered transient and minor. None was serious enough to require discontinuation of treatment.

The authors of this study, which was conducted at the Hennepin County Medical Center in Minneapolis, Minn., were frank to point out that the amount of soluble fiber can be increased by diet, and they emphasize that the psyllium seed was used in addition to a diet for lowering serum cholesterol. The decision to use Metamucil as a cholesterol-lowering adjunct to your diet should be made only after a discussion with your physician to determine if it is appropriate.

Q: Can you tell me how to choose one of the "preferred" margarines, if one is trying to control serum cholesterol?

A: The nutrition-labeling panel is the best guide. Directly below the number of grams per serving, on most margarines you will find two other numbers. The number next to the "p" is for the amount of polyunsaturated fat it contains; the "s" is for the amount of saturated fat. To select those you describe as "preferred," choose among the many options where the amount of polyunsaturated is larger than the amount of saturates. And, the larger the better. The two numbers will not add up to the total amount of fat, because the margarines also contain monounsaturates.

People often inquire about why margarines advertised as "rich in polyunsaturates" contain a more saturated fat, such as partially hydrogenated soybean oil, as the second ingredient. The explanation is simply that, without it, you would have liquid oil, not solid margarine.

Q: I have always avoided buying meats packaged in heavy plastic wrap. While the price is often less than for meats in standard packaging, I have been put off by the unusually dark color. I assumed that it meant either that it was a lower grade of meat or that additives had been added to prolong freshness. While staying with a friend recently, she bought and served a large piece of vacuum-packed meat. It tasted perfectly fine, but before I'm sold on it I'd like an explanation for the color difference.

A: The dark color indicates neither a quality difference nor the use of additives. It is the result of a lack of exposure of the meat to oxygen.

The type of cuts you are describing are called subprimals. Whole beef carcasses are usually split into five large primal cuts, which are then divided into subprimals. Most beef is now cut into subprimals at the packing plant, vacuum-packed immediately and shipped to the supermarket. Those available directly to consumers are usually the best parts of those cuts, with much of the bone and other waste already trimmed.

When the package is opened, the meat "blooms" -- turning the bright color with which you are familiar. Vacuum-packed meats do have an odor, described as sour or off when opened, but it disappears within an hour after opening. These meats have not been previously frozen, so they can be stored in the freezer as is or cut into smaller pieces and repackaged.
