BFN [Report by Kostas Venizelos] [Text] Today or tomorrow UN Secretary General Butrus Butrus-Ghali will send the Security Council an official letter praising the Turkish side. According to informed sources, the letter will constitute a supplement (upon a Turkish request) to the UN secretary general's latest report and will note the progress achieved in the talks between Gustave Feissel, the UN secretary general's alternate special representative in Cyprus, and Turkish Cypriot leader Rauf Denktas after 30 May. The letter will mainly be based on a report prepared by Joe Clark, the UN secretary general's special representative in Cyprus, following his recent visit to the island and will be as important as an official document. Because the events will be listed, the clarifications and positions raised by Denktas in his meetings with the United Nations will be given official status. Despite Nicosia's objection, the submission of the letter will certainly mean that the procedure is continuing. From there the Security Council will make decisions on the Cyprus issue. Obviously, at least behind the scenes, the British will proceed to "marry," promote, two of the options included in Butrus-Ghali's latest report. Reliable circles say the British element -- having long left the initiative to the United States -- will attempt to undertake an initiative on the Cyprus issue. Analyzing developments and the facts in the Cyprus issue, the Foreign Office believes that no move must force President Glavkos Kliridhis to "reject" the procedure that will be formulated. Clark already received a negative reply when he asked the president to agree to incorporate the Vienna agreements in the 21 March UN document. In their own interests, as usual the British will try to find a formula that will be accepted both by Kliridhis and Denktas. How will they proceed? A diplomatic source noted yesterday that according to all the signs the "five" are being oriented in promoting a formula that will keep the confidence-building measures on the negotiating table and at the same time begin a long-term discussion of the Cyprus issue. The same source mentioned that signs are being reinforced that the Security Council believes every move is made on the basis of Butrus-Ghali's set of ideas and the confidence-building measures. They claim there is 90 percent agreement on the confidence-building measures. The Turkish side has launched a large-scale diplomatic campaign abroad to convey the idea that Denktas accepts the confidence-building measures. The reality is this: The military officers, the National Security Council, recently confirmed a decision to remain "open" on the Cyprus issue. The orders that have been given insist that a policy be followed on a political level to show that the Turkish side is contributing positively to developments. This is why Denktas, who has recently been calling on Turkey (obviously for advice), must give "yes" and "no" answers to the mediators. The current Turkish strategy dictates that many fronts must be kept open with Greece. All that is certain is that the UN Security Council will decide on the next steps within the next few days. The substance of the decision is known to an extent. The question is how to express it, because it requires a positive reply from both sides.
