January 15, 1990, Monday, Home Edition

MARKET BEAT: PORTFOLIO MANAGER ZEROES IN ON REAL ESTATE-RELATED STOCKS

Forget about those real estate jitters that have rocked the market lately. Portfolio manager Allen A. Parker is undaunted. "Wealth accumulation has always involved substantial real estate," he says. Only now it will be through real estate-related stocks more than direct investment.

Parker, who runs the U.S. Real Estate Fund, operated by San Antonio-based United Services Advisors, argues that the "wave of the future" is the securitization of real estate (an unpopular subject these days). That's because the end of most tax breaks on real estate investments and the illiquid nature of such ventures have made them unattractive to many institutional and wealthy investors.

These days, he's particularly high on real estate investment trusts. About 160 are listed on stock exchanges, but only half are actively traded, and he considers only 25 to be of institutional quality. Of those, he sticks to a subgroup that he calls "property specific," which he believes are most recession-resistant, especially those that either own local strip shopping centers anchored by grocery or discount drug chains, or properties leased to psychiatric or substance abuse hospitals.

What makes REITs especially attractive, he says, is that they "pay you to wait" with robust dividends. For example, Boston-based Copley Properties, "an example of being in the right thing in the wrong place," owns industrial and office properties in its headquarters city. It trades for about $11.50 on the American Stock Exchange, but has a dividend yield (dividend as a percent of share price) of more than 8%. "So you're going to wait for this thing to come back," he says.

Parker also likes Bethesda, Md.-based Federal Realty Investment Trust, which owns shopping centers in the mid-Atlantic region; Wellesley, Ma.-based Meditrust; Los Angeles-based Health Care Property Investors; Beverly Hills-based American Health Properties, and Toledo-based Health Care REIT.

P.S.: Another Parker pick is home builder Kaufman & Broad, whose stock has plunged about 25% in the past six months. "We'll buy on the pullback," he says. That's because he believes that real estate investing is a little like investing in mutual funds: It should be done for the long haul, just "more so with real estate."

Thrifty Investing in Closed-End Funds

Like those closed-end country funds that are the rage these days, several similar funds are devoted exclusively to savings and loans. (Shares of closed-end funds, unlike conventional mutual funds, are traded on exchanges.) The idea is that you can get thrift stocks cheaper by buying shares in the closed-end fund rather than buying the individual thrift stocks, while eliminating the risk of trying to pick a winner.

The favorite of Prudential-Bache Securities closed-end fund analyst Steven Cress is First Financial Fund, which trades as a stock on the New York Stock Exchange, and closed Friday at $7.375 a share. "The stocks in the fund are oversold, and eventually the market will place a fair valuation on it," he says.

Cress isn't alone with that opinion: Savvy New York hedge fund manager Julian Robertson hasn't sold a share even though First Financial's price has dropped by nearly half since he nearly doubled his position last August to 27.5%. Robertson, who is quick to admit a mistake and then sell off, told me: "This is one we don't think is a mistake."

Looking for a Corporate Co-Pilot

Informed sources say: Pan Am, which is known to be seeking a merger partner, is in discussions with NWA (and not just Trans World Airlines, as has been widely speculated). If a deal is struck, it is likely to be structured (on paper at least) with Pan Am buying NWA (which owns Northwest Airlines), which itself recently went private . . . New York real estate developer Donald J. Trump has quietly sold all of his stock in AMR, the parent of American Airlines, which has slumped about 30 points since he bought it and launched a short-lived bid.
