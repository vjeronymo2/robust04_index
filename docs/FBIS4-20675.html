Abstract
Rotary disk modules, developed for the separation of highly concentrated activated sludge, can be used in place of biological settling ponds to efficiently recycle sewage or waste water. Applied to sewage treatment, the rotary disk module allowed us to consistently obtain recycled water of T-N < 5 mg/l, BOD < 2 mg/l, SS trace. When the module was applied to cafeteria waste water treatment, we were able to obtain recycled water of BOD < 2 mg/l, SS trace. Flux through the module was, in both cases, able to be maintained at 1.5 m[.sup]3[/]/m[.sup]2[/]d while filtering liquid of 10 to 15 g/l MLSS.
Introduction
As pollution in closed water regions grows worse and urban redevelopment becomes more extensive, it becomes all the more urgent to recycle sewage and commercial building waste water. Here, the Ministry of International Trade and Industry conducted a six-year (1985 through 1990) project, Research and Development on General Recycling Systems for Water. In conjunction with this, the authors supervised a project, Research and Development on Biological Nitrogen Removal Technique, directed at developing membrane reactors for sewage recycling. In the field of water treatment, membrane reactors have been utilized for grey water production devices[.sup]1[/] that combine membrane treatment with the activated sludge process. However, while this approach does have its advantages, it is limited by the large amount of power required to membrane-separate the highly concentrated activated sludge. Here, we created a rotary disk module[.sup]2[/] for the low-power separation of highly concentrated liquid and, in extension, developed a membrane reactor that combines this with the biological nitrogen removal process. In this paper, we introduce this rotary disk module and the newly developed membrane reactor for sewage treatment. In addition, we access the performance of this system as applied to the recycling of cafeteria waste water and compare its cost to that of tubular-type modules.
2. Design of the Rotary Disk Module
A conventional membrane module circulates the treatment liquid at a high flow rate so as to obtain a rapid flow at the membrane surface and a high flux through the surface. However, much power is consumed, because the pressure loss within the module is correspondingly high (i.e., a higher pressure is maintained than what is required for filtration alone). As opposed to this, rotating the membrane would, we thought, allow us to maintain a high liquid flow rate at the membrane surface without having to maintain high pressure. Therefore, relatively little power would be required to run the system. The water collection mechanism of a rotary disk module is shown in Figure 1. A membrane disk, comprised of two support plates covered with a membrane over their surface, is fastened to a hollow rotary shaft which acts as a water collection tube. Filtering is carried out while the shaft rotates. To modularize the system, it is of course necessary to increase the filling density of the membrane by attaching many such disks to the rotary shaft. However, merely adding on extra disks decreases the flux through the surface by approximately one-half in a liquid having a high concentration of microorganisms. Here, installing baffle plates between the disks restores the flux to very nearly what it would be with only one disk.[.sup]3[/] Figure 1. Water Collection Mechanism of Rotary Disk Module The engagement-type module (Figure 2) utilizes the disks of an adjacent rotary shaft in place of the baffle plates. Here, two shafts comprise one module. Not only is membrane filling density increased with this arrangement, but turbulent flow at the membrane surface is encouraged, thereby increasing the flux. 3. Advanced Sewage Recycling System (a part of the Aqua
Renaissance '90 Project) 3-1. Experimental Apparatus The experimental apparatus is comprised of a nitrogen removal tank (1.25 m[.sup]3[/]), a nitrification tank (1.25 m[.sup]3[/]), and a rotary disk module (Membrane Area 7.44 m[.sup]2[/]). Standard treatment capacity is 10 m[.sup]3[/]/d. Retention time in the bioreaction tank was set at 6 hours. The rotary disk module consisted of two shafts, each with 12 disks of 500mm in diameter. The membrane was made out of a polysulfone. The data presented here was taken when using membranes having a cut-off molecular weight of 600,000. The types of raw water were tested: sewage (case 1); water from methane-fermentation of sewage (case 2); and water from SS separation and from SS methane-fermentation of sewage (case 3). Membrane Reactor Effectiveness The rate of nitrogen removal in the activated sludge process increases with an increase in the recirculation ratio. Figure 3 [not reproduced] presents data showing the effect of recirculation ratio in a membrane reactor. In both cases 1 and 2, we see that, relative to theoretical removal rates for conventional processing, higher removal rates were obtained even at lower circulation rates. This is because of (a) nitrogen removal in the nitrification tank due to a higher concentration of microorganisms and (b) removal by the membrane of nitrogen components resulting from SS. Here, we note that using membranes is very effective. Furthermore, the very high rate of nitrogen removal observed in case 2 is due to nearly complete nitrification and denitrification resulting from efforts to remove nitrogen after nitrification. The microorganisms had been acclimated from some time back, so this trial did not last long. Even so, we did achieve stable operation from the 15th to 70th day, producing, with retention times of 3 to 6 hours, treating water having 5 ml/l or less of T-N and 2 mg/l or less of BOD. Furthermore, the amount of excess sludge in this period was one-fifth of that resulting from conventional process. Operating results for case 2 are shown in Figure 4 [not reproduced]. From the 150th day of operation, we began adding ammonium chloride to the raw water to increase the nitrogen load. Even with this, we were able to maintain stable operation throughout the year. In average values throughout the year, water quality was 31.3 mg/l total nitrogen and 9.5 mg/l BOD for the raw water and 0.93 mg/l nitrogen and less than 2 mg/l BOD for the treated water -- the treated water was fully able to be recycled. Very little excess sludge was generated during the operating period, and nitrogen was removed very efficiently. Furthermore, use of the rotary disk module forestalled any problems with flow blockage or the like -- the membrane reactor operated very smoothly. 3-3. Membrane Module Performance Figure 5 [not reproduced] shows the change with time in flux upon continuous operation. Nearly the same flux is obtained in cases 1 and 2. In case 2, a flux of 1.5 m[.sup]3[/]/m[.sup]2[/]d was obtained over 180 days of operation without chemical cleaning. The pressure at this time was low at 0.09 MPa, and the concentration of microorganisms in the module was 12 to 15 g/l. The power required to draw water through the membrane at this time (cutoff molecular weight = 600,000, motor efficiency = 85 percent, pump efficiency = 60 percent) was about 1 kWh/m[.sup]3[/]. By this, we confirmed that we could separate highly concentration liquid with relatively little power. 3-4. Comparison With Conventional Method Table 2 presents a comparison for sewage between the conventional process (nitrified liquor recycling activated sludge process[.sup]4[/]) and the newly developed membrane reactor. Because a high concentration of microorganisms was able to be maintained with the membrane reactor, treatment time could be reduced by about two-thirds, even at low temperatures, and the volume of excess sludge was reduced by about four-fifths. The newly developed system consistently produced water of a quality -BOD = 2 mg/l max., T-N = 5 ml/l max. -- significantly higher than that produced by the conventional system. These and other results demonstrate the effectiveness of membrane reactors. However, it should be mentioned that the membrane reactor is probably not advantageous if the water is to be discharged after treatment -- it is only economically feasible when the treated water is to be recycled. 4. Cafeteria Waste Water Recycling System
4-1. Experimental Apparatus and Methods Figure 6 shows the flow of the experimental apparatus (treatment capacity: 3 m[.sup]3[/]/d). The raw water is waste water from a cafeteria within the Matsudo Works of Hitachi Plant Engineering and Construction. After screening (2.0mm mesh) and oil separation (in a tank), the raw water is fed into a bioreaction tank (0.8 m[.sup]3[/]) for anaoxic and oxic treatment. Afterwards, the water is fed to a rotary disk module for solid/liquid separation. By this, treated water is obtained for recycling. Concentrated liquid from the module is fed back to the anaoxic tank. As treated water is removed from the system, an equal quantity of raw water is fed into the bioreaction treatment tank. Figure 6. Cafeteria Waste Water Recycling System Flow The rotary disk module is comprised of two rotary shafts; the disks on one shaft run between the disks of the other. In this experiment, disk diameter is 500mm, and each shaft has six disks. The membrane is made of a polysulfone; the cutoff molecular weight is 750,000. A constant volume pump was attached to the treated water line (offtake) of the rotary disk module. The water was pulled through the membrane by the suction force of this pump. No chemical cleaning was performed within the period of this experiment. 4-2. Results of Continuous System Operation The experimental apparatus was run in such a way that BOD-SS load was kept at 0.15 kg/kg-d. Figure 7 [not reproduced] shows the progression in flux over two months in continuous operation; Table 3 shows the results of water quality measurements taken over that period (``raw water'' in the table refers to water at the inlet of the biological treatment tank). At this time, liquid temperature in the biological treatment tank was 25 to 33C, and MLSS was controlled to about 10 g/l. From Table 3, we note that high removal rates of over 90 percent were obtained for all water quality items and that the treated water was of a quality sufficient for recycling. Through this experiment, we have confirmed the efficiency of membrane treatment -- flux in the rotary disk module averaged 1.5 m[.sup]3[/]/m[.sup]2[/]d, and simple screening was enough to prevent any problems from blockage by foreign objects. 5. Summary
Advanced sewage recycling system (a part of the Aqua Renaissance '90 Project) (1) The use of a biological nitrogen removal process has enabled us to obtain recycled water of T-N < 5 mg/l, BOD < 2 mg/l, SS trace. (2) By maintaining the microorganism concentration in the bioreaction tank at 10 to 15 g/l, we have succeeded in shortening the treatment time to one-third of that required by the conventional biological nitrogen removal method. (3) With the rotary disk module, we have achieved stable performance and high flux. We also showed that it is possible to drive the rotary disk module with less power than required for a plate & frame-type module. Cafeteria waste water re-use system In continuous operation, we obtained a stable supply of recycled water with a good quality of BOD < 2 mg/l, SS trace. Also, we were able to maintain a flux of approximately 1.5 m[.sup]3[/]/m[.sup]2[/]d without chemical cleaning.
