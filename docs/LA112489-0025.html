November 24, 1989, Friday, Home Edition

DR. JOYCE BROTHERS: WHEN A WIFE IS ADDICTED TO SEX

DEAR DR. BROTHERS: My 40-year-old wife was always an exceptionally beautiful woman, but she was never sexually promiscuous until recently. Since I still love her, this pains me and causes me considerable embarrassment. She claims she's addicted to sex and that she can never get enough. Is this possible, and, if so, what does it mean? F.K.

DEAR F.K.: Before I answer this question, I'd like to know if you've talked with your wife about what's happening in your marriage, if you've asked her why she feels there was this sudden change and if she has any theories on why she feels "addicted" now but never before?

It's possible for people to be addicted to sex. Those who are use sex as an escape for their problems in much the same way other addicts reach for their "fix." There's little doubt that women who've been great beauties have an especially difficult time with the aging process. Unfortunately, remarkable beauties often fail to develop their potential because they get used to relying on their beauty even as small girls. If you have valued your wife primarily for her beauty, she may be concerned that you're losing interest as you witness her beauty fade.

She may be using sexual conquests as a way to reassure herself that she's still attractive. If you want to salvage your marriage, get professional help.

DEAR DR. BROTHERS: My son-in-law is physically abusive to my daughter. She won't leave him because she feels she can get him to change if he stops drinking. Each time, after he hits her, he swears it won't happen again and each time he tells her he'll stop drinking. This last time, he bet his entire bank account that he won't touch another drop and that he'll never under any circumstances hit her. What do you think the chances are that he'll keep his promise? G.W.

DEAR G.W.: Not good. This isn't to say that this man isn't capable of change, but unless he is highly motivated and gets the help he needs, there's little chance that he'll suddenly become the kind of man your daughter wants.

First of all, his abuse of alcohol isn't necessarily linked to his abuse of your daughter. Alcoholics may sometimes blame spouse abuse on alcohol, but studies indicate that abstention alone doesn't solve the problem. If this man is an alcoholic, then he should become a part of the Alcoholic Anonymous program, go to meetings every evening and dedicate himself to living their doctrine one day at a time. This will undoubtedly help him gain control of his temper, but I suspect he also needs special therapy to teach him how to talk constructively about what upsets him and now to deal with his rage in a positive way, rather than simply striking out at a convenient target, usually someone or something weaker and smaller.
