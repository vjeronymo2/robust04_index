August 8, 1990, Wednesday, Orange County Edition

CHILDREN'S THEATER REVIEW;

'LI'L ABNER' OFFERS RELIEF FROM FAST-LANE LIFE

Forget Martinique. If Orange County's hectic lifestyle is a gettin' you down, pack up the chillun and escape to Dogpatch, U.S.A., a town that moves slower than maple syrup a-slidin' down a stack of hot cakes.

This weekend, Dogpatch comes to Brea's Curtis Theatre via "Li'l Abner," a "Hee-Haw"-style family musical presented by the Brea Youth Theatre under the direction of Gregory and Kysa Cohen. Closing performances are scheduled Thursday through Saturday.

Created in 1956 by Norman Panama and Melvin Frank, the stage version of "Li'l Abner" is based on Al Capp's syndicated comic strip of the same name, which premiered in 1934 and ran a whopping 44 years.

Things haven't changed much in Dogpatch. Daisy Mae is still mooning over Abner, Mammy Yokum still runs the roost and "most everybody else can still be found lazin' around the fishin' hole and moochin' a little moonshine."

But, there's trouble a-brewin'. It seems the worthy Senator Phogbound has proclaimed Dogpatch to be the "most worthless town in the whole U.S. of A.," earning it the right to be blown off the map in an A-bomb test. This would be just fine with the residents, if it didn't interfere with the annual Sadie Hawkins Day race, in which members of the fairer sex (and in Dogpatch, you use that term loosely) nab unwilling husbands.

The search for something -- anything -- to make Dogpatch worthwhile turns up Yokumberry tonic, the secret elixir that has made Abner the strapping youth he is. Government types whisk the boy and the brew off to Washington for extensive testing, setting the stage for lots of jolly jabs at Big Business and dimwitted politicians. (There's a not-too-subtle irony in that this "Abner" plays inside Brea's slick Civic and Cultural Center, just a stone's throw from the city's seat of power.)

"Abner" features a 36-member cast of area youngsters, ages 8 to 17. The kids have varying degrees of theatrical and vocal expertise, but all put out equal shares of energy and enthusiasm.

Kristyn Jenkins, 10, is the real standout as the sassy Mammy Yokum, hobbling madly about the stage, barking orders between puffs on her corncob pipe. Doug Book, 17, falls short as a singer, but his portrayal of the loveable country hunk Abner is larger than life. Wendy See, 15, is slim, blond and has a sweet soprano, making her a winsome Daisy Mae. And Roger Alan Ross, 15, is a slippery Marryin' Sam, a fast-talking huckster with more dee-lux wedding packages up his sleeve than a Vegas chapel. Eric Bleau, 14, plays Earthquake McGoon, a solid block wall of a man with an intellect to match.

The opening number, "It's a Typical Day," is meant to introduce several of the minor roles, but the overamplified background music made it tough to make out the lyrics. Additionally, the program lists the cast alphabetically, instead of in order of appearance, making it harder to identify cast members.

Musically speaking, however, things do improve later in the show. "Jubilation T. Cornpone" and "The Country's in the Very Best of Hands" are a couple of rousing numbers highlighted with some surprisingly tight dancing. And "Love in a Home" is a tender duet between Abner and Daisy Mae that shows off See's honeyed voice.

Sets and lighting by Trent Nelson are serviceable and allow the large cast plenty of room to whoop it up on the theater's expansive stage. Annie McQuitty's costumes ape Capp's original drawings, down to Abner's single-strap overalls and Daisy's polka-dot blouse (See chose to wear hers a little more modestly than the comic strip's original, amply-endowed Daisy); and Senator Phogbound's 40-gallon Uncle Sam hat was a hoot.

According to program director D. Scott Riordan, the Brea Youth Theatre has operated under various names and directors for the last six years, presenting one family musical each summer in the Curtis Theatre. The city took over the program in 1988, bringing in local directors, designers and choreographers to stage each show. Participation is open to any student in the county, but the bulk of the casts are made up of children from the Brea, Yorba Linda and Fullerton areas.

'LI'L ABNER'

A Brea Youth Theatre production of the Norman Panama/Melvin Frank musical. Based on the comic strip by Al Capp. Directed by Gregory and Kysa Cohen. Runs 2 hours and 30 minutes, with one intermission. With Eric Bleau, Doug Cook, Adam de la Pena, Justin Dickson, Kristyn Jenkins, Roger Alan Ross and Wendy See. Sets and lighting by Trent Nelson. Costumes by Annie McQuitty. Audio design by Jenson Crawford. Performances are Thursday and Friday at 7:30 p.m., Saturday at 2 and 7:30 p.m., Sunday at 2 p.m. in the Curtis Theatre, Brea Civic and Cultural Center, 1 Civic Center Circle, Brea. Tickets: $6 to $7. Information: (714) 990-7722.
