October 20, 1989, Friday, Orange County Edition

BAY AREA QUAKE;

'NOT KNOWING' WEARS DOWN CALLERS TRYING TO GET THROUGH

The Brock family in San Clemente kept trying to get through. After 2 1/2 days, nerves were frazzled and worn thin. Then, late Thursday afternoon from Santa Cruz came the telephone call from Bob Brock's sister, who said: "We're safe. No, we didn't get hurt, and Mom's OK."

Brock's sister, Betty Rucker, and his 90-year-old mother, Freda Brock, were emotionally shaken but safe after Tuesday's 6.9 temblor, which left much of downtown Santa Cruz in rubble. And Rucker herself had escaped injury because she left her work at a department store in the Santa Cruz mall about about an hour before the earthquake hit at 5:04 p.m. One woman died in the store, and another was critically injured when the roof collapsed.

Magdalena Ortega's vigil also ended Thursday when the Huntington Beach woman finally got word that both her nieces -- Maria Prado of Hollister and Lupe Palomares of San Jose, and their families -- were safe. "It was the fear of not knowing," a much-relieved Ortega said.

Thousands of other Orange County residents unable to reach relatives in quake-devastated Northern California jammed telephone lines to the local offices of the American Red Cross and the Salvation Army in hopes of getting news.

But Red Cross officials in Santa Ana said Thursday that they have been powerless to try to reach loved ones in the San Francisco Bay Area on behalf of thousands of people who called them.

"Our volunteers have just been overwhelmed with the flood of people calling in here. With no way to communicate to Northern California, we are, essentially, helping people wait," Red Cross spokeswoman Sylvia Stewart said.

And so the thousands of requests -- called "disaster welfare inquiries" -- from local residents to contact loved ones sat untouched in a bin at the Santa Ana headquarters.

"What we do is try and reassure family members down here. We're giving them options," Stewart said. "Like advising them to contact relatives in other states on the chance that they may have received word from relatives in San Francisco."

Stewart said contributions for quake relief reached $41,835 Thursday, up 300% from Wednesday. And more than 900 pints of blood had been collected in the first 24 hours after the quake, prompting the Red Cross to reduce its blood-donating hours on Friday and during the weekend, she said.

"Right now, we have no indication that we will be needing more blood," Stewart said.

Meanwhile, a Pacific Bell spokeswoman in Orange County said telephone service to the Bay Area had improved Thursday, but many people continued to experience difficulty calling the Santa Cruz and San Francisco areas. Pacific Bell officials continued to advise customers to call only if necessary.

"The network was clogged," said Linda Bonnicksen of Pacific Bell. "But the network came through OK. It's just that customers began picking up their telephones all at the same time and it almost collapsed our system. (But) more calls are going through more freely throughout the state."

In Santa Ana, where 80 bilingual Pacific Bell operators handled calls from throughout the state from Spanish-speaking customers, volume jumped 25% Wednesday, up from an average of 14,000 calls a day, she said.

"Our operators talked to one man who had tried to reach his son in San Jose. The minute he heard the voice of one of our Spanish-speaking operators, he started crying. Apparently it had been a very frustrating time for him," Bonnicksen said.

Despite the Brocks' days of frantic calls to Santa Cruz, it was Betty Rucker who finally reached them in San Clemente, said Rose-Marie Brock, adding: "She said that Tuesday's quake was a close call."

"At least we know they're safe," Bob Brock said.

Rucker, a saleswoman at Ford's Department Store in the downtown mall, told them she usually works until 5 p.m. But on Tuesday, she went in an hour earlier than usual and left about 4 p.m. When the earthquake struck little more than a hour later, the front of the building collapsed, killing one woman inside and critically injuring another on the sidewalk, the Brocks said.

"My sister was upset because she couldn't get hold of me," he said. "Is she frightened? She doesn't even want to go back downtown to look at the rubble."

Maria Prado told Magdalena Ortega and other relatives in Orange County that to call them, she had to drive to Gilroy from Hollister, where telephone service had been interrupted.

Damage was extensive in Hollister, an older rural community with many wood-frame homes, she said.
