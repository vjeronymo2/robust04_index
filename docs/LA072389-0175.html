July 23, 1989, Sunday, Home Edition

UNDER TWO DIFFERENT SHADOWS OF THE APOCALYPSE;

COMPARING 'NEBRASKA,' 'WALK IN THE WOODS'

There is a scene somewhere around the middle of Keith Reddin's "Nebraska" in which not one word is spoken.

It happens when two military officers -- Swift and Fielding -- are on 24-hour duty in the hermetic launch center of a nuclear missile silo 70 feet underground.

The lights go up. The men stare into space. Awkward minutes go by. Blackout.

The scene speaks volumes.

We've seen these guys there before. Alone together. Bored. Whiling away the suffocating tedium. We've also seen them earlier, in jest, rehearse the commands they'd have to execute to "enable" a successful launch. We know -- as they know -- that we're really talking about the possibility of nuclear annihilation.

"Up there, who knows what's exactly going down," Fielding had said grandly over drinks one night to a bug-eyed army clerk he wanted to impress. "You gotta figure the worst."

"Everything could be dust by now," echoed his buddy Swift, playing along. But something of that flippant bar-room banter lingered. One day on that stupefying duty in the pit, Swift asks Fielding, "Could you shoot me?"

"Right now?"

"Say I balk."

"You wouldn't."

"Say I do."

"Come on." Fielding is incredulous. Swift insists. "I get cold feet, you gonna train the metal on me?"

"Of course. And you would do the same." Would he? Some exchanges later the conversation is dropped. But the harm's been done.

At the La Jolla Playhouse, where "Nebraska's" playing, we discover by degrees that no one on this forlorn base in the middle of nowhere is entirely rational. This business of baby-sitting rockets with the ability to pulverize civilization is not exactly life-enhancing. It provokes derangements.

Swift, who entered the service because his father before him had proudly served as a career military officer, comes hard and slowly to the realization that this choice of profession may leave something to be desired, especially now that it has been nuclearly redefined. But not before he's done some things that don't add up -- such as asking Fielding the $64 question that puts his very fitness for the job on the line -- and straying from a rocky marriage (with the wife of his commanding officer, no less), a marriage he claims he wants to save.

Nor is he the only person on base with performance problems. Gurney, the CO, is so tied up in knots he can't even complete a sentence. Any sentence.

No wonder his wife takes courses at the local college, visits bars and has affairs as recreational therapy. They are warning flares to a husband as inarticulate sexually as he is verbally.

Reddin doesn't have to fill in the blanks. They speak to us loud and clear. By the time that wordless scene in the middle of the play comes around, we know exactly what is not being said.

All of this strange behavior is not strictly the effect of living in the shadow of the Apocalypse. Like Lee Blessing's "A Walk in the Woods" (seen two years ago at La Jolla), Reddin's "Nebraska" is not directly concerned with nuclear deterrence (as is "Woods") but with the deadlier terms of a life lived in the negative mode. Dedication to making sure something doesn't happen, instead of making things happen, is a killer. It's "living" spelled backwards.

Blessing's "Walk," a discourse for two voices, is much more forward-looking. This zestful series of private conversations between an American arms negotiator and his Soviet counterpart as they roam the woods outside Geneva is engaging, personal, surprising, amusing and garrulous. They discuss everything -- from Mickey Mouse and banjos and brown suits and the likelihood of friendship to country Western music and -- oh yes, alternatives to the arms race. Even its outdoor setting is life-embracing. It does get around to testing some knotty questions, but it is, in a phrase, all happy talk.

"Nebraska," on the other hand, is all strained silences. Its messages are found between, not in the lines.

This is particularly unusual when you consider the origins of the piece. Reddin had been asked to develop a documentary for PBS on nuclear weapons systems. It never materialized but the playwright was left with stacks of research material and "became interested in looking at the subject from a smaller angle." Documentaries are anathema to plays, but "Nebraska" director Les Waters' description of the final product as "Strindberg Meets Pacman" fits. Aside from a mutual reliance on humor, what links the plays is contrast -- the degree to which they demonstrate that you can achieve similar ends through diametrically opposite means.

The loquacious "Woods" sparkles with accessible wit. It thrives on verbal left turns as well as the difference between the fledgling American, naively convinced that his diplomacy can make a difference, and the world-weary Soviet, droll and down-to-earth, who knows that political systems shift without really changing. (This argument may have lost some of its bite now that glasnost is pointing another way.)

"Nebraska" is more chilling. Its fundamental proposition has a negative thrust -- and we're not talking missiles -- that ultimately implodes. It's attrition vs. nutrition. The ravages of such an existence are subtle. They do their worst almost imperceptibly. But it doesn't take a savant or even a psychologist to tell us that a perpetual death watch is murder on the elan vital. A skillful playwright will do.

That is where "Nebraska" shines -- not so much as it unfolds scene after taciturn scene, in an understated episodic build, but in the power of its cumulative effect. Unlike the lip-smacking "Woods," which declares its intentions early and tackles them head on, "Nebraska" is all implication and sour aftertaste.

"Woods" comes sugarcoated. It is, therefore, much more of a crowd pleaser. Also a dream exercise for two actors. So it comes as no surprise that it was embraced for airing over PBS or that Des McAnuff, the artistic director at La Jolla who staged the original "Woods" (at the Yale Repertory, in La Jolla and in New York), has decided to mount two more versions of the piece next fall: In Moscow, with a pair of Soviet actors -- and in Vilnius, with a pair of Lithuanians. A perfect choice when you consider the play's portability, its dual nationality and topical appeal.

It's unlikely that something as subtle and semaphoric as "Nebraska" would work particularly well on television which, as a medium, favors bolder and more declarative statements. And it's even less likely that it will find ready commercial markets. But a performance in the USSR or Lithuania would be an interesting idea -- in rep, perhaps, with "A Walk in the Woods"?

Who is to say, on balance, if the play that says the least speaks the loudest? Or the other way around? There's virtue in both. What remains fascinating is the infinite variety of approaches to a subject.
