930507 FT 07 MAY 93 / BCCI depositors win compensation ruling A LEGAL device used by a group of BCCI depositors to increase many-fold the compensation they received following the collapse of the bank was declared legal yesterday by the Court of Appeal. The scheme - used by a group of about 60 depositors - involved splitting the money held on deposit into individual lots of Pounds 20,000 and then formally transferring the ownership of these to trusted family members or friends. The depositors were able to claim Pounds 15,000 for each lot - the maximum small investors' compensation for deposits of Pounds 20,000 or less - under the Deposit Protection Scheme, which is designed to alleviate hardship when a bank becomes insolvent. The overall sum required to meet payments due to all BCCI depositors under the protection scheme is about Pounds 78m. It is estimated that the splitting device will cost the Deposit Protection Board about Pounds 3.7m. By a two-to-one majority, Lord Justice Russell, sitting with Lord Justice Simon Brown and Sir Michael Fox, dismissed appeals by the Deposit Protection Board and Barclays Bank against a ruling by the Vice Chancellor Sir Donald Nicholls in July last year upholding the scheme's legality. The judges also refused the Deposit Protection Board and Barclays leave to appeal to the House of Lords over the issue. The scheme was devised by a firm of north London accountants, Pearlman, Kahan and Joseph. It operated in July 1991 before the government blocked its use by passing legislation. Mr Keith Kahan, one of the firm's partners, said: 'We are delighted with the ruling. About 60 original BCCI depositors split their money. Most of them were small depositors who made assignments to their wives and children and they will be the ones who benefit.'
