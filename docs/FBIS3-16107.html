Language: Russian Article Type:BFN [Aleksandr Shalnev report: "Washington's White House Knew About Nixon's Meetings. The Kremlin Also Knew of Them in Advance"] [Text] The Washington White House and President William Clinton personally knew that ex-President Richard Nixon, on setting out to visit Moscow, intended to meet with leaders of the Russian political opposition, including Zyuganov, Rutskoy, and Zhirinovskiy. No objections were made to Nixon on this score, nor was approval given, since these meetings were planned within the framework of the unofficial part of the trip by the ex-president, who had invitations both from the Russian Security Council and from the Foreign Policy Association. The fact that this is so was confirmed by Clinton himself, who offered explanations to this effect on U.S. television Wednesday. According to our data, Thomas Pickering, the U.S. ambassador to Moscow, was given instructions, also Wednesday, to investigate the reasons for the diplomatic mini-scandal which marked the trip by Nixon, who was refused his preplanned meetings with Boris Yeltsin, Viktor Chernomyrdin, and other high-ranking staffers of the government and the presidential entourage. Pickering has already had contacts with the Russian Ministry of Foreign Affairs in this connection, in particular with Deputy Foreign Minister Georgiy Mamedov. According to members of Nixon's delegation, the Russian president's statement that William Clinton had "distanced himself from the trip" was groundless. Official Moscow was informed on the day the ex-president arrived in Russia, i.e. last Sunday [6 March], that Nixon would meet with opposition leaders: Foreign Ministry staffers and representatives of the Protocol Department of the Russian president's apparatus were issued with the program of the visit, listing the meetings. There was more than enough time to voice their objections and to demand that meetings be refused. But at that stage, so I was told by Nixon's delegation, no objections were forthcoming. According to data from the Nixon delegation, the ex-president is gradually filling the gaps resulting from the refusals to meet with him. In particular, talks with Russian parliamentarians are scheduled for Thursday. Attempts to obtain official comments from Russian sources -- from the Foreign Ministry, above all -- had not met with success by the time this issue was signed to press. On a strictly unofficial basis some diplomats regarded the report that Pickering had contacted Mamedov as "disinformation." It was also pointed out that the Russian Foreign Ministry has nothing to do with Nixon's visit. It must follow from this that the Foreign Ministry also was not familiar with the program of meetings in advance.
