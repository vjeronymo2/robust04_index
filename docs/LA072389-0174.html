July 23, 1989, Sunday, Home Edition

CRITIC'S NOTEBOOK: MOVIE COMMENTARY;

JUST HOW FAR CAN (OR SHOULD) MOVIES GO?;

DEATH AND DESTRUCTION SEEM TO DRIP FROM THE SCREEN AS NEVER BEFORE

Time magazine's recent cover story is devoted to the way 464 Americans were killed by guns in a single week, and some of the biggest box-office movies of the summer are bloodier than ever. The topics don't seem unrelated.

Films, still the most potent message-carriers around, seem to be fostering a greenhouse effect of violence. They're murderous, they present new, bigger, more insidious methods of killing people, they do well at the box office and they come back having to top themselves.

Two of the summer sequels -- "Licence to Kill," a "new" James Bond (the first not to have come from one of the books and the furthest in spirit from them), and "Lethal Weapon 2" -- appear hellbent on setting some new high-water mark in violent death and noisy destruction. "Batman," which gets to create its own precedents, adds a nasty twist to its brutality. And so the ante is upped everywhere along the line.

The point about "Batman's" Joker is destruction and mutilation for the sheer joy of it. He lets the floodgates loose on savagery, Jack Nicholson's spurting energy pushing the amps higher and higher. The murderees are legion -- co-horts, girl friends, Gotham Cityites at random. When he goes on that slashing, paint-slashing rampage in a pretentiously named art museum, we're supposed to get the joke; smile indulgently because these are such frankly bogus copies of Old Masters, their very fakeness picked up by the museum's lighting.

But there are audiences who won't get the gag; all they'll pick up on is the glee with which Nicholson and his henchmen prance through that high-toned place, turning it into a heap of torn canvas and spray-painted walls. Why aren't the people who have been wringing their hands and worrying about the potential effects of "Do the Right Thing" concerned about a scene as seductively conducive to imitation as this one?

The Bond film, which this time is a revenge vendetta on Bond's part for the mutilation (there's that subject again) of his close friend, dangles men over shark pits, indeed has them half-eaten and survive, and blows up others in decompression tanks, spattering the porthole windows with their blood. I suppose it's the usual crunch and desperation of every Bond film, but the tone is more extreme, more sadistic, the blood-letting more torrential.

If these films weren't speaking to their audiences as viscerally as they are, they'd be laughable. "Lethal Weapon 2" is a joke of a story, a charade on the subject of apartheid held together by the enormous warmth and likability of Mel Gibson and Danny Glover, separately and especially together. But when the excesses of the action make audiences cheer instead of hoot, it's a thermometer reading, and that thermometer is going up as we sit here.

Were the Lethal Weaponeers dealing in crimes that involved millions of dollars in the first film? You can feel film-making minds whirring frantically: OK, OK, this time we'll make it billions. Did people drop from buildings in the original? So this time, we'll drop whole buildings on them. Or entire cargo containers.

If one of our heroes was strafed by machine-gun fire and left for dead before, now the villains blow up very nearly the entire Los Angeles police force's detective squad, at their homes, at their poker games, whoosh!

Most disturbing of all is "Lethal Weapon 2's" simplistic lesson in political science: White South Africans are the new Nazis. All of them. Yes, we are told that the eminently hissible Joss Ackland is not the South African ambassador but their "Minister of Diplomatic Affairs." But to unsophisticated audiences, you can bet that he and his staff of blond, snarling henchmen personify white South Africa.

And they are all sadists, with thick Boer accents, who sneer at "Kaffir-lovers" the way Helmut Dantine or Kurt Kruger used to sneer when Hollywood had real Nazis to characterize. To be sure, we have one "good" South African,

a very sweet, white-blond, completely dim young woman who works at this non-embassy but doesn't exactly agree with her country's policies. Useless to ask why she then works for them. Agreed that it's getting harder and harder to find an all-purpose villain for movies these days, one whose constituencies won't be marching outside your theaters come opening day. It would seem that the atrocious and heart-rending practices of apartheid would make that government fair game. Unfortunately, life is not quite that simple. The people who have been ignored in "Lethal Weapon 2's" rush to fill us in politically are that core of white South Africans who have doggedly and at no slight personal risk been working for the rights of South Africans of color for years. (Books and newspapers aside, did no one see "A World Apart?") To ignore their very existence is insulting, at the least.

In order to solidify our loathing of these bloody Boers, to make it personal as well as political, the film makers have gone back to the first movie and explained that the death of Mel Gibson's beloved but fortunately unseen wife was also a deliberate act by these very same mega-villains, drug traffickers as well as sadists.

All this is to pave the way for the movie's unceasing vigilantism, to give Gibson free rein to invade the South Africans' official offices, rough up their goons, even shatter their tropical fish tank with rifle fire. (Fish have politics too?) And finally, to shoot their "Minister of Diplomatic Affairs" even as he holds up his card of diplomatic immunity. Why is that different from an American Embassy member showing his papers of immunity in, say, Tehran, and expecting the protection they provide?

The point is that, trivial as it may seem to complain about a film like "Lethal Weapon 2," what is at issue are the principles of civilized behavior. By violating them -- no matter the provocation -- you weaken what separates us from governments we rightly deplore.

For its part, in addition to frightening youngsters dramatically, a film like "Batman" teaches them that there is a kick that goes with destruction not unlike the rush of sniffing glue or a first hit of crack.

What's most offensive about these formidably effective movies, however, is the way they trivialize life itself. If in a decade or so, we find ourselves in a society so de-based it is intolerable, we might look back to this summer as one of the signposts along the way.
