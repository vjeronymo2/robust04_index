920122 FT 22 JAN 92 / Observer: Midnight in Moscow Could it have been just in fun that a Russian late-night newscast heralded the 68th anniversary of Lenin's death by announcing his body was to be shifted from Moscow's Red Square mausoleum and buried, along with his family, in St Petersburg? If so, the joke was lost on about 100 old believers, who turned out at midnight to guard the sarcophagus in temperatures of 25 below. Since everything else had been taken from them, one sobbed, they would fight for Lenin's right to lie in state forever. Came the day, and a crowd of 2,000 in the Lenin Museum heard a succession of neo-revolutionaries lambast falling living-standards, and moral decay. 'Not hundreds, not thousands, but tens of thousands are demonstrating once more under the red flag,' said Igor Malerov, who's reviving the Young Communist League. Up at the tomb, its two white-faced guards looked on frigidly as a few hundred milled about with red banners proclaiming 'the ideas of Lenin will revive'. But it was an old woman who somewhat stole the show before the TV cameras. Asked by the interviewer for her opinion of the historic leader, she replied: 'Lenin? Who cares about Lenin? I'm not getting my pension.'
