940409 FT 09 APR 94 / Picture of working Tyne fogged Traditional river industries risk being squeezed off the banks of the Tyne by housing and leisure developments which could leave some areas 'sterile', the Tyne Port Users' Association says. Spurred by the struggle for survival of Swan Hunter, the Tyne's last shipbuilder, and the area's success in winning Nissan's new UK car terminal, the association launched a campaign yesterday to reassert the Tyne's importance. Mr Douglas Hamilton, chairman, told a seminar in Gateshead that the association's 50 members employed 8,000 people with a further 10,000 jobs dependent on the river. Mr Hamilton, managing director of Tyne and Wear Tugs, called for a long-term strategy to safeguard riverside industrial sites and prevent them being 'sterilised' by housing and hotels. Speakers expressed concern that marine industry was being squeezed out. Their fears are based on the dramatic transformation of the lower reaches of the Tyne. Mr Hamilton said he applauded much of the redevelopment spearheaded by the government-backed Tyne and Wear Development Corporation, but was worried about loss of berths and industrial frontage near the river mouth. Mr Alastair Balls, chief executive of the corporation, which has financed offshore industry, ship repair, fishing and port developments, denied the criticisms. 'There's not a single site we have taken out of industrial use where a viable project has been supported by a business,' he said. South Tyneside Council, which is supporting a proposal to turn the disused Hawthorn Leslie site at Hebburn, the country's only intact shipyard dating from the early 20th century, into a hotel and leisure development, said: 'We have to work with the world as it is, not . . . as we'd like it to be.' The association's tough new line was warmly welcomed by Mr Paul Winch, an unemployed naval architect who has spent five years campaigning for a yachtbuilding yard and maritime craft centre at the Hawthorn Leslie site. 'There's this label which had been put on the river, that it's a lame duck,' he said. 'But now I'm very heartened.'
