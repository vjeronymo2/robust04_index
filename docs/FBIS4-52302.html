BFN [Text] Seoul, May 7 KYODO -- Japan's ambassador to South Korea met with Korea's foreign minister Saturday [7 May] to try to defuse some of the outrage over remarks by Japanese Justice Minister Shigeto Nagano about Japan's role during World War II. Foreign Minister Han Sung-chu in a meeting with Japanese Ambassador to South Korea Toshio Goto, conveyed to the Japanese Government the concerns and regrets that Seoul has over Nagano's remarks, Foreign Ministry spokesman Chang Ki-ho said. Goto told Han that the official position of the Japanese Government will be taken after Prime Minister Tsutomu Hata returns home Saturday evening from an overseas trip. The ambassador also said that he received an instruction from the Japanese Government to convey to Seoul that Japan takes the official stance that South Korea and Japan must build future-oriented relations while looking squarely at the past. Han, in responding to the ambassador's remarks, said that Japan should take proper action to prevent any recurrence of the matter and heal the wounds of the past, adding, "South Koreans got hurt seriously and expressed indignation over the remarks." On Friday, Han summoned the ambassador to the Foreign Ministry and expressed "shock" and deep regrets over Nagano's remarks, demanding that Japan clarify the remarks and present a clear position. In Tokyo, the Japanese justice minister offered Saturday to resign after setting off a flurry of international condemnation with his remarks that Japan was not an aggressor during World War II and the 1937 Nanjing Massacre was a fabrication. Following the invasion of the then Chinese capital of Nanjing in December 1937, Imperial Japanese Army troops slaughtered more than 300,000 Chinese civilians, according to Chinese estimates. Japanese historians, however, tend to cite lower estimates. According to estimates given at the war crimes tribunal in Tokyo, about 42,000 civilians were killed in Nanjing and more than 100,000 civilians and prisoners of war were killed in the vicinity over the following six weeks. Also on Friday, South Korea lodged a strong protest with Japan over Nagano's separate remark terming "comfort women" as licensed prostitutes. Yu Byung-woo, director general of the Foreign Ministry's Asian Affairs Bureau, summoned Japanese Embassy official Hiroshi Shigeta on Friday and expressed deep regrets over Nagano's remarks on the "comfort women," whom the Japanese military took to front line brothels during World War II, spokesman Chang said. Nagano made the remarks in an interview with KYODO NEWS SERVICE on Tuesday. Historians estimate that as many as 200,000 women, most of them from the Korean Peninsula, were taken to wartime brothels to provide sex as "comfort women," as they were euphemistically called, for Japanese soldiers.
