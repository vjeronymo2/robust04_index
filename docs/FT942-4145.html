940611 FT 11 JUN 94 / Stockholm doubts over Vienna vote Mr Carl Bildt, Sweden's prime minister, said the uphill task of persuading Swedes to vote to join the EU this autumn would be even more difficult if fellow-applicant Austria rejects membership in its referendum tomorrow. But he admitted the severe doubts over the outcome of Sweden's November plebiscite on the issue were unlikely to be dispelled by an Austrian Yes vote, or by Finnish acceptance of membership in a similar referendum due in October. 'We can be affected negatively if things go wrong (in Austria) - not to mention the major catastrophe if things go wrong in both countries that will hold a referendum before us. Whether there will be an effect in the other direction - a positive effect (from a Yes vote) - is more doubtful,' he said in an interview with the Financial Times. Mr Bildt, who originally wanted to hold the Swedish referendum later this month, expressed concern that the EU issue would be eclipsed by the campaign for the general election on September 18, and the formation of a new government in October. This would leave little time afterwards to overturn opinion, at present running strongly against membership. Mr Bildt, who leads a right-centre coalition, was sharply critical of the leadership of the opposition Social Democratic party, the majority of whose supporters are opposed to the EU, for not campaigning more actively for a Yes vote: 'It is strange that the largest party in Sweden has not taken a position on the largest issue in Sweden. . . I fear that we are past the point at which it will be possible for the Social Democrats to get a majority of their people on the Yes side.' Mr Ingvar Carlsson, the Social Democratic leader, denied he was ducking the EU question. But he said the general election should be fought on domestic issues, leaving the EU campaign for afterwards. He said Mr Bildt's strident advocacy of membership was alienating many voters: 'Every time he speaks, we lose more votes to the No side.'
