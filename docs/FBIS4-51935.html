BFN [Text] The Patent Office has begun to study revision of the Patent Law to enable foreign individuals and corporations to present their patent applications in English. So far, patent applications have been allowed to be made only in Japanese. This will make the expedited review of patent applications for technology from overseas applicants possible. The patents issue is an important item on the agenda of the Japan-U.S. economic consultations, and the United States insists that the requirement for applying in Japanese only has worked as a trade barrier. The Patent Office has come to believe the revision will lead to growth of not only applications from the United States, but also of such English-speaking regions as Europe and Oceania. The office plans to present the revision bill to the Diet's extraordinary session in the Fall for enactment from July 1995. The current Patent Law does not accept applications in English, which means overseas inventors can submit their applications only after translating the contents of the invented technology into Japanese. Moreover, at present, correction of submitted translations is not allowed in principle. Sometimes problems occur of misinterpretation as a result of hasty translation done in order to submit applications as quickly as possible and of inventors failing to get patents approved as a result of this. For instance, when a U.S. company applied for a patent on the technology of junctioning nickel alloys, boron [hoso] was mistranslated as shuso [bromine], and the company was unable to win the patent even though it brought the matter to the Tokyo Appellate Court for review. Under the revised law, the foreign applicants: 1) Will be allowed to submit their patent applications in English on the condition that Japanese translations will be submitted within two months after submission of applications; and 2) will be allowed to make corrections on mistranslations, if any, within three months of the Patent Office announcement of "not approving a patent" at the end of a review period. Japan receives about 34,000 patent applications from foreign nationals each year, and the number is close to 10 percent of the total 370,000 applications received nationally. As for types of inventions applied for, those which compete with Japanese-pioneered technologies such as semiconductors are growing. As such, the merits of making quick applications in English without taking the time to translate them into Japanese will be substantial. The Patent Office said: "This means setting up a system wherein patent approval review will be done on terms equal to those for Japanese inventions. It should contribute to alleviating the trade surplus which the United States charges is unfair. In January 1994, the Japanese and U.S. Governments agreed to revise the Patent Laws of the two nations in part toward the goal of unifying their patent systems, and this revision of the law will be the first action taken under the agreement. The next step Japan wants to take is to ask the United States to change its current system of basing its patent rights on who "first invented" to one used by Japan and Europe of basing it on who "first applied."
