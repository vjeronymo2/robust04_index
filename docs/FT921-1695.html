920323 FT 23 MAR 92 / Bush and Kohl committed to early Gatt agreement PRESIDENT George Bush of the US and Chancellor Helmut Kohl of Germany yesterday committed themselves to reaching an early Gatt trade agreement, but disclosed no new breakthrough in the protracted negotiations. Reporting on two days of talks at Camp David, the presidential retreat, Mr Kohl said he wanted an agreement 'by the end of April at the very latest'. But he stressed he had not come to the US in any official negotiating capacity. He said he would report back to the heads of other European governments and to Mr Jacques Delors, president of the European Commission. Prior to Mr Kohl's arrival, the German government spokesman said the Chancellor saw the possibility of a trade-off along the lines that the US should accept a freeze on its sales of cereals substitutes to the EC, while the Community would accept restrictions on its own exports. All Mr Kohl would say was: 'Obviously, when we talk about compromise, both sides have to move.' But he made it clear he felt a Gatt agreement would be an essential precursor of the next economic summit due in Munich in July. Mr Bush had little to say about Gatt in his remarks, except when pushed, at the end of the press conference, with a domestic question contrasting his unwillingness to compromise with Congress over economic reform with his presumed inclination to cut a new world trade deal. He then asserted that a new Gatt agreement would demonstrate his desire to achieve 'change' in the world. Trade, he said, was more beneficial than foreign aid for the neediest. Perhaps the most extraordinary element of the press conference was Mr Kohl's virtual endorsement of Mr Bush for re-election. 'I don't want to interfere in US politics,' the Chancellor began, and then listed reasons why the American people should pay attention to foreign policy. 'Had George Bush not proved to be such a strong leader,' Mr Kohl argued, 'all these dramatic changes would not have taken place in the world.'
