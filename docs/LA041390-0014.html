April 13, 1990, Friday, Home Edition

ISRAEL WHO WILL LEAD?;

PREDICAMENT CRIES OUT FOR A DE GAULLE;

WORD THAT A BROOKLYN RABBI BLOCKED SHIMON PERES' NEW GOVERNING COALITION MAY SPARK POLITICAL REFORM.

The Israeli government has become so fragile and unstable in the 1980s that it can barely decide anything. A mere word from President Bush deploring new settlements in East Jerusalem, a reiteration of familiar U.S. policy, was blamed by some for the fall of the Likud government in March. A few days ago, the embryo coalition that was to have replaced that government fell apart as the prospective prime minister, Shimon Peres, prepared to seek his first vote of confidence in the Knesset.

The story of the coalition's collapse -- at the whim, it was said, of an aged Brooklyn rabbi who has never set foot in Israel -- may be exaggerated. Even so, it is richly symbolic of the besetting problems of Israeli democracy.

In the narrowly divided Knesset, Peres looked to a small sectarian party, the Agudat Israel, to support him. It has become routine practice for would-be prime ministers to court the favor of these mini-parties, many of which have extremist and sectarian views. The Agudat Israel, after weeks of negotiations, was pledged to support Peres. Then, at the last moment, two of its members bolted.

One of them explained that he had sought long-distance counsel from the Rabbi Menachem Schneerson of Brooklyn, a charismatic Hasidic leader in whom many followers see messianic qualities. The rabbi is vehemently opposed to the Labor Party's standing promise to swap land (in the occupied territories) for peace. So much for the Peres coalition.

In Israel, no political consensus has existed on the vital issues for more than 10 years -- since the signing of the Camp David accords, in fact. Meanwhile, the Palestinian revolt in the occupied territories is a continuing embarrassment.

Since the collapse of Menachem Begin during the invasion of Lebanon, in 1982, no government has had the strength to negotiate for a West Bank agreement with the Palestinians or to control a settlement policy which, however technically legal, is an irritant and affront to every American Administration.

The institutional structure of Israeli government, moreover, accentuates rather than eases its divisions. The Knesset confers proportional representation on every fractional party, however insular or irrelevant its views. Every government coalition is therefore a fragile mosaic, no stronger than its most sectarian constituent. This is a recipe for extremism and drift in Israeli politics.

Parallels are never exact, but Israel's predicament recalls, in nearly every particular, the nearly fatal paralysis of France in the '50s due to the Algerian uprising.

When the crisis came, France could call on Charles de Gaulle, a great man of character who cared nothing for public-opinion polls or for the squalid compromises on which factional pseudo-democracy operates. A strong presidency with power to make decisions was grafted onto parliamentary anarchy.

Happily, the spectacle of the Peres disaster seems to have galvanized public opinion in Israel. The idea that the government itself might turn upon the whim of some sectarian rabbi in Brooklyn who has never so much as visited Israel has revolted every sensible Israeli. Reform is in the air. But what kind of reform? A mild dose of De Gaullism would probably be good for Israel. But who will play De Gaulle?
