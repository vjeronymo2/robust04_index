October 8, 1989, Sunday, Home Edition

VYING FOR THE WORLD'S TOP CONDUCTING POST;

MEMBERS OF BERLIN PHILHARMONIC BEGIN VOTING TODAY TO CHOOSE A SUCCESSOR TO THE LATE HERBERT VON KARAJAN

Sometime today, the 120 members of the Berlin Philharmonic Orchestra will gather in their meeting room in the Philharmonie, Berlin's largest concert hall, to begin the final selection of a replacement for music director Herbert von Karajan, who resigned his lifetime position in April, three months before his death at the age of 81.

Speculation on who will be chosen by the Berlin's colleagues, as they refer to one another, has been intense. Last spring, the musicians were besieged after a rumor spread that they were meeting to select Karajan's replacement.

"There was an army of TV people, cameras, lights," said Fergus McWilliam, a Canadian who is one of the orchestra's horn players. "You couldn't even get through them and the more you'd protest, the more they'd disbelieve you."

Newspapers, meanwhile, have offered lists of possible candidates. Lorin Maazel was described as a leading candidate by the New York Times. A Wall Street Journal article said Maazel "heads the short list, which also includes Bernard Haitink and Riccardo Muti."

All of which has not been lost on the musicians, an organized and powerful group. "One local paper wrote a cogent article that the orchestra shouldn't feel pressure from the international press," McWilliam said. "We're used to pressure and being in the limelight and have a pretty good success rate at keeping our traps shut."

Whatever the speculation, it is the musicians who will select the Berlin Philharmonic's next music director, which many -- certainly the Berliners -- see as the world's top conducting job. The Berlin Cultural Senate has a theoretical veto, but there is small chance that it might object to the orchestra's selection.

Although having orchestra members choose their conductor is unheard of in the United States, it has a long German tradition. In America, a music director is usually chosen by a search committee, which consists of the orchestra's management, several board members and perhaps a musician selected as the orchestra's representative.

In fact, Maazel would not have been chosen as music director of the Cleveland Orchestra in 1971 had it been up to the musicians, who awarded him only two votes in an informal poll In contrast, most German orchestras have the right to select not only the music director, but the other musicians as well.

The colleagues form two orchestras, the Berlin Philharmonic Orchestra, which receives a $10-million annual subsidy from the Berlin Cultural Senate and plays 110 concerts in Berlin, and the Berlin Philharmonic, which plays tours, makes recordings and broadcasts.

Tradition is the central concern. In fact, the players call the Berlin Philharmonic Orchestra a living tradition, with only Arthur Nikisch, Wilhelm Furtwangler and Karajan as its music directors in this century. Musicians speak proudly of playing in the Berlin style, not only of sound, but of tempo and expression. Most conductors -- although not all -- would rather work with the orchestra than face a battle they will probably lose over trying to make radical changes in the Berlin style.

"We're very set in our ways," said Robert Platt, a trumpet player who is one of two Americans in the orchestra. "Our repertoire is repeated over and over. So when a conductor comes in, we know the piece and are set in our ways. There's not too much he can change."

Selecting a conductor -- although certainly among their most important acts -- is not the only choice that the colleagues make. The entire orchestra is required to attend the auditions of prospective members, who perform in full view of the orchestra, unlike their U.S. counterparts, who usually play their audition behind a screen.

After a probationary period that may last up to two years, the orchestra votes on whether to accept the musician -- for life. While colleagues may theoretically be fired, it is more likely that they will be moved to a less demanding post.

"Life with the orchestra doesn't get dull," said Stefan Jezierski, an American in the orchestra's horn section. "It's one urgent meeting after another, like trying to put 120 individualists together and having them form an orchestra."

Of course, the orchestra's authority grants certain provisions. Karajan had the right to veto the selection of a player he deemed unsuitable and the colleagues have the same right, which they exercised in the widely publicized case of clarinetist Sabine Meyer.

Karajan wanted to accept Meyer, and several players, though acknowledging her technical ability, said the orchestra rejected her because her tone did not fit with the Berlin sound, not because she was a woman, despite news accounts to the contrary.

The orchestra has three women players and in fact, McWilliam said, at the time of the Meyer incident the orchestra had already accepted its first female colleague, Madeleine Carruzzo, a violinist who joined several years before.

Nevertheless, the result was a bitter, divisive conflict which, along with a dispute between Karajan and the orchestra's management, finally resulted in the ailing conductor's resignation.

Make no mistake about it, this is a powerful orchestra. When a performance of Mahler's Ninth Symphony with Claudio Abbado was scheduled in London this summer a few days before the beginning of the orchestra's traditional vacation, the colleagues refused, saying: "This is ridiculous," Jezierski said.

The selection process also reflects the musicians' viewpoint: In June, they were told to compile a "wish list," in which every player submitted the names of three prospective candidates -- any three candidates. For example, just because Muti has signed a lifetime contract with the Philadelphia Orchestra and told a press conference in May that he didn't want the Berlin job doesn't mean he isn't in the running.

"There were no constraints -- anyone," Jezierski said.

Once the colleagues have made their choice, they plan to simply ask him if he wants to be their conductor, secure in the knowledge that they will be nearly impossible to turn down.

But despite all of this, one shouldn't imagine that the process is ticking away with well-oiled efficiency. For if the musicians have the right to select a conductor, it's something they haven't done often.

"We're really doing it for the first time," said McWilliam, who began playing full time with the orchestra in 1985. "The 1955 election was pretty black and white. The orchestra's choice was a confirmation, asking all those who were against Karajan, who was the obvious candidate."

As a result, the "wish list" included some candidates who had only a theoretical presence. Conductors at this level have long careers and often make guest appearances; all of the candidates are well known to the orchestra. As McWilliam said, the colleagues might not wish to offend someone they will have to work with by excluding him even though they feel he would not be appropriate.

"One of the arguments by those who wanted an early decision was that we know them all," McWilliam said. "But others are more inclined to say, like climbing a mountain, that you reach a resting spot before you begin the next leg of the journey."

In general terms, the list was to be narrowed to eight "favorites" and ultimately to two or three candidates from which the final selection would be made. But this is not necessarily being done, depending on the source.

"How it is going to work is very hard to say," said Platt. "The plan has already changed two or three times."

Although there is an emphasis on keeping the orchestra mostly German as far as players' nationalities are concerned, the conductor's nationality is not so important. Karajan was Austrian and only one possible candidate, Carlos Kleiber, is German. At least one candidate, Muti, does not speak German, but although this poses some difficulties, it is manageable.

"It doesn't really matter what they're saying if the message is clear," McWilliam said. "Some work in Italian, some in French; it can be a mishmash of four languages in one sentence. It's not a big issue."

There is certainly no unanimity as to the first choice among the players. Although no one was willing to name the eight favorites directly, Jezierski, saying "Anything is really guessing," listed the those who might have received the most votes -- in no special order -- as Kleiber, James Levine, Muti, Haitink, Zubin Mehta, Maazel, Abbado, Leonard Bernstein and Daniel Barenboim. "It could be any combination of those," he said, "Maybe Seiji Ozawa."

In keeping with the Berlin philosophy, Jezierski offered insights on some of the candidates, but in positive terms:

* "Maazel has a lot of support. He's a genius, but I don't know if he's willing to make the commitment. It would be hard to find someone better. But he doesn't have the position at this time."

* "Muti and Levine have done some good concerts. Muti might prefer to be king in Philadelphia because the music director in the United States calls the shots. But no one knows if he wants to come to Berlin."

* "Bernstein hasn't expressed an interest, but if he'd want to make a commitment it would be very interesting. A lot of people in the orchestra would like it."

* "Mehta is also a possibility. He might get some votes."

* "Ozawa will probably also get some votes. Everyone respects him a lot, but it's hard to say."

The musicians are expecting another media onslaught once the choice has been made. "All the details will be under strict secrecy," McWilliam said. "There's an image of being in a locked room with no phones, unable to talk to our wives. We'll probably face that army of TV people again."

And whomever the colleagues choose, it will be someone who wants to work with the orchestra as it is.

"Tradition is vital to the orchestra's identity, to it's self-image," McWilliam said. "It's a responsibility handed down to us, a heritage we must preserve. We must find an appropriate guardian for tradition and also someone who can chart new paths, take the orchestra into the next century without a terrible break with the past."

He added: "It's a breathtaking opportunity to be there when the choice is made."
