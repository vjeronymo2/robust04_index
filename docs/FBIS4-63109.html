BFN [Report by Kenneth Gooding in London and John Lloyd in Moscow: "De Beers in Russian Diamond Accord"] [Text] De Beers of South Africa stepped back from a potentially serious conflict yesterday by agreeing to consider changes to its $5bn (3.3bn pound) diamond marketing contract with Russia. At the same time the Russian government representatives agreed "to take necessary steps" to stop rough (uncut) diamonds leaking from Russia through unofficial channels to the west where they threatened to destabilise the market. Trade sources suggested that, after the Russians were publicly rebuked by De Beers in December for breaking the contract, the flow of unofficial diamonds eased but recently it had been increasing again. There were sighs of relief in the diamond trade yesterday because Russia supplies about 25 per cent of the diamonds De Beers sells through its London-based Central Selling Organisation [CSO]. The CSO controls more than 80 per cent of the world's trade in rough diamonds. In 1990 De Beers lent Russia $1bn, shipped some of the Russian stockpile to London as collateral and signed the five-year marketing agreement. This permits Russia to sell 5 per cent of its rough diamond output directly to the west for "market testing". While there are no indications that Russia wished to end the contract with the world's most successful cartel, some officials have been pressing for the federation to keep a bigger share. Mr Yevgeniy Bychkev, head of Komdragment, the Russian diamond and precious stones committee, who was in the Russian negotiating team this week, is demanding that Russia be able to keep 25 per cent of its rough diamond trade. The De Beers team was headed by Mr Nicholas Oppenheimer, its deputy chairman and chairman of the CSO. According to diamond trade sources he stepped in at the last moment because relationships had become strained. Mr Gary Ralfe, who negotiated the Russian contract and publicly castigated Russia for breaking it, was previously expected to head the De Beers team. He was in Moscow with Mr Oppenheimer, however. Mr Richard Wake-Walker, previously De Beers' representative in Moscow, quit the group recently after being replaced by Mr Ray Clark. The Diamantaire trade newsletter suggested Mr Wake-Walker "advocated a flexible negotiating strategy with Russia while Mr Ralfe and Mr Clark appear to be taking a tough, no-concessions approach". The large delegation with which Russia faced Mr Oppenheimer included representatives from the Sakha diamond-producing region, as well as the Russian diamond committee and the Ministries of Finance and Foreign Economic Affairs -- among whom the right approach is still being debated. Russia's need for increased income from exports has been aggravated by the recent fall in the oil price, its main export earner -- and by the virtual disappearance of its manufactured goods from the world market. A joint communique yesterday said "the parties remain committed to the existing agreement, pending their agreeing on any modification. They are also committed to the stability of the international diamond market and to taking the necessary steps to curtail Russian rough gem diamonds reaching the market outside the current marketing arrangements."
