November 23, 1989, Thursday, Valley Edition

ANTELOPE VALLEY DELIVERS 'REPORT CARD' ON HIGH SCHOOLS;

EDUCATION: THE DISTRICT'S PARENTS ARE THE FIRST IN THE COUNTY TO BENEFIT FROM A NEW STATE LAW REQUIRING EVALUATIONS.

Parents of high school students in the Antelope Valley this week will become the first in Los Angeles County to get annual report cards assessing the performance of their children's schools, the result of a new statewide requirement.

The Antelope Valley Union High School District on Wednesday began mailing out nearly 8,600 "school accountability report cards" it prepared in compliance with a provision of Proposition 98, which was narrowly approved by state voters in November, 1988.

The proposition, sponsored by the California Teachers Assn. and other education groups, was intended to channel more state money into schools and carried the "report card" provision as an added inducement for voters.

It required every public school, from elementary to high school, to prepare an annual report covering 13 areas of school performance, including student test scores, dropout rates, class sizes, spending per student, textbook quality, counseling services, teacher data, and school safety and cleanliness.

State and local school officials said they believe the Antelope Valley district is the first in Los Angeles County and one of only several districts statewide to distribute the reports. Other districts must follow suit before the end of the school year next spring.

The Antelope Valley reports painted a mostly upbeat picture of the district's six campuses. "I think we're very happy with them for a first go-around," said Robert Sanchez, the district's assistant superintendent for educational services.

The four-page reports vary somewhat from school to school. But all generally give a positive, although not always telling, picture of the campuses.

For instance, among the six school reports, only one mentioned gangs. However, concern about school security and mounting gang problems have dominated the debate among district board members for months and one campus had a gang melee recently. The district is considering adopting a tough anti-gang student dress code and a host of security measures.

The last paragraph of the report on Antelope Valley High School noted, "Although the school is thought to be safe in general, there is a growing insecurity among the staff and students about the number of gang members and outbreaks of fights."

Sanchez, who oversaw the preparation of the reports, acknowledged the lack of discussion about gang problems but insisted the district was not trying to gloss over the issue.

He characterized this year's version "as basically a numbers report card" and promised that next year's would have "a more comprehensive approach to school climate and safety. It was not an intent to escape the issue entirely."

On other topics, the reports addressed the district's problems with overcrowding, high dropout rates and a shortage of substitute teachers. They detailed district efforts to deal with those issues. They also referred to increases in students test scores, which generally improved throughout the district.

Under state guidelines, school districts must produce the reports and make them available to parents or others upon request. Districts are not required to send them out. But Sanchez said his district chose to mail them so they would be seen by as many parents as possible.

The Los Angeles Unified School District, the second largest in the country with more than 600 schools, will not release its reports until early April, said Joan Evans, an administrative consultant for the district. And Los Angeles, like many larger districts, does not plan to mail out the reports because of the cost and work involved, Evans said.

Originally, many school officials were unhappy about the added burden of producing the annual reports, which all schools must release in November in future years. But many have come to see the reports as a way to bolster needed public support for school issues, education officials said.
