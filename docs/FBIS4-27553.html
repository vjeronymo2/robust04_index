BFN [Text] Tokyo, June 2 KYODO -- Chinese Foreign Trade and Economic Minister Wu Yi on Thursday [2 Jun] asked Japan to exercise prudence in taking a unilateral measure to curb textile imports from China. Meeting Japanese International Trade and Industry Minister Eijiro Hata in Tokyo, Wu said, "China is concerned about Japan's invocation of a discriminatory curb on textile imports," according to ministry officials. "Beijing cannot accept such a step," Wu was quoted as saying. The Chinese trade minister was referring to a set of rules recently worked out by a Japanese Government panel for activating a safeguard measure on textile imports under the multifiber agreement (MFA). The rules are designed to protect the Japanese textile industry from possible damage following massive textile imports, especially from China. Japanese trade minister Hata said Tokyo is prepared to discuss the issue with China on a working-level. Wu said China will strive to make its textile exports orderly, the officials told reporters. The remark hints at Beijing's preparedness to introduce a voluntary curb on textile exports to Japan in order to prevent Japanese invocation of the safeguard measure. During the one-hour meeting, Wu requested that Japan map out a framework for its fourth yen-loan program for China, the ministry officials said. Hata replied that Japan will work out specifics of 60 projects being selected under the five-year lending program from 1996 "as early as possible," they said. Hata was quoted as saying Japan will support China's early admission to the General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade (GATT). But there remain various problems to clear the way for Beijing's smooth entry to GATT, including China's quantitative import restrictions, dual pricing systems and inconsistent enforcement of administrative rules between the central government and local governments, Hata said. "China should reform these domestic systems so that its economy can be incorporated into the global economic system at an early time," he said. Wu arrived in Tokyo on Tuesday for an eight-day visit to Japan aimed at encouraging Japanese investment in China. She is the first Chinese cabinet minister to visit Japan since the government of Prime Minister Tsutomu Hata was formed in late April.
