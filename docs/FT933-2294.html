930920 FT 20 SEP 93 / Umpire refuses to play games: The race for the 2000 Olympic Games will be decided on Thursday / A look at the prizes to be won 'I HAD three letters from Chinese people this morning,' said Dame Mary Glen Haig last week. 'But it doesn't make any difference who writes.' After 11 years as one of the UK's two representatives to the International Olympic Committee - the other is the Princess Royal - Dame Mary is used to resisting the blandishments of Olympics-hungry cities such as Beijing. 'When Tokyo wanted the games I got postcards from Japanese schoolchildren,' she said. For Dame Mary, who fenced for Britain in four consecutive Olympics from 1948, the decision on venue is simple. You consider the skills of the local organisers. You ensure they have the required money and ability. 'Then you look at it from the athlete's point of view,' she said. 'How far, for example, is the athletes' village from the arena.' To her, the process seems to be rather tedious. 'The thing that captures the imagination,' she said, 'is working with the IOC medical commission, which deals among other things with the misuse of drugs by athletes.' Even this issue has been linked with the bids this year. Chinese women athletes have broken world records by huge margins, triggering speculation of help from drugs. At 75, Dame Mary must retire from the IOC after this week's vote. Her businesslike self-control only wavered when she talked of what the atmosphere would be like when this year's voting was over. There would be an end-of-term feeling, she said, 'and a party.'
