Language: English Article Type:BFN [Text] Moscow, March 1 (AFP) -- Bosnian Serb leader Radovan Karadzic and Russian Foreign Minister Andrey Kozyrev announced Tuesday [1 March] they had agreed to open the airport in the besieged Bosnian city of Tuzla for shipments of humanitarian aid. Kozyrev and Karadzic told reporters that Russian "observers" would be deployed at the airport to guarantee it was used for delivery of aid including a Russian humanitarian aid shipment to be flown there soon. "Russian observers... will guarantee control under UNPROFOR and make sure the airport is used for humanitarian and not military deliveries," Kozyrev told reporters following two hours of talks with the Bosnian Serb leader. UNPROFOR is the United Nations Protection Force. Karadzic said his meeting with Kozyrev was "not easy" and the Russian minister described the talks as "difficult." Neither gave a date for the opening of the airport at Tuzla, but Kozyrev said a shipment of humanitarian aid from Russia would be flown into the airport "soon." The city, including the airport, is surrounded by Bosnian Serb forces. The airport has been closed for more than a year and pressure for it to be opened was stepped up at the North Atlantic Treaty Organization (NATO) summit in January when Britain and France called for its reopening. Meanwhile UN Secretary General Butrus Butrus-Ghali's special envoy in the former Yugoslavia, Yasushi Akashi, visited Tuzla airport early Tuesday, his spokesman said in Zagreb. It was Akashi's first visit to Tuzla, where he arrived from the Croatian port of Split early Tuesday. Spokesman Michael Williams said, "He'll be looking at the situation with regard to the airport and it remains our strong hope that the airport can be opened soon for humanitarian purposes." "He has made a tour of the airfield this morning, and this afternoon he'll be meeting with local and regional authorities," Williams said. "At the end of the afternoon he'll be visiting a refugee center in Tuzla." Williams said that in Tuzla itself the situation remained relatively calm. A total of 14 shells hit the airfield Monday, of which about half fell close to the main building.
