March 24, 1989, Friday, Home Edition

CANDY MAKERS EXPAND THEIR EASTER OFFERINGS

America's big chocolate makers are trying to sweeten their Easter profits, and analysts say the expansion of special holiday products will increase sales for everyone.

"There was a time when many of the major companies were not involved in seasonal-type packaging," said Jan Kitt, associate publisher of The Confection magazine, based in Orlando, Fla. Small producers of chocolate bunnies and candy eggs provided most of the specialty Easter products.

While industry watchers don't have statistics on who controls what part of the Easter market, they agree that times have changed. Chocolate giants such as Hershey Foods Corp. and M&M-Mars have entered the fray with seasonal versions of their regular products, and manufacturers are giving more attention to special holiday displays, Kitt said.

"More and more packages are geared to the holiday season," she said.

Easter Variations

The National Confectioners Assn. is projecting sales of $815 million of Easter chocolate and candy sales this year, a 2% increase from 1988.

"Easter's been doing very well," said Dick O'Connell, president of the McLean, Va.-based Chocolate Manufacturers Assn. "Part of it is we're going back to more traditional thinking in terms of holidays. More attention is being paid to chocolate bunnies and fancy Easter eggs and jelly beans -- all the things that go with Easter."

Hershey and Mars, which together control most of the domestic chocolate market, offer Easter variations of their popular year-round products. Hershey has been marketing Kisses wrapped in Christmas and Easter colors for years, but its recent acquisition of Cadbury's, which makes several chocolate Easter egg products, should boost holiday sales, said spokesman Carl Andrews.

"The company found that there's quite a market for special packaging for existing products," said analyst R. Bentley Offutt of Offutt Securities Inc.
