April 3, 1989, Monday, Home Edition

THE WEST MUST DECIDE IF IT WOULD FEEL SECURE WITH AN INDEPENDENT EASTERN EUROPE

Secretary of State James A. Baker III's remark last week that he is considering a proposal that "a new political arrangement for Eastern Europe" be discussed with Moscow brings Henry Kissinger back onto the U.S. foreign-policy scene. The proposal is Kissinger's. It has not been made public.

Baker characterized the proposal as offering the Soviet Union a "type of formalized status in Eastern Europe" in exchange for greater freedom for the East European governments to run their own affairs. The West would promise not to exploit the situation to the Soviet Union's disadvantage.

Baker went on to ask, however, if this is a sensible thing to do "when countries in the region, like Poland and Hungary, are already moving towards a more Western orientation in their economies and political systems." His evident inclination to leave well enough alone is defensible, but possibly too complacent.

It is true that the overactive and overambitious approach to complicated problems is dangerous, and has produced much grief for America. It is also true that the tide is running in the West's favor in Eastern Europe. But this is a turbulent tide with an undertow; it could, to press the metaphor, end in a riptide.

There is merit in Kissinger's concern that the United States look for a way constructively to influence events in Eastern Europe and reduce some of the risks there. The question is how best to do it, and that depends on how you see the future of Eastern Europe.

There are two fundamentally different views of Eastern Europe held in the West. One says that the Soviet Union is there to stay. Like it or not, Eastern Europe is part of a Soviet ideological and security system that Moscow will not alter. As Leonid Brezhnev said to Alexander Dubcek after the 1968 Soviet invasion of Czechoslovakia, "Your frontier . . . is that of the Socialist camp. It is the result of the Second World War for which the U.S.S.R. paid a high cost. The Soviet Politburo does not have the right to place in jeopardy the results of that war."

This view of Eastern Europe was held in the State Department a decade and a half ago, when Kissinger was in charge of U.S. foreign policy. At that time one of his associates described the aim of U.S. policy thusly: to "strive for an evolution that makes the relationship between the East Europeans and the Soviet Union an organic one" -- hence more stable, less prone to crisis.

The other view of Eastern Europe holds that it is unreasonable and unhistorical to think that ancient and culturally autonomous nations -- Hungary, Poland and Bohemia (Czechoslovakia) -- historically linked to West European civilization, can indefinitely be deprived of their independence by the Soviet Union. The attempt to do so, in this view, is the cause of enduring instability in Europe, and in East-West relations.

Despite 40 years of military occupation, the Soviet Union has failed to win the allegiance of any significant part of public opinion in the region. It enjoys less sympathy today than it did in the 1940s, when there were real, revolutionary Communist parties in Eastern Europe that genuinely believed in the Soviet Union as a workers' state and as the promise of a new and more just human order. Nothing remotely like that exists today. Every vital political force in Eastern Europe is directed against the Soviet Union and toward national independence.

If you hold this view, you must conclude that the proper objective of the Western policy-maker is to bring about a replacement of the European security system, which rests ultimately on military compulsion, by one based on consent and mutual interest.

Western guarantees of legitimate Soviet security concerns, and of NATO non-interference and non-exploitation of a changed situation, with mutual arms reductions, troop withdrawals, confidence-building measures, etc., all are ways to achieve that objective. But the objective must be clearly understood. It is not to make it possible for the Soviet Union to safely stay in Eastern Europe. It is to make it possible for it to safely leave.

The lack of policy initiative from Washington has produced an uneasy situation in Western Europe, hinging on the debate -- or the lack of an open debate -- over Germany's role in Eastern Europe's evolution. The situation is developing more rapidly than Washington seems to appreciate, and West German and French policy-makers are concerned.

Even if Baker chooses to let the situation develop without American intervention, the question of objective still must be answered. Do we look for security in an independent Eastern Europe? Or do we see an independent Eastern Europe as a threat to stability, hence to security? Kissinger has one answer. Others have another. The merit of his proposal is that it has confronted us with that question.
