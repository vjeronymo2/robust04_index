April 19, 1989, Wednesday, Home Edition

OTHER COMMENTARY / EXCERPTS;

LIKE SO MANY TEXAS POLITICIANS, WRIGHT'S RUT LANDED HIM IN MUD

It's not any fun unmasking Jim Wright anymore.

There is a certain delight in depomping the more gaseous representatives of the political species, but only when they are fully inflated. Once they start twisting in the wind, the party's over. Where's the sport? When the old amusement gives way to the first pangs of pity, there's nothing left but the formalities. Whatever verdict his colleagues in the House may pass down, The Hon. Jim Wright has been found out; the rest is anticlimax.

"The Wizard of Oz" is a great book -- but only until Toto draws back the curtain and the Wonderful Wizard of Oz is revealed as a poor, pitiable mortal with a good public relations program. Then all the author has to do is tie a few loose ends together and get Dorothy back to Kansas. The suspense has gone, and with it any sense of indignation or satisfaction.

It's hard not to think of Jim Wright as the Speaker of Oz, and by now he incites not scorn but pity. Oh, he's still pontificating. His denials/excuses/sentimentalities are as numerous as ever, but the curtain has been drawn back. Soon even congressmen will have to admit the transparent, or deny it so unconvincingly that they will indict their own judgment -- and their own institution.

L. Frank Baum could have written the Speaker's lines at this point: "I didn't do it, but if I did, I didn't mean to, and doesn't everybody do this sort of thing, and how dare you?" If the audience is supposed to feel pity for this poor, defrocked wizard, it cannot hope to match the Speaker's own self-pity, which is now as thick as his bluster was before. He's reached the point of rolling out his wife and family. It is not pleasant to watch. Pathos has become bathos.

There is still something in this petty affair that is satisfying to expose, and that is the attitude among the oh-so-knowing in press and politics that it really doesn't matter if a Speaker of the House takes a little baksheesh. It is explained sotto voce, just between us sophisticates, that this fuss over ethics is a luxury, considering the really important issues facing the country -- like the drug war, environmental protection and what to do with Oliver North.

It's a terribly sophisticated attitude, with the emphasis on terribly. Because it shows not the vaguest understanding of the importance of an ethical standard in leaders and thus in a society. Has Watergate been so long ago that the country has forgotten that a government without character is soon enough a government without conviction or credibility? Ethics is not an afterthought in society, much as it has been treated that way in this one. It is the foundation, as those who ignore it will find out soon enough. And that is the moral of this story.
