Becirovic: "Private Operators Are Ruining the Dinar"] [Text] Podgorica, 5 Apr -- Montenegrin Prime Minister Milo Djukanovic today invited bankers to Podgorica for a discussion of what is happening to the money supply, saying that disturbing problems have been arising in recent days after the favorable results in the first stage of stabilizing the monetary system. According to Djukanovic, these problems require urgent measures. He mentioned scalping, of which he said that the "participant banks" are not the only ones. Montenegrin Deputy Prime Minister Krunoslav Vukcevic asked the bankers to present their view of how the currency exchange business operates. He presented figures to the effect that by 4 April Montenegrin banks had purchased 13.2 million German marks [DM]. When the sale of foreign exchange is taken into account, there was a positive result of about DM12 million. In view of the fact that the result for the entire country is DM300 million, the share of Montenegrin banking is not really at an enviable level in that amount. He also pointed to the phenomenon of scalping. Petar Vujovic, director of the Montenegrin branch of the National Bank, mentioned other figures, saying that the purchases of foreign exchange in Montenegro amounted to about DM26 million, but that banks in Montenegro headquartered in Serbia are not turning over their entire purchases to the Montenegrin branch of the National Bank of Yugoslavia. "When we asked the Beobanka office about the purchases of foreign exchange, they told us that headquarters gave out the figures. Later they told us that they were not prohibited from giving out figures, but they could not provide them," Vujovic said. Radmila Kalezic, Jugobanka manager in Podgorica, stated categorically that her bank was not engaging in scalping. She said that the purchasing of foreign exchange had been good up until 10 days ago, but since that time things had changed, and her bank had a deficit of DM397,000 in sales and purchases of foreign exchange. "The tellers noticed that the same people are coming to buy foreign exchange again and again in the course of the day. Those who earlier purchased it on the street have now moved to the banks. It seems that private operators are not promptly turning in their cash receipts for the day," Kalezic said, calling for the government to monitor strictly the cash flow of private firms. Milorad Lakovic, director of Pljevaljska Bank, was also categorical about the private operators. He said that the dealers are working for the private operators and that the black market is already beginning to operate, and Cetkovic, the director of Jugobanka in Herceg-Novi, presented the figure that until a few days ago the ratio in the purchase and sale of foreign exchange was 4:1 to the advantage of the bank, but now that ratio is even. He said that the private sector is probably engaged in purchasing from farmers, so that the movement of the money is not being monitored, and certain new people are coming to bank windows and doing so several times a day. The bankers in Kotor say that a check should be run on how people with a monthly salary of no more than 100 dinars can come several times a day and present 100 dinars to buy marks. When bank personnel notice this and offer another currency instead of marks, these people call for the assistance of the financial police, and the officials must then pay them. It is obvious that scalping and the dealers are slowly becoming reality once again.
