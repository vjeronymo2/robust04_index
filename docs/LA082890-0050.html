August 28, 1990, Tuesday, Home Edition

36 OF IRAQ'S 55 EMBASSY STAFFERS EXPELLED BY U.S.

The Bush Administration on Monday expelled two-thirds of the Iraqi diplomatic staff in Washington and encouraged 34 other nations with embassies in Kuwait to take similar steps against Iraq.

In Kuwait and Iraq, meanwhile, Iraqi military authorities intensified efforts to round up foreigners. State Department officials said that over the weekend, seven more private American citizens were taken into Iraqi custody.

The U.S. Embassy in Kuwait city is now operating only with its own water and food supplies, which State Department spokeswoman Margaret Tutwiler said are sufficient for U.S. diplomats to hold out "at least several days" against the siege by Iraqi troops. The embassy has been operating with its own electricity generator since troops cut off its supplies, water and power from outside.

President Bush told a news conference in Maine that the United States is requiring Iraqi diplomats to leave the country by Thursday, and he contrasted that order with Iraq's policy of preventing American diplomats from leaving.

"Nobody will be held against their will," Bush said of the Iraqi diplomats. "They're all free to leave."

The State Department spokeswoman said the expulsions were in response to Iraq's recent violations of international law, including its efforts to annex Kuwait and, "in particular, the illegal order to close our embassy in Kuwait."

Until now, Iraq has been allowed to maintain 55 of its nationals -- including both diplomats and non-diplomatic staff members -- at its embassy in Washington. The order Monday requires Iraq to cut its embassy back to 19 people.

U.S. officials acknowledged they chose the figure of 19 because it matches the total number of American diplomats still working in Iraq and Kuwait. About 55 other American diplomats who had been working out of the U.S. Embassy in Kuwait left that nation with the intention of leaving the region but were barred by Iraqi officials from leaving Baghdad.

U.S. officials hinted broadly that if more American diplomats are forced out of the U.S. embassies in Kuwait and Iraq, more Iraqis will be expelled from Washington.

The President told reporters that the United States is "trying to keep a reasonable parallel in terms of numbers."

Mohammed Mashat, the Iraqi ambassador to Washington, told reporters that the U.S. decision to expel most of his staff was "uncalled for, unfair and unjust. We feel we have been victimized once more." Mashat said the U.S. action will reduce the effectiveness of Iraqi diplomats in Washington.

The U.S. order requires seven diplomats and 29 non-diplomatic staff members at Iraq's embassy to pack up and leave the United States within 72 hours -- or by 4:30 p.m. Thursday. Included in the order is the embassy's entire commercial section, which is responsible for promoting Iraq's trade with the United States.

"We don't feel there's much need for the Iraqis to be here trying to buy at this particular time," Tutwiler told a State Department briefing.

In addition, the State Department imposed a series of new restrictions on the remaining Iraqi personnel at the embassy.

They will be prohibited from traveling more than 25 miles from Washington, and they and their families will be given only single-entry visas to the United States instead of the customary multiple-entry visas that allow them to enter and leave the country virtually at will.

Furthermore, Tutwiler said, use of Iraqi Embassy funds for Iraqi students and for other humanitarian needs will be "closely controlled." She did not explain what this last restriction will mean.

The new restrictions do not apply to Iraq's United Nations mission in New York, which will be allowed to continue operating at full strength.

On Sunday night, Deputy Assistant Secretary of State David Mack called in representatives of the 34 countries to tell them about the new U.S. move against Iraqi diplomats. Those nations have refused to close their embassies in Kuwait despite threats from Iraq.

"The United States is urging other governments, particularly those whose embassies have been affected by Iraqi actions in Kuwait, to consider taking similar actions against Iraq's official and non-official presence in their countries," Tutwiler explained.

Deputy Secretary of State Lawrence S. Eagleburger summoned Mashat, the Iraqi ambassador, to the State Department at 9 a.m. Monday to inform him of the expulsion order. Mashat himself was not covered by the order and will be permitted to remain here.

"I was surprised when I was informed of this action," Mashat said later. " . . . We will try to be cooperative with the United States, because we have nothing against the American people."

State Department officials said Monday that over the weekend, two private Americans in Kuwait were taken from their apartments by Iraqi soldiers. "Once again, the Iraqis knocked on the door, and when the two answered they were taken to the Regency Hotel in Kuwait city," said Tutwiler.

Four other Americans also were picked up in Kuwait on Saturday and brought to the same hotel, where they and the others were held by Iraqi authorities. State Department officials said they did not know how the four had been captured.

In addition, one private American in Iraq was taken away from his place of work. U.S. officials did not know where this American was taken.

In all, State Department officials say, Iraqi authorities have rounded up 42 Americans inside Iraq and another 21 in Kuwait. They are the only Americans under direct Iraqi detention or control, out of the total of about 3,000 Americans who were barred from leaving Iraq and Kuwait.

"In Kuwait, food availability appears adequate, although stocks of some items, especially fruits and vegetables, are not plentiful," Tutwiler said Monday. "In Iraq, we know that internal food prices are going through the roof for certain commodities like rice and sugar, and we know that there are bread lines in Iraq."

DIPLOMATS AT EMBASSIES IN KUWAIT

Many countries have refused to bow to Iraq's demands to pull their embassies out of Kuwait and move their diplomats to Baghdad. Here is the status of envoys at the embassies of a number of key countries:

Country: United States

Status: Defied order to close; embassy surrounded. Ambassador W. Nathaniel Howell remains insidewith staff of nine.

Country: Soviet Union

Status: Moscow said all Soviet citizens in Kuwait including diplomats had left, leaving the embassy empty. But this did not mean the Kremlin had ended Soviet representation.

Country: Britain

Status: Defied order to close; embassy reported surrounded. Four diplomats inside, including Ambassador Michael Weston and Consul Larry Banks.

Country: Belgium

Status: Ambassador Marcel van Roey and a first secretary staff the embassy.

Country: Denmark

Status: Ambassador Birger Dan Nielsen told not to leave embassy voluntarily but what this means has been left to his discretion, according to the Foreign Ministry.

Country: France

Status: Six diplomats still in Kuwait, led by Charge d'Affaires Jean-Pierre Galtier.

Country: Greece

Status: Charge d'Affaires Dimitris Zavoritis remains with a staff of four Greeks and three Kuwaitis.

Country: Italy

Status: Ambassador Marco Colombo remains with his second Secretary.

Country: Netherlands

Status: Four diplomats and four dependents remain, led by Ambassador Joop Veling.

Country: Spain

Status: Trade attache Juan Jose Buitrago is in charge of the embassy. (Ambassador Juan Jose Arboli was in Spain at time of the invasion and was refused permission to return.)

Country: West Germany

Status: Ambassador Claus Soenksen remains in the mission. Bonn refused to say how many diplomats are inside.

Country: Egypt

Status: Embassy functioning with staff of 20, including Ambassador Saeed Rifaat.

Country: Bahrain

Status: Bahrain refuses to close mission. The ambassador and another diplomat remain in Kuwait.

Country: Morocco

Status: Said its diplomats would stay.

Country: Japan

Status: Defied orders to close embassy; not clear how many diplomats are left.

Country: Iran

Status: Embassy open.

Country: Canada

Status: Bolstered staff with diplomat from Baghdad, for total of six.

Country: Bangladesh

Status: Ambassador Shahabuddin Ahmad believed to have remained with four diplomats.

Country: Czechoslovakia

Status: Ambassador Strakos was ordered to stay put unless threatened with violence. Accompanied by one other diplomat.

Country: East Germany

Status: Ambassador Kurt Merkel and his wife remain with orders to keep the mission open.

Country: Poland

Status: The embassy will stay open. Charge d'Affaires Jan Dworak leads the mission with three officials of State Trade Office.

Country: Sweden

Status: Two diplomats still in Kuwait, including Ambassador Ingolf Kiesow.

Country: Norway

Status: Ambassador Hans Wilhelm Longva, two other envoys remain in embassy.

Country: Switzerland

Status: Charge d'Affaires Franco Besomi and one other diplomat still in place.

Country: Thailand

Status: Three embassy officials still in Kuwait, headed by Charge d'Affaires Atsiphol Chabchitchaidol.

Source: Reuters
