May 15, 1990, Tuesday, San Diego County Edition

REVIEW;

POWAY CENTER EARNS POP KUDOS

Opening nights aren't supposed to go this smoothly, particularly the premiere production in a brand-new venue.

You always expect at least some complications: a malfunctioning sound system, an out-of-sync spotlight, a problem with getting people seated on time, a miscued curtain drop.

But when the Poway Center for the Performing Arts celebrated its grand opening Saturday night with a black-tie benefit concert by pop singer-songwriter Melissa Manchester, patrons were treated to a remarkably glitch-free production, from start to finish.

It was as much a tribute to the staff's careful prep work as a good omen for the facility's future as a cultural center.

The sound system worked perfectly, beaming Manchester's voice into every nook and cranny of the 800-seat theater, regardless of whether she was belting out her parade of late-1970s, early-'80s Top 40 hits with full-band accompaniment or singing softly into the microphone, a cappella.

The spotlight tracked her every move, even when she was rapidly pacing the stage during an inspired rendition of the gospelish "Oh Heaven."

The lobby lights flickered on and off a few minutes prior to show time, giving lollygagging patrons a little encouragement to step along.

Flashing their tickets to the ushers, they were then promptly directed, and sometimes escorted, to their seats -- the ushers having a good handle on the floor plan.

And, at the end of the concert, the curtain came down at precisely the right moment, just as Manchester and her band members were taking their final bows.

Even the pre- and post-concert receptions went off without a hitch.

Before the show, there was an ample supply of Champagne, wine and hors d'oeuvres. And exiting patrons were handed boxes of truffles as they made their way into the lobby for a nightcap of fresh-brewed coffee and cookies.

The only complaints heard all night were that the "Telephones" sign in the lobby pointed to an empty hole in the wall and that the absence of working doorstops somewhat impeded the exodus.

And these were but minor, easily resolved annoyances with the facility itself, which was built at a cost of $8.4 million by the city of Poway, the Poway Unified School District, and the nonprofit Poway Foundation for the Performing Arts.

Manchester's 90-minute performance was almost as flawless as the production. Her middle-of-the road repertoire and Las Vegasy delivery might not be for everyone, but anyone who caught her show must concede that she's extremely good at what she does.

After a brief orchestral overture consisting of several bars of her best-known hits, Manchester came on stage wearing a sequined, black dress and immediately sat down behind a grand piano for a seductive rendition of "Easy," a quintessential torch song.

She then chatted with the audience ("You all look absolutely stunning") before resuming her set with a pair of originals, "Shine Like You Should" and "Midnight Blue," her first big hit, which peaked at No. 6 on Billboard's Hot 100 chart in 1975.

As the evening progressed, it became increasingly apparent that Manchester has matured considerably as a singer in the eight years since her last chart hit, 1982's "You Should Hear How She Talks About You."

Her earlier recordings were too often marred by excessive vocal histrionics, but singing them here on stage, many years later, she exercised a lot more restraint, allowing the purity and clarity of her rich, rangy voice to shine through at last.

As a result, Manchester's four big hits -- the other two are "Don't Cry Out Loud," with which she ended the show, and "Just You and I," her encore -- sounded a lot better live than they did on record.

Manchester also sang compelling versions of Dionne Warwick's "Walk On By" and "Whenever I Call You Friend," the smash Kenny Loggins-Stevie Nicks duet she co-wrote in 1978.

Her finest vocal performance, however, was reserved for a medley of vintage pop classics from her recently released comeback album, "Tribute." The album consists entirely of covers of songs made famous by legendary female singers she cites as her early influences, including Billie Holiday, Judy Garland and Rosemary Clooney.

Manchester did these songs justice. Her version of Garland's "Over the Rainbow," in particular, was simply superb.LINDA HECHT / Photo for The Times
