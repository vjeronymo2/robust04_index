940324 FT 24 MAR 94 / Debt trading reform rejected in Bank U-turn The Bank of England yesterday 'firmly rejected' its own controversial suggestion that lenders be prohibited from trading the debt of companies while a financial restructuring is taking place. The Bank, after discussions with British bankers, has decided that even a formal code of practice governing sales of distressed company debt is unwise. The Bank has been concerned that the ability of creditors to sell loans in the secondary market - and the ability of other investors to buy them - could disrupt efforts to find arrangements which would allow a viable company to continue trading. It has asked banks to try to secureco-operation between a troubled company and all its lenders. Mr Pen Kent, executive director at the Bank of England, said at a conference on distressed debt: 'One of the ideas initially mooted by myself in earlier speeches was the idea of a closed season on trading to avoid disruption at sensitive times. After a fairly wide discussion among a range of banks, both British and foreign, this has now been firmly rejected.' Mr Kent said, however, that banks remained free to agree among themselves to restrict trading during sensitive periods, either when they were drawing up loan documentation or when a borrower's difficulties became apparent. Mr Kent also said that after considerable discussion the Bank had decided against formally codifying best practice for those trading distressed corporate debt. Instead, Mr Kent said, the Bank was outlining the spirit of the 'London approach' - co-operation between banks and lenders - and asking debt traders to honour it. Specifically, debt trading should occur in a 'positive and constructive spirit', potential investors should be informed of the London approach before buying debt and institutions should inform fellow lenders when they intend to sell debt. Also, the Bank wants further discussions relating to such matters as inside information. It wants clarification on how much about a company's affairs can be revealed to potential investors by existing lenders. Debt traders welcomed the statement. Mr Peter Young, head of loan trading at Continental Bank said: 'The Bank has probably come to the view that trading in distressed debt is valuable for the market and actually helps liquidity.'
