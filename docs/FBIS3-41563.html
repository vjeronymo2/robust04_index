Language: English Article Type:BFN [Text] Tokyo, Dec 3 KYODO -- The quality of air improved in general throughout the nation in fiscal 1992 but deteriorated in metropolitan areas, according to a report filed Friday [3 December] by the Environment Agency. Levels of five air pollutants, including nitrogen oxide and suspended particulate matter (SPM), were measured by general monitoring stations in residential areas, and by special automobile exhaust monitors along the nation's highways. Less than half of the monitoring stations in Tokyo, Osaka and other metropolitan areas recorded levels of nitrogen oxide below the environmentally safe standard. Throughout the nation, 97.4 percent of the general monitoring stations and 71.4 percent of the highway monitors recorded a level of nitrogen oxide below the environmental standard of 0.06 parts per million. The figures show an improvement of 3.3 percentage points at general monitoring stations and 8.6 percentage points at highway monitors over the previous year. In Tokyo and the Osaka-Kobe region, however, only 46.1 percent of the highway monitoring stations recorded levels at or below the environmental standard, even with tougher automobile exhaust laws in those areas. The number of highway monitors failing to reach the environmental standard increased in Aichi, Kyoto, Okayama, Hiroshima, Fukuoka, and Nagasaki, suggesting air pollution is spreading in those metropolitan areas. Meanwhile, the number of monitoring stations recording safe levels of SPM increased by 7.9 points to 57.6 percent, and by 3.4 points at highway monitoring stations to 33.5 percent. The environmental standard is a daily average of less than 0.1 milligrams of SPM per 1 cubic meter. Tokyo, Gunma, Saitama and Yamanashi failed to have a single monitoring station record the recommended standard. Exhaust from diesel engines is said to cause 20 to 40 percent of the SPM in the atmosphere.
