March 19, 1989, Sunday, Home Edition

ERMA BOMBECK: OFFICE WORK ALWAYS WAS A PIECE OF CAKE

Occasionally, when the boredom of working from my home gets to me, I think of the good old days when I went to an office.

I remember the excitement of a day when I had something new to wear. There was the stimulation of being around other people and the luxury of having a restroom with a key. And then I remember the down side of working in an office: Seven hours of snacking with an hour off for lunch!

That workplace was like a giant cookie. I didn't work at a desk; I worked at a buffet table. The filing cabinets were china closets. The desk lamps were warming lights. I grew at a faster rate than the company.

Every morning when I arrived, I had to run the course between two rows of desks that groaned under the load of doughnuts, sweet rolls and coffee cakes. The out-baskets were lined with Danish. It was worse than facing the Notre Dame backfield. I knew if I made it to my desk, I was safe until coffee break.

Deep breath. First, I had to make it past Mother Mildred, who couldn't stand to see anyone miss breakfast. Breakfast was her answer to irregularity, a bad marriage, deep depression, early menopause, bad skin or a dead car battery. If you refused juice and coffee, she spread rumors about your sexual preference.

Next, there was Shirley, who weighed 85 pounds (30 of that was hair). I dubbed her Meal on Heels. She never ate anything herself, but insisted you "just taste" some new, exotic dish she made the night before in her experimental kitchen.

Every office has a Cake Queen. Ours was Charlotte, who baked, decorated and torched a cake not only on everyone's birthday, but also for every other occasion you could imagine. I once refused a slice of cake in honor of the first anniversary of her dog's hysterectomy, and she was cool to me for two weeks.

You had to admire the adventurous spirit of Wanda the Wonder Worker, who turned office cooking into an Outward Bound exercise. She begged for challenges. If you had two raw potatoes that needed baking for lunch, Wanda made a solar oven on the windowsill out of the tray that held paper in the copier and foil from a thousand packs of chewing gum. She once served lamb shish kebabs on a letter opener over a fire set in the wastebasket. You don't want to know where she chilled the wine for Lois' baby shower.

One day I took stock of the giant tins of popcorn, boxes of candy bars, crackers, jars of peanut butter and cheeses. Our whole office building could have been under siege for three months, and we still would have gained weight.

I work from my home now, where the only distractions are a microwave, two ovens, a full refrigerator, freezer and pantry of fast foods.

To this day, I cannot answer a phone without automatically shaking the cookie crumbs out of the mouthpiece before I say "hello."
