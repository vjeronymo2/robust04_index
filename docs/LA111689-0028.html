November 16, 1989, Thursday, Home Edition

HINTS FROM HELOISE: CHEESECLOTH LINING SOLVES PROBLEM OF LEFT-BEHIND DRESSING IN TURKEY

DEAR HELOISE: As the holiday season draws near, I am reminded of the terrific hint I received several years ago.

To avoid any of the dangers of left-behind dressing in the turkey, first line the cavity with cheesecloth, being careful to leave a generous amount outside to be tucked in after the dressing has been added. Then add the dressing as always -- loosely, as it swells during cooking.

Follow the same procedure if you stuff both ends of the turkey. It can be removed like a sack and no stuffing is left behind. Of course, the taste isn't changed one bit.

-- C. J., Minneapolis

DEAR READERS: Cured hams, smoked turkeys and pheasants have become popular holiday gift items, but sometimes the cooking and/or storage instructions for these goodies aren't clear or, in some cases, there are no instructions at all.

If you have any questions about conditions or storage of meats or poultry received in the mail, call the United States Department of Agriculture's (USDA) Meat and Poultry Hotline, (800) 535-4555, from 7 a.m. to 1 p.m. Pacific Standard Time.

The Hotline staff has the answers to your questions about the safety of meat and poultry.

DEAR HELOISE: Before storing your barbecue grill away for the winter months, be sure and wash the grate well. An unwashed grate sitting all winter can build up bacteria that will cause food poisoning.

This happened to my family this past summer. The barbecue grate was not cleaned well before storing, and when we brought it out for the first summer barbecue, my husband did not wash the grate nor did we use aluminum foil over it. We both ended up with food poisoning.

Even though we now wash the grate after each use, a good rule of thumb is to also wash it before using, especially after storage.

-- EVELYN, Canfield, Ohio

DEAR HELOISE: I found an excellent way to reduce my weight. Although I may have 2,000 calories each day, I try to limit them to 1,700.

I bought a small book that lists every kind of food and the calorie content of each. On a pad, I keep track of everything I eat at each meal, every day.

DEAR HELOISE: We had some really bad weather lately and the electricity was off from time to time. The other night it was off for a couple of hours.

I am wondering if I should have thrown away the dairy items that were in my refrigerator. Do you have any information on how long they can stay in a refrigerator with the electricity off and still not spoil?

-- JODIE, Houston

DEAR JODIE: When electricity goes out, the first thing we do is open the refrigerator to check on the items. Well, this is the worst thing to do. You should leave the refrigerator door shut to allow the cool air to stay in it.

Generally dairy items will not spoil for four to six hours in an unopened refrigerator. You may also want to check the "use by" date. If it's an item that is almost out of date, it may not last as long as an item that has a newer date.

Some dairy foods are more perishable than others, so if you have any doubts about the freshness of an item, toss it.

And next time, don't open that door.

-- HELOISE

DEAR HELOISE: I've always known white vinegar can be useful for many things, but never realized there are hundreds of uses. I'd like to pass on a few.

Did you know that most decals and stickers on windows can be removed by putting vinegar on them? Paint a few coats on, let soak for a few minutes and the stickers will wash off easily.

My husband's lunch box started getting a terrible odor, so I dampened a piece of bread with vinegar and put it in the lunch box overnight. I washed it the next morning and was surprised to see how fresh-smelling it was.

One more thing I did with vinegar was remove new deodorant stains from my husband's shirts. All I did was rub the area before washing them. I'm so happy with it I always keep a gallon handy.

-- M. B., Rochester, N.Y.

DEAR HELOISE: My brand-new wooden bread box taints our bread with a terrible flavor and odor. Airing hasn't helped any. What else can I do?

-- JONI, Columbus, Ohio

DEAR JONI: Most of the bread boxes that have an odor problem are made of pine, which has a resin in the wood that gives off the strong smell. If this is the case, it may be impossible to get rid of the odor.

You can try putting charcoal in the box for a few days and see if it will absorb the odor. You might also want to try an air-tight plastic container that would fit inside your bread box to actually hold the bread, with the wooden box just being decorative.

If you can't get rid of the odor, try to find another use for the bread box. They're really attractive in the kitchen and can be used to store things that the odor wouldn't be a problem for, such as coupons, stationery or even small appliances like your hand mixer.

-- HELOISE

DEAR HELOISE: Please settle a question for me. How are potatoes supposed to be stored and which type is the best? I'm getting a lot of conflicting ideas from my mother and my new mother-in-law.

-- CONFUSED, Boston

DEAR CONFUSED: Potatoes are easy to store. Just keep them in a dark, dry place that's relatively cool, about 40 to 50 degrees. Don't put them in the fridge; the cold makes the starch change to sugar and that changes their taste and texture. Also, don't store them with onions; the moisture from the potatoes can cause the onions to start sprouting.

Potatoes will keep for several weeks if you purchase good ones. That means pick those that aren't soft, bruised, cracked, wrinkled, sprouting "eyes" or look greenish.

As to which one is best, it depends on what it will be used for. There are several varieties and each has different characteristics.

Russet Burbank potatoes are the large, oblong ones with dark, rough skin. They cook up fluffy and make great mashed potatoes and French fries. Idaho russets are favorites for baking. California and White Rose all-purpose long potatoes are oblong with thin, smooth skin. They're best for roasting, boiling, steaming and pan frying.

Round white potatoes, such as all-purpose round, Eastern and Maine varieties, also have thin, smooth, beige skins and are used much the same way as the long ones as well as in soups and stews. Round red potatoes are perfect for steaming or boiling and so are excellent for salads. Always cook them with the skins on.

It's best to cook all potatoes whole with the skins on. They retain more vitamins that way. Potatoes are high in Vitamin C and potassium, as well as being a great source of fiber and complex carbohydrates.

-- HELOISE
