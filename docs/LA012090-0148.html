January 20, 1990, Saturday, Orange County Edition

PIECE WORK;

BROKEN TILES BECOME FUNCTIONAL ART IN HANDS OF LAGUNA BEACH CERAMIST

You may not know Marlo Bartels by name, but chances are you sat on his art.

At the Laguna Art Museum, you may have rested on "Elements," a bench aptly titled for the sphere, cube, cone and cylindrical shapes that adorn it. You may have quenched your thirst at his drinking fountain wall. Or watched the sun set while relaxing at his "Canyon Chess Table and Chairs," a permanent exhibit at Laguna's Main Beach.

Bartels transforms mundane subjects -- furniture, bathrooms, Jacuzzis -- into functional art from broken tiles that he buys secondhand or finds while rooting through dumpsters behind commercial tile companies. He crowns each piece with handmade tiles of swirls and flame shapes he designs in his Laguna Canyon studio.

His work can be found in homes throughout Orange County and the nation.

The 41-year-old former art teacher claims inspiration from Antonio Gaudi, an internationally acclaimed Barcelona architect and pioneer in organic architecture.

In 1973, Bartels and his wife Cathy -- also an artist -- visited Spain and were excited by Gaudi's work. "Organic architecture follows the laws of nature. The Parque Guell is a huge park that Gaudi designed. He added benches that he had his assistant, Jujol, create out of broken, colored tiles," he said.

This came about, he explains, when one of Gaudi's patrons who owned a tile plant gave the assistant access to castoffs that later became an integral part of Gaudi's creation.

Taking the idea a step further seemed natural to the ceramist, who sought to express his feelings about Southern California life style through his art.

Enamored of the American Arts and Crafts Movement of 1880-1930 and the vivid bursts of color found in tableware and pottery during the period, Bartels began incorporating bold colors into his tile work.

"During the early 1970s, artists were producing art that related to them and their environment. My experience wasn't 'gritty New York'; it was California landscapes, the beach, the bold sunsets in reds and blues and purples."

As a child growing up in Los Angeles, the native says he knew early on that he "wanted to leave a lasting impression on the world." At 20, he took a first step on the road he was to travel: "I happened upon a ceramics class in junior college and it just felt right; I found that I liked working with my hands."

He went on to earn a master's degree in art at Cal State Fullerton. His one-of-a-kind pottery caught the attention of a tableware design broker who hired him to create dinnerware patterns for a Japan-based manufacturer.

From 1974 until 1977, Bartels taught art classes at the Art Institute of Southern California (formerly the Laguna Beach School of Art) and continues to serve on the Artists' Advisory Board.

"I am very much into recycling. I prefer using castoffs. I hate to throw anything away. I save bits and scraps until I have a use for them." His first creation -- a chair -- evolved from a base of polystyrene remnants that he pieced together and used as a form.

The best results have often come by accident, Bartels said.

"One of the side effects of working in clay is breakage. Tiles crack, they warp, they shatter. I go with the flow." According to Bartels, when he started making murals, tile borders would invariably warp during the drying phase: "I thought this shape would make an interesting chair back."

Starting with a form made from wood and polystyrene or steel covered with metal mesh, Bartels then coats the foundation with mortar.

Next, he applies the broken tile pieces. Handmade tile shapes are added to fit the form; swirled tiles for armrests, curved tiles for backs and around corners. Several painstaking steps follow -- drying, bisque firing, glaze applications and glaze firing.

Finally, the handmade tiles can be applied to the surface with a thin-set mortar. The last step is grouting to fill spaces between tile. He delivers finished pieces within a few days of completion, but it may take up to a month for grout work to "cure."

Home Savings -- famous for exterior murals -- has commissioned his design work at branches in California, Florida and Ohio.

But Bartels' favorite clients are ones who give him carte blanche; clients including CBS Morning News' Kathleen Sullivan, whose New York penthouse kitchen features his work. Or Steven Gumpert, a Newport Beach commercial real estate developer and his wife, Cassandra. They gave him artistic license to design a wall headboard and bedside tables, hat rack and umbrella stand to complement their Belcourt home.

"The Gumperts had seen photographs of the Sullivan kitchen during a visit to New York. They had an idea of what they wanted, then let me take over," Bartels said.

Steven Gumpert said he likes black as a color. "Marlo uses a lot of black with bright color; his work is very exciting to me," Gumpert said.

For Cassandra Gumpert, it was a combination of drama and fun that struck her fancy. "When we were at his studio looking at his pieces it was so tempting to say 'Let's do the whole house in Marlo's work,' " she said.

Ted Paulson, a Newport Beach stockbroker, and his wife, Suzanne, imposed no restrictions when Bartels designed the electric blue Jacuzzi and oversized shower stall for their newly remodeled Emerald Bay master bedroom. "Suzanne and I were leaving for India (and) Nepal; we said, 'Surprise us.' We were very pleased with the results," he said.

Future plans include grand corporate projects for clients in Japan, Italy and Spain. "I find Spain a most inspiring country because of the Moorish tile influence."

Other plans include the completion of the family "dream home" in Bluebird Canyon.

"We're very excited because our neighbor and architect, Dann McMann, specializes in hillside homes. This living space will be a laboratory; a testing ground for tile ideas I am experimenting with," he said. The entire home will be "festooned with tile."

From the time a custom order is confirmed -- his trademark multicolor bordered bench is $2,000 -- it takes Bartels about six weeks to deliver. A recently completed 9-by-5-foot fountain with five-foot long ceramic, mythical, dragonlike fish sold for $10,000. Large commercial murals range from $30,000 to $50,000.

Bartels' passion extends beyond working in tile; he lends his staunch support to Tile Heritage, based in Northern California, and Friends of Terra Cotta, based in New York.

Both groups are made up of artists and tile historians devoted to preserving the history of American tile and architectural ceramics.

"Architectural terra cotta became an art form unto itself; it was an inexpensive way of reproducing neoclassic carved stone on building facades," he said. "It is imperative that building owners be aware of the care needed to preserve the lasting beauty of this incredible material."
