March 18, 1989, Saturday, Home Edition

REFORMERS RISK ALL IN ROMANIA

The dramatic, even hysterical, physical construction, reconstruction and destruction of Romania on the will or whim of Nicolae Ceausescu has conveyed an intimation of the end -- an end that may now be approaching.

An open letter of protest addressed to the Romanian dictator March 10 by six senior figures in the Romanian Communist Party, including one of the party founders, 94-year-old Constantin Parvulescu, is certain to have serious backing elsewhere in the party and government.

It is virtually certain, as well, to enjoy support from the Soviet Union, although one of the signers, Silviu Brucan, former ambassador to the United Nations and former editor of the Communist Party newspaper, was arrested last Saturday.

The ability of the Soviet authorities to intervene in Romania's internal situation or even to protect the six is by no means assured. The program of the Ceausescu regime is implicitly anti-Soviet, exploiting the old hostility of Romanians towards Russia.

The statement by the six, that they are acting "at the risk of their liberty and even of their lives," is thus the exact truth. These men say that they are making this protest out of duty, "because the present policy of Romania compromises the idea of socialism for which they have struggled, and has isolated Romania internationally."

Their initiative brings the internal Romanian crisis into the open; until now it has been stifled by police repression and obscured by Nicolae Ceausescu's program of false nationalism. Sumptuous boulevards and public buildings have been constructed for the grandeur of the Romanian nation, while the Romanian people groaned in cold and winter darkness, trekking from shop to market in the daily search for something to eat.

The six say in their letter: "A government which for five winters has failed to resolve problems so vital as those of heating, the provision of electricity, transport and food for the population is incompetent, incapable of governing." Among their demands is that the export of Romania's agricultural output, "which threatens the biological survival of our people," be stopped.

The charges made are those that exiled Romanians and foreign observers have made for years. This letter was issued the day after Romania was condemned by the Human Rights Commission of the United Nations in Geneva -- a vote in which the Soviet Union abstained, as did Bulgaria and the East German delegation.

The emigre writer, Eugene Ionesco, told the political commission of the European Parliament in February that Romania's situation is one of "cultural genocide." Romania, he said, is "making a final departure from Europe itself."

Infant mortality in Romania is now the highest in Europe, despite a government program of population increase demanding five children from every woman of child-bearing age and forbidding abortion as well as contraceptives. The old are expected to die without burdening the country's declining medical facilities. Male life expectancy now is only 66 years. The ration of meat for a worker in Brasov, Romania's second city, in 1987 was 8.5 kilos (less than 20 pounds) for the entire year.

The independent culture and languages of national minorities -- Hungarian, German and Serbian -- are cruelly repressed. More than 13,000 ethnic Hungarian refugees from Romania were given residence permits in Hungary last year alone, and it is believed that a like number are living undeclared in Hungary. Hungary's consulates in Bucharest and the Transylvanian city of Cluj have been closed by the Romanian authorities.

In Bucharest at the beginning of March, one of the signers of the open letter (its existence then still unknown) made it possible for me to see another document he had signed. It argued that multi-party government was inappropriate for Romania today, but called for pluralism inside the Romanian Communist Party -- real and open debate among the party's members and leaders, with decisions taken openly as the outcome of those debates.

I did not understand at the time what he wanted me to do about this document. It now seems clear that he wanted it made known internationally that the aims of these Communist patriarchs -- who include a former foreign minister, a former president of the National Assembly, the No. 2 figure in the party before 1972 and head of the Communist unions -- are wholly consistent with contemporary Communist orthodoxy and reform.

These six old men challenging Ceausescu do not ask for communism to end in Romania. They ask for restoration of the constitution, "virtually suspended" today, "constructive dialogue" and "restoration of the constitutional rights and individual liberties of citizens." They want to save the system, not wreck it.

Theirs, no doubt, is an aim that may properly provoke bitterness; they helped make this system what it was when Nicolae Ceausescu took over. Nonetheless, today they take their lives in their hands to defy manifest injustice. They honor the Romanian nation; they deserve urgent support from Western governments and Western public opinion.

International outrage can make a difference, even in Ceausescu's Romania.
