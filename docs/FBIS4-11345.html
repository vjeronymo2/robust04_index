BFN [Interview with Deputy Carlos Sosa Coello, member of the Congressional Commission on Drug Trafficking, by Raul Valladares in Tegucigalpa; from "The Morning Paper" newscast -- live] [Text] [Valladares] Good morning. We are talking with Deputy Carlos Sosa Coello, a member of the Congressional Commission on Drug Trafficking. What can you tell the Honduran people when once again the country is mentioned in international news dispatches? This time, a former Colombian drug trafficker is saying that drug traffickers are protected in Honduras as well as in other countries. He also mentioned Mexico, Brazil, and the Dominican Republic. [Sosa] [Words indistinct] at some time some drug trafficker received some type of protection from some authority in Honduras. These types of people frequently appear at the Senate subcommittee headed by Senator John Kerry. Obviously, the more charges they make, the better it will go for them in the future. In 1989, we had a similar experience. We investigated the statement by a witness of Senator Kerry's subcommittee. He was a former Panamanian consul named Jose Blandon. On that occasion, the accusation dealt with the links of some Honduran military chiefs with former Panamanian dictator Noriega and drug trafficking. The accusation was investigated [words indistinct] I was able to testify and confirm to a congressional commission chaired by Carlos Montoya that the trip of a Honduran military intelligence chief to Panama, during which he had some kind of verbal contact with General Noriega, was for the purpose of meeting with some leaders of the Farabundo Marti National Liberation Front, who at that time were in Panama because they did not have the legal status in their country that they now fortunately have. [Valladares] Who was accused? [Sosa] No [words indistinct] Honduran military intelligence, whose participation in these types of dealings was not confirmed. Therefore, I do not see why I should mention his name. I personally confirmed that the information was false because by chance I was the one who established contact with the late Guillermo Ungo so that this military intelligence chief would meet with him. Therefore, the trip to Panama and the link with Noriega had nothing to do with drug trafficking. It was a meeting with a Salvadoran guerrilla leader, which is what Guillermo Ungo was at that time. The U.S. Senate subcommittee and Attorney General's Office have the habit of making deals with drug traffickers, who know that the more statements they make, the more benefits they will obtain from the Attorney General's Office and the subcommittee. I am talking about this subcommittee. We experienced this situation with consul Blandon, who made these types of accusations. The current accusation will have to be verified. I think [words indistinct], will have an opportunity to begin or continue investigations. The first thing will be to confirm whether [Colombian drug trafficker] Taboada, under his real name or alias, legally visited Honduras. Immigration documents will have to be checked for this. Contacts will also have to be made with the Senate subcommittee. I will consult with some of our U.S. friends to see to what extent these accusations involve Honduran authorities and at what level. What we now have is the fact that our country was mentioned among others, such as Mexico, which in the latest State Department report have been recognized by the U.S. Government for their great efforts against drug trafficking. Now, in this testimony, it said that drug cartels are protected in Mexico. There have always been judges, police chiefs, and officials of attorney general's offices who at some time have been negligent or not done their duty and [words indistinct]. But I think the accusation must be investigated [words indistinct] is not the policy of the Honduran State, and no state organization protects drug cartels. This is an assertion by a witness at a U.S. Senate subcommittee. One does not know whether they are serious. I repeat that in the past we investigated these types of statements by witnesses at Senator Kerry's subcommittee, and we found them groundless. It was much ado about nothing.
