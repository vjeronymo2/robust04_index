BFN [Commentary by Karol Wolf: "Slovak Hungarians Are Looking Across the Danube"] [Text] It is no secret that shifts on the Hungarian political scene tend to be carried over to the countries inhabited by Hungarian minorities. This holds even more true for Slovakia, with its politically differentiated Hungarian minority. The electoral defeat suffered by the ruling Hungarian Democratic Forum [MDF] will find its expression in Slovakia in the loss of influence of Miklos Duray, chairman of the Coexistence movement, who directly participated in the MDF's election campaign. Both the chief of the victorious Socialists, Gyula Horn, and the leader of the Alliance of Free Democrats, Gabor Kuncze, do not conceal their distaste for Duray's positions. Horn even used the word "dummy" [hlupak] when referring to Duray during his last visit to Slovakia before the elections. The second Hungarian political party in Slovakia, the Hungarian Christian Democratic Movement [MKDH], will probably retain its former standing after the change of power in Hungary and will continue to strive for the integration of all Hungarian ethnic parties. The chances of the Hungarian Civic Party [MOS], which is not represented in the present parliament, have improved. It is a member of the Liberal International and a direct partner of the Alliance of Free Democrats [SZDSZ], which will probably participate in the new government in Budapest. Similar to the MKDH, the MOS pursues as its basic political goals the formation of a three-member [ethnic Hungarian] coalition and the weakening of the influence of Coexistence. However, the Socialists' victory in Hungary offers yet another alternative concerning the political evolution of the Hungarian minority in Slovakia. It is no secret that the Party of the Democratic Left [SDL], which does not conceal its joy over the success of its colleagues on the other side of the Danube, has long been trying to gain broader support in the south of the country. Compared with other political parties, the SDL is purposefully building its structures there, and, today, when SDL Chairman Peter Weiss can boast of his friendship with Horn, its chances are growing bigger. The Hungarians in southern Slovakia wish calm and peace, which is exactly what the Weiss-Horn duo offers. The political evolution of the Hungarian minority in Slovakia thus offers two alternatives. The propaganda of Horn's Socialists can help the SDL, which will most probably open its ticket for ethnic Hungarian candidates. The second alternative is that, with the MOS's entry into the Hungarian coalition, more liberal ideas will appear in the policy of this minority, and right-wing currents will come to prevail in it. In view of the permanently proreform attitudes demonstrated by Slovak Hungarians in sociological surveys, the second alternative is more likely. It needs to be said, however, that both alternatives bear in themselves the potential to introduce greater calm into the Slovak-Hungarian dialogue.
