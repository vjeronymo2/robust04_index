Language: English Article Type:BFN [From Hardev Kaur] [Text] Honolulu, Sat.-- Japan and China said the Asia Pacific Economic Cooperation (APEC) Finance Ministers meeting should not be institutionalised, a sentiment which falls in line with the feeling of their counterpart from ASEAN. They said there was no requirement whatsoever to institutionalise the meeting. Their feeling on this was conveyed to Malaysian Deputy Prime Minister and Finance Minister Datuk Sri Anwar Ibrahim during their bilateral discussions here yesterday. Anwar had meetings with Chinese Finance Minister Liu Zhongli, Japanese Finance Minister Hirohisa Fujii and U.S. Treasury secretary Lloyd Bentsen. Anwar also had an informal meeting with his Taiwanese counterpart Chen-kuo Lin. They are all here to attend the APEC Finance Ministers meeting which begins today. ASEAN Finance Ministers had already met on Thursday where they reached consensus on the matter. Thursday's meeting was hosted by Thai Finance Minister Aran Thammano. The common ASEAN position will be presented to the APEC Finance Ministers today. The consensus is that the APEC Finance Ministers meeting should not be institutionalised and that they can meet and discuss relevant issues during the meetings such as the International Monetary Fund (IMF), World Bank and Asian Development Bank annual meetings. They also felt that there was no need for a joint statement at the end of the day-long meeting and that a statement from Bentsen as chairman of the meeting would suffice. Anwar told the Malaysian Press after his bilateral meetings that response to ASEAN's common position from the three Ministers was "positive". On his discussion with Bentsen, Anwar said they also touched on other issues to be raised at today's meeting. Anwar said he was very pleased that Bentsen had asked Malaysia to take the lead in discussions on capital flows and capital markets, including the positive developments and achievements made by Malaysia thus far. They discussed the APEC Finance Ministers' meeting and said that it should remain an informal meeting. If there is a need for a meeting next year, it could be tied to some meeting that "we are all already due to attend". The issue of the East Asia Economic Caucus (EAEC) was also raised. Anwar told Bentsen that just as Malaysia was supportive of some of the meetings that had been held within the context of APEC, an informal caucus within the East Asian context and in the same spirit were also desirable. Anwar described Bentsen's response as being positive following the explanation, adding that "if it can happen in an informal framework within the APEC context, I see no reason why it cannot be supported within the EAEC context". Meanwhile, Anwar said Liu was keen to exchange views on Malaysia's development experience. Liu suggested that a special session be held in China to enable an exchange of views with the Chinese leaders and economic planners in terms of macro planning and strategies for growth with low inflation when Anwar visits China. Other issues raised included the possibility of joint investments between Chinese companies or subsidiaries of government controlled companies and some Malaysian private sector ventures in Malaysia and in China. The Kuala Lumpur International Airport (KLIA) project was also discussed when Anwar met Fujii. Anwar assured him that the project would continue as scheduled. Fujii, on his part, gave an assurance of continued support, including soft loan for the project and the possibility of continued assistance in manpower planning and expertise. He also said experts for some of Malaysia's development and educational projects and programmes could also be made available if and when needed. Asked about increased capital flows and arguments that these could complicate issues and thus the need for an institution to regulate these flows, Anwar said he did not think increased capital flows would complicate matters "but it certainly will boost economic growth" It requires effective cooperation with other countries and authorities. "That is why in principle, we support the need for an indepth study on issues relating to capital inflows and to seek ways and means to effectively coordinate or cooperate even though it is essentially a private sector initiative," he said. On whether APEC could step in for this purpose, Anwar pointed out that "it was never the intention of APEC to be a regulatory body and each country has its own effective regulatory framework for coordination with the relevant authorities".
