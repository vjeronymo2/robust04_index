921223 FT 23 DEC 92 / Drift net salmon fishing to dwindle THE centuries-old tradition of drift net fishing for salmon off England's north-east coast is to be phased out, it was confirmed yesterday. Fishermen will no longer be able to hand down the skill to their sons under a reform that will end the fishery during the next 30 years. The National Rivers Authority was invited by the Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food to draw up plans to phase out the 143 drift net salmon licences between Holy Island in Northumberland and the Humber. Last year, the licence holders netted about 30,000 salmon and a similar number of sea trout. The licensing reform follows claims from anglers and river bank owners that drift net fishing is depleting river salmon stocks. The ministry said although there was no evidence of an immediate threat, the stocks could be better managed if netting ceased. The river authority's North East Coast (Limitation of Net Licences) Order, which has been accepted by the ministry, establishes a policy of non-replacement of licences as holders retire, rather than outlawing the 600-yard nets are played out across the tide. Mr Richard Banks, chief executive of the National Federation of Fishermen's Organisations, said a public inquiry would not be sought: 'The angling interests had a lot to gain but we had a lot to lose.'
