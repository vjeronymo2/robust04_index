November 21, 1989, Tuesday, P.M. Final

LIZ SMITH: BRITISH HUMOR RANKLES THE HOITY-TOITY IN N.Y.

"IF YOU would succeed, you must not be too good," goes the Italian proverb.

The British are very different from us Americans. Yes, they are. I am not writing this to poke fun at them, either -- I wouldn't want to insult people I like and admire, such as Nigel Dempster of the London Daily Mail, or Anna Wintour of Vogue, or Tina Brown of Vanity Fair.

The two top editor ladies tossed a very nice party last week at 150 Wooster Street in New York for their gossip columnist pal, Nigel, who is in the Big Apple to promote his book on the late Tina Onassis, "Heiress." But I am writing this to point out that sometimes, when it comes to British and American mores, never the twain shall meet.

On the party night, Wintour and Brown had invited a gang of Class A talents and literary "Masters of the Universe." As you've no doubt read, the urbane and polished Dempster stood up to toast his glamorous hostesses and in so doing, he introduced a male friend, who he said had "murdered Christina Onassis" and then another, who had "taken her virginity." When this didn't draw such a big yuk from the barbaric Americans on hand, Nigel repeated some of it. There were embarrassed titters.

One lady left the room for the bar. Numbers of others came up to me after to protest Nigel's bad manners. (I guess I AM responsible for the behavior of all my naughty fellows. At least I figure when one IS a gossip columnist going to a gala for another gossip columnist, one shouldn't act too hoity-toity.) I tried to explain that all this was just the English manner of fun and games. Twitting one's friends insultingly, writing about the aristocracy in the most awful way, expressing the unexpressable -- all as a joke.

It was macho Brit time for Nigel and his friends. But it still didn't sit well with the New York gaggle of editors, writers, publishers and what-have-you. New York is perhaps too fraught -- and perhaps too hypocritical to "get real." Most of the folks on hand didn't even know the late Tina Onassis, but after all, she IS tragically dead at a young age. To make fun of her seemed a bit much, some said.

Incidentally, Dempster's book is bang-up and first-rate, head and shoulders above his rather, but oh-so-British, juvenile after-dinner remarks. But then, Americans are so supersensitive. After all, most of us have never had titles to protect us.

IRVING LAZAR has tossed another thunderbolt and brought another talent onto the writing scene. He got close to a million bucks from Putnam for a book to be written by Barbara Sinatra, to be titled, "The Luckiest Girl in the World/Life With Frank Sinatra."

The beautiful blond Mrs. S. has done yeoman work for battered and abused children and maybe she will throw caution to the winds when she sits down at her word processor to tell us what it is like to live cheek by jowl with Himself.

LENORE HERSHEY, the veteran one-time editor of the Ladies Home Journal, decided she would enter Esquire's short story contest, open to one and all, sponsored by Absolut vodka. Well, out of 1,400 entries, Lenore won with her steamy and sexy tale of political romance that she titled "Absolut Discretion." You can read this in the December issue while Lenore sits, proud as a peacock and gloating over all those whippersnappers she beat to the finish line.
