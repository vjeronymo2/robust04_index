930129 FT 29 JAN 93 / Parliament and Politics: Smith raps standards of government MR JOHN SMITH, the Labour leader, last night launched a scathing attack on what he claimed were declining standards in government, warning that unchecked they would 'corrode public confidence' in the political system. In a strongly worded speech Mr Smith accused Mr John Major's administration of 'arrogance and insensitivity' in its behaviour and of compromising the traditional impartiality of civil servants. Calling for a Freedom of Information Act and a code of conduct to oversee the roles and responsibilities of civil servants and ministers, he argued that the consequence of a single party holding power for 13 years had been to expose serious weaknesses in the constitution. The Labour leader went on to demand that parliament be given more powers over the executive, that greater devolution be given to the nations and regions, and the restoration of power and influence to local government. Mr Smith's attack signals a determination by Labour to tap what it believes is mounting public anger at cavalier behaviour by senior ministers and systematic abuses of power. His 10-page speech insisted that his office had been inundated with letters from the public expressing anger at efforts by the prime minister to brush aside criticism. He went on to single out as an example Mr Major's refusal to answer his questions on the payment by the Treasury of some of Mr Norman Lamont's legal fees over the eviction of a tenant from his London home. 'Hard-working British people do not pay taxes in order that they should be used to bail out a Tory minister from some wholly private embarrassment for which he himself was responsible,' he said. As other examples of what he viewed as unacceptable behaviour, he went on to cite Mr Michael Heseltine's failure to consult on the pit closure programme that was later judged illegal. The Labour leader then turned his fire on Mr Douglas Hurd, the foreign secretary, whom he claimed had authorised British companies to pursue telecommunications projects in Iraq at a time when the RAF was enforcing UN sanctions against the country. 'This is a government from which nobody resigns unless absolutely forced to do so by overwhelming pressure from the public, the media and their own backbenchers,' he said, adding: 'Nobody takes responsibility.'
