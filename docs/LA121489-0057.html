December 14, 1989, Thursday, Home Edition

DR. JOYCE BROTHERS: POSSIBLE TO HEAR DURING SURGERY

DEAR DR. BROTHERS: Recently, I underwent surgery in our local hospital, and later I told my husband that I recalled the doctors and anesthesiologists discussing the scores of a ballgame while I was under the knife. My husband, who is a doctor, laughed at this and told me I couldn't possibly remember anything. Could I have just imagined this? -- C.J.

DEAR C.J.: Of course it's possible that you may have imagined it, or you may have heard this conversation before you were fully anesthetized. It's also possible, however, that some part of your brain recorded such a conversation.

New studies have given credence to the idea that patients who are completely anesthetized can still register some of what goes on around them. This is significant information because it indicates that patients may comprehend enough of what is said during surgery to affect the course of their recovery. They may respond to positive comments and negative comments may upset patients long after surgery.

The ability of patients to learn under anesthetic was revealed in a study by researchers at Cambridge University where patients were given information about a number of odd facts and later when questioned on the topics scored significantly higher than would be expected.

DEAR DR. BROTHERS: My 22-year-old daughter has an annoying, alarming habit of buying out department stores. We're not poor, but if she continues to use her credit cards with such abandon, we soon will be. What really irritates me is that she buys things she doesn't need or use. My late wife told me about the joys of buying, but why buy what you don't need? I've talked with my daughter about this. She agrees with me, but she doesn't change. What's wrong? -- P.W.

DEAR P.W.: Some people use shopping as a way to escape from their problems. If they're depressed, they rush to a department store and buy a new dress, new shoes or new coat. Such purchases are usually unrelated to need -- at least, not the need of clothing. They are closely related to emotional needs. This kind of misuse of shopping is dangerous because this can quickly develop into an addiction.

Buying something -- anything -- becomes a quick fix in much the same way a shot of drugs is a fix for a junkie, or placing a bet relieves anxiety for a compulsive gambler. Whether the addiction is to food, drugs, alcohol or shopping, the relief it gives is only temporary and does nothing to solve the real problem.

Addicts typically suffer from loneliness, a feeling of emptiness and a low sense of self-esteem. I suspect that your daughter would profit from some professional counseling.
