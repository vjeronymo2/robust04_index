BFN [Report on interview with Costa Rican President-elect Jose Figueres by reporter Roberto Reyes at Toncontin International Airport; from "The Morning Paper" newscast; date not given -- recorded] [Text] Costa Rican President-elect Jose Maria Figueres and his entourage are en route to El Salvador. Members of his cabinet are also accompanying Figueres, who will take office on 8 May. We have Figueres' remarks while he was at the country's largest airport, Toncontin International Airport. [Begin recording, in progress] [Figueres] ....the meetings I will hold during the coming months, such as the Ibero American meeting, which will be held in Cartagena in June. (?My meeting here) was very advantageous. I want to have the best of relations with Honduras. I want to have constant, permanent, and very fluid relations that will allow us to deal with our problems. [Reyes] Mr. President-elect, will Central America's integration be strengthened now that Costa Rica will be participating directly and firmly, and did you discuss Central America's drug-trafficking problem with President Reina? [Figueres] President Reina told me of his concern and firm decision to combat every manifestation of drug trafficking. We have that same idea in Costa Rica. I believe this is an area in which we can cooperate and truly strengthen both countries' efforts to be more effective. Drug trafficking is one of modern society's everyday problems today, and we must do everything within our capability so that the problem will not affect Honduras, Costa Rica and, of course, other countries. I believe this is an area in which we can make great progress with our other Central American neighbors. [Reyes] Will you continue to visit Honduras after you are president? [Figueres] Of course, quite frequently. [Words indistinct] of seeing me here. [Reyes] Was Central American integration the main topic you discussed with the Honduran president? [Figueres] Yes. Costa Rica is keenly interested in making a significant contribution to a more effective and beneficial integration by all of our countries. [Reyes] What effect will the North American Free Trade Agreement between Mexico, Canada, and the United States have on Central American unity? [Figueres] That must be decided by all the presidents and countries. It is, of course, a topic we must study, and I am sure it will take up a considerable portion of agendas for future meetings we will hold. [Reyes] Has your assembly made any arrangements for Costa Rica to finally become a member of all the Central American organizations? [Figueres] We are going to have a new assembly, and we will have to see what the deputies think. Meanwhile, we can make progress in many other areas. There is much to be done concerning the economy and trade, and we want to do all we can to emulate Honduras, which is engaged in a permanent struggle for Central American integration. [Reyes] Are your views on this issue essential to a decision by the assembly? [Figueres] I do not think so. Our legislative branch is independent of the executive branch. I can contribute my grain of sand so that things in Central America will go as well as possible within the legislative sphere. Certain sectors in Costa Rica oppose joining the Central American Parliament, however, and I hope that as time passes we will gradually defeat that position. I will not engage in that debate for now; we must progress in other issues, an attitude which is much more positive and constructive. [Reyes] In conclusion, Mr. Figueres, did the Honduran president confirm to you and your entourage he will attend your inauguration on 8 May? [Figueres] He did, and I am very pleased and satisfied over that. [Reyes] A final word for the Honduran people? [Figueres] A strong embrace, a warm greeting, and wishes of success for the four years that lie ahead. Costa Rica will always be at your service to help as much as it can, be it a little or much. [end recording]
