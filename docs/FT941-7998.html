940222 FT 22 FEB 94 / Fears over jobs hit consumer optimism Fear of unemployment and concern about April's tax rises has brought an abrupt end to the consumer optimism seen at the beginning of the year, says a survey of consumer confidence. Gallup says in its latest survey, completed before the announcement of a rise in unemployment in January, that 51 per cent of adults expect unemployment to rise compared with 18 per cent who expect it to fall. The balance of pessimists over optimists now stands at 33 per cent, 10 per cent higher than last month. Compared with the same period last year, however, Gallup reported that the fear of unemployment has receded markedly. The survey was conducted for the European Commission. The number of people who expected their household's financial position to improve over the next 12 months declined to 17 per cent in February, while 37 per cent thought it would worsen. The balance of the two is the lowest recorded since April 1990. Consumers were also pessimistic about general economic prospects. While 29 per cent of people thought the economic situation would improve over the next 12 months, 35 per cent thought that it would deteriorate. Also, fewer people see February as the right time to make a major purchase compared with January. 'Given the optimism expressed in January this month's figures are rather disappointing,' Gallup says. 'Moreover, with the recent announcement of a rise in unemployment and the apparent realisation that people will be paying more taxes come April, the figures could deteriorate further.'
