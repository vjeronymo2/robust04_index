BFN [Text] La Paz -- The Armed Forces general staff is assessing a series of state intelligence and security reports suggesting that Chapare, in Cochabamba Department, be declared a "military zone," taking into account that a series of factors in the region could place the country's political and social stability at stake. The reports state that the presence of individuals linked to terrorism has been detected in this tropical region of Cochabamba. It is even suspected that they may have some relationship with subversive groups operating in Peru, also precisely in coca production areas, such as Upper Huallaga, which has characteristics similar to those of Chapare. The reports mention, moreover, that the presence of drug traffickers that have come into Bolivia from Colombia has been detected. These traffickers are allegedly inciting the peasants to oppose the implementation of programs to eradicate surplus coca plantations, through demonstrations that recently almost resulted in violent clashes. According to the intelligence report submitted to the Armed Forces high command suggesting that Chapare be declared a military zone, these and other factors detected in the area represent a worrisome prospect. National institutions must work to prevent the unleashing of conflict that could place internal security and the country's sociopolitical stability at stake. Moreover, military sources who asked not to be identified stated that high-ranking government echelons are concerned over the existence of acts of sabotage against the alternative development program. This seriously affects the country as a whole regarding the fulfillment of bilateral agreements with the United States. The Bolivian and U.S. Governments have reached drug enforcement agreements, including the gradual elimination of surplus coca plantations, in which large sums of money have been earmarked for alternative development programs, interdiction actions against drug trafficking, and aid to help maintain Bolivia's balance of payments. President Gonzalo Sanchez de Lozada recently voiced his concern over the presence of domestic disturbance factors in some regions of the country when he referred to the possibility of "narco-guerrilla" groups appearing in the country and warned of the need to look into this possibility more carefully. Sanchez de Lozada said that there are signs of underlying violence in Chapare, and that if adequate and timely preventive measures are not taken the region could become a territory occupied by international drug cartels or even "narco-guerrillas." The president of the Republic's concern also reached Congress, where it was stated that the possibility of violent actions promoted by drug traffickers and terrorists in Chapare "is real," according to recent comments by Cochabamba Deputy Hector Cartagena, of the UCS [Solidarity Civic Union]. Military sources consulted by LOS TIEMPOS in La Paz stated that the mentioned reports are being carefully assessed in order to reach a specific institutional evaluation, but that in any case it is up to President Gonzalo Sanchez de Lozada, in his capacity as the commander in chief of the Armed Forces, to make the decision regarding the possibility of declaring Chapare, in Cochabamba, a "military zone."
