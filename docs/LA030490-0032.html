March 4, 1990, Sunday, Home Edition

RECORD RACK

MISSION U.K. -- -- * 1/2

"Carved in Sand"

Mercury/Polygram

The passion, the passion. The horror, the horror. Listening to Mission U.K. frontman Wayne Hussey sing his dedicated innards out on song after song here, as if raging with every last gulp of air against the dying of some light or another, you may find yourself wondering,"Didn't Englishmen used to be reserved?" Moreover, you may wish they still were after suffering Hussey's unbridled intensity through 10 unforgiving, aggravated tracks.

In the opening "Amelia," the most unsubtle look possible at father/daughter incest, the anger Hussey expresses is, of course, justified, if way over the top for musical purposes. This subject has obviously distressed him so that he stays in the same mood for the rest of the album, regardless of lyric. Whether it's realistic, socially relevant rock ("Grapes of Wrath," for the workin' man) or the plethora of moody love songs embracing tacky mythological imagery ("Mistress of Avalon cast a spell" and so on), Hussey just sounds royally ticked off, with few breaks from the relentless gush of indignancy and ardor. CHRIS WILLMAN
