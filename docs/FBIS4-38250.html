BFN [Report on interview with Foreign Minister Muhammad Salim by Claude Lorieux at the Yemeni Embassy in Paris; date not given: "`Aden Would Capitulate Without Foreign Aid'" -- all place names as published] [Text] "The Aden rebels would not last a day if they stopped using innocent people as human shields and if foreign aid in the form of arms and money dried up," Foreign Minister Muhammad Salim Basindwah of the Republic of Yemen (capital: Sanaa) told LE FIGARO. On a visit to Paris, where he was supposed to meet with Foreign Minister Alain Juppe yesterday, the Yemeni foreign minister made the following accusation: "The rebels are kept alive like a dying man on a life support system. The aid arrives in large quantities from Ukraine and other eastern European countries. The arms come from the black market. The Aden separatists do not buy them directly. Other people do it for them. I will not say who. I do not want to fall out with my neighbors. After flying over the province of al-Mahrah (which borders Oman and Saudi Arabia), the airplanes unload their hardware in Rayyan, near Mukalla (the eastern province of Hadramaout). Boats transport the arms and ammunition to Aden." Although he has spent "half his life in South Yemen and the other half in North Yemen," Mr. Basindwah says he is "more attached to North Yemen," where he was often a minister before 1990, the year of unification. Loyal to the unitary line of President 'Ali 'Abdallah Salih, the minister gets irritated when mention is made of the establishment of a "democratic Yemen" (capital: Aden). "I would prefer," he said, "to dig my grave with my own hands." He jumps up when asked if the embassy in Paris, where he met with LE FIGARO, is the embassy of North Yemen or South Yemen, which are once again separated: "It is the embassy of the Republic of Yemen. There is only one state in Yemen, the other one (the Aden one) is illegal, and no capital has recognized it. Yemen was unified by referendum and elected a government in 1993." The minister, who visited London, the Hague, Bonn, and Copenhagen, and who is leaving Paris today for Moscow, stressed that the ministers with whom he met were generally "in favor of unity and democracy in Yemen." Could one of the causes of the south's insurrection be the muscular conception which (northern) President 'Ali 'Abdallah Salih has of democracy? "President Salih's party won the elections held in April 1993," retorted Mr. Basindwah. "Nevertheless, we insisted that the country be run by a coalition government. We chose this formula so that the country could get used to democracy. We need time. Do not forget that we live in a region where democracy is not liked." No matter how much the envoy from Sanaa emphasizes that the current crisis "is not a fight between North and South, but between the majority of Yemenis and the renegades," the survivors will have a lot of trouble picking up the pieces of the shattered unity. The authorities have solutions to propose: "Each government (province) could benefit from very broad autonomy, while naturally ruling out functions such as defense and diplomacy."

`KGB and Stasi'
This kind of development would apply both to the 12 governorships of the North and the six governorships of the South. President 'Ali 'Abdallah Salih is in effect rejecting any compromise which would give the South different treatment than the North. This means there is no question of a federation. "This kind of solution can be applied to different peoples in a single state, but not to Yemenis. We are a single people in terms of race, religion, and language," Mr. Basindwah stressed. The conciliatory -- to say the least -- attitude of the monarchies in the Gulf Cooperation Council (GCC) toward the separatist regime in Aden clearly embarrasses Sanaa, which was formerly the capital of a conservative North Yemen pampered by Saudi Arabia. The minister explained his discretion in this matter: "My role is to heal the wounds, not make them worse." He did acknowledge, however, that "some of our neighbors would like Yemen to explode. They do not want a strong, unified Yemen." This is an example of a "short-term vision, because a strong, unified Yemen would bring greater peace and stability to the region." "The oil wealth of the southern part of Yemen could be one reason for the crisis," the Sanaa foreign minister felt. "I think that certain leaders in the Yemeni Socialist Party (comprised primarily of southerners) were unable to resist the temptations, and that they received money from everywhere. It must not be forgotten that some were paid by the KBG and the Stasi." Weakening -- not to mention crushing -- the South is likely to strengthen conservatism in the North, which is more tribal and more religious. Mr. Basindwah admits that "the existence of the Yemeni Socialist Party helped to maintain a balance between secular and traditionalist groups," before adding that "Muslim fundamentalism has not gained a lot of ground among the Yemeni people."
