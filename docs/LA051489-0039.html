May 14, 1989, Sunday, Home Edition

THE FOCUS OF ANTI-TOBACCO CAMPAIGNS SHOULD SHIFT TO PUBLICATIONS THAT CARRY CIGARETTE ADS

The article describing the moral issues faced by the advertising companies that create ads for tobacco products was interesting, but I felt that it was a bit off the mark. ("A Matter of Conscience: Doonesbury Strip Draws Ad Agency Creative Types Into Moral Fray Over Assignments," May 2.)

The real focus of the article should have been on the thousands of magazines and newspapers that publish the slick ads created by the advertising companies. After all, it is these newspapers and magazines that really do the work of spreading the messages of the tobacco companies and thus help perpetuate the use and addiction to the most deadly drug in our country.

The tobacco industry knows that the $2.4 billion it spends each year on promotion and advertising can buy the conscience and loyalty of almost any publication or group. An advertising manager who would be aghast and outraged if a major cocaine dealer tried to run an ad for cocaine thinks nothing of taking huge sums of money from the tobacco industry, whose products kill and injure thousands of times more people than illicit drugs ever will.

A related, and highly disturbing issue, is the way tobacco companies have branched out into hundreds of non-tobacco industries. This now allows them to subtly blackmail magazine and newspaper editors with the implied loss of non-tobacco advertising if any articles are published that are overly critical of the tobacco industry or if the publications refuse to carry tobacco ads.

Your article also quoted a tobacco industry spokesman giving the standard line that the tobacco industry does not want kids to begin smoking. What hogwash! With the tobacco industry losing more than 300,000 of its best customers every single year to tobacco-caused illnesses, it must recruit young replacement smokers to stay in business. Many tobacco ads are so obviously aimed at the youth market that only the hopelessly naive or tobacco company executives themselves could really believe that the industry doesn't want to seduce young, impressionable kids into becoming tobacco addicts.

I think that all magazine and newspaper editors should take a hard look at themselves and ask themselves if they have sold out their morals and consciences to the tobacco industry, and thus become accomplices in thee deaths of over 300,000 people each year in this country.

WILLARD T. WHEELER

Upland
