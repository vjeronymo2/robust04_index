October 14, 1989, Saturday, Home Edition

REPARATIONS FOR JAPANESE INTERNEES

I want to applaud The Times for its advocacy on behalf of the 117,000 Japanese Americans who were forcibly interned by the American government during World War II (editorial, "For Redress at Last," Oct. 3). In August, 1988, when President Reagan signed the act into law to provide speedy monetary compensation and an apology to the Japanese American citizens unjustly imprisoned, the former internees finally felt that this stain of injustice would soon be purged away.

But, one year later, no monies have been appropriated and more than 2,400 internees have died, separated from final justice by a barbed-wire fence of promises made, promises broken, promises unfulfilled.

I urge Congress, on behalf of the American people, to keep its word. Justice delayed is justice denied. As the leader of the free world, America can exhort other nations to guarantee human rights, but only when it fulfills its own moral and legal obligations at home.

JONATHAN T. KAJI

Gardena
