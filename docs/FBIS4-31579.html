BFN [Commentary by Khristo Bardaliev: "Bulgarian Socialist Party Made Painful Step Toward Power"] [Text] It is no exaggeration to say that the 41st BSP [Bulgarian Socialist Party] Congress was awaited with interest not only by society but also by the various parties and coalitions. The BSP supporters had good reason to expect (and demand) a clear stance on the current political and economic crisis. The main opponent of the BSP, the SDS [Union of Democratic Forces], convened a special session of the National Coordinating Council to enable it to react immediately to the congress decisions. The other parliamentary and extraparliamentary parties impatiently waited for the congress to end, mostly so that they could reorient themselves regarding the date of possible early elections and the potential chance of utilizing the "red" carrier rocket for new parliamentary seats. Naturally, it is as yet too early to speak of any full and precise answer to these issues. Nevertheless, the main tendencies in the development of the BSP can be divined. The most important thing -- for both the BSP and the whole of Bulgarian society -- was that the congress prevented restorational and nostalgic longings for the "bright past" to gain the upper hand in its decisions on the BSP program and statute. The rejection of the draft documents submitted by the "Marxist Alternative" faction is proof enough of this. The convincing majority by which Zhan Videnov was reelected as leader indicates that his views will form the basis of the parties practical politics in the future. Incidentally, the conservatives did gain their revenge by preventing a number of prominent members of the OSD [Alliance for Social Democracy] from being elected to the Supreme Council -- people like Dimitur Yonchev, Velislava Dureva, Rosen Karadimov, and Elena Poptodorova. In this way the provincial delegates showed that they do not like free thinkers, or perhaps they merely think that they can thus make room for new deputies in the next parliament. Andrey Lukanov's star also faded. Evidently, the accusations heaped upon him of banking swindles had their effect, or perhaps he had simply become an embarrassment, being too closely connected with the former leadership of the Bulgarian Communist Party, or again, maybe both factors played a part. Surprisingly, Aleksandur Marinov also met with failure. It is difficult to say what he paid the price for -- his excessive personal ambitions or his too close connections with Aleksandur Lilov. These personnel changes indicate something of real importance, namely that the BSP is taking difficult and painful steps along the road of replacing its dogmatic ideology with pragmatism. They also show that it already realizes the need to break out of the magic circle of insider quarrels about the kind of "social democracy" or "democratic socialism" that mean nothing in themselves, and is moving toward a specific program and concrete actions arising therefrom. The party's specific program and actions will be determined by the internal balance within the BSP, between the party elite, linked with young Bulgarian business, and the mood of the party mass membership, which is dominated by the feelings of the sections of Bulgarian society with fewest rights -- the pensioners, peasants, and unemployed. The BSP can no longer rely on cheap populist ploys, especially since it has attracted much opprobrium by its support of the Berov cabinet, support provided in the name of economic and social stabilization -- hopes that proved illusory. The renunciation of direct ideologizing is even more necessary if the party wants to find allies for the upcoming election duel. The less said about concepts and the more about the nation's problems, the easier the BSP will find it to attract more of the better-quality parties and personalities. This will provide the best answer to the meaningless question: "Is the BSP changing?" "He who does not advance loses ground" -- the BSP headquarters at 20 Pozitano Street has enough historians and philosophers who are well aware of the truth of this Latin saying. The question is whether the new leadership will succeed to turn "the word into flesh," as the Gospel puts it.
