June 10, 1990, Sunday, Home Edition

ALICE KAHN: NEW, USED DREAMS FOR SALE

For us, the dream is over. We sold out and someone from Los Angeles bought it. Why not? The property is only a 700-mile drive for the new owner.

According to the lore, anyone living between Guadalajara and Anchorage lives in terror of being taken over by Californians, a fact celebrated in bumper stickers that say, "Don't Californicate Oregon." Portland and Seattle have become hot spots of these real estate ravishment fantasies. But even quiet little towns in central and northern California such as San Luis Obispo and Mendocino seem worried.

Of course, when the locals speak of the Californians they fear, they are not referring to tweedy, ecologically minded, culturally sensitive San Franciscans. The nightmare is hordes of muscular blondes in fluorescent bras on roller-skates together with tan, hairless-chested surfer boys on bodyboards careening down Main Street.

The locals also fear the couple who drive up in front of the Victorian-plus-five-acres in their powder-blue Mercedes 450SL. Will the visitors get on their cellular phone and say, "One eighty-nine five? Is all cash OK?"

The monsters in the Mercedes are the modern Attila: Rich Invading People from L.A. RIPLAs are unwanted by everyone. Except the sellers.

Even rumors that rich people from L.A. are in an area can send real estate prices higher than a palm tree on a Beverly Hills lawn. Just a whiff of Robin Williams in the air sent real estate prices soaring in Gualala, a tiny coastal town. Ditto with Sylvester Stallone in a remote part of Maui. If he wanted to, Jack Nicholson alone could boost Portland prices 25% overnight.

I don't think Nigel Fingerman is actually rich. All I know about him is that he lives in L.A., he has something to do with the music business, he made us an offer, and he now owns our dream.

The dream we sold to Nigel was 20 acres of raw land with what is known as a "blue water view." That means the Pacific Ocean sits out there like a mirage -- too far to walk to, too beautiful to walk away from. You can't really walk anywhere because the land is densely forested except for the deer trails and the old dirt logging road that leads you to it. There's no water, no power and no people. All there is is a place to imagine.

Imagine a cabin here. Imagine if we find a spring there. Imagine a little garden here. Imagine a play structure there.

When we bought the land 10 years ago, our dream was this: We would go up every weekend despite many hours driving on the twisted coastal highway with a carsickness-prone child. ("Prone" here means you pull over in terror every time she coughs.) We would improve the land but still keep its natural character. (Ha! How little we understood nature's imperative to maintain her own overgrown self.) Eventually we would retire there and live like Ma and Pa Hippie Kettle, sleeping by moon, cooking by wood, taking only what we needed, back to the land.

In 1980, one still had these pre-Reagan visions.

My husband and I forgot one thing: We were city people. Our parents had struggled to get away from the land they came from and get to the city, where they could earn a living and buy some luxuries like a refrigerator.

One other problem: We had no skills and no tools (except the checkbook). And we had no time to acquire them. We hardly had time to drive up there. We were too busy working the jobs that allowed us to indulge in a 20-acre dream.

As dreams go, it wasn't all that expensive. Less than a time-share. Way less than a house -- even in Portland. Less than even the cheapest Mercedes.

So, we got a little trailer up there and kept it stocked with all the necessities -- dishes, diapers, fresh roast coffee. For a few years, we went up maybe 10 times a summer. Then the kids got bigger and work got more hectic and we had what is known as "scheduling problems."

Nature soon let us know that unless we planned to stay there and fight her, she would reclaim the land. We would have to regrade the dirt road. We'd have to evict the critters in the trailer. We'd have to keep clearing the trails we had cleared. We'd have to cut trees to keep the view. Someone suggested we get us some marijuana sharecroppers to maintain the land.

Instead, we sold to Nigel Fingerman of Los Angeles, making no money on the deal. If we had put the money in a passbook account at a crumbling S&L, we'd have done better.

Yet, I'm not even sure why we sold it. I think we just felt like jerks as we realized how far our fantasy was from reality.

The day after we signed the paper giving Nigel Fingerman permission to act out on our dreamscape, all the newspapers reported that a movie star had bought a house down the road and real estate prices in the area were expected to jump. Did Nigel know something from the old L.A. Dudes Network or was it just our bad luck?

When we bought the land in 1980 with our earnest chunk of cash and our infatuation with redwoods/ocean/sun, the agent said in all candor, "I sell new and used dreams."

Ours is used up now, and the land is more natural than we found it. This land was our land, this land is Fingerman's land -- until the next used-dream sale.

All I'm saying is that if you're in the north woods and see a guy in Ray-Bans and a Lakers T-shirt gathering twigs and carrying a bucket of water, give that ugly Californian his chance.
