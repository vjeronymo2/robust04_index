March 5, 1989, Sunday, Home Edition

AMNESTY COURSE MAY COME TO END FOR LACK OF FUNDS

Irene Facio faced the 46 Latino men and women, and cheerfully led them in a slow chant extolling the virtues of the toothbrush.

Holding up a drawing of the instrument, Facio urged the students in the crowded classroom to repeat in unison: "The toothbrush keeps our teeth clean!"

She then took them through a dozen more practice sentences that helped familiarize the students with other household vocabulary words, such as salt, coffee, food, store and clothes.

"Muy bien," she congratulated the class members -- many of whom spoke no English. "You are all getting better."

No Government Funding

Because of a lack of funds, however, the students may be the last to enroll in a four-week class in rudimentary English, government and U.S. history that the Bell council of the League of United Latin American Citizens (LULAC) offers to amnesty applicants.

"We're out of money," complained Bell LULAC head Rudy Garcia, who has been running the classes without any government funding since last November. "These people want to continue to come to class, but they won't be able to if the state doesn't give us money."

The once-illegal immigrants who have applied for amnesty need to take at least 40 hours of such class instruction, one of the last requirements for applicants to gain U.S. citizenship.

The Latino community-based organization has certified more than 400 amnesty applicants who have attended the classes, which are held at the Cudahy Social Services Center. About 250 are enrolled in day and evening classes, and another 450 are on a waiting list, Garcia said.

Missed the Deadline

But state Department of Education officials have rejected the council's funding request, saying the organization was one of a handful of nonprofit agencies in the state that filed after the department ran out of money allocated for amnesty classes. The education department administers the state's portion of the $1.8 billion allocated by the federal government over a five-year period for amnesty classes.

As a result, hundreds of eligible amnesty applicants on waiting lists from Bell, Bell Gardens, Maywood, Cudahy and Huntington Park would have to look elsewhere for classes. Although most public schools in the area offer amnesty classes, many also have waiting lists, officials say.

The possible closure of the Bell LULAC classes comes at a time when lawmakers, community leaders and educators, citing a lack of classroom space and proper organization, are estimating that thousands of Latinos who have applied for amnesty nationwide may not fulfill the class requirement in time.

Applicants have 16 months from the time their amnesty application is approved by U.S. Immigration and Naturalization Service officials to find a class that teaches them civics and English proficiency.

"We are very concerned that the bottleneck that's been created by the lack of funding is detrimental to the amnesty program," said Beverly Hunter-Curtis, a staff member for state Sen. Art Torres (D-Los Angeles). Torres is chairman of the Joint Committee on Refugee Resettlement, International Migration and Cooperative Development.

The Bell LULAC council has relied on local private donations to pay for office space, utilities and teaching materials, Garcia said. The classes offered by the Bell LULAC council are approved by the INS, and Garcia argues that he should qualify for the $500 per student that other community-based organizations receive.

Garcia said that even though he received $1,200 last month from local business owners, he does not have money to pay the five full-time staff members who teach the classes, fill out the necessary paper work and occasionally accompany applicants to the local INS office once they complete the class.

Sought $1 Million

The teachers have been conducting the classes on a voluntary basis until the organization's fiscal problems could be resolved.

Garcia's problems with the state Department of Education began early last fall when he applied for state funding. Based on an enrollment projection of 2,000 students, Garcia asked for $1 million.

Garcia said state education officials lost his original application, then told him when he reapplied in late December that his application was submitted too late. He said he was told no more money was available in the fiscal year that ends in June.

"It was incompetence," Garcia said. "We waited and waited, but never heard about (the original application)." Garcia also said that education department officials did not list the Nov. 1 deadline to submit a funding request, a claim that education officials dispute.

"I was surprised when they said there was a deadline," Garcia said. Hunter-Curtis said a number of other organizations -- including some that did receive funding -- have said that they were unaware of a deadline. "We were never really clear ourselves that there was a deadline," Hunter-Curtis said.

John Schaeffer, head of amnesty funding for the state education department, said he could not comment on whether the state had lost the original application.

Up to 15 Groups Lost Funds

But, he said, the Bell LULAC had applied much later than most other organizations, and was not the only organization that lost out by applying for money past the November deadline.

"By November, it started getting tough," Schaeffer said, saying that the deadline had been extended a few weeks to allow new providers to get funding. Between 10 and 15 organizations eventually had to be denied funding, he estimated.

"It got to be ridiculous to keep on funding projects that came in very, very late," Schaeffer said.

"Had we had the money, we would have funded (Bell LULAC)," Schaeffer continued. "We want all the providers we can get because there is a tremendous demand out there. We just came to the point that we were out of money."

Schaeffer said that the department has argued for the past year that there would not be enough funding to support the need for classes. The $80 million that was allocated this year to class providers, including public school systems, was far short of the department's needs to provide class space for all applicants, he said.

"We have said that we needed $50 million to $70 million more than that to make it through this year," Schaeffer said.

Bill Would Add $50 Million

Garcia said that the organization "wasn't geared up to teach" until late summer. However, after deciding to open classes and receiving INS certification, Garcia said that he would have made the November deadline if the state had not lost the original application.

Torres has authored a bill, now in the Senate Education Committee, that would provide an additional $50 million to fund amnesty classes.

"No one should be denied full participation simply because there is not enough money," Torres said. "The agencies (providing money) have to get their act together to make sure that people will not be denied the opportunity to participate."

A recent study found that 1.2 million of the nation's estimated 2 million amnesty applicants reside in California, with 51% living in Los Angeles County, said Joseph Flores, Los Angeles Community College District facilitator for amnesty programs.

"It is clear now that the need is far greater than anticipated," Flores said. He added that 77% of amnesty applicants in the county are illiterate in English, and about 30% of those are illiterate in their native language.

"The need for educating these people is obvious," Flores said. "But they want education. They are going after education like there's no tomorrow, taking whatever classes they can get."
