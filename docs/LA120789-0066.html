December 7, 1989, Thursday, Home Edition

FOOD AND COMMON SENSE: BEST OF '89'S FOOD, NUTRITION AND FITNESS BOOKS

"There is no frigate like a book," wrote Emily Dickinson, describing literature's power to transport. True enough, but some books need take us no farther than our own kitchens to provide year-round pleasure.

To our mind, the best place to make headway with holiday shopping is at a local bookstore, especially the food, nutrition and fitness section, with a side trip to the cookbook department. To help you choose gifts, we've listed the cream of this year's crop:

Topping the roster is the splendid "Beyond Cholesterol" (Johns Hopkins University Press, $18.95). If you want to learn once and for all about cardiovascular disease, this is a first-rate choice. Dr. Peter Kwiterovich, director of the Lipid Research Clinic at Johns Hopkins School of Medicine, guides you step-by-step through the maze of evidence about risk factors. He explains how to modify them with diet, exercise and, if necessary, drugs. The book also contains recipes, complete with nutrient analyses.

Speaking of recipes, we have two cookbook suggestions, both designed to promote a heart-healthy diet. One, "The American Heart Assn. Low-Fat, Low-Cholesterol Cookbook" edited by Scott Grundy MD (Times Books, $18.95) opens with an overview of diet and cholesterol links, but focuses mainly on buying and preparing food. Recipes range from low-fat appetizers to such apparently rich desserts as chocolate souffle with vanilla sauce.

The second, "Mediterranean Light" by Martha Rose Shulman (Bantam Books, $21.95), will tingle taste buds with dishes like classic ratatouille, orange-flavored North African beet salad, hot yogurt soups and chicken bouillabaisse. Again, nutritional information is provided. Seasonal menus are a nice additional touch, especially useful for the novice.

On another tack, how long can you keep chicken in the refrigerator? How do you tell whether eggs are fresh? How do you know if nuts in the shell are stale? The answer to these and hundreds of other questions appear in the well-researched "Keeping Food Fresh" by Janet Bailey (Harper and Row, $10.95). Judging from the questions we receive every week, this would be a great choice for almost anyone on your list.

Pomegranates may be among the oldest fruits ever eaten. Yet to millions, they're a new food, only recently seen in the upscale section of the supermarket. Cassava, a dietary staple in Africa, is similarly alien to most Americans. In "The New Foods: A Shopper's Guide With Recipes" (Henry Holt, $19.95), Camille Cusumano provides descriptions and recipes for foods and herbs new to the supermarket scene.

For athletes, both casual and serious, we suggest "The Sports Nutrition Guidebook" by Nancy Clark, (Leisure Press, $12.95). Beginning with basic nutrition advice, Clark also considers fluid replacement, carbohydrate loading, pre-event meals and use of supplements. Using case studies from her clinical experience, she presents reasonable solutions to nutrition problems (including weight control) faced by athletes and nonathletes alike. A final section gives recipes with analysis of carbohydrate, protein, fat and calorie content.

Another choice for athletes is the recently revised "Food For Sport" by Nathan Smith MD and Bonnie Worthington-Roberts Ph.D. (Bull Publishing, $9.95), a sports-medicine physician and a nutritionist, respectively. Giving tips to the selection of foods and fluids, it also explains nutrient needs geared to particular sports and discusses nutrition for athletes of all ages, beginning in elementary school.

For those whose interest in food extends beyond mealtime, consider "Food in History" by Reay Tannahill, a British historian and novelist (Crown Publishers, $19.95). First published in 1973, this completely revised volume covers 500,000 years of fascinating information and anecdotes about the history of man's diet.

Another wonderful gift is "Pomp and Sustenance: 25 Centuries of Sicilian Food" by Mary Taylor Simeti (Alfred Knopf, $25). While there are practical concessions to dietary concern (such as the suggestion to broil eggplant rather than fry it), many recipes here don't fit easily into the daily routine of a prudent diet. No matter, the interweaving of recipes and Sicilian history and the wealth of beautiful illustrations are reason enough to enjoy this book.

For anyone interested in women's history, try "Never Too Thin" by Robert Pollack Seid (Prentice Hall, $19.95). The author, a social historian, traces the evolution of the widespread dissatisfaction with weight and body shape that has plagued generations of women.

Last, we think there's no better gift in print than a subscription to the "Tufts Diet and Nutrition Letter." Order from the Tufts University Diet and Nutrition Letter, Subscription Dept., P.O. Box 57843, Boulder, Colo. 80321-7843, or call (800) 937-9465. The first subscription is $20; subsequent orders are $16. The newsletter will provide friends and relatives on a holiday list with accurate, up-to-date information on nutrition all year long.

Keep Eggs in Carton When Refrigerated

Question: A friend told me that it's better to store eggs in the carton than in the egg rack on the refrigerator door. Is that true? Second, how long can you keep fresh eggs in the refrigerator?

Answer: Eggs lose moisture and absorb odor through the shell, so keep them in the carton, not the door. Also, the temperature on the door is warmer than in the interior, leading to more rapid deterioration in quality.

Provided eggs are clean and unbroken, they can be stored safely for as long as four to five weeks. They will not, however, be at the same level of quality as they were when you first brought them home. Deterioration will be both in flavor and texture. Ideally, they are best used up within a week, especially if they are to be eaten as is.

You can freeze whole eggs that you don't use up within a reasonable period. Simply pierce the yolk with a fork and stir it slowly to mix the yolk and white together. Do not beat it, because incorporating air bubbles will cause the eggs to dry out as they freeze. You'll also need to add sugar or salt (depending how you eventually will use them) to stabilize the texture. Add 1/2 teaspoon salt or 1/2 teaspoon sugar per cup of eggs.

Individual eggs can be frozen in ice-cube trays, then unmolded and stored in plastic bags.

Q: I was in a health-food store looking at supplements for athletes. The sales clerk advised me that carnitine supplements can help improve performance in endurance contests. Is there any truth to that?

A: No. Carnitine is an amino-acid derivative that functions in the metabolism of fatty acids. Several years ago, Italy's World Cup soccer team publicly attributed its success to carnitine supplements, but the claim is unfounded.

Carnitine is found in abundant amounts of animal flesh as well as in dairy products, but in only limited amounts in vegetables. But that is not important, even to strict vegetarians, because it is produced in the liver and kidney. Thus, while carnitine is essential, healthy individuals have all they need.

Supplements may actually be counterproductive. The reason is that they usually contain two different forms of the compound, and one inhibits the action of the other. In short, your diet and body will provide all the carnitine you need, and supplements are best left on the health-food shelf.

Q: During the past several years I have acquired 25 unwanted pounds. It took bad news to motivate me to attack the problem: My doctor says my blood sugar is too high. He advised me to follow a sensible, low-calorie diet and to come back for reevaluation in a few weeks. I know how to cut calories, but is there anything special I should be doing because of the blood-sugar problem?

A: No. A sensible, low-calorie diet, just as your doctor advised, is really all that's needed. However, that does imply certain ground rules. First, fats are the most concentrated form of calories. But it is especially useful to cut down on saturated fat, which drives blood cholesterol up and increases risk of heart disease.

Second, to ensure a nutritionally adequate diet, it's important to make the calories you do consume count. That means it's a good idea to dispense with sugar.

This translates into a diet based on skim milk and low-fat dairy products, two or three eggs a week, small servings of lean meat, fish or poultry, and alternates of dried beans, plenty of fresh vegetables and fresh fruits -- especially those rich in vitamins A and C -- and enriched breads and cereals.

You know best how drastically your own diet must be revamped to fit this model. For some people it takes no more than cutting back on portion sizes, trimming off excess fat and eliminating occasional desserts or calorie-rich, nutrient-poor snacks.

Others realize that the problems with their diet go well beyond an excess of calories. For many, difficult work schedules have led to dietary habits in need of major reshaping. These individuals might do well to seek the help of a registered dietitian.
