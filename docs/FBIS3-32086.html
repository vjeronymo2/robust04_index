Language: Spanish Article Type:BFN [Text] Tegucigalpa, 28 Jan (ACAN-EFE) -- Costa Rican Foreign Minister Bernd Niehaus considers Colombian President Cesar Gaviria's hope of getting elected to the OAS general secretariat post has failed following the "no" he received in Central America. Niehaus, who is also working to replace Brazilian Joao Clemente Baena Soares at the head of the OAS, told ACAN-EFE that Colombia's intense efforts at the inauguration ceremony of new Honduran President Carlos Roberto Reina were their "last attempts" in this direction. Central American support for Niehaus was, he asserted, "absolute and total." "I feel that this (the efforts made in Honduras) was the decisive point and that the Colombian foreign minister (Noemi Sanin) will recommend that the president desist from running as an official candidate, because he has no possibility of winning the race," Niehaus added. Alfredo Cristiani, president of El Salvador, Ramiro de Leon, of Guatemala, and the new Honduran chief of state publicly expressed support of Niehaus for the OAS post. Nicaraguan President Violeta Chamorro, who also attended Reina's inauguration, termed "perfect" Gaviria's desire and, showing a change of position, did not say who she would support alleging that "the vote is secret." Niehaus told ACAN-EFE that Nicaragua has reiterated its support and that the Nicaraguan president's expressions appear to be "diplomatic words" in view of Gaviria's presence in Tegucigalpa. The Colombian chief of state was the only non Central American president who attended the Honduran inauguration and took advantage of his visit to make a stopover in Panama and to visit El Salvador today. Panamanian President Guillermo Endara, who did not go to Honduras, reiterated last week that Panama will vote for Niehaus. He said so during the visit by Foreign Minister Noemi Sanin to sound out possible support for Gaviria to the OAS post. The Costa Rican foreign minister said that he has 23 votes guaranteed for the 27 March election while only 18 are needed to win. Former Venezuelan Foreign Minister Miguel Angel Burelli, a third candidate to the post, told ACAN-EFE that the figures cited by Niehaus "are inflated." For his part, the Costa Rican foreign minister insisted today he has the support of the six Central American countries, the 13 countries of the Caribbean, who are committed to him in a letter of the regional economic cooperation organization, Caricom [Caribbean Common Market], and also the support of the Dominican Republic, Chile, Ecuador, and Argentina. Niehaus insisted that it is a "just" desire based on the principle of rotation, that Central America and the Caribbean occupy, for the first time in history, the OAS secretariat. Burelli said that to try to "subregionalize" the organization and impose a numeric force of small countries on the democratic importance and economic support of the large countries could do away with the OAS or promote a "small security council" with UN-style veto power. Niehaus, however, rejected the power that the large countries have had in OAS executive posts and recalled that the organization is based on the equality of states, regardless of their size and population. Although he considered it acceptable that Burelli vie for the post since Venezuela has never held it, he stated it would be "unacceptable" to give it to Colombia "for the third time." Regarding the possibility of Gaviria as a candidate, something that has not been made official, he agreed with Burelli that there would be an anomaly to elect him secretary in March for him to take office in June when he is not scheduled to leave his post until August. Niehaus said that he hopes to leave the Foreign Ministry on 8 May, when the new Costa Rican Government that is elected 6 February takes office. "We must give an opportunity to other countries and other subregions, and not only South America," Niehaus said, expressing his intention not to go up for reelection after the first five years in the secretariat in order to give a chance to other nations. "After Central America, a Caribbean country should be next to occupy the secretariat, then a South American one," he said.
