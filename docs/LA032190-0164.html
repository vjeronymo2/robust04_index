March 21, 1990, Wednesday, P.M. Final

PEOPLE'S PHARMACY: THE HUMBLE ASPIRIN MAY BE THE WORLD'S WONDER DRUG

There is a wonder drug in your medicine cabinet and the chances are you don't even appreciate it.

The greatest medication of all time was introduced in 1899 and even after 90 years, medical science is still discovering new uses for the humble aspirin tablet.

Back around the turn of the century, aspirin earned a reputation for relieving pain, lowering fever and reducing inflammation. Eighty years later researchers are discovering that aspirin may prevent heart attacks, migraine headaches, diabetic complications and maybe even Alzheimer's disease.

In the Physicians' Health Study, 22,000 doctors were recruited. Half took one aspirin tablet every other day, while the other half received a placebo. The study was halted prematurely because the results were so impressive.

The physicians receiving aspirin had only half as many heart attacks as the others, and the researchers felt it would be unethical to deprive their MD guinea pigs of this powerful prophylactic.

Further analysis of the data reveals that the doctors on aspirin also suffered 20% fewer migraine headaches. Although one aspirin every other day wouldn't halt a migraine in progress, it seemed to discourage these vicious headaches from getting started.

Researchers suspect that aspirin works this magic by keeping blood platelets from sticking together. These cells are often responsible for blood clots that can trigger a heart attack.

Dr. Richard Peto reported equally encouraging results from a study of British doctors. Aspirin-users had almost 30% fewer migraines. He suggests, "A migraine patient should consider taking a baby aspirin a day."

Such a dose (80 mg -- one-fourth of a regular aspirin tablet) may end up being the optimal dose against heart attacks as well. Although most people find it hard to believe that such a tiny amount of ordinary aspirin could do anything, these very low doses have profound effects on the blood.

A study of diabetics demonstrated that aspirin could slow deterioration of the tiny blood vessels feeding the retina of the eye. As a consequence, aspirin may help hold off damage to eyesight that is one of the serious consequences of diabetes.

Perhaps the most extraordinary new use for aspirin may come from theories about Alzheimer's disease. Researchers are speculating that inflammation in the nervous system may be part of the process behind this destructive disease.

Dr. Patrick McGeer, professor of psychiatry at the University of British Columbia in Vancouver, Canada, observes that aspirin may prevent inflammation that leads to cell death in the brain. "I've never seen a rheumatoid arthritis patient with signs of Alzheimer's on autopsy," he notes.
