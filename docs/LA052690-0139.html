May 26, 1990, Saturday, Home Edition

ADL, SOVIET COUNCIL TRACK RUMORS, FACTS OF ATTACKS ON JEWS;

ANTI-SEMITISM: THREATS HAVE INCREASED BUT NO VIOLENCE HAS BEEN REPORTED. EMIGRATION IS ON THE RISE.

Jews in the Soviet Union have been fearful for months that anti-Semitic taunts and threats may turn into violence against them, but a Jewish agency in Moscow with connections to the Anti-Defamation League in this country is being careful not to raise false alarms.

Early this month, the Vaad, an umbrella council for Jewish organizations in the Soviet Union, investigated reports of violent acts against Jews and found in each case that they were not motivated by anti-Jewish sentiment.

In turn, the ADL offices in New York passed along the information to other agencies and to the public through news releases. That kind of caution has been an ADL hallmark, according to National Director Abraham H. Foxman.

"It is important in fighting anti-Semitism in the United States or in the Soviet Union to be accurate so that, God forbid, we won't find ourselves yelling 'wolf' and the next time, when it really happens, there would be no credibility," Foxman said.

Earlier this year, a widely circulated rumor said Soviet Jews would be attacked in a pogrom on May 5, but the date passed without incident. Soviet government officials had warned the citizenry that such violence would not be tolerated.

"The government didn't speak out with the crescendo that we would have hoped," said Foxman, "but it did speak out, and there was heightened security around Jewish institutions. We are aware that the Soviet Union is taking (the threat of a pogrom) more seriously today than it did six months ago."

At the same time, Foxman said while visiting ADL's new Los Angeles office near Century City, Jews are faced with an uncomfortable irony in the changing Soviet Union. Glasnost now allows Jewish groups to function openly, but perestroika, the decentralizing reforms, has unleashed social turbulence, including anti-Jewish feelings.

"In our country," Foxman said, "we have skinheads, nuts and hatemongers, but at least the U.S. Jewish communities, while anxious, know that the law enforcement agencies will be there, will protect, will defend. In the Soviet Union, that is still an open question."

When Soviet Jews formed Vaad (Hebrew for "board") to give them a central voice, the ADL signed a contract with the board to exchange information on reports of anti-Semitism.

"If we read in the newspaper that such and such is happening, we check with them: Is it true? What are the circumstances?" Foxman said.

According to Foxman, a Jerusalem newspaper reported this month the looting of Jewish homes during a riot in Uzbekistan and the slaying of a Jewish man and torture of his wife in another region of the Soviet Union.

But Mikhail Chlenov, a Vaad co-president, told ADL officials that the rampage by Uzbekis on May 2 followed the cancellation of a soccer match and "was not a specific Jewish problem." Nor was the slaying related to anti-Semitism. The Vaad said that Jews are victims along with many others during times of increased criminal activity in the Soviet Union.

Nevertheless, nervousness about the possibility of anti-Semitic violence in the Soviet Union is said to be feeding record rates of emigration to Israel. Soviet Jews have been landing in Israel at the rate of nearly 3,000 a week, prompting the worldwide Operation Exodus fund-raising campaign by Jewish communities to help finance their settlement.

"In Jewish history, Jews usually run after bloodshed, after a pogrom has occurred," said Foxman, who was born in Poland during the Nazi-era Holocaust and says he owes his life a Catholic woman who hid him.

"I'm delighted that here is a situation (in the Soviet Union) where, thank God, blood has not been spilled," he said.

Christians at times accuse Jewish leaders of overreacting to perceived threats, he said. "We may be guilty of that," Foxman said. "But I say, 'I'm sorry if we've upset you. I do not have the luxury to err on not speaking out.' "
