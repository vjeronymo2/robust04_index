Language: German Article Type:BFN ["fk"-signed report: "The European Central Bank Will Not Be Simply Modelled on the German One"] [Text] Frankfurt/Main -- Alexandre Lamfalussy firmly believes that European monetary union "will come sooner rather than later" and that it will be a union to promote stability. Addressing the German banking community, the president of the European Monetary Institute [EMI] made it clear that the European Central Bank (EZB) would not be modelled solely on the Bundesbank. An extensive shift, a kind of "cultural revolution" geared toward price stability has taken place in Europe, explained the former general director of the Basel Bank for International Settlements (BIZ) in Frankfurt, addressing representatives of the German banking industry for the first time in his capacity as EWI president. It is no coincidence that inflation in a number of European countries has stood at below 3 percent for years and has even exhibited a downward trend in countries that have seen the value of their currencies fall. According to Lamfalussy, Germany and the Bundesbank have earned the respect of their partners for the successful pursuit of a monetary policy geared toward price stability over a longer period of time. The decision to base the EMI and the future European Central Bank in Frankfurt is a tribute to that success. "However, it should come as no surprise to you that other European countries also have learned something that merits praise. A process of learning from one another" has commenced. Lamfalussy recalled that the EMI's main task is to enhance the coordination of member-states' monetary policies while the responsibility for the formulation and implementation of monetary policies is still firmly in the hands of national central banks. He cannot share the view that this amounts to squaring a circle, because the Maastricht Treaty expressly describes "ensuring price stability" as the goal of coordination efforts. Speaking about the preparations that the EMI has to make for the future European Central Bank, Lamfalussy focussed on the monetary supply target envisaged by the Bundesbank. The central banks could use goals of that kind to make themselves to a certain extent independent of political pressure and ensure that there is no relaxation of policy rigors. In any case, there has to be a sufficiently stable relationship between the money supply and prices, and this is something central banks are in a position to influence by means of medium-term money supply targets. Germany and several other countries believe that both conditions "have been satisfied, not completely, but adequately," explained Lamfalussy. In the case of Great Britain, which is the most important example in Europe, at least one of these conditions is no longer relevant (the same applies to Canada and the United States). The EMI's chief's assessment of the present situation is "irrespective of what we agree on regarding a medium-term goal, the European Central Bank will in any event require a clear, operational medium-term goal, such as the level of short-term interest rates." According to Lamfalussy, monetary union can be the result of a peaceful and gradual convergence process (with the EMI making its contribution in this respect) or possibly the result of a crisis (which the EMI could try to prevent). In any event, he believes that monetary union will occur "and sooner than sometime next century," because economic and financial markets "are forcing us to do so." Lamfalussy warned that without a genuine monetary union, the reality of the European single market will be endangered by pronounced negative developments with regard to real exchange rates. The European Monetary System is a provisional solution, which can be torpedoed by pressure from speculation in a situation in which there is free movement of capital. It is "self-evident," said the EMI chief, that only those countries that have achieved a reasonable degree of convergence will maintain stable nominal exchange rates within the European Union.
