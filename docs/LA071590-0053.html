July 15, 1990, Sunday, Home Edition

GOVERNMENT STING USED AGAINST WASHINGTON'S MAYOR BARRY

The smoke-screen claims of racism and law enforcement overkill to explain the Mayor Barry sting are missing the main point of the government's extraordinary but necessary efforts to arrest Barry, who at the time of his arrest was the No. 1 law enforcement officer in the District of Columbia. To catch a big criminal fish the government's legal net has to be larger and stronger than one required to arrest and convict an ordinary criminal. No ordinary police officer could walk in and arrest Richard Nixon for conspiracy, or Michael Milken for fraud. The status and power of the alleged offender has to factor into the law enforcement effort necessary to bring the offender to justice.

For example, the legal process for attempting to convict the criminal conspiracies of Richard Nixon required the entire Congress and even the Supreme Court. Despite all of this legal firepower Nixon eluded being convicted when his deal with his selected President, Gerald Ford, resulted in his comprehensive pardon. Former President Reagan (who no longer remembers the alleged criminal offenses committed within the context of the Boland Amendment), and President Bush because of their political positions and power have eluded the criminal net for their alleged crimes in the Iran-Contra mess. Former U.S. Atty. Gen. Edwin Meese through various legal maneuvers managed to avoid prosecution for various deviant acts he committed in his powerful position. It is simply more difficult to arrest and convict people in power, who control some of the legal machinery that is in place to deal with their crimes, than ordinary offenders.

In Barry's case, it should not be forgotten, that in an earlier effort to apprehend him for using drugs in a hotel (a criminal act testified to by several witnesses), the police in Barry's police department were called off at the last moment. Power-status crimes require extraordinary and powerful police and judicial procedures for the effective administration of justice.

LEWIS YABLONSKY

Professor of Criminology

Cal State Northridge
