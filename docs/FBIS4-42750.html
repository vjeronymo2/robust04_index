BFN ["Lt."-signed report: "Kohl and Gonzalez To Push European Integration"] [Text] Schwerin, 7 Jun -- Germany, France, and Spain are seeking to achieve "the highest possible degree of agreement" regarding European policy and leadership in the European Council. Beginning on 1 July, the three countries will each have the presidency of the council for six months in succession. Federal Chancellor Helmut Kohl and Spanish Prime Minister Felipe Gonzalez said at the end of the German-Spanish talks in Schwerin on 7 June that in view of the "specially friendly relations between the three countries," there was a "great chance for the three to start work jointly." Kohl briefed the Spanish prime minister on the talks he held with French Prime Minister Edouard Balladur and President Francois Mitterrand last week. The chancellor said he had "personally the impression" that he had also reached a "very high degree" of agreement with Felipe Gonzalez on problems related to the "building of Europe." Kohl said that prior to the meeting of EU chiefs of government on the Greek island of Korfu on 24 June, there would be "intensive" talks with the governments of the partner states in the European Union [EU], not least to achieve agreement on the succession of outgoing EU Commission President Jacques Delors. Kohl said he had also discussed this topic with Gonzalez. He added that bringing about such a coordinated approach prior to the Korfu meeting also referred to the white paper submitted by Jacques Delors, which contains, among other things, proposals on employment, economic recovery, and the improvement of the infrastructure within the EU. At the Schwerin meeting, differences of opinion between Economics Minister Guenter Rexrodt and the Spanish economics minister came to light on whether the EU Commission should in the future have the right to raise loans on the capital market. Reportedly, the option sought by both sides was not to give the relevant permission generally, but at best only on a case-by-case basis. Kohl said he agreed with Gonzalez that a "consistent continuation of the policy of European integration" was "of importance for the whole continent." He had discussed with Gonzalez the problem of bringing the Central and East European states closer to the EU. It was "an essential goal" of both politicians to further deepen relations with the EU neighbors; this held also true for the Mediterranean countries, which are of particular interest to Spain. Germany, France, and Spain also intended to cooperate in preparing the EU conference in 1996, which would examine the question of whether the conditions for further steps on the road to monetary and economic union were met. Kohl said it was a "good coincidence" for the three countries to hold the EU presidency in succession; this should, however, not be misunderstood as "hegemony of the three." The chancellor stressed that monetary union, which is envisaged in the Maastricht Treaty, is linked to stability criteria. He said these criteria were more important for the implementation of monetary union than the "time schedule." Kohl said he was "absolutely satisfied for people to say that we are working for it as fast as possible." Gonzalez added that for the Spanish Government, too, stability was the most important thing. He said there was "a certain strictness" regarding the convergency criteria, and "a certain flexibility" regarding the timeframe agreed upon. Kohl also said that the Federal Republic had reached the limits of its possibilities regarding financial aid for the Central and East European states. The Federal Government believed that new funds for EU programs or projects should be paid by member states on a new basis of assessment. He said in this context that it was advisable to make the per capita income in member states the basis of assessment. Commenting on economic difficulties of the Spanish Volkswagen subsidiary, Seat, Gonzalez said the Spanish Government would hold talks with the Volkswagen management. The Spanish Government was willing "to a certain extent" to do something to preserve the jobs; however, only such aid could be granted as was in line with EU directives.
