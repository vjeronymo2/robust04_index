940304 FT 04 MAR 94 / Arts: Today's Television American television has, on the whole, managed the race conundrum better than its British counterpart. In Britain broadcasters wring their hands and say 'Blacks are disadvantaged so we will provide special slots where they can do their own thing and showcase their own culture'. This unthinking form of apartheid assumes that black people cannot co-operate let alone compete with whites but must be treated like babies. Often the results have been programmes watched by a minority of a minority. Americans, with their tradition of the melting pot philosophy, tend to say 'Bill Cosby? Who cares what colour he is? Is he talented? Give him a series', and the series tops the ratings. Today BBC2 begins an acclaimed American mini-series, Laurel Avenue, stripped across three nights and played out in real time. It is about a lower middle class family which, as the executive producer points out, just happens to be black. The appeal is in the impact and honesty of the drama (9.30). Earlier Alexei Sayle winds up his BBC1 series about driving skills, Drive (8.50).
