February 2, 1990, Friday, Home Edition

SHERIFF BLOCK SHOWS A WISE HEAD

Sheriff Sherman Block has issued some sound and sensitive guidelines for his deputies in the aftermath of a fatal confrontation with black members of the Nation of Islam that began with a minor traffic stop.

Block has advised against taking "enforcement actions in minor incidents" in black neighborhoods because of the "heightened tensions" that have resulted from the incident in which a deputy shot and killed a Muslim on Jan. 23. If taken to heart by his street cops, the sheriff's advice can defuse escalating tensions, and maybe save lives.

The shooting followed a non-fatal confrontation between Los Angeles police and Muslims last month. Both incidents prompted a series of unprecedented meetings between high-level law- enforcement officials and black leaders representing the Muslims, other religious groups, civil rights organizations and community groups. The meetings, organized largely by Danny Bakewell of the Brotherhood Crusade and Councilman Nate Holden, have started a beneficial dialogue.

As a result of the sessions, the Sheriff's Department produced a training video for all deputies that outlines the Nation of Islam's history and its small presence in Los Angeles. The presentation also acknowledges that Muslims and law enforcement share some worthwhile goals, specifically, "to get young black men and women out of gangs and to keep them off of drugs." The message -- and effort to reach an understanding -- are commendable.

Minister Louis Farrakhan, the controversial leader of the Nation of Islam, should respond in kind when he visits Los Angeles this weekend. Farrakhan can be offensive and incendiary -- his virulent anti-Semitic speeches are well known -- but he can also be eloquent. He should tone down his hate-mongering and advocate a continuing dialogue to ease tensions between local police and Los Angeles' black community.

Sheriff Block has made it clear his department is not at war with Muslims. Farrakhan must send out a similarly sensitive message. No one wants more bloodshed.
