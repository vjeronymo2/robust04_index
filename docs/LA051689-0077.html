May 16, 1989, Tuesday, Home Edition

MURDOCH TV UNIT SUES DISNEY FOR $1.5 BILLION;

SKY TELEVISION CLAIMS AGREEMENT FOR SATELLITE VENTURE WAS DERAILED BY UNFAIR BUSINESS TACTICS

Sky Television, media baron Rupert Murdoch's British TV unit, demanded $1.5 billion from Walt Disney Co. in a lawsuit filed Monday that charged Disney with breaching its agreement to join the Murdoch company in a satellite TV venture.

The suit accuses Disney of fraud and pits Barry Diller, who heads Fox Inc. and other Murdoch entertainment ventures, against Disney Chairman Michael Eisner, a longtime former associate of Diller's at Paramount Pictures.

The complaint, filed in Los Angeles Superior Court, claims that Disney used unfair tactics to derail an agreement under which it was to join the unit of Murdoch's Australian-based News Corp. in launching two satellite movie channels in Great Britain this year. It demands $1 billion in punitive damages and $500 million in actual damages.

Four Broadcast Channels

At one point, the complaint says: "While Disney spends millions to create the appearance that they are honest and wholesome and that the Disney name stands for such qualities, the fact is that defendants' common pattern and practice is to ignore and deny their agreements and to use economic leverage to exact unjustified concessions from those with whom they contract and deal."

Disney spokesman Thomas Deegan read a one-line statement in response to the suit. It said: "We have great regard for Mr. Murdoch and his accomplishments, but we are surprised at this suit, which has absolutely no merit."

Sky Television operates four direct broadcast British TV channels over a satellite that was launched Feb. 5. Last November, Disney agreed to join Murdoch in operating one of the channels, Sky Movies, as a pay service offering feature films and in launching the Disney Channel on the satellite.

'Available to Defuse Problems'

According to the suit, however, Disney executives failed to provide their share of funding for the venture, dragged their feet on decisions to force a change in terms and finally "repudiated" the agreement May 12, despite repeated pleas by Diller for cooperation from Disney executives.

In an April 28 letter to Eisner and Disney President Frank Wells, a copy of which was filed with the suit, Diller wrote:

"We have experienced the normal difficulties that one would expect in the case of a new venture, particularly in a new and ground-breaking business. We believe, however, these pale to insignificance when measured against the accomplishments of each of us. . . . To further the process, I offer to make myself personally available on a 'hotline' basis in order to defuse and resolve any potential problems that may arise."

According to the complaint, Eisner and Wells never answered the letter.

In its suit, Murdoch's company asks the court to block Disney from selling its programming in Great Britain until the dispute is resolved. Bertram Fields, an attorney for Murdoch, said no hearing has been set in the case, which was assigned to Judge Dion Morrow.

Sky Television, one of several ventures seeking to tap the increasingly deregulated European television market, has been hampered by the initial unavailability of its satellite receiving dishes but still aims to reach more than a million British homes by year-end. Satellite TV is broadcast directly to a receiver in the viewer's home rather than over cable.

The service has been competing with British Satellite Broadcasting, another satellite service that expects to begin operation later this year, for packages of Hollywood films.

BSB secured rights to films from Warner Bros. and MGM/UA, among others, last year. According to the lawsuit, Sky Television's negotiations with Warner and other studios have been hampered by Disney's "dilatory behavior" -- which Sky Television alleged was meant to force a readjustment in terms or cancellation of the venture.

The suit is the latest in a series of big-money disputes that have rocked the entertainment business. In other recent actions, Paramount Pictures sued Viacom for breaching a major pay TV contract, even as the two were said by Wall Street analysts to be discussing a merger; Viacom sued competitor HBO, in a move that some observers believed might complicate a pending merger between Warner Communications and HBO's parent, Time Inc., and MCA locked horns in a Canadian court battle with Cineplex-Odeon, its partly owned subsidiary.

Diller, as chairman of Paramount, and Eisner, as president, were one of the most successful teams in the history of movie making before the breakup of their regime, which produced "Flashdance," "Raiders of the Lost Ark," "Beverly Hills Cop" and other hits. Diller left to join Twentieth Century Fox Film Corp., then owned by Marvin Davis, in 1984. Eisner immediately left Paramount for Disney, and was soon joined by many ex-Paramount executives.
