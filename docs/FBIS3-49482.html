Language: English Article Type:BFN [Text] Bucharest ROMPRES, 7/1/1994 -- At the press conference of 7 January given by Ambassador Traian Chebeleu, spokesman for the Presidency of Romania, the following points were made about the steps taken by the head of the Romanian state for Romania's adherence to NATO: "It is not incidental that the president [Ion Iliescu] paid an official visit to NATO in February 1993. Availing himself of this opportunity, President Ion Iliescu said before the NATO Council: `As a true partner, Romania wishes to work ever closer with NATO and to strengthen its ties with the structures of the alliance, to draw closer to them, by taking part in any form of cooperation open to the Central European countries. Besides the immediate positive effects, all this would be of a nature to contribute to creating the prerequisites for reaching the strategic aim which is Romania's joining the North Atlantic Alliance. This would offer the necessary guarantees for a normal evolution of the irreversible process of Romania's democratization.' "Subsequent to these official efforts made by the president, which were among the first statements of this kind on the topic of permitting the countries in this part of Europe access to NATO, messages were addressed on two different occasions -- in September and December 1993 -- to NATO heads of state and NATO Secretary General Woerner, including President Iliescu's considerations on the role of NATO in maintaining security in Central and East Europe, and the official position relative to the inclusion in the alliance of the countries in this part of Europe. "Those messages were well received by the member states of the alliance and found by the greatest part of their representatives as most welcome, straightforward, and clearly worded," the presidential spokesman said. On receiving the letter from the president of Romania, the representatives of several (NATO) states made a point of showing that they would support Romania in its strive for integration in NATO and other Euro-Atlantic structures, knowing that Romania was resolutely committed to the course of democratization and the rule of law, and therefore it should stay in the family of the democratic European states. It was also shown that Romania's security interests would be considered, as well as its readiness to assume responsibilities in ensuring security in Europe. A positive assessment was made of the way Romania responded to the initiative of Partnership for Peace, which several member countries see as a transitional stage in the gradual extension process of the alliance. The message of the Romanian president was unanimously appreciated as a significant signal for the priority Romania gives to enlarging its political, economic, and security relations with the Western countries, the spokesman for the presidency said.
