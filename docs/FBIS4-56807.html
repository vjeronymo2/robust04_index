BFN [By Cristina Hasbun de Merino] [Text] San Jose, 7 May (ACAN-EFE) -- Salvadoran President Alfredo Cristiani said today that Central America is en route to political stability and that he believes that with the change in governments in the area the integration process will receive new impetus. In a statement to ACAN-EFE, Cristiani said that Honduras, Costa Rica, and El Salvador "have consolidated their democracies" with their recent electoral processes and that the countries that still "give reason for concern" are Guatemala and Nicaragua. Notwithstanding, the Salvadoran president emphasized that last year Guatemala was able to solve, "within the framework of constitutional legality," the self-inflicted coup of Former President Jorge Serrano Elias, after which Ramiro de Leon Carpio became president. Cristiani, who signed peace agreements with the former Salvadoran guerrilla forces in January 1992, is confident that the peace negotiations to end the armed conflict in Guatemala, which dates back to 1960, will end successfully and emphasized that "the science of peace is patience." He emphasized, however, that it is difficult to predict when the government and the Guatemalan guerrilla forces will sign a peace agreement, because "negotiations are sometimes slow and difficult and one cannot be hasty in something so delicate." With respect to Nicaragua's political situation, Cristiani said "there are still some details" to clear up, but "little by little the Nicaraguan people are overcoming their problems." "I believe Central America is headed toward stability; there are no destabilizing elements," stated Cristiani, who on 1 June will turn the presidency over to President-elect Armando Calderon Sol, leader of Cristiani's own right-wing Nationalist Republican Alliance party. Cristiani is confident that changes in Central American governments will give new impetus to the region's integration process, which in recent months have become bogged down due to the general elections being carried out in most of these countries. He said that, since the beginning of the integration process, governments of different ideologies have participated and made satisfactory headway, and that, because of this, he does not believe there will be any delay in this issue as a result of the administrative changes going on in El Salvador, Honduras, Costa Rica, and, just around the corner, in Panama. Cristiani said he spoke of this with Honduran President Carlos Roberto Reina, and with Costa Rican President-elect Jose Maria Figueres, who will be sworn in on Sunday, adding: "The two are willing to continue the integration effort." In El Salvador's case, the president said his party's policy "is in favor of integration." Cristiani admitted that the major advances in the Central American integration process have centered on economic issues, but he did not rule out that in the coming years the social aspect might climb higher on the agenda. In addition, the president believes it improbable that his country's peace process could suffer a setback as a result of the internal crisis of the former rebel Farabundo Marti National Liberation Front (FMLN), because the five groups that comprise the FMLN signed the accords that put an end to the 12-year war. Cristiani believes the differences that arose within the ranks of the FMLN one week ago are "logical" given "their different leftist ideologies," which may have united them for the armed struggle but could lead to their break up in the political arena. "The only problem I consider delicate is the internal threats that could deteriorate into violence within the FMLN, but the government will be ready not only to try to prevent this, but, in case violence does occur, to apprehend those responsible and put them where they belong," Cristiani said. A clandestine group of dubious ideology threatened the life of various former FMLN commanders, including Joaquin Villalobos, for having "betrayed revolutionary principles" by negotiating with the government on the make-up of the Legislative Assembly Directorate.
