930709 FT 09 JUL 93 / Arts: Today's Television Since almost all soap opera leaves me stone cold, it is difficult to summon up much concern at the passing of Eldorado which ends today (7.00 BBC1). There seems little doubt that, had it been allowed to continue for a few more months, it would have begun to do its intended task: hold up the ratings on week-nights when 'EastEnders' is off. But given the present politics of broadcasting, it needed to succeed instantly to survive, and no soap opera has ever done that. Will Alan Yentob, now controller of BBC1 as well as BBC2, do what his predecessor was dissuaded from and commission Casualty as a three-times-a-week half-hour drama to replace 'Eldorado'? Hospital drama is one of the earliest, commonest and most successful types of soap. In his third Reith Lecture (9.30 Radio 3), Professor Edward Said considers intellectuals both as expatriates and as people on the margins of their own societies, and ponders whether exile inspires their thinking. Said, himself both an intellectual and a Palestinian who lives in the US, seems ideally placed to judge.
