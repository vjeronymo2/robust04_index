BFN [Interview with General Paul Kagame, leader of the rebel Rwandan Patriotic Front, by Renaud Girard in Byumba; date not given: "Kagame Still Distrusts France"] [Text] [Girard] Alain Juppe is envisaging French intervention. What is your reaction? [Kagame] I have heard your minister's statement. I do not understand exactly what he is demanding. I do not see how an intervention of the kind he is considering could improve the situation. If France wants to do what we are doing itself, it would be better if it left us to do it. [Girard] Do you not actually fear that the arrival of French soldiers might prevent you from continuing your action? [Kagame] France's involvement in the Rwandan conflict has been proved. It has clearly shown that it is partisan. The return of its soldiers here would merely complicate matters. [Girard] French policy has nonetheless changed from Mitterrand to Balladur. [Kagame] In Rwanda, France has taken sides since the beginning of the war in 1990. This partisan spirit has continued over the past few months. I have not noticed any change of French policy. It has given its support to the so-called interim government, this regime of militiamen, and Rwandan officials have recently been received in Paris. France has not openly condemned the interim government for the crimes it has committed in Rwanda. And if France now wants to do something here, it would do better to wait for us to invite it. [Girard] The French are nonetheless not responsible for the genocide of which Rwanda has been the theater since 7 April, when President Juvenal Habyarimana was assassinated? [Kagame] It is obvious that the French are partly responsible for what has happened. France trained the militias and the Army. It has not condemned the assassins. [Girard] What role would you agree to the United Nations playing in your country? [Kagame] The United Nations can pursue its humanitarian mission, its aid to the populations that have survived the massacres. This is the maximum that the United Nations can achieve here. The Blue Helmets have shown themselves to be incapable of protecting the civilians who are being murdered by the militias. At present, I do not see who they could aspire to save by military means. [Girard] Do you support the Security Council's decision to send 5,000 additional Blue Helmets to Rwanda? [Kagame] It is a question which still has to be debated. First, the United Nations must clearly define its mission. Only then will we be able to tackle the question of the number of men likely to accomplish that mission effectively. [Girard] But once the United Nations has clearly defined its humanitarian mission, you would not oppose the dispatch of these reinforcements? [Kagame] General Dallaire (head of UNAMIR, the UN Assistance Mission in Rwanda) is in fact demanding 5,500 Blue Helmets. We think that is a large number. We are currently negotiating to arrive at a reasonable figure. [Girard] What figure do you advocate? [Kagame] The same sort of number that UNAMIR had when the war resumed in April, in other words, around 2,500 men. But the United Nations must first have a clear humanitarian mandate. What it lacks in this crisis is not numbers but a mandate. Otherwise the United Nations would have been able to protect the civilians from the start of the massacres. The Blue Helmets have always told us that they did not have a mandate to do so at the time.

`Minimizing Losses in Our Ranks'
[Girard] Your movement, the Rwandan Patriotic Front [RPF], was known for its discipline. But the archbishop of Kigali was murdered by the RPF men who were guarding him. What is your explanation? [Kagame] We still maintain very strict discipline in our movement. But this does not rule out some loss of control, individual crimes. You have blunders of this kind in all armies, even those of the developed countries. [Girard] Will those responsible be prosecuted? [Kagame] Of course. One of the assassins was killed when resisting arrest. Three others are on the run. We are actively seeking them. I will arrest the officer commanding the unit in question. He is not directly responsible for what happened, but as chief he is responsible. [Girard] What is stopping you from capturing Kigali? [Kagame] We are taking our time. We are making plans to minimize the losses in our ranks. [Girard] Do you still refuse to negotiate with the interim government while agreeing to have discussions with the military? [Kagame] Yes. But the FAR (Rwandan Armed Forces) have disappointed us. They do not keep their promises. Scarcely was the cease-fire signed than the massacres resumed and the civilians were prevented from going to the area of their choice. The general staff told us that they did not control the militias. But how can anybody think that professional soldiers are unable to control militiamen armed with machetes?
