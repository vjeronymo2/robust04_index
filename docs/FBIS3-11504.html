Language: Spanish Article Type:BFN [Text] Brasilia, 22 Mar (EFE) -- Diplomatic source told EFE News Agency that some Rio Group foreign ministers -- who have been holding talks in Brasilia for the last two days -- expressed certain reservations about the U.S. proposal that Miami should host the next summit of the Americas convened by the United States. Although the heads of Latin American diplomacy were enthusiastic about U.S. President Bill Clinton's proposal, some of them expounded the "inadvisability" of holding the summit in Miami, where anti-Castro groups have a lot of clout. The diplomats who assisted at the Brasilia meeting, which ended today, told EFE that the issue was discussed at the meeting, but did not disclose what delegations were opposed to Miami being the venue for the summit meeting. The reservations stem from the fear that anti-Castro groups will harass some heads of state on account of the independent stance adopted by their countries and the Rio Group concerning the U.S. policy vis-a-vis Cuba. The reservations seemingly reached the ears of U.S. Vice President Albert Gore, who made a seven-hour visit to Brasilia -- which extended from the evening of 21 March to the early hours of 22 March -- to invite Brazilian President Itamar Franco to the Miami summit. After the Rio Group meeting, Brazilian Foreign Minister Celso Amorim said Gore guaranteed him that matters alien to the summit -- alluding to anti-Castro Cubans -- will not interfere in its organization and agenda. Amorim noted that it is "too early" to say whether Cuba's political and economic situation will be included on the December summit's agenda. He further stated that both the summit's date and venue depend on the decision of the U.S. Government, which convened the meeting. Venezuelan Foreign Minister Miguel Angel Burelli commented that, in his view, Miami was not a "risky" place to bring together all the heads of state in the Americas. Several Rio Group foreign ministers praised the flexibility of the issues proposed by Clinton, who has asked governments to offer suggestions to complete the agenda. The subjects put forward by Clinton to his Western Hemisphere counterparts include the strengthening of democracy, human rights, trade, poverty, the environment, and the streamlining of public administration. Paraguayan Foreign Minister Luis Maria Ramirez Boettner told EFE that his country is interested in highlighting trade liberalization, economic investments, and cooperation at the summit. Burelli, for his part, noted that questions like education, poverty, and access to technology had to be part of the agenda. Despite the fact that they differ widely on the types of pressure that should be exerted on Cuba so it will adopt a pluralistic system to replace the single-party system, Rio Group countries have condemned the trade embargo on Cuba imposed by the United States more than 30 years ago. Brazil and Colombia recently have become the target of criticism and threats by anti-Castro groups due to their stances on the situation in Havana.
