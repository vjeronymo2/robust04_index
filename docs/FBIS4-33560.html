BFN [Editorial Report] The following is a compilation of reports on economic developments monitored through 7 June. The Mexican economy grew slightly in the first quarter with the GDP increasing 0.5 percent over the same period in 1993. Nevertheless, for thousands of Mexican companies and their employees, the recession that began in 1993 continued. This was particularly true in the service subsectors of trade, restaurants, and hotels. The recession in the service sector has lasted longer than in the other areas of the economy. The last time this sector showed growth was in the first quarter of 1993, when it registered an annual growth rate of 2.2 percent. (Mexico City Canal 13 Television Azteca Network in Spanish 1300 GMT 2 Jun 94) CORPAMEX [Mexican Employers Confederation] President Antonio Sanchez Diaz de Rivera told UNOMASUNO yesterday that economic growth has been "delayed" and that its reactivation will not be seen until 1995. In a news conference, Diaz proposed that new family businesses be exempt from all types of taxes and a 3 percent cap be placed on sales taxes. According to Diaz, these measures will allow "groups in the underground economy to pass into the formal economy, and this will prevent the unemployment problem from worsening." (Mexico City UNOMASUNO in Spanish 1 Jun 94 pp 1, 19) The under secretary of trade has said NAFTA is "progressing very well," adding that proof of this is that manufacturing exports in the first quarter rose 25.4 percent over the same period last year. The under secretary also announced that a free trade agreement will be signed among the G-3 nations -- Colombia, Mexico, and Venezuela -- during the upcoming Ibero-American presidential summit in Cartagena de Indias, Colombia. The official also said the free trade agreement signed with Costa Rica has paved the way for negotiations with Nicaragua, Guatemala, Honduras, and El Salvador. (Mexico City NOTIMEX in Spanish 2024 GMT 2 Jun 94) The Bancomer Financial Group has announced that the lifting of trade barriers and lagging technology have had an adverse effect on the Mexican textile industry, even though in the long-run this could be the one of the sectors to benefit most from NAFTA. The Bancomer Group forecasts a 3.9 percent increase in the GDP this year, but only a 2.1 percent increase in the garment industry's gross product. Problems faced by the industry include a lack of integration in terms of production, a shortage of raw materials, low quality, limited designs, and high financing costs. (Mexico City UNOMASUNO in Spanish 2 Jun 94 p 17) A poll conducted by the Secretariat of Commerce and Industrial Development (SECOFI) among industrialists, asking whether it should move to expedite the liberalization of trade barriers on some products listed under NAFTA, has met with a very poor response, while most respondents announced their opposition to the expediting of liberalization. The industrial sectors voicing the greatest opposition were those that produce glass, wine, household appliances, pharmaceuticals, heavy machinery, and chemicals. Meanwhile, industries in favor of speeding up the lifting of trade barriers were those that produce shoes, textiles, citrus fruits, flowers, and tropical fruits. (Mexico City LA JORNADA in Spanish 2 Jun 94 p 49) The president of the National Agrarian Council has said problems still remain six months after NAFTA's implementation, particularly in terms of customs and the overall understanding of the trilateral agreement's terminology. The president noted that the majority of producers in this sector have expressed their opposition to speeding up the lifting of tariffs. Nevertheless, it was reported that the sector grew 7 percent in the first quarter, mainly because of strong exports of coffee and cattle. (Mexico City LA JORNADA in Spanish 2 Jun 94 p 52) The governments of Mexico and the UK yesterday signed an agreement on tax issues. The agreement is aimed at averting double taxation and tax evasion on income and capital gains. The agreement seeks to eliminate excessive taxes resulting in double taxation on investments and capital flow between the two nations. The agreement also makes it easier for tax authorities from both nations to exchange information to combat fraud and tax evasion. (Mexico City UNOMASUNO in Spanish 3 Jun 94 p 14) Japanese businessmen said during a meeting with President Carlos Salinas de Gortari that Japanese companies in the electric, banking, automobile, heavy machinery, construction, and food industries are interested in investing in Mexico in light of the nation's secure economic future. Among the companies interested in investing are: Bank of Tokyo, Honda Motors, Canon Ltd., Sanyo, Nissan, Clarion, Aoki Corp., Marubeni Corp., and Terumo. (Mexico City NOTIMEX in Spanish 0333 GMT 1 Jun 94) Yesterday, Mexican Petroleum [PEMEX] announced a 25-50 cent increase in the per-barrel price of petroleum as of 1 June for oil sales to the Americas, Europe, and the Far East. The measure is designed to keep Mexican crude aligned with prices on the international market. (Mexico City UNOMASUNO in Spanish 1 Jun 94 p 18) PEMEX Gas Company and Basic Petrochemical Company -- PEMEX subsidiaries -- showed a 444 million new peso gross profit for the first quarter of 1994. In a press release, state-owned PEMEX reported that with larger revenues mainly from sales and a reduction in expenditures, the operation and investment budget showed a 570 million new peso surplus as opposed to the budgeted deficit of about 110 million new pesos. (Mexico City LA JORNADA in Spanish 6 Jun 94 p 48) SECOFI will apply, as of Saturday, a retaliatory quota of 45.74 percent on beef imports from the EU. SECOFI also reported that an "antidumping" investigation has been initiated into beef imports from the United States. The retaliatory quota was imposed on meat from the EU after it was confirmed that European nations still subsidize this sector. If the investigations into the U.S. imports demonstrate questionable trade practices, a retaliatory quota will also be imposed on these imports. (Mexico City NOTIMEX in Spanish 1651 GMT 3 Jun 94) The public sector, banking sector, and private industry's increase in contracting net international debts has prompted considerable growth in Mexico's overall foreign debt in the first quarter. According to SECOFI and the Bank of Mexico, foreign debt reached an all-time high in March at $119.179 billion. That is 6.7 percent higher than accumulated debt recorded in December 1993. Most foreign funds were used to finance export projects, as well as small and medium-sized businesses. (Mexico City EL FINANCIERO in Spanish 6 Jun 94 p 3A) During 1993, private foreign debt increased by $10.791 billion, of which 67.8 percent was incurred by the nonfinancial sector and 32.2 percent by the banking sector. After the renegotiation of government liabilities in 1990, the placing of bonds and securities became the main foreign financing tool for a total of $5.285 billion in 1993. Of this total, 73.6 percent corresponds to 44 emissions by nonbanking firms and 26.4 percent to six emissions by the banking sector. (Mexico City EL FINANCIERO in Spanish 6 Jun 94 pp 1, 10) SECOFI confirmed that in the first 1994 quarter, 19 percent of the 41.4 billion new pesos earmarked for public investments in 1994 had already been invested in public agencies and corporations. The total investment budget was increased by 7.324 billion new pesos to include investments in the National Solidarity program. The Group of Economists and Associates indicated that for the first time in several years public savings was not enough to finance public investments. (Mexico City EL FINANCIERO in Spanish 6 Jun 94 p 7)
