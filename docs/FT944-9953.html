941112 FT 12 NOV 94 / Weekend Money: Finance week BUSINESS Bill Clinton answered the US Republican party's sweeping mid-term election triumph by promising to block opposition attempts to 'turn the clock back' to the economic policies of the 1980s. John Major won cabinet backing for public spending cuts of between Pounds 4bn and Pounds 5bn in this month's Budget. The details were agreed after ministers accepted compromise proposals for cuts to the Pounds 8bn housing benefit budget. The UK government is expected to revise upwards its estimate of third-quarter economic growth after official figures showed industrial production grew unexpectedly strongly in the three months to September. Businesses are not investing enough because they expect too great a return on their investments, Michael Heseltine, the trade and industry secretary, told the CBI's annual conference. Lloyd's of London gave the go-ahead for individual members of the market to make a switch away from underwriting insurance policies on the basis of unlimited liability. A group of investment banks and former professional advisers to pension schemes controlled by the late Robert Maxwell offered to pay between Pounds 200m and Pounds 300m in a 'global settlement' of all outstanding claims. Norwich Union, one of the UK's largest insurers, announced plans to shed up to 2,000 jobs - a fifth of its workforce - over five years. Non-executive directors of the Cheltenham & Gloucester Building Society are to be urged by some of the society's members to think again about the board's agreement to the Lloyds Bank Pounds 1.8bn cash offer. The Treasury set out part of the regulatory framework it intends to put in place for open-ended investment companies when they are allowed in the UK next year. David Sieff, chairman of the National Lottery Charities Board, admitted the board, which could have Pounds 100m a year to distribute to charities, might not start making grants until late next year. COMPANIES The cost of cutting prices and jobs held back revenues and profitability at British Telecommunications in the first six months of the year. TeleWest, the largest UK cable operator, pushed ahead with flotation plans with a pathfinder prospectus suggesting a total value for the company of between Pounds 1.25bn and Pounds 1.45bn. British Sky Broadcasting, the satellite television venture, is pushing ahead with a December flotation in London and New York, expected to value the company at between Pounds 4bn and Pounds 4.5bn. Anglian Water announced a 20 per cent rise in pre-tax profits to Pounds 120.6m for the half-year to September 30 and declared a 9.6 per cent increase in its interim dividend from 7.3p to 8p. North West Water announced a wide-ranging review review of its activities, which will mean continued job losses. The Prudential Corporation, the UK's largest investing institution, is poised to follow the trend of other large companies and cut the length of its directors' service contracts. Burton, the clothing retail group that includes Debenhams, Burtons and Dorothy Perkins, more than doubled profits last year despite falling into the red in its multiples division. Production losses from the recent oil strike in Nigeria, and tighter refining margins in Asia, caused third-quarter profits for the Royal Dutch/Shell group to ease back 1 per cent to Pounds 853m. Royal Insurance, the composite insurer, forecast a 10 per cent reduction in its UK workforce over the next three years in spite of a near-tripling of pre-tax profits in the first nine months to Pounds 311m. Commercial Union, another composite insurer, reported pre-tax profits up Pounds 165m at Pounds 305m for the first nine months. Amstrad launched a scheme to allow consumers to buy its personal computers and faxes direct from the company through Amstrad Business Direct. Marks and Spencer, the UK's most profitable retailer, lifted interim profits by 15 per cent on the back of a 7 per cent increase in sales. It lifted continental European sales by 20 per cent. Cable & Wireless, the UK telecommunications group, announced a 'substantial cost and head-count reduction programme' at its UK-based Mercury Communications subsidiary while reporting an 11 per cent rise in interim pre-tax profits. A 7 per cent increase in airline passengers helped to fuel a Pounds 28m rise in pre-tax profits, to Pounds 265m, at privatised airports group BAA during the six months ended September. Forget soap wars - the latest battle is for babies' bottoms. Procter & Gamble, which produces Pampers, complained to the Advertising Standards Authority about magazine adverts by nappy rival Kimberly-Clark, which makes Huggies. Gartmore, the UK fund manager, formed a joint venture with NationsBank, America's third-biggest bank, which will allow it to sell international fund management expertise to US retail and pension fund investors. British Airways announced record second-quarter profits and an 'encouraging' outlook for the full year, as it reaped the rewards of a campaign to win business travellers and tighten cost controls. The figures were above expectations, and lifted BA shares. SG Warburg, the UK-based investment bank, said it would review staff levels and delay planned investments as part of a drive to cut costs after losing money on trading. Sony Electronic Publishing is to open a base in Liverpool as its European centre for design and development of computer and video games, creating 259 jobs. Henderson Administration, the fund management group, increased pre-tax profits by 22 per cent to Pounds 11.2m for the six months to September 30, compared with Pounds 9.2m last time. MARKETS The dollar rallied initially on foreign exchanges after the US mid-term elections, but Wall Street retreated after early gains amid concerns the result would undermine investor confidence. After a sluggish start to the week, the FT-SE 100 index rose - due partly to the US election. It broke the 3,100 level by Thursday's close, but retreated on Friday. Broadly-based selling in Tokyo depressed equities on Thursday as a decline in speculative stocks and privatised issues prompted arbitrage-linked sales. The Nikkei 225 average ended 159.03 lower at 19,264.85. UK utility stocks moved against a strong market trend on Wednesday after Labour's shadow chancellor, Gordon Brown, called for a one-off windfall tax on the 'excess profits' of the privatised utility companies. Two newcomers to the stock market, TLG, formerly the lighting division of Thorn EMI, and Tele-Cine made reasonably successful debuts on Thursday, both moving to modest premiums over their issue prices. Shares in Coal Investments, the mining company, lost another 7 per cent of their value on Wednesday after the company reported a 10-week delay in its mine re-opening programme and interim pre-tax losses of Pounds 10.2m.
