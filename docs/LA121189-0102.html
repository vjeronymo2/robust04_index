December 11, 1989, Monday, P.M. Final

PEOPLE'S PHARMACY: FDA AWARDS GENERIC DRUGS A 99% CLEAN BILL OF HEALTH

The dust is slowly starting to settle on the generic drug scandal. The Food and Drug Administration has completed a preliminary survey of top-selling generic medications and found that 99% were fine.

The news came as a tremendous relief for Henry. His father and grandfather both died of heart attacks at an early age. His own blood pressure is high and his cholesterol is elevated. To control these health threats, Henry takes Calan and Lasix for hypertension and Nicobid for cholesterol control.

Before questions were raised about the safety and effectiveness of generic drugs, Henry's monthly bill was $37. But Henry got scared after reading the headlines, and had his pharmacist fill his prescriptions with only brand names. His bill soared to over $175.

This was a hardship for Henry and his wife, but like millions of other Americans, he didn't want to risk his health on dubious generic drugs. Now, Henry should be reassured that the vast majority of generic drugs meet federal standards.

Answering the question, "Are the generic drugs on the market safe and effective?" FDA Commissioner Dr. Frank Young recently responded, "The answer appears to be a qualified yes."

The FDA's conclusion was reached after examining 30 of the most widely used generics, medicines like hydrochlorothiazide, prednisone, antibiotics such as amoxicillin, cephalexin and erythromycin, pain-relievers containing acetaminophen and codeine or propoxyphene, and many others.

The tests considered whether these generic medications were indeed chemically identical to their brand-name counterparts; if the active ingredients were as strong as those in the brand-name medicines, and if the pills dissolved properly -- a short-cut to determining if these medicines get into the bloodstream as they should.

The results show that in most cases generic drugs are as good as brand-name medications. Drugs that flunk the tests are being withdrawn.

The generic drug mess was brought to light as a result of whistle-blowing from within the industry itself. One of the major manufacturers of generic products felt that FDA officials had shown favoritism to its rivals.

Now that the feds have cracked down, physicians, pharmacists and patients should return to using generic drugs with confidence. The savings are too important to ignore.
