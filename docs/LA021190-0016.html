February 11, 1990, Sunday, Home Edition

ERMA BOMBECK: DREAM HOUSE CAN IRON OUT HASSLES

Family Circle magazine recently surveyed 22 million female readers to find out what features they wanted most in a dream house. Their ideas are incorporated in a house being built in suburban Atlanta.

Most of the things the women suggested, I held out for in a home we built seven years ago. Our utility room is not off the kitchen; it's near the bedrooms so I can "dunk shot" my sheets into the washer. There is a walk-in pantry where you walk in at your own risk, but my kitchen looks great. No doors cover my cabinets. I always felt like a game contestant standing in front of my old ones, wondering which door held the brown sugar and which one the ant traps.

There are no draperies in the house and no shower curtains. All I ever did anyway was to cut off the mildew periodically until all I had was a valance.

My ironing board is attached to the wall of the utility room and is up all the time. This way I can tell people, "I'd take my ironing board down, but it's attached to the wall."

I had a greenhouse window installed in the kitchen for "sick" plants that need TLC and more sun. It holds every live plant in the house.

Sounds like an enchanted cottage, doesn't it? Wrong!

Given a second chance, I'd make a lot of major changes. I don't mean just the doggie door we cut through an eight-inch wall and the dog ran away from home. I mean more technology.

The kitchen table would go. In its place I'd have a trough centered over a drain that you would just hose down between meals.

I'd install a conveyor belt from the garage to the pantry so I'd never again have to make eight trips on grocery day.

There would be a timer in the bathroom; at the end of an hour, the water would shut off, the lights, blow dryers and curling irons would go dead, and the door would swing open.

I haven't worked out all the bugs on this one, but I'd like a chute off the utility room for hand-washables. Anything left in the chute for three years would drop into a compost, and when broken down with a chemical, would turn into mulch for roses.

The trouble with most homes is that men design them and women clean them. I'll never forget the first time I visited Monticello, the Virginia home of Thomas Jefferson. The greatest statesman of all time, who had one of the most creative minds in the world, was a do-it-your-selfer. He introduced bunk beds to America (did God give us orangutan arms?), a dumbwaiter for wine (the food came from a kitchen a mile away), and just inside the hallway, he built a clock that measured time by a weight that went down the other side of the door. There is a hole in the floor because he misjudged the minutes.

I'm years ahead of my time.
