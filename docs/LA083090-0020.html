August 30, 1990, Thursday, Home Edition

STAGE REVIEW;

'AUGUST 29': CHICANO ANGST

You know the old saying about too many cooks. It can apply in the theater as well as in the kitchen, and it is to the credit of the Los Angeles Theatre Center's Latino Theatre Lab that the play they created collectively under the pseudonym Violeta Calles (a rakish stand-in for "violent streets") avoids spoiling the caldo.

"August 29," seen at a press preview Tuesday, is a rousing tribute not so much to the fallen newsman Ruben Salazar, but more perhaps to what he has come to represent in the 20 years since he was killed by a 10-inch tear-gas projectile that pierced his skull.

It was launched by a sheriff's deputy into the Silver Dollar Bar in East Los Angeles, where Salazar had stopped for a beer. He was covering the Chicano Moratorium of Aug. 29, 1970, when, nobody quite knows why, the peaceful march from Belvedere Park to Laguna Park (now Salazar Park), turned into a riot.

Salazar, who had been a Times bureau chief in Mexico City and still wrote a weekly column for the paper, was then news director at KMEX. Was he a victim of random violence or a targeted man? The question was never satisfactorily answered, but he became a powerful symbol in his death.

The lab ensemble and director Jose Luis Valenzuela have attempted to dramatize the present in the light of the past, hoping to assess how things have changed -- not necessarily for the better.

Evelina Fernandez plays Lucero, a Chicana activist at the time of the Moratorium who has since become a "chuppie" (a Chicano yuppie), complete with nice apartment, stylish clothes and BMW (license plate: PHD). A respected college professor and historian, Lucero is writing a book on Salazar that she considers her ticket to tenure.

But a favorite student becomes a victim of police brutality. And when Lucero resists pressure from friends and ex-comrades in activism to publicly denounce the incident, she's forced to confront herself and re-examine her values.

This takes us on a journey of reminiscence and re-enactment wherein Lucero is assisted, or at least prodded, by the presence of an imaginary Salazar (E.J. Castillo). His desire to know all that has happened since he died is a convenient device for a look at how times have changed and how Chicanos -- now known as "Hispanics," or what Lucero's friend Molly (Angela Moya) calls "the H word" -- have become as complacent and self-involved as the rest of the citizenry.

The result is a theatrically correct piece that pours a little salsa on the expected. Using rear projections of Aug. 29 footage, as well as vivid abstractions by set designers Douglas D. Smith and Gronk, it offers a nostalgic look at the spent ardor of the '70s and the bloodlessness of the '80s.

It does not quite sink to separating history into good guys and bad guys, but even while "August 29" acknowledges that Salazar's early writings were politically noncommittal and his lifestyle more Anglo than Chicano, it only glances off the issues rather than digging into them with bared fangs.

In addition, there is an aura of calculation that such manufactured pieces have trouble averting, and a few too many halos to pass around. But Valenzuela has directed with dispatch and the acting is balanced and buoyant throughout.

Castillo and Fernandez do a nimble, restrained dance around the issues, skillfully touching history and each other. Vanessa Marquez is a bit too soft to be believable as the militant young Lucy, while Sal Lopez is so sweetly jocular as Benny, the boyfriend who betrays her, that it's hard to accept him as the traitor he is in the end.

Abel Franco and Lupe Ontiveros, the lab's all-purpose Latino parents, are especially successful as Lucero's -- neither overly rambunctious and stereotypical nor overly subdued.

Moya and Mike Gomez are forceful as Lucero's activist friends (and in other cameos), and a nod goes to Tony Maggio, playing a virtual gallery of cops and other villains, to whom he manages to impart distinctive individual traits.

What one had hoped for was more meat on the bones. We get a listing and a naming of what's still terribly wrong for Hispanics/Latinos/Chicanos today -- plenty -- but not much more. The play is a Cook's Tour of issues, not a penetrating expedition into the underbrush.

'AUGUST 29'

A new play by Violeta Calles. Director Jose Luis Valenzuela. Sets and projections Gronk, Douglas D. Smith. Lights Douglas D. Smith. Costumes Gronk. Sound Mark Friedman. Original music Marcos Loya. Choreography Rene Olivas Gubernick. Stage manager Cari Norton. Cast E.J. Castillo, Evelina Fernandez, Abel Franco, Mike Gomez, Sal Lopez, Tony Maggio, Vanessa Marquez, Angela Moya, Lupe Ontiveros, Olga M. Perez.
