November 29, 1990, Thursday, Valley Edition

THE BUTCHER: GOOD WURST IS THE BEST OF MEATS

If you have read this column with any regularity throughout the years, you are aware that I am something of a "nut" for sausage.

To my taste, a good wurst is the best of meats. My wife and I had dinner not long ago at Campton Place, the lovely little hotel on Stockton Street in San Francisco that the talented young chef Bradley Ogden made famous. Ogden is no longer at Campton Place; but fortunately for those of us who live in Marin County, he moved to Marin and opened his own place -- the Lark Creek Inn in Larkspur. Campton Place was lucky to have Jan Birnbaum, another young and talented chef, there to take over when Ogden left.

Not only did he work with Ogden for some time, Birnbaum spent four years with Paul Prudhomme in New Orleans before heading off to New York to take over as chef at the Quilted Giraffe. He then stopped off for a spell to cook at the Rattlesnake Club in Denver before moving on to San Francisco. Campton Place is fortunate.

One night after dinner, Birnbaum joined us for coffee. In almost no time, after "How's Brad doing?" and "Have you heard from Paul?" the conversation turned to sausage -- Birnbaum is a sausage "nut" too. "I've got a couple new ones that I think you'll like. I'll fax you the recipes." He did, and I did, and I think you will like them also.

CHICKEN-FENNEL SAUSAGE

3 pounds chicken thigh meat

3 pounds pork butt

5 cloves garlic, chopped

1/4 cup fennel seeds

2 tablespoons paprika

2 teaspoons white pepper

1 tablespoon black pepper

2 teaspoons cayenne pepper

1/2 bunch Italian parsley, chopped

1 bunch fresh sage, chopped

1/3 cup Pernod

Hog casings

Cut chicken and pork into strips appropriate for grinding. Combine chicken, pork, garlic, fennel seeds, paprika, white pepper, black pepper, cayenne pepper, parsley, sage and Pernod in stainless steel or glass bowl. Let stand in refrigerator overnight to marinate.

Grind mixture twice through medium plate of meat grinder. Stuff into hog casings and twist into links. Makes about 6 pounds.

DUCK AND APPLE SAUSAGE

1 pound pork fatback

1 1/2 pounds duck meat

1 1/2 pounds pork butt

1 quart unfiltered apple juice, reduced to 1/2 cup

1/2 teaspoon ground cumin

1/2 teaspoon cayenne

1/2 teaspoon chili powder

1/2 teaspoon paprika

1 to 1 1/2 teaspoons seasoned salt

3 cups diced pippin apples

1 cup yellow mustard seeds

2 tablespoons finely chopped garlic

1 cup chopped Italian parsley

1 tablespoon chopped fresh sage

1 tablespoon chopped fresh thyme

1 tablespoon chopped fresh rosemary

Hog casings

Cut fatback, duck and pork butt into 1-inch cubes. Simmer apple juice in saucepan until reduced to about 1/2 cup, then cool.

Combine meats with apple juice, cumin, cayenne, chili powder, paprika, seasoned salt, diced apples, mustard seeds, garlic, parsley, sage, thyme and rosemary in stainless steel or glass bowl. Mix well. Let stand in refrigerator overnight to marinate.

Grind mixture twice through medium plate of meat grinder. Stuff into hog casings and twist into links. Makes about 4 pounds.

CHICKEN-MUSHROOM SAUSAGE

1 1/2 pounds chanterelle mushrooms

1 tablespoon olive oil

1 pound pork fatback

3 pounds pork butt

3 pounds chicken meat

2 bunches thyme, chopped

1 bunch Italian parsley, chopped

1 bunch sage, chopped

1 head garlic, chopped

1/2 cup rich chicken stock

3 tablespoons cayenne pepper

Salt, pepper

1/2 cup fennel seeds

Hog casings

Place mushrooms in roasting pan with olive oil. Roast in 250-degree oven until somewhat dry (some moisture needs to evaporate). Dice small enough to fit through piping nozzle of sausage stuffer.

Cut fatback, pork butt and chicken meat into 1-inch cubes. Combine meats with thyme, parsley, sage, garlic, chicken stock and cayenne. Season to taste with salt and pepper.

Grind once through large die and once through medium die. Fold in fennel seeds and mushrooms. Stuff into hog casings. Makes about 8 pounds.

Note: To cook, poach sausage lightly and then roast until cooked to medium rare. Cool and slice into medallions.
