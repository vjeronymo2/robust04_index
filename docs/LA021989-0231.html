February 19, 1989, Sunday, Orange County Edition

KEEPING A SECRET;

ROCKWELL'S NEW 'STAR WARS' FACILITY IN SEAL BEACH IS DESIGNED TO FOIL SPIES

Inside Building No. 90, a portable radio blares rock music as construction workers put the finishing touches on the most secretive defense plant ever built by Rockwell International Corp.

The sound of Bob Seger seems somehow incongruous with the serious purpose of this $30-million structure: the development of top-secret plans for the "Star Wars" missile-defense program.

The nearly completed four-story structure at Rockwell's Seal Beach facility is the centerpiece of the company's effort to land lucrative "Star Wars" contracts. Called the Strategic Defense Center, it is also part of an ambitious corporate experiment aimed at finding a better way to manage large, complex programs.

Although the future of the controversial "Star Wars" program is far from certain, Rockwell is convinced that its investment in the Strategic Defense Center will be money well spent.

Rockwell executives believe that in one form or another, "Star Wars" technology will be around for a long time. Like earlier space programs, they said, the research effort is expected to serve as the engine for many new technological breakthroughs with applications for other projects at the $12-billion aerospace and defense firm.

Rockwell has assembled at the center a group consisting of hundreds of top engineers, scientists and computer experts -- many of whom were teamed together on the Apollo space program 2 decades ago. About 1,000 people will work at the center when it is fully staffed.

The personnel assigned to the center will come mostly from Rockwell's other divisions throughout Southern California, where the company employs 35,000 people.

They will work inside a mostly windowless facility with an array of security precautions reminiscent of scenes from a spy thriller.

The building is a bewildering maze of stark hallways, soundproof rooms and offices protected by high-security locks, infrared sensors and double layers of doors. In the most sensitive areas, offices are protected by huge steel doors like those used in bank vaults.

The floor, ceiling and walls are extra-thick and loaded with special materials to prevent radio or computer transmissions from escaping outside, where they might be intercepted by spies using sophisticated electronic equipment.

Air-conditioning ducts have been sealed off to prevent intruders from crawling their way into trouble. And telephone lines have been secured.

Nothing, it seems, has been taken for granted. Even a simple thing like reminding employees to close a door behind them warrants a sign reading: "Please verify door is closed. For door problems, please call immediately." Construction workers had to undergo security checks to ensure they didn't plant eavesdropping devices in the building. That precaution was taken to prevent a replay of what happened at the U.S. Embassy in Moscow. Last year, former President Reagan ordered the partially completed embassy torn down and reconstructed because the building's Soviet-made concrete casings were found to be riddled with listening devices.

Access to some areas of the Rockwell facility is so difficult that center director George W. Jeffs, even with the use of a map, had difficulty locating the "battle management" program offices during a recent tour with a Times reporter.

The battle management program involves the development of computer systems to manage a "Star Wars" system. The battle management area includes large computer displays in which a "Star Wars" scenario could be simulated.

The center's main purpose will be to oversee Rockwell's work on the "Star Wars" program, formally known as the Strategic Defense Initiative.

El Segundo-based Rockwell was the nation's fourth-largest SDI contractor in 1988 and has current contracts totaling $778 million.

"Star Wars" is a proposed space-based, anti-missile system designed to act as both a shield and deterrent to a Soviet nuclear attack. Viewed by supporters as a strategic necessity and by critics as the ultimate boondoggle, billions of research dollars have been poured into the program during the past 6 years. Some of the program's prototype systems are beginning to undergo tests, but a decision about deployment is years away.

A number of Orange County defense contractors are getting a piece of the "Star Wars" action, including McDonnell Douglas Corp. in Huntington Beach, Hughes Aircraft Co. in Fullerton and Interstate Electronics Corp. in Anaheim.

Rockwell is a prime contractor on one of the centerpiece projects for "Star Wars," the Space-Based Interceptor program. That project involves a satellite designed to fire high-energy projectiles known as "smart rocks" at ballistic missiles, destroying them by the speed and force of impact. The company's other programs include electronic sensors, high-energy lasers and computer systems to manage a "Star Wars" battle.

The Space-Based Interceptor program was threatened last year after the Defense Acquisition Board issued an unfavorable review of the program, saying it would cost too much. However, recent changes in the design of the program have lowered its proposed costs from $52 billion to $17.7 billion.

Rockwell's investment in the new facility, which is expected to grow to $50 million over the next few years, is "a practical effort to help us marshal our resources from throughout the corporation," said Jeffs, the center's director and Rockwell's executive vice president for strategic defense and technologies.

The center represents "a new adventure in management," said Jeffs, a 42-year veteran of Rockwell who headed the company's work on the Apollo, Skylab and Space Shuttle programs. The center is unique, he said, because it is Rockwell's first attempt to the centralize management of programs involving many different divisions. Eight Rockwell units are presently involved in "Star Wars"-related activities.

"In the past, we would try to do projects out of a particular division," Jeffs said. "But the expertise of any one division might not be the best of fits for a given job. We can tailor this organization to better match the requirements of some of the new jobs that don't fall naturally within the business structure of our existing divisions."

By locating key personnel from various Rockwell units under a single roof, the corporation also hopes to foster a team spirit and eliminate problems of inter-division rivalries. "Co-locating people tends to totally eliminate the parochial self-interest" of any one division, a Rockwell official explained.

The center will perform program management, research, design and engineering work for various "Star Wars" systems. But the production of test hardware will be done at different Rockwell units, including Seal Beach and Anaheim in Orange County.

Unlike other Rockwell operations, the center won't be evaluated on the basis of how much it contributes to the corporation's bottom line. It will function as a "nonprofit" facility, with all profits gained from programs managed at the center being credited to the Rockwell division that performs the bulk of the work.

Rockwell is promoting the center as the first of its kind devoted solely to "Star Wars" programs. Company executives believe the center's high-security features will prove attractive to its security-conscious customers at the Pentagon, providing Rockwell with a potential leg up on its competitors.

When the company began construction of the center in May, 1987, plans called for a two-story, 100,000-square-foot structure -- half the size of what has been built. The building was designed so that two additional floors could be added later.

"We very quickly realized the building size wouldn't match our requirements," Jeffs said. "We concluded that from an overall point of view, it would be more economical to finish the other two stories now rather than add on to it later."

The Seal Beach site was chosen because it is close to several other "Star Wars" projects under way at the company's Satellite Systems Division and computer center, also in Seal Beach.

Although the center was designed specifically for SDI work, Jeffs said the facility could be used for future classified projects if the controversial "Star Wars" program encounters political problems in the years ahead.

Jeffs said he believes that the "Star Wars" program "for one reason or another, will continue for a long period of time."

"We feel that SDI is a good technology driver, just like the Apollo program was," Jeffs said. "It offers the possibility of a number of spinoffs" to other military and commercial businesses in which Rockwell is involved.
