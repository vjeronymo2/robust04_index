BFN [Report by Iva Toncheva and Andrey Sharkov] [Text] Sofia, June 28 (BTA) -- The leadership of the two major parliamentary forces -- the Bulgarian Socialist Party (BSP) and the Union of Democratic Forces (SDS) -- discussed the current political situation at their regular sessions today. Later on their leaders spoke before journalists of possible early elections. The SDS has been insisting on early general elections for almost 18 months. The Socialists declared themselves for early parliamentary elections at their latest congress. Representatives of the ethnic Turks' Movement for Rights and Freedoms (DPS), on the mandate of which the incumbent cabinet was formed, have also mentioned early elections this autumn. However, these elections have not been scheduled yet. On June 26 President Zhelyu Zhelev repeated his proposal to the political forces to reach an agreement on the date of the elections, which according to him, should be held this autumn. Prime Minister Lyuben Berov has also proposed an agreement between the political forces on early elections. The idea for an agreement between the political forces on the holding of elections was also launched last Sunday in an interview with BSP deputy leader Yanaki Stoilov. Before the elections it is necessary to tackle urgent tasks such as the launching of mass privatization, completion of the work of the liquidation councils (liquidating the former cooperative farms and returning the land to its former owners), normalization of Bulgaria's international finance and economic positions and guaranteeing this country's internal and external security, BSP leader Zhan Videnov said today. We should also decide on the withdrawal of the government and the giving up of the mandates of the parliamentary forces to form a new cabinet within this parliament, which would be obligatory for the faction of the Socialists too. It is also necessary to prepare the elections from a purely legal point of view -- amendments to the constitution if necessary, amendments to the electoral law and the laws on local government, Videnov said. According to SDS leader Filip Dimitrov, there are considerable differences between the stand of the SDS and the other political forces on the issue of early parliamentary elections. "Last Tuesday the SDS committed itself to early elections as a political force," Dimitrov stressed. According to him, it is not enough for leaders, including Socialists, to express their personal views in favour of early elections. The decision of the supreme SDS leadership of last Tuesday entails commitments which should be undertaken by all political forces insisting on early elections. According to the SDS, the political forces should commit themselves to no-confidence in the cabinet of Lyuben Berov, to giving up their mandate for forming a new cabinet within the framework of the incumbent parliament and to opposing any attempts to form such a cabinet. The leadership of the SDS and the DPS or part of them are insincere in their call for early parliamentary elections, BSP leader Zhan Videnov said today. By way of illustration he cited the draft of Prime Minister Berov of a framework agreement between the political forces providing for elections in the autumn of 1994, which according to him was spoilt by the DPS, and the preconditions and tactical schemes leading to elections set by SDS leaders during the negotiations held with the SDS last week at the request of the BSP. The BSP is seeking prospects for a left-of-centre agreement on pre-elections and post-elections actions, Videnov recalled. An official agreement between parliamentary and extraparliamentary forces on early elections and the accession to this agreement of state institutions would confirm the attitude of the different political formations on early elections this autumn, BSP deputy leader Yanaki Stoilov said at today's press conference. "I believe that such a proposal would help us verify the sincerity of all who have shown an attitude to early elections," he said. In an interview for today's issue of DEMOKRATSIYA SDS leader Filip Dimitrov says that what matters is not the word agreement but party commitment. "I have learnt from experience that an agreement may be full of traps, such as for example certain laws which will have to be passed," Dimitrov said.
